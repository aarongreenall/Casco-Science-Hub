7.5.1 Chemical and physical properties

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define and give examples of physical properties.
  2. Define and give examples of chemical properties.
  3. Distinguish between a physical change and a chemical change.

Describing Matter

Scientists describe substances using their **properties**. These properties can be divided into two main types: physical properties and chemical properties.

Analogy: Baking a Cake

Think about the ingredients for a cake sitting on the kitchen counter. We can use this to understand the difference between physical and chemical properties.

  • Physical Properties: These are the characteristics of the ingredients *before* you start baking. You can observe them without changing what the ingredients are. For example, flour is a white powder, sugar is made of white crystals, and an egg is an oval with a brittle shell.
  • Chemical Properties: These describe what the ingredients *do* when they react. You can't know these just by looking. A key chemical property of the mixture is its ability to **react with heat to form a cake**. You only see this property when you put it in the oven and cause a chemical change.

Physical vs. Chemical Changes

The key difference between a physical and a chemical change is whether a new substance is formed.

Physical Change

A physical change alters the form of a substance, but not its chemical identity. No new substance is made. These changes are often easy to reverse. Examples include changing state (melting ice into water), dissolving sugar in tea, or mixing sand and salt.

Chemical Change

A chemical change (or chemical reaction) results in the formation of one or more new substances with different properties. These changes are usually difficult or impossible to reverse. Examples include burning wood (which turns into ash and smoke) or a nail rusting.

Explanation: Physical vs. Chemical Changes

In-School Activity: Classifying Changes

Your teacher will give you a list of different changes. For each one, decide if it is a physical change or a chemical change, and be ready to explain your reasoning.

Activity 1: Classify the Changes
  • Melting an ice cube
  • Frying an egg
  • Dissolving salt in water
  • A firework exploding
  • Tearing a piece of paper
Activity 2: The Skittles Test
  1. Your teacher will give you a small pot of Skittles. Pour them out onto a piece of paper and mix them up. Is this a physical or chemical change? Can you easily separate the colours again?
  2. Now, take a few Skittles and chew them thoroughly. Spit the chewed mixture onto a paper plate. Is this a physical or chemical change? Can you separate the colours now? The chewing and the action of your saliva have caused an irreversible chemical change!

Important Safety Warning!

Do not do this at home your parents will not forgive you for showing them half chewed skittle spittle on a plate....no matter how much they might like science.
