8.3.2 Gas Exchange and the Respiratory System

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will describe the structure of the human respiratory system and the process of gas exchange in the lungs.

The Respiratory System:

  • Function: To take in oxygen and remove carbon dioxide from the body.
  • Main Organs:
    • Lungs: The primary organs where gas exchange occurs.
    • Trachea (windpipe): The tube that carries air to the lungs.
    • Bronchi: The trachea divides into two bronchi, one for each lung.
    • Bronchioles: The bronchi branch into smaller tubes called bronchioles.
    • Alveoli (air sacs): Tiny air sacs at the end of the bronchioles where gas exchange happens.
    • Diaphragm: A large muscle below the lungs that helps with breathing.

Gas Exchange in the Alveoli:

  • The alveoli are the site of gas exchange between the air and the blood.
  • They have special features for efficient gas exchange:
    • Large surface area: Millions of alveoli provide a huge surface for gases to diffuse across.
    • Thin walls: The walls of the alveoli and the surrounding capillaries are only one cell thick, providing a short diffusion distance.
    • Good blood supply: A dense network of capillaries surrounds each alveolus, maintaining a steep concentration gradient for gases.
    • Moist surface: Gases dissolve in the moist lining before diffusing.
  • Process:
    • Oxygen from the inhaled air diffuses from the alveoli (high concentration) into the blood (low concentration).
    • Carbon dioxide from the blood (high concentration) diffuses into the alveoli (low concentration) to be exhaled.
