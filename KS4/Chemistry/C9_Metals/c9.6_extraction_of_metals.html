C9.6 Extraction of metals

Most metals are found naturally in the Earth's crust as compounds within **ores**, rather than as pure elements. The process of obtaining a metal from its ore is called **extraction**. The method used for extraction depends on the metal's position in the reactivity series, as this indicates its chemical stability and tendency to form positive ions.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the ease in obtaining metals from their ores, related to the position of the metal in the reactivity series.
  2. State that the main ore of aluminium is bauxite, and that aluminium is extracted by electrolysis.
  3. State that iron from hematite is extracted by reduction of iron(III) oxide in the blast furnace.
  4. Describe the extraction of iron from hematite in the blast furnace, limited to: the burning of carbon (coke) to provide heat and produce carbon dioxide C + O$_2$ $\rightarrow$ CO$_2$; the reduction of carbon dioxide to carbon monoxide C + CO$_2$ $\rightarrow$ 2CO; the reduction of iron(III) oxide by carbon monoxide Fe$_2$O$_3$ + 3CO $\rightarrow$ 2Fe + 3CO$_2$; the thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate/limestone to produce calcium oxide CaCO$_3$ $\rightarrow$ CaO + CO$_2$; the formation of slag CaO + SiO$_2$ $\rightarrow$ CaSiO$_3$.

Extraction Methods and Reactivity Series:

The method used to extract a metal from its ore is directly related to its position in the **reactivity series**:

  • **Very Reactive Metals (above Carbon in reactivity series, e.g., Potassium, Sodium, Calcium, Magnesium, Aluminium):** These metals are very stable as compounds and have a strong tendency to form positive ions. They are extracted by **electrolysis** of their molten compounds, which requires a large input of electrical energy.
  • **Moderately Reactive Metals (below Carbon but above Hydrogen, e.g., Zinc, Iron):** These metals can be extracted by **reduction using carbon** (e.g., in a blast furnace). Carbon is cheaper than electricity and is reactive enough to remove oxygen from these metal oxides at high temperatures.
  • **Least Reactive Metals (below Hydrogen, e.g., Copper, Silver, Gold):** These metals are often found as uncombined elements (native metals) or can be easily extracted by heating their ores with a reducing agent, or even just by heating.

Extraction of Aluminium:

The main ore of aluminium is **bauxite** (impure aluminium oxide, Al$_2$O$_3$). Since aluminium is a very reactive metal (above carbon in the reactivity series), it cannot be extracted by reduction with carbon. Instead, aluminium is extracted by **electrolysis** of molten aluminium oxide (dissolved in molten cryolite to lower its melting point). This process is highly energy-intensive.

*Figure: Bauxite ore, the primary source of aluminium.*

Extraction of Iron in the Blast Furnace:

Iron is extracted from its main ore, **hematite** (iron(III) oxide, Fe$_2$O$_3$), by **reduction in a blast furnace**. This is a continuous process involving several key reactions:

  • **1. Burning of Carbon (Coke):** Coke (carbon) burns in hot air to provide heat and produce carbon dioxide. * `C(s) + O2(g) -> CO2(g)` (Exothermic, provides heat)
  • **2. Reduction of Carbon Dioxide to Carbon Monoxide:** Carbon dioxide reacts with more hot coke to form carbon monoxide, which is the main reducing agent. * `C(s) + CO2(g) -> 2CO(g)`
  • **3. Reduction of Iron(III) Oxide by Carbon Monoxide:** Carbon monoxide reduces the iron(III) oxide to molten iron. * `Fe2O3(s) + 3CO(g) -> 2Fe(l) + 3CO2(g)`
  • **4. Thermal Decomposition of Calcium Carbonate (Limestone):** Limestone (calcium carbonate) decomposes in the heat to produce calcium oxide, which removes impurities. * `CaCO3(s) -> CaO(s) + CO2(g)`
  • **5. Formation of Slag:** Calcium oxide (a basic oxide) reacts with acidic impurities in the ore, primarily silicon dioxide (SiO$_2$), to form molten **slag** (calcium silicate). Slag floats on top of the molten iron and is tapped off. * `CaO(s) + SiO2(s) -> CaSiO3(l)`
