12.2 Centripetal acceleration

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that an object travelling in a circle at constant speed has an acceleration.
  2. Explain that the acceleration is directed towards the centre of the circle.
  3. Derive and use the formula for centripetal acceleration $a = v^2/r$.
  4. Derive and use the formula for centripetal acceleration $a = r\omega^2$.

LO1 & LO2: The Nature of Centripetal Acceleration

A common misconception is that an object moving in a circle at a constant speed has zero acceleration. This is incorrect. **Acceleration** is defined as the rate of change of **velocity**, and velocity is a vector quantity with both magnitude (speed) and direction.

For an object in uniform circular motion, its speed is constant, but its direction of motion is continuously changing. Since the velocity vector is changing, the object **must be accelerating**. This acceleration, directed towards the centre of the circle, is called **centripetal acceleration**.

LO3 & LO4: Formulae for Centripetal Acceleration

Detailed Derivation using Similar Triangles

We can derive the formula for centripetal acceleration by considering the geometry of the motion over a very small time interval, $\Delta t$.

The "position triangle" is formed by the two radii ($r$) and the arc length ($s$) the object travels. The "velocity triangle" is formed by the initial velocity ($v_A$), the final velocity ($v_B$), and the change in velocity ($\Delta v$).

Because the speed is constant, $|v_A| = |v_B| = v$, so the velocity triangle is isosceles. For a very small time interval, the arc length $s$ is almost a straight line, making the position triangle also isosceles. These two triangles are geometrically similar.

1. Set up the ratio of corresponding sides from the similar triangles:

$$ \frac{\text{Change in Velocity}}{\text{Velocity}} = \frac{\text{Arc Length}}{\text{Radius}} $$

$$ \frac{\Delta v}{v} = \frac{s}{r} $$

2. Express arc length in terms of speed and time:

For an object moving at a constant speed $v$ for a time $\Delta t$, the distance travelled is $s = v \Delta t$.

3. Substitute this expression for $s$ into the ratio from step 1:

$$ \frac{\Delta v}{v} = \frac{v \Delta t}{r} $$

4. Rearrange to find an expression for the change in velocity, $\Delta v$:

$$ \Delta v = \frac{v^2}{r} \Delta t $$

5. Use the definition of acceleration, $a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$:

$$ a = \frac{\frac{v^2}{r} \Delta t}{\Delta t} $$

6. The $\Delta t$ terms cancel, giving the final formula:

$$ a = \frac{v^2}{r} $$

7. To derive the second form, substitute $v = r\omega$:

$$ a = \frac{(r\omega)^2}{r} = \frac{r^2\omega^2}{r} $$

$$ a = r\omega^2 $$

Worked Example: Calculating Centripetal Acceleration

A car travels at a constant speed of 12 m s⁻¹ around a circular track of radius 50 m. Calculate its centripetal acceleration.

1. Identify the known variables: $v = 12$ m s⁻¹, $r = 50$ m.

2. Choose the appropriate formula: We know $v$ and $r$, so we use $a = v^2/r$.

$$ a = \frac{v^2}{r} $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ a = \frac{(12)^2}{50} = \frac{144}{50} = 2.88 \text{ m s}^{-2} $$

The acceleration is 2.9 m s⁻² towards the centre of the track.
