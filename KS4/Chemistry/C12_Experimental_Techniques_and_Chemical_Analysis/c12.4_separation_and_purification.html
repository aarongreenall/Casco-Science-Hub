C12.4 Separation and purification

In chemistry, mixtures are common, and often we need to separate their components or purify a desired substance. Various **separation and purification techniques** are employed based on the different physical properties of the substances in the mixture. This section explores common methods like filtration, crystallization, and distillation, and how to assess the purity of substances.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe and explain methods of separation and purification using: a suitable solvent; filtration; crystallisation; simple distillation; fractional distillation.
  2. Suggest suitable separation and purification techniques, given information about the substances involved.
  3. Identify substances and assess their purity using melting point and boiling point information.
  4. Describe how to test for the purity of water using melting point and boiling point.
  5. State that distilled water is used in practical chemistry rather than tap water because it contains fewer chemical impurities.

Separation Techniques:

The choice of separation technique depends on the nature of the mixture and the properties of its components (e.g., solubility, boiling point, particle size).

1. Using a Suitable Solvent:

If a mixture contains components with different solubilities, a **suitable solvent** can be used to dissolve one component, leaving the others undissolved. For example, salt can be separated from sand by dissolving the salt in water (the solvent), and then filtering off the sand.

2. Filtration:

**Filtration** is used to separate an insoluble solid from a liquid (or solution). The mixture is passed through a filter medium (like filter paper) that allows the liquid (filtrate) to pass through but traps the solid (residue).

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the setup for filtration, separating an insoluble solid from a liquid.*

3. Crystallisation:

**Crystallisation** is used to obtain a pure solid from a solution. The solvent is evaporated (often by heating) until the solution becomes concentrated (saturated). As the solution cools, the dissolved solid becomes less soluble and forms pure crystals, which can then be filtered off and dried.

*Figure: Copper(II) sulfate crystals formed by crystallization from a solution.*

4. Simple Distillation:

**Simple distillation** is used to separate a soluble solid from a liquid (e.g., salt from water) or to separate two liquids with significantly different boiling points. The mixture is heated, the liquid with the lower boiling point evaporates, its vapor is then condensed back into a liquid, and collected. The solid or the liquid with the higher boiling point remains in the distillation flask.

*Figure: Diagram of a simple distillation apparatus.*

5. Fractional Distillation:

**Fractional distillation** is used to separate liquids with similar (but different) boiling points (e.g., ethanol from water, or petroleum fractions). It uses a fractionating column placed between the distillation flask and the condenser, providing a large surface area for repeated vaporization and condensation cycles, leading to a better separation.

*Figure: Diagram of a fractional distillation apparatus.*

Assessing Purity: Melting and Boiling Points:

The purity of a substance can be assessed by comparing its measured melting point or boiling point to the known, pure values.

  • **Pure substances** have sharp and specific melting points and boiling points.
  • **Impure substances** (mixtures) will melt or boil over a range of temperatures, and their melting point will be lower and boiling point higher than the pure substance.

**Distilled water** is used in practical chemistry rather than tap water because it contains fewer chemical impurities. Its purity can be tested by checking its boiling point (should be 100Â°C at standard pressure) and its electrical conductivity (should be very low).
