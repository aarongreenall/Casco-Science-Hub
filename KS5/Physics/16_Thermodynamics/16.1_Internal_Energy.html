16.1 Internal energy

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that internal energy is determined by the state of the system and that it can be expressed as the sum of a random distribution of kinetic and potential energies associated with the molecules of a system.
  2. Relate a rise in temperature of an object to an increase in its internal energy.

LO1: The Nature of Internal Energy

The **internal energy ($U$)** of a system is a fundamental concept in thermodynamics. It is determined by the **state of the system**, which is defined by its macroscopic properties like pressure ($p$), volume ($V$), and temperature ($T$). If these properties are known, the internal energy is fixed.

At a microscopic level, the internal energy is the sum of the energies of all the individual molecules within the system. This total energy can be broken down into two components:

$$ U = \sum E_k + \sum E_p $$

Random Kinetic Energy ($\sum E_k$)

This is the energy the molecules have due to their random, disordered motion. This motion can be translational (moving from place to place), rotational (spinning), or vibrational (atoms within a molecule oscillating).

Random Potential Energy ($\sum E_p$)

This is the energy stored in the electrostatic intermolecular forces between the molecules. It depends on the separation of the molecules. In solids and liquids, where molecules are close, these forces are significant. In an ideal gas, the molecules are assumed to be so far apart that these forces are negligible, so the potential energy component is taken to be zero.

LO2: Temperature and Internal Energy

Temperature is directly related to the internal energy of a system. Specifically:

The thermodynamic temperature (in kelvin) of a substance is a measure of the **average random kinetic energy** of its constituent molecules.

When you heat an object (without changing its state), you are transferring thermal energy to it. This supplied energy increases the average random kinetic energy of the molecules. Since temperature is a measure of this average kinetic energy, the temperature of the object rises.

Because the internal energy $U$ is the sum of the kinetic and potential energies, an increase in the kinetic energy of the molecules directly leads to an **increase in the internal energy** of the object.

Example: Heating a Block of Metal

Consider a solid block of copper at 20 Â°C. Its copper atoms are vibrating about fixed positions in a crystal lattice.

1. **Initial State:** The internal energy is the sum of the kinetic energy of these vibrations and the potential energy stored in the bonds between the atoms.

2. **Heating:** If we place the block over a Bunsen burner, thermal energy is transferred to it. This energy is absorbed by the copper atoms, causing them to vibrate more vigorously and with a larger amplitude.

3. **Result:** The average random kinetic energy of the atoms increases. This is observed macroscopically as a rise in the block's temperature. Because the kinetic energy component has increased, the total internal energy of the copper block has also increased.
