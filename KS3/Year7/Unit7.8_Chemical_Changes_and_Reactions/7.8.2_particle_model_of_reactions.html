7.8.2 Particle model of reactions

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe a chemical reaction as a rearrangement of atoms.
  2. Define reactants and products.
  3. Explain that mass is conserved in a chemical reaction.

Rearranging Atoms

What actually happens during a chemical reaction? The **particle model** helps us to understand. In a chemical reaction, no atoms are created or destroyed. They are simply **rearranged** to form new substances.

  • The starting substances are called the **reactants**.
  • The new substances that are formed are called the **products**.

Because atoms are only rearranged and none are lost or gained, the total mass of the products is always equal to the total mass of the reactants. This is known as the **conservation of mass**.

Analogy: LEGO Models

Imagine you have two LEGO models: a car and a plane. These are your **reactants**. If you take them completely apart, you are left with a pile of individual bricks. This is like the chemical bonds in the reactants breaking. You can then use the exact same pile of bricks to build two new models: a house and a boat. These are your **products**. You haven't created or destroyed any LEGO bricks; you have just rearranged them to make something new. This is exactly what happens to atoms in a chemical reaction.

In-School Activity: Modelling Reactions

You can use molecular model kits or different coloured LEGO bricks to build your own models of reactions and prove that mass is conserved.

Your Task:

Model the reaction between carbon and oxygen to make carbon dioxide: $C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2$.

  1. Choose one colour of brick to represent carbon atoms and another colour to represent oxygen atoms.
  2. Build your reactants: one single carbon atom, and one molecule of oxygen (two oxygen atoms bonded together).
  3. "React" them by taking them apart.
  4. Use the exact same bricks to build your product: one molecule of carbon dioxide (one carbon atom bonded to two oxygen atoms).
  5. Count the number of each type of atom before and after. You should see that you have the same number of atoms at the end as you started with!
