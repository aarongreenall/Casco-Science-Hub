P4.3 Electric current

Electric current is the flow of electric charge. It is a fundamental concept in electricity, powering everything from small electronic devices to entire cities. Understanding electric current involves knowing how it is measured, the difference between direct and alternating current, and how current behaves in series and parallel circuits.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Know that electric current is related to the flow of charge.
  2. Know that electric current in metals is related to the flow of electrons.
  3. Describe the use of ammeters (analogue and digital) with different ranges.
  4. Know the difference between direct current (d.c) and alternating current (a.c.).
  5. Know that the current at every point in a series circuit is the same.
  6. Know that, for a parallel circuit, the current from the source is larger than the current in each branch.
  7. Define electric current as the charge passing a point per unit time; recall and use the equation: $I = Q/t$.
  8. State that conventional current is from positive to negative and that the flow of electrons is from negative to positive.
  9. Recall and use in calculations, the fact that: the sum of the currents entering a junction in a parallel circuit is equal to the sum of the currents that leave the junction.

Understanding Electric Current:

**Electric current** is defined as the rate of flow of electric charge. In metals, this flow of charge is primarily due to the movement of **delocalised electrons**. The standard SI unit for electric current is the Ampere (A).

The relationship between current ($I$), charge ($Q$), and time ($t$) is given by the equation:

$I = \frac{Q}{t}$

Where $I$ is current in Amperes, $Q$ is charge in Coulombs, and $t$ is time in seconds.

*Figure: Illustration showing the flow of electric current (conventional current from positive to negative).*

Measuring Current (Ammeters):

Current is measured using an **ammeter**. Ammeters must always be connected in **series** within a circuit so that the current flows directly through them. They have a very low resistance to avoid affecting the circuit's current. Ammeters can be analogue (with a needle and scale) or digital (with a numerical display) and come in various ranges to measure different magnitudes of current.

Direct Current (d.c.) vs. Alternating Current (a.c.):

There are two main types of electric current:

  • **Direct Current (d.c.):** Charge flows in only one direction. Examples include current from batteries or solar cells.
  • **Alternating Current (a.c.):** Charge periodically reverses its direction. This is the type of current supplied by mains electricity in homes and businesses.

While electrons flow from negative to positive terminals, **conventional current** is defined as flowing from the positive terminal to the negative terminal. This convention is widely used in circuit diagrams and calculations.

Current in Series and Parallel Circuits:

The behavior of current differs significantly in series and parallel circuits:

  • **Series Circuits:** The current is **the same at every point** in a series circuit. There is only one path for the charge to flow, so the same amount of charge passes through each component in a given time.
  • **Parallel Circuits:** The current from the source is **larger than the current in each individual branch**. This is because the total current splits among the different parallel branches. The sum of the currents entering a junction in a parallel circuit is equal to the sum of the currents that leave the junction (Kirchhoff's First Law, which is a statement of conservation of charge).
