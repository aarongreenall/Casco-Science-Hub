9.4.3 Simple and Giant Structures

In this lesson, you will learn that:

  1. Elements and compounds exist in structures (simple or giant), and this influences their physical properties.

Beyond Single Molecules

Knowing how atoms bond together is only half the story. The way these atoms and molecules are arranged on a large scale determines the physical properties of a substance, like its melting point and whether it conducts electricity. We can classify substances into two main types of structure: **simple molecular structures** and **giant structures**.

Analogy: Building with Bricks

Imagine you have thousands of identical LEGOÂ® bricks (these are your molecules).

  • You could leave them as a loose pile in a box. The bricks themselves are strong, but there's nothing really holding them to each other. It's easy to separate them. This is like a **simple molecular structure**.
  • Alternatively, you could click every single brick together to build a huge, solid wall. Now, it's very difficult to break apart. This is like a **giant structure**.

Simple Molecular Structures

These substances are made of individual, separate molecules, such as water ($H_2O$), oxygen ($O_2$), and carbon dioxide ($CO_2$). A key point to understand is that while the covalent bonds *inside* each molecule are very strong, the forces of attraction *between* the molecules (intermolecular forces) are very weak.

Properties:

  • Low Melting and Boiling Points: Because the forces between molecules are weak, it takes very little energy to overcome them and separate the molecules. This is why these substances are often gases or liquids at room temperature.
  • Do Not Conduct Electricity: The molecules have no overall charge, and there are no free-moving electrons or ions to carry an electrical current.

In water, the forces *between* the molecules are weak and easily broken.

Giant Structures

In a giant structure, there are no individual molecules. Instead, billions of atoms are joined together by a vast network of strong bonds, forming a crystal lattice.

1. Giant Covalent Structures

All the atoms are joined to each other by strong covalent bonds. Examples include diamond and graphite (both made of carbon) and silicon dioxide (sand).

Properties:

They have extremely high melting and boiling points because a huge amount of energy is needed to break the millions of strong covalent bonds throughout the structure.

2. Giant Ionic Structures

These are formed from positive and negative ions held together by strong electrostatic forces of attraction. An example is sodium chloride (table salt).

Properties:

They have high melting and boiling points because a lot of energy is needed to overcome the strong forces between the ions. They can conduct electricity when molten or dissolved because the ions are then free to move and carry charge.
