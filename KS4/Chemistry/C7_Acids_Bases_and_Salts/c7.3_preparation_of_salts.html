C7.3 Preparation of salts

Salts are ionic compounds formed when the hydrogen ion of an acid is replaced by a metal ion or an ammonium ion. They are a vast and diverse group of compounds with numerous applications. This section explores various methods for preparing both soluble and insoluble salts, along with important related concepts like hydration.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the preparation, separation and purification of soluble salts by reaction of an acid with: an alkali by titration; excess metal; excess insoluble base; excess insoluble carbonate (the general solubility rules for salts are not required).
  2. Define a hydrated substance as a substance that is chemically combined with water and an anhydrous substance as a substance containing no water.
  3. Describe the preparation of insoluble salts by precipitation (the general solubility rules for salts are not required).

Preparation of Soluble Salts:

Soluble salts can be prepared using several methods, typically involving the reaction of an acid with a suitable reactant, followed by separation and purification steps (filtration, evaporation, crystallization).

1. Acid + Alkali (Titration Method):

This method is used when both the acid and the alkali (soluble base) are soluble. A **titration** is performed to determine the exact volume of acid needed to neutralize a known volume of alkali, ensuring a pure salt solution is formed. The resulting solution is then evaporated to crystallize the salt.

  • Example: `Hydrochloric Acid + Sodium Hydroxide -> Sodium Chloride + Water`
  • `HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) -> NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)`

*Figure: A typical titration setup, used to find the exact volumes for neutralization.*

2. Acid + Excess Metal:

This method is used for reactive metals (above hydrogen in the reactivity series) that react with acids. An excess of the metal is used to ensure all the acid reacts. The excess metal is then removed by filtration, and the salt solution is crystallized.

  • Example: `Zinc + Sulfuric Acid -> Zinc Sulfate + Hydrogen`
  • `Zn(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> ZnSO4(aq) + H2(g)`

3. Acid + Excess Insoluble Base:

This method is used when the base is insoluble in water. An excess of the insoluble base is added to the acid, stirred, and heated gently to ensure complete reaction. The excess insoluble base is then removed by filtration, and the salt solution is crystallized.

  • Example: `Copper(II) Oxide + Sulfuric Acid -> Copper(II) Sulfate + Water`
  • `CuO(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> CuSO4(aq) + H2O(l)`

4. Acid + Excess Insoluble Carbonate:

Similar to using an insoluble base, an excess of the insoluble carbonate is added to the acid. The reaction produces carbon dioxide gas, which bubbles off. The excess carbonate is filtered off, and the salt solution is crystallized.

  • Example: `Magnesium Carbonate + Sulfuric Acid -> Magnesium Sulfate + Water + Carbon Dioxide`
  • `MgCO3(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> MgSO4(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g)`

*Figure: Copper(II) sulfate crystals formed by crystallization from a solution.*

Hydrated and Anhydrous Substances:

Many salts can incorporate water molecules into their crystal structure.

  • A **hydrated substance** is a substance that is chemically combined with water molecules (known as water of crystallization). For example, hydrated copper(II) sulfate is CuSO$_4 \cdot 5H_2O$, which is blue.
  • An **anhydrous substance** is a substance containing no water of crystallization. Anhydrous copper(II) sulfate is CuSO$_4$, which is white.

Preparation of Insoluble Salts (Precipitation):

**Insoluble salts** are prepared by a method called **precipitation**. This involves mixing two soluble salt solutions, where the cation from one solution combines with the anion from the other to form an insoluble product (the precipitate). The precipitate can then be separated from the solution by filtration, washed, and dried.

  • Example: `Lead(II) Nitrate(aq) + Sodium Chloride(aq) -> Lead(II) Chloride(s) + Sodium Nitrate(aq)`
  • `Pb(NO3)2(aq) + 2NaCl(aq) -> PbCl2(s) + 2NaNO3(aq)`
  • Here, lead(II) chloride (PbCl$_2$) is an insoluble salt and forms a white precipitate.

*Figure: Illustration of a precipitation reaction, where two soluble solutions combine to form an insoluble solid.*
