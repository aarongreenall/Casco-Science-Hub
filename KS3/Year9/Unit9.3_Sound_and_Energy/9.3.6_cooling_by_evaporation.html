9.3.6 Cooling by Evaporation

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain how evaporation causes cooling.

Evaporation:

  • The process of a liquid turning into a gas at the surface of the liquid, without the liquid having to reach its boiling point.

The Cooling Effect:

  • The particles in a liquid have a range of kinetic energies. Some have high energy, and some have low energy.
  • To evaporate, the most energetic particles (the "hottest" ones) at the surface must have enough energy to overcome the forces holding them in the liquid and escape as a gas.
  • When these high-energy particles leave, the average kinetic energy of the remaining particles in the liquid decreases.
  • Since temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy, a decrease in the average kinetic energy means the temperature of the remaining liquid drops. It becomes cooler.

Factors Affecting the Rate of Evaporation (and thus cooling):

  • Temperature: Higher temperature means more particles have enough energy to escape. Evaporation is faster.
  • Surface Area: A larger surface area allows more particles to be at the surface, ready to escape. Evaporation is faster.
  • Air Movement (Wind): Wind blows away the evaporated particles from above the liquid surface, maintaining a steep concentration gradient and allowing more particles to evaporate. Evaporation is faster.
  • Humidity: Lower humidity (drier air) means there is less water vapor already in the air, so it's easier for more liquid to evaporate. Evaporation is faster.

Examples:

  • Sweating: Sweat evaporates from your skin, taking heat energy from your body and cooling you down.
  • Feeling cold after getting out of a swimming pool.
