7.2.3 Metals and non-metals properties

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Identify the location of metals and non-metals on the periodic table.
  2. Describe the typical physical properties of metals.
  3. Describe the typical physical properties of non-metals.
  4. Compare the properties of metals and non-metals.

A Great Divide

The periodic table has a clear dividing line. The vast majority of elements, found on the left-hand side and in the middle, are **metals**. The elements on the top-right of the table are **non-metals**. This division is one of the most important ways we classify matter.

Analogy: The City Map

Let's revisit our "City Map" of elements. The dividing line on the periodic table is like a major river or highway that separates the city into two very different districts.

  • Metals are the buildings in the main, bustling **industrial and commercial districts**. They are strong, shiny, and have excellent power and communication lines (they are good conductors of heat and electricity).
  • Non-Metals are the buildings and structures in the quieter, **residential and park areas** on the other side of the city. They might be solid but brittle (like brick houses), or gases (like the air in the parks), and they don't have the same heavy-duty power infrastructure (they are poor conductors, or insulators).

Comparing Properties

Metals and non-metals have very different sets of typical physical properties.

Property Typical Metals Typical Non-Metals
AppearanceShiny (lustrous)Dull
State at Room Temp.Solid (except mercury)Solid, liquid, or gas
DensityHighLow
MalleabilityMalleable (can be hammered into shape)Brittle (shatter if hammered)
ConductivityGood electrical and thermal conductorsPoor conductors (insulators)
SonoritySonorous (make a ringing sound when hit)Not sonorous

Explanation: Metals vs. Non-Metals

In-School Experiment: Classifying Elements

You can test the properties of different elements to decide if they are metals or non-metals.

Equipment:
  • Samples of different elements (e.g., copper wire, iron nail, carbon rod, sulfur powder, aluminium foil)
  • A low-voltage power supply, a bulb, and connecting wires
  • A small hammer
Method:
  1. Appearance: Observe each sample. Is it shiny or dull? Record your observations.
  2. Conductivity: Set up a simple circuit with the power supply and bulb. Use each element sample to complete the circuit. Does the bulb light up? If it does, the element is a conductor.
  3. Malleability: Gently tap the solid samples with a hammer (wear safety goggles!). Does the material bend and flatten (malleable), or does it shatter (brittle)?
  4. Conclusion: Based on your results, classify each element as a metal or a non-metal.
