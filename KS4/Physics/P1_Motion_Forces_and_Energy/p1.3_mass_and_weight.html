P1.3 Mass and weight

In physics, the terms 'mass' and 'weight' are often used interchangeably in everyday language, but they represent distinct physical quantities. Understanding the difference between them is crucial for accurate scientific descriptions and calculations.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State that mass is a measure of the quantity of matter in an object.
  2. State that weight is the gravitational force on an object that has mass.
  3. Define gravitational field strength ($g$) as the gravitational force per unit mass; recall and use the equation $W=mg$ and so $g=W/m$ and know that near to the surface of the Earth, $g$ is approximately 9.8 N/kg.
  4. Describe, and use the concept of, weight as the effect of a gravitational field on a mass.
  5. Know that gravitational field strength is equivalent to the acceleration of free fall.
  6. Know that the acceleration of free fall ($g$) for an object near to the surface of the Earth is approximately constant and is approximately 9.8 m/s$^2$.

Understanding Mass and Weight:

**Mass** is a fundamental property of matter. It is a measure of the amount of 'stuff' an object contains and is constant regardless of where the object is located in the universe. The standard unit for mass is the kilogram (kg). You would measure mass using a balance.

**Weight**, on the other hand, is the force of gravity acting on an object's mass. Unlike mass, weight is not constant; it depends on the strength of the gravitational field the object is in. The unit for weight is the Newton (N), as it is a force. You would measure weight using a force meter (or spring balance).

The relationship between weight ($W$), mass ($m$), and gravitational field strength ($g$) is given by the equation:

$W = m \times g$

From this, we can derive the gravitational field strength:

$g = \frac{W}{m}$

Near the surface of the Earth, $g$ is approximately 9.8 N/kg. This value is also equivalent to the acceleration of free fall, which is approximately 9.8 m/s$^2$. This means that all objects, regardless of their mass, accelerate downwards at the same rate in a vacuum near Earth's surface.
