8.9.3 Electromagnets

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe how to make an electromagnet.
  2. Explain that an electromagnet is a temporary magnet that can be switched on and off.
  3. Identify factors that can change the strength of an electromagnet.

What is an Electromagnet?

We know that an electric current creates a magnetic field. An **electromagnet** is a special type of magnet that uses this principle. It is a temporary magnet made by passing an electric current through a coil of wire.

The simplest electromagnet is a coil of wire wrapped around an iron core (like an iron nail). When the current is switched on, the iron core becomes a strong magnet. When the current is switched off, the magnetism disappears. This ability to switch the magnetic field on and off is what makes electromagnets so useful.

Analogy: The Switchable Superhero

A normal permanent magnet is like a superhero who is always strong. An **electromagnet** is like a superhero who can turn their powers on and off. When the electricity (current) is flowing, they are super strong and can lift heavy metal objects. When you turn the electricity off, they become a normal person again and drop everything. This on/off ability is what makes them so useful in things like scrapyard cranes.

You can make an electromagnet stronger by:

  • Increasing the number of coils (turns) of wire.
  • Increasing the size of the electric current.
  • Using a soft iron core inside the coil.

At-Home Experiment: Making a Simple Electromagnet

You can build your own working electromagnet using a few simple items.

Equipment:
  • A large iron nail
  • A D-cell battery
  • About 1 metre of insulated copper wire
  • Some paperclips
  • Wire strippers (ask an adult for help)
Method:
  1. Ask an adult to help you use the wire strippers to remove about 2 cm of the plastic insulation from both ends of the wire.
  2. Leave a short tail of wire at one end, and then neatly wrap the wire tightly around the iron nail. Keep wrapping until you have a short tail left at the other end. The more coils you can make, the stronger your magnet will be.
  3. Connect one stripped end of the wire to the positive (+) terminal of the battery and the other end to the negative (-) terminal. **The wire will get hot, so be careful!**
  4. Your electromagnet is now on! Try to pick up some paperclips with the end of the nail.
  5. Disconnect the wire from the battery. What happens to the paperclips?
