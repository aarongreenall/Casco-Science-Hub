LO1 & LO2: Resistance and Ohm's Law

**Resistance ($R$)** is a measure of the opposition to the flow of electric current. It is defined as the ratio of the potential difference across a component to the current flowing through it.

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} $$

The SI unit of resistance is the **ohm ($\Omega$)**. One ohm is the resistance of a component when a potential difference of one volt drives a current of one ampere through it ($1 \, \Omega = 1 \text{ V A}^{-1}$).

LO3: I-V Characteristics

An **I-V characteristic** is a graph that shows how the current ($I$) through a component varies as the potential difference ($V$) across it is changed. The shape of this graph reveals whether the component's resistance is constant.

Metallic Conductor (Ohmic)

A straight line through the origin. The resistance ($R=V/I$) is constant. The gradient is $1/R$.

Filament Lamp (Non-ohmic)

A curve with a decreasing gradient. As voltage increases, the filament heats up, its resistance increases, and less current flows for each additional volt.

Semiconductor Diode (Non-ohmic)

Allows current to flow easily in one direction (forward bias) after a threshold voltage (~0.6V), but has a very high resistance in the opposite direction (reverse bias).

LO4 & LO5: Resistivity

The resistance of a conductor depends on its physical dimensions and the material it is made from.

Dependence on Length and Area

1. Resistance is directly proportional to length ($R \propto L$): From a microscopic view, resistance is caused by collisions between the charge carriers (electrons) and the fixed positive ions in the metal lattice. If you double the length of a wire, an electron must travel twice as far to get from one end to the other. On average, it will therefore undergo twice as many collisions, experiencing twice the opposition to its flow.

2. Resistance is inversely proportional to cross-sectional area ($R \propto 1/A$): The cross-sectional area of a wire determines how many charge carriers can move through a section of the wire at any one time. If you double the cross-sectional area, you are essentially providing twice as many parallel paths for the electrons to flow. For the same applied potential difference, this allows twice the number of electrons to pass a point per second, effectively doubling the current. According to Ohm's Law ($R=V/I$), if the current doubles for the same voltage, the resistance must have halved.

Combining these proportionalities ($R \propto L$ and $R \propto 1/A$) gives $R \propto \frac{L}{A}$. To turn this into an equation, we introduce a constant of proportionality, **resistivity ($\rho$)**.

$$ R = \frac{\rho L}{A} $$

Resistivity is an intrinsic property of a material that quantifies how strongly it resists current flow. The SI unit of resistivity is the ohm-metre ($\Omega$ m).

Experiment to Determine Resistivity

To find the resistivity of a metal, you need to measure the resistance, length, and cross-sectional area of a sample (usually a wire). By plotting a graph of resistance $R$ against length $L$, a straight line through the origin is obtained. Since $R = (\frac{\rho}{A})L$, the gradient of this graph is equal to $\frac{\rho}{A}$. Resistivity can then be found using $\rho = \text{gradient} \times A$.

Worked Example: Calculating Resistivity

A wire of length 1.50 m has a diameter of 0.40 mm. A potential difference of 1.2 V across the wire causes a current of 0.25 A to flow. Calculate the resistivity of the material.

1. Calculate the resistance ($R$):

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} = \frac{1.2}{0.25} = 4.8 \, \Omega $$

2. Calculate the cross-sectional area ($A$):

Radius $r = d/2 = 0.20 \text{ mm} = 0.20 \times 10^{-3}$ m.

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.20 \times 10^{-3})^2 = 1.257 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}^2 $$

3. Rearrange the resistivity formula and solve for $\rho$:

$$ R = \frac{\rho L}{A} \implies \rho = \frac{RA}{L} $$

$$ \rho = \frac{(4.8) \times (1.257 \times 10^{-7})}{1.50} $$

$$ \rho = 4.02 \times 10^{-7} \, \Omega \text{m} $$
