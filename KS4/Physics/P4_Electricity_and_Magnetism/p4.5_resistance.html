P4.5 Resistance

Resistance is a fundamental property of electrical components that opposes the flow of electric current. It determines how much current will flow through a component for a given voltage. Understanding resistance is crucial for designing and troubleshooting electrical circuits, as well as for understanding how various electrical devices operate.

Understanding Resistance:

**Resistance ($R$)** is the opposition to the flow of electric current. It is measured in **Ohms ($\Omega$)**. The relationship between voltage ($V$), current ($I$), and resistance ($R$) is described by Ohm's Law:

$R = \frac{V}{I}$

Where $V$ is the voltage across the component (in Volts) and $I$ is the current flowing through it (in Amperes).

Determining Resistance (Ohm's Law Experiment):

The resistance of a component can be determined experimentally using a voltmeter and an ammeter. The experiment involves building a simple circuit with a power supply, an ammeter (in series), a voltmeter (in parallel across the component), and the component itself (e.g., a resistor). By varying the voltage and measuring the corresponding current, a set of data can be collected.

For a **resistor of constant resistance** (an ohmic resistor), a graph of current versus voltage will be a straight line passing through the origin. This indicates that current is directly proportional to voltage, as long as temperature remains constant.

Factors Affecting Resistance in Metallic Conductors:

For a metallic electrical conductor (like a wire), its resistance depends on several factors:

  • **Length:** Resistance is **directly proportional to its length**. A longer wire offers more opposition to the flow of electrons.
  • **Cross-sectional Area:** Resistance is **inversely proportional to its cross-sectional area**. A thicker wire has a larger area for electrons to flow through, reducing resistance.
  • **Material:** Different materials have different inherent resistivities.
  • **Temperature:** For most metals, resistance increases with temperature.

Combined Resistance in Circuits:

When multiple resistors are connected in a circuit, their combined resistance can be calculated:

  • **Resistors in Series:** The combined resistance of two or more resistors in series is simply the **sum of their individual resistances**. * $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + ...$
  • **Resistors in Parallel:** The combined resistance of two or more resistors in parallel is **less than that of any single resistor** in the parallel combination. This is because adding more parallel paths provides more routes for current to flow, effectively reducing the overall opposition. * $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + ...$
