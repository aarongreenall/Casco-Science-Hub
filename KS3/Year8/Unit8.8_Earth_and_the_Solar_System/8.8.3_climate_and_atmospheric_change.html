8.8.3 Climate and atmospheric change

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the difference between climate and weather.
  2. Understand that the Earth's climate has changed naturally over long time periods.
  3. Explain how changes in the atmosphere can affect the Earth's climate.

Climate vs. Weather

People often use the words "weather" and "climate" interchangeably, but they mean very different things.

  • Weather is the state of the atmosphere at a specific place and time. It's what's happening right now or over a short period. Is it sunny, rainy, windy, or cold today? That's the weather.
  • Climate is the long-term average of the weather in a particular region. It's the pattern of weather you would expect to see over many years. Panama has a hot and humid tropical climate, while Antarctica has a cold, polar climate.

Analogy: Outfit vs. Wardrobe

A good way to remember the difference is to think about your clothes. The **weather** is the specific **outfit** you choose to wear today (e.g., shorts and a t-shirt because it's sunny). The **climate** is your entire **wardrobe**. Because you live in a place with a hot climate, your wardrobe is probably full of light clothes. You don't own many thick winter coats, because the long-term weather pattern doesn't require them.

A Changing Climate

The Earth's climate is not fixed. Over millions of years, it has gone through natural cycles of cold periods (ice ages) and warm periods. However, scientists have observed that the climate is now changing much more rapidly than these natural cycles can explain.

This rapid change is linked to changes in our **atmosphere**, particularly an increase in **greenhouse gases** like carbon dioxide ($CO_2$). These gases are released when we burn fossil fuels. They act like a blanket around the Earth, trapping heat from the Sun and causing the planet's average temperature to rise. This is called the **greenhouse effect**.
