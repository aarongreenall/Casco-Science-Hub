B18.2 Food chains and food webs

In an ecosystem, organisms are interconnected through their feeding relationships, forming **food chains** and **food webs**. These diagrams illustrate the flow of energy and nutrients from one organism to another, defining the trophic levels and showing the complex interactions within a community.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe food chains as showing the transfer of energy from one organism to the next, beginning with a producer.
  2. Identify producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, tertiary consumers and decomposers in food chains and food webs.
  3. Describe food webs as a network of interconnected food chains.
  4. Explain that food chains and food webs show the feeding relationships in a community.

Food Chains: Energy Transfer:

A **food chain** is a simple linear sequence that shows the **transfer of energy from one organism to the next** through feeding. Every food chain begins with a **producer**.

The arrows in a food chain represent the direction of energy flow (from the organism being eaten to the organism that eats it).

Example of a food chain:

`Grass (Producer) -> Rabbit (Primary Consumer) -> Fox (Secondary Consumer)`

*Figure: A simple food chain illustrating the flow of energy.*

Trophic Levels:

Organisms within a food chain occupy different **trophic levels**:

  • **Producers:** Organisms (e.g., plants, algae) that produce their own food, usually through photosynthesis. They form the base of the food chain.
  • **Primary Consumers (Herbivores):** Organisms that feed directly on producers.
  • **Secondary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):** Organisms that feed on primary consumers.
  • **Tertiary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):** Organisms that feed on secondary consumers.
  • **Decomposers:** Organisms (e.g., bacteria, fungi) that break down dead organic matter from all trophic levels, returning nutrients to the ecosystem. They are not typically part of the linear food chain but are essential for nutrient cycling.

Food Webs: Interconnected Food Chains:

In reality, feeding relationships in an ecosystem are rarely as simple as a single food chain. Most organisms feed on, and are fed on by, multiple other organisms. A **food web** is a more realistic representation of these complex feeding relationships, showing a **network of interconnected food chains** within a community.

Food webs show that:

  • Organisms often have multiple food sources.
  • Organisms can be eaten by multiple predators.
  • Changes in one population can have ripple effects throughout the entire ecosystem.
