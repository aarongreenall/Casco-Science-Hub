5.2 Gravitational Potential Energy and Kinetic Energy

Learning Objectives:

  1. Derive, using $W=Fs$, the formula $\Delta E_{p}=mg\Delta h$ for gravitational potential energy changes in a uniform gravitational field.
  2. Recall and use the formula $\Delta E_{P}=mg\Delta h$ for gravitational potential energy changes in a uniform gravitational field.
  3. Derive, using the equations of motion, the formula for kinetic energy $E_{K}=\frac{1}{2}mv^{2}$.
  4. Recall and use $E_{K}=\frac{1}{2}mv^{2}$.

LO1 & LO2: Gravitational Potential Energy ($E_p$)

**Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE)** is the energy an object possesses due to its position in a gravitational field. When an object is lifted against a gravitational field, work is done on the object, and this work is stored as GPE. We are concerned with the *change* in GPE, $\Delta E_p$, when an object's height changes.

Detailed Derivation of $\Delta E_p = mg\Delta h$

This formula applies in a uniform gravitational field (where $g$ is constant), such as near the Earth's surface.

1. By definition, the work done ($W$) on an object is the energy transferred to it. The change in GPE is equal to the work done to lift the object vertically at a constant velocity.

$$ \Delta E_p = W $$

2. The formula for work done is $W = Fs$, where $F$ is the force applied and $s$ is the displacement in the direction of the force.

3. To lift an object of mass $m$ at a constant velocity, the upward lifting force $F$ must be equal in magnitude to the object's weight, $W_{weight}$.

$$ F = W_{weight} = mg $$

4. The vertical displacement is the change in height, $\Delta h$. So, $s = \Delta h$.

5. Substituting these into the work done equation:

$$ W = (mg) \times (\Delta h) $$

6. Since $\Delta E_p = W$, we arrive at the final equation:

$$ \Delta E_p = mg\Delta h $$

LO3 & LO4: Kinetic Energy ($E_k$)

**Kinetic Energy (KE)** is the energy an object possesses due to its motion. Work must be done on an object to increase its speed, and this work is transferred into kinetic energy.

Detailed Derivation of $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$

We can derive this formula by considering the work done to accelerate an object from rest.

1. The kinetic energy gained by the object is equal to the work done on it by a resultant force, $F$. Assume the object starts from rest ($u=0$) and reaches a final velocity $v$ over a displacement $s$.

$$ E_k = W = Fs $$

2. From Newton's Second Law, we know that $F = ma$.

$$ E_k = (ma)s = mas $$

3. We need to eliminate $s$ and $a$. We can use the SUVAT equation that links $u, v, a,$ and $s$:

$$ v^2 = u^2 + 2as $$

4. Since the object starts from rest, $u=0$:

$$ v^2 = 2as $$

5. Rearrange this equation to make the term '$as$' the subject:

$$ as = \frac{v^2}{2} $$

6. Substitute this expression for '$as$' back into our equation for kinetic energy ($E_k = mas$):

$$ E_k = m \left( \frac{v^2}{2} \right) $$

7. This gives the final, familiar formula for kinetic energy:

$$ E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 $$

Explanation: GPE and KE

Application: Conservation of Mechanical Energy

In a system where no work is done by resistive forces (like friction or air resistance), the total mechanical energy (the sum of GPE and KE) is conserved. This means that GPE can be converted into KE, and vice versa.

$$ \text{Loss in } E_p = \text{Gain in } E_k $$

$$ mg\Delta h = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 $$

Worked Example: A Falling Ball

A 2.0 kg ball is dropped from a height of 10 m. Assuming air resistance is negligible, calculate its speed just before it hits the ground.

1. State the principle of energy conservation:

The GPE lost by the ball as it falls is converted into KE.

$$ \text{GPE lost} = \text{KE gained} $$

2. Write the equation:

$$ mg\Delta h = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 $$

Note that the initial KE is zero since it was dropped from rest.

3. Solve for v: Notice that mass ($m$) appears on both sides, so it can be cancelled.

$$ g\Delta h = \frac{1}{2}v^2 $$

$$ v^2 = 2g\Delta h $$

$$ v = \sqrt{2g\Delta h} $$

4. Substitute values:

$$ v = \sqrt{2 \times 9.81 \times 10} = \sqrt{196.2} = 14.0 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$
