B5.1 Enzymes

**Enzymes** are biological catalysts that play an indispensable role in all metabolic reactions within living organisms. They speed up the rate of these reactions without being used up themselves. Understanding enzyme action, their specificity, and how environmental factors like temperature and pH affect their activity is fundamental to biochemistry.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe enzymes as proteins that are involved in all metabolic reactions, where they function as biological catalysts.
  2. Investigate and describe the effect of changes in temperature and pH on enzyme activity.
  3. Describe and explain enzyme action with reference to: the active site, enzyme-substrate complex, substrate and product.
  4. Describe and explain the specificity of enzymes in terms of the complementary shape and fit of the active site with the substrate.
  5. Explain the effect of changes in temperature on enzyme activity in terms of kinetic energy, shape and fit, frequency of effective collisions and denaturation.
  6. Explain the effect of changes in pH on enzyme activity in terms of shape and fit and denaturation.

Enzymes as Biological Catalysts:

**Enzymes** are large protein molecules that act as **biological catalysts**. This means they:

  • **Speed up the rate of metabolic reactions** (chemical reactions occurring in living cells).
  • Are **unchanged** at the end of the reaction, meaning they can be reused.

Enzymes are involved in virtually all metabolic processes, from digestion and respiration to protein synthesis and cell division.

Enzyme Action and Specificity (Lock and Key Hypothesis):

Enzyme action is often explained by the **"lock and key" hypothesis**, which describes their high specificity:

  • Every enzyme has a unique three-dimensional shape, with a specific region called the **active site**.
  • The **substrate** is the molecule upon which the enzyme acts. The shape of the substrate is **complementary** to the shape of the enzyme's active site, fitting together like a key in a lock.
  • When the substrate binds to the active site, an **enzyme-substrate complex** is formed.
  • Within this complex, the enzyme facilitates the chemical reaction, converting the substrate into **products**.
  • Once the products are formed, they are released from the active site, and the enzyme is free to catalyze another reaction.

Effect of Temperature on Enzyme Activity:

Temperature has a significant effect on enzyme activity:

  • **Increasing Temperature (up to optimum):** As temperature increases, particles (enzyme and substrate molecules) gain more **kinetic energy**. This leads to more frequent and energetic collisions, increasing the **frequency of effective collisions** between the enzyme's active site and the substrate. This increases the rate of reaction.
  • **Optimum Temperature:** Each enzyme has an **optimum temperature** at which it exhibits maximum activity. For most human enzymes, this is around 37Â°C.
  • **Decreasing Temperature:** Below the optimum, decreasing temperature reduces kinetic energy, leading to fewer collisions and a slower reaction rate. The enzyme is not denatured, but its activity is reduced.
  • **High Temperatures (above optimum):** Beyond the optimum temperature, the high kinetic energy causes the enzyme molecule to vibrate excessively. This breaks the weak bonds holding the enzyme's specific 3D shape, particularly the active site. The enzyme loses its specific shape and can no longer bind to the substrate. This irreversible change is called **denaturation**. A denatured enzyme loses its catalytic function.

*Figure: Graph showing the effect of temperature on enzyme activity, illustrating optimum temperature and denaturation.*

Effect of pH on Enzyme Activity:

pH also has a significant effect on enzyme activity:

  • **Optimum pH:** Each enzyme has an **optimum pH** at which its active site has the most complementary shape to the substrate, allowing for maximum activity. For example, pepsin (in the stomach) has an optimum pH of around 2 (acidic), while amylase (in the small intestine) has an optimum pH of around 7-8 (alkaline).
  • **Extreme pH (too acidic or too alkaline):** Changes in pH (either too high or too low from the optimum) alter the charges on the amino acids that make up the enzyme. This disrupts the enzyme's three-dimensional structure, particularly the shape of the active site. This irreversible change is also called **denaturation**. A denatured enzyme loses its catalytic function.
