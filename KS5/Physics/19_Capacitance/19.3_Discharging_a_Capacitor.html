19.3 Discharging a capacitor

Learning Objectives:

  1. Analyse graphs of the variation with time of potential difference, charge and current for a capacitor discharging through a resistor.
  2. Recall and use $\tau = RC$ for the time constant for a capacitor discharging through a resistor.
  3. Use equations of the form $x = x_0 e^{â€“(t / RC)}$ where $x$ could represent current, charge or potential difference for a capacitor discharging through a resistor.

LO1: Analysing the Discharge Process

When a charged capacitor is connected to a resistor, it begins to discharge. Electrons flow from the negative plate, through the resistor, to the positive plate, neutralizing the charge. This process is not instantaneous.

The rate of discharge is fastest at the beginning because the potential difference across the capacitor is at its maximum, driving the largest current through the resistor. As the charge on the plates decreases, the potential difference also decreases (since $V=Q/C$). This smaller p.d. drives a smaller current, so the rate of discharge slows down. This results in an **exponential decay** for the charge, potential difference, and current.

LO2: The Time Constant ($\tau$)

The rate of discharge is determined by the product of the resistance and the capacitance. This product is called the **time constant ($\tau$)**.

$$ \tau = RC $$

The time constant has units of seconds and represents the time taken for the charge, potential difference, or current to decrease to $1/e$ (approximately 37%) of its initial value. A larger time constant means a slower discharge.

Understanding the Exponential Term $e^{-t/RC}$

The exponent in the decay equation, $-t/RC$, can be confusing. It's best understood as a dimensionless ratio. Since $\tau = RC$, the term can be written as $-t/\tau$.

This ratio, $t/\tau$, simply answers the question: **"How many time constants have passed?"**

  • When $t = \tau$ (one time constant has passed), the exponent is -1. The charge remaining is $Q = Q_0e^{-1} \approx 0.37 Q_0$.
  • When $t = 2\tau$ (two time constants have passed), the exponent is -2. The charge remaining is $Q = Q_0e^{-2} \approx 0.135 Q_0$.

This is very similar to the concept of half-life in radioactive decay. Just as half-life is the time for half of the nuclei to decay, the time constant is the time for the charge to fall to about 37% of its initial value.

LO3: Exponential Decay Equations

The exponential decay of charge, potential difference, and current during discharge can be described by the following equations, where $x_0$ is the initial value at $t=0$.

$$ Q = Q_0 e^{-t/RC} $$

$$ V = V_0 e^{-t/RC} $$

$$ I = I_0 e^{-t/RC} $$

Worked Example 1: Finding Values after a Time

A 4700 $\mu$F capacitor is charged to a potential difference of 12 V and then discharged through a 2.0 k$\Omega$ resistor. Calculate the charge remaining on the capacitor after 10 s.

1. Calculate the initial charge ($Q_0$):

$$ Q_0 = CV_0 = (4700 \times 10^{-6} \text{ F}) \times (12 \text{ V}) = 0.0564 \text{ C} $$

2. Calculate the time constant ($\tau = RC$):

$$ \tau = (2000 \, \Omega) \times (4700 \times 10^{-6} \text{ F}) = 9.4 \text{ s} $$

3. Apply the decay equation for charge:

$$ Q = Q_0 e^{-t/RC} $$

$$ Q = 0.0564 \times e^{-10/9.4} $$

$$ Q = 0.0564 \times e^{-1.064} = 0.0564 \times 0.345 = 0.0195 \text{ C} $$

Worked Example 2: Finding the Time Taken

For the same circuit, calculate the time taken for the potential difference across the capacitor to fall to 3.0 V.

1. State the decay equation for voltage:

$$ V = V_0 e^{-t/RC} $$

2. Substitute values and rearrange:

$$ 3.0 = 12 e^{-t/9.4} $$

$$ \frac{3.0}{12} = e^{-t/9.4} \implies 0.25 = e^{-t/9.4} $$

3. Take natural logarithms (ln) of both sides:

$$ \ln(0.25) = \ln(e^{-t/9.4}) $$

$$ -1.386 = -\frac{t}{9.4} $$

4. Solve for $t$:

$$ t = 1.386 \times 9.4 = 13.0 \text{ s} $$
