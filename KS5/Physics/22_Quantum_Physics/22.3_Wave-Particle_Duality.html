22.3 Wave-particle duality

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that the photoelectric effect provides evidence for a particulate nature of electromagnetic radiation while phenomena such as interference and diffraction provide evidence for a wave nature.
  2. Describe and interpret qualitatively the evidence provided by electron diffraction for the wave nature of particles.
  3. Understand the de Broglie wavelength as the wavelength associated with a moving particle.
  4. Recall and use $\lambda = h / p$.

LO1: Wave-Particle Duality

One of the most profound and counter-intuitive concepts in modern physics is **wave-particle duality**. This principle states that all matter and radiation exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties. Which property is observed depends on the experiment being performed.

Evidence for Wave Nature

Phenomena like **interference** and **diffraction** can only be explained by considering electromagnetic radiation as a wave. The constructive and destructive superposition of wavefronts is a purely wave-like behaviour.

Evidence for Particle Nature

The **photoelectric effect** can only be explained by considering electromagnetic radiation as a stream of discrete particles (photons), where each photon carries a specific quantum of energy.

Therefore, light is not simply a wave or simply a particle; it is a more complex quantum entity that reveals one aspect of its nature depending on how we interact with it.

LO2: Electron Diffraction - Evidence for the Wave Nature of Particles

If waves (light) can behave like particles, can particles (like electrons) behave like waves? The answer is yes. The definitive proof for the wave nature of particles came from the phenomenon of **electron diffraction**.

In an electron diffraction experiment, a beam of electrons is accelerated through a high voltage and then directed at a target. The target is a very thin crystalline material, such as graphite. The regular, repeating structure of the atoms in the crystal acts like a natural diffraction grating.

If electrons were only particles, they would pass straight through or scatter randomly. Instead, a distinct pattern of concentric rings is observed on a fluorescent screen. This pattern is an **interference pattern**, which can only be produced if the electrons are diffracting as they pass through the atomic layers of the crystal and then interfering constructively and destructively. This provides clear evidence that particles like electrons have a wave-like nature.

LO3 & LO4: The de Broglie Wavelength

In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that all moving particles have a wavelength associated with them. This **de Broglie wavelength ($\lambda$)** is related to the particle's momentum ($p$).

We already know that for a photon, momentum is given by $p = h/\lambda$. De Broglie hypothesised that this relationship applies to all particles, not just photons.

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} $$

Worked Example 1: Wavelength of an Electron

Calculate the de Broglie wavelength of an electron accelerated through a potential difference of 150 V. (Mass of electron = $9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ kg).

1. Find the speed of the electron ($v$): The kinetic energy gained is equal to the work done by the electric field.

$$ E_k = VQ = (150 \text{ V}) \times (1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) = 2.4 \times 10^{-17} \text{ J} $$

$$ E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(2.4 \times 10^{-17})}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}}} = 7.26 \times 10^6 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

2. Calculate the momentum ($p$):

$$ p = mv = (9.11 \times 10^{-31}) \times (7.26 \times 10^6) = 6.61 \times 10^{-24} \text{ kg m s}^{-1} $$

3. Calculate the de Broglie wavelength ($\lambda$):

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}{6.61 \times 10^{-24}} = 1.0 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m} $$

This wavelength (0.1 nm) is comparable to the spacing of atoms in a crystal, which is why diffraction effects are observable.

Worked Example 2: Wavelength of a Tennis Ball

A tennis ball of mass 60 g is travelling at a speed of 40 m s⁻¹. Calculate its de Broglie wavelength.

1. Calculate the momentum ($p$):

$$ p = mv = (0.060 \text{ kg}) \times (40 \text{ m s}^{-1}) = 2.4 \text{ kg m s}^{-1} $$

2. Calculate the de Broglie wavelength ($\lambda$):

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}{2.4} = 2.76 \times 10^{-34} \text{ m} $$

This wavelength is incredibly small, many orders of magnitude smaller than any possible gap or obstacle. This is why we do not observe wave-like properties for macroscopic objects in our everyday lives.
