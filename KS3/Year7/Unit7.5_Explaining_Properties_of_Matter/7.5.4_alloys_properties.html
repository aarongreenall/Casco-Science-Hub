7.5.4 Alloys properties

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define an alloy as a mixture containing a metal.
  2. Explain why alloys are often harder and stronger than pure metals.
  3. Give examples of common alloys and their uses.

What is an Alloy?

Pure metals are often surprisingly soft and not very useful for many jobs. To improve their properties, we can mix them with other elements.

An **alloy** is a mixture containing a metal and at least one other element. This other element can be another metal or a non-metal like carbon.

Alloys are created to give them improved or specific properties that the pure metal doesn't have, such as increased strength, hardness, or resistance to rust.

Analogy: Reinforced Concrete

A good analogy for an alloy is reinforced concrete. On its own, concrete is strong when you squash it, but it can be brittle and crack if you try to bend it. Steel rods are strong when you stretch them, but they can bend. When you combine them by putting the steel rods inside the concrete, you create a new material that is strong against both squashing and stretching. It has the best properties of both.

A Key Difference:

It's important to remember that this is an analogy. Reinforced concrete is a *physical* mixture of two different materials. An alloy is different because the mixing happens at the atomic level. The atoms of the different elements are mixed together and form new metallic bonds, which is a *chemical* change that results in a completely new material with its own unique properties.

Why are Alloys Harder?

In a pure metal, all the atoms are the same size and are arranged in neat, regular layers. These layers can easily slide over one another, which is what makes pure metals soft and malleable.

When we create an alloy, we introduce atoms of a different element, which are usually a different size. These different-sized atoms disrupt the neat, regular layers. This makes it much more difficult for the layers of atoms to slide over each other, making the alloy harder and stronger than the original pure metal.
