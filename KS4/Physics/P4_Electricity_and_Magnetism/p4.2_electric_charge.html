P4.2 Electric charge

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed in an electromagnetic field. It is the basis of all electrical phenomena, from static electricity to the flow of current in circuits. Understanding electric charge involves knowing its types, how objects become charged, and how charges interact.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State that there are positive and negative charges.
  2. State that positive charges repel other positive charges, negative charges repel other negative charges, but positive charges attract negative charges.
  3. Describe electrostatic charging by friction and simple methods to determine if an object is charged.
  4. Know that charging of solids by friction involves only a transfer of negative charge (electrons).
  5. State that charge is measured in coulombs.
  6. Describe an electric field as a region in which an electric charge experiences a force.
  7. State that the direction of an electric field at a point is the direction of the force on a positive charge at that point.
  8. Distinguish between electrical conductors and insulators and give typical examples.
  9. Describe electrical conduction in metals in terms of the movement of delocalised (mobile) electrons.

Types of Charge and Electrostatic Interactions:

There are two types of electric charge: **positive** and **negative**. These charges interact in specific ways:

  • **Like charges repel:** Positive charges repel other positive charges, and negative charges repel other negative charges.
  • **Unlike charges attract:** Positive charges attract negative charges.

*Figure: Illustration of electric charges showing attraction between unlike charges and repulsion between like charges.*

Electrostatic Charging by Friction:

Objects can become electrically charged through **friction**. When two different materials are rubbed together, electrons (which carry a negative charge) can be transferred from one material to the other. The material that gains electrons becomes negatively charged, while the material that loses electrons becomes positively charged. It's important to remember that in solids, only negative charges (electrons) are transferred during friction; protons (positive charges) are fixed within the nucleus of atoms.

Simple methods to determine if an object is charged include observing its ability to attract small, uncharged pieces of paper or to repel another charged object.

Electric charge is measured in **coulombs (C)**.

Electric Fields:

An **electric field** is a region around an electric charge where another electric charge would experience a force. The direction of an electric field at any point is defined as the direction of the force that a small, positive test charge would experience if placed at that point. Electric field lines are used to visualize these fields, originating from positive charges and ending on negative charges.

*Figure: Electric field lines around a positive point charge, showing lines radiating outwards.*

Electrical Conductors and Insulators:

Materials can be classified based on their ability to conduct electricity:

  • **Electrical Conductors:** Materials that allow electric charge (electrons) to flow through them easily. Most metals (e.g., copper, silver, aluminum) are good conductors due to the presence of **delocalised (mobile) electrons** that are free to move throughout the material.
  • **Electrical Insulators:** Materials that do not allow electric charge to flow through them easily. Examples include plastic, rubber, glass, and wood. In insulators, electrons are tightly bound to their atoms and are not free to move.
