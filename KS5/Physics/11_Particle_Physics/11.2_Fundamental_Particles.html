11.2 Fundamental particles

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that a proton and a neutron are not fundamental particles.
  2. Describe the simple quark model of the proton (up, up, down) and the neutron (up, down, down).
  3. Describe how the quark model can be used to explain the charges of the proton and the neutron.
  4. Understand that a beta ($\beta$) particle is either an electron or a positron.
  5. State that electrons and positrons are leptons.
  6. Describe $\beta^−$ and $\beta^+$ decay in terms of a simple quark model.
  7. Understand that neutrinos and antineutrinos are also leptons.

LO1, LO2 & LO3: The Quark Model of Protons and Neutrons

A **fundamental particle** is a particle that is not made up of any smaller components. For a long time, protons and neutrons were thought to be fundamental. However, experiments in the mid-20th century showed that they have an internal structure. Protons and neutrons are **not** fundamental; they are composite particles called **hadrons**.

Hadrons are made of fundamental particles called **quarks**. In the Standard Model of particle physics, there are six "flavours" of quarks, which are grouped into three generations of increasing mass. All everyday matter is made from the first-generation quarks: the **up quark** and the **down quark**.

Quarks and Antiquarks

Every quark has a corresponding **antiquark**. An antiquark is a type of antimatter; it has the same mass as its corresponding quark but the opposite electric charge. The symbol for an antiquark is a bar over the letter for the quark (e.g., the up antiquark is $\bar{u}$).

Worked Example: Verifying Hadron Charges

Use the quark model to show that the charge of (a) a proton is $+e$ and (b) an antiproton is $-e$.

(a) Proton (uud):

$$ \text{Charge} = (\text{charge of u}) + (\text{charge of u}) + (\text{charge of d}) $$

$$ = \left(+\frac{2}{3}e\right) + \left(+\frac{2}{3}e\right) + \left(-\frac{1}{3}e\right) $$

$$ = \frac{2+2-1}{3}e = \frac{3}{3}e = +e $$

(b) Antiproton ($\bar{u}\bar{u}\bar{d}$): An antiproton is made of the corresponding antiquarks.

$$ \text{Charge} = (\text{charge of }\bar{u}) + (\text{charge of }\bar{u}) + (\text{charge of }\bar{d}) $$

$$ = \left(-\frac{2}{3}e\right) + \left(-\frac{2}{3}e\right) + \left(+\frac{1}{3}e\right) $$

$$ = \frac{-2-2+1}{3}e = \frac{-3}{3}e = -e $$

LO4, LO5 & LO7: The Lepton Family

The second family of fundamental particles in the Standard Model are the **leptons**. Unlike quarks, leptons are standalone particles that do not experience the strong nuclear force. They are truly fundamental.

Electrons and Positrons

The most common and well-known lepton is the **electron ($e^-$)**. Its antimatter counterpart is the **positron ($e^+$)**. A positron has the exact same mass as an electron but an opposite charge (+e).

When we talk about beta ($\beta$) radiation, we are talking about these two particles:

  • Beta-minus ($\beta^−$) decay** emits an electron.
  • Beta-plus ($\beta^+$) decay** emits a positron.

Neutrinos and Antineutrinos

Also in the lepton family are **neutrinos ($\nu$)** and their antimatter counterparts, **antineutrinos ($\bar{\nu}$)**. These are fundamental particles with very little mass and no electric charge. They are produced during beta decay to ensure that energy and momentum are conserved.

  • An **antineutrino** is produced alongside an electron in $\beta^−$ decay.
  • A **neutrino** is produced alongside a positron in $\beta^+$ decay.

Summary of the Standard Model and Conservation Laws

In all particle interactions, several fundamental quantities must be conserved. This means the total value of the quantity before the interaction must equal the total value after. For A-Level Physics, the key conserved quantities are Charge, Baryon Number, and Lepton Number.

The following table summarizes the properties of the first generation of fundamental particles (quarks and leptons) which make up all everyday matter.

Down Antiquark $\bar{d}$ $+\frac{1}{3}e$ $-\frac{1}{3}$ 0

Electron Neutrino $\nu_e$ 0 0 +1
Electron Antineutrino $\bar{\nu}_e$ 0 0 -1

**Note:** Protons (uud) and neutrons (udd) are **baryons**. All baryons have a Baryon Number of +1. Antibaryons (like the antiproton, $\bar{u}\bar{u}\bar{d}$) have a Baryon Number of -1. Leptons and other particles have a Baryon Number of 0.

Worked Example 1: Beta-Minus Decay

Show that charge, baryon number, and lepton number are conserved in the beta-minus decay of a neutron: $n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$.

1. Check Charge (Q):

$$ \text{Before: } 0 $$

$$ \text{After: } (+1) + (-1) + 0 = 0 $$

Charge is conserved.

2. Check Baryon Number (B):

$$ \text{Before: } +1 $$

$$ \text{After: } (+1) + 0 + 0 = +1 $$

Baryon number is conserved.

3. Check Lepton Number (L):

$$ \text{Before: } 0 $$

$$ \text{After: } 0 + (+1) + (-1) = 0 $$

Lepton number is conserved. Since all quantities are conserved, the decay is possible.

Worked Example 2: Impossible Decay

Explain why the decay $p \rightarrow e^+ + \nu_e$ is not possible.

1. Check Charge (Q):

$$ \text{Before: } +1 $$

$$ \text{After: } (+1) + 0 = +1 $$

Charge is conserved.

2. Check Baryon Number (B):

$$ \text{Before: } +1 $$

$$ \text{After: } 0 + 0 = 0 $$

Baryon number is **not** conserved ($1 \neq 0$).

3. Check Lepton Number (L):

$$ \text{Before: } 0 $$

$$ \text{After: } (-1) + (+1) = 0 $$

Lepton number is conserved. However, because baryon number is not conserved, this decay cannot happen.

Worked Example 3: Another Impossible Decay

Explain why the decay $n \rightarrow p + e^-$ is not possible.

1. Check Charge (Q):

$$ \text{Before: } 0 $$

$$ \text{After: } (+1) + (-1) = 0 $$

Charge is conserved.

2. Check Baryon Number (B):

$$ \text{Before: } +1 $$

$$ \text{After: } (+1) + 0 = +1 $$

Baryon number is conserved.

3. Check Lepton Number (L):

$$ \text{Before: } 0 $$

$$ \text{After: } 0 + (+1) = +1 $$

Lepton number is **not** conserved ($0 \neq 1$). Because lepton number is not conserved, this decay cannot happen (it is missing the antineutrino).

LO6: Beta Decay in the Quark Model

Beta decay is not just a nucleus emitting a particle; it is a fundamental process where a nucleon (a proton or neutron) transforms into the other. This transformation happens at the quark level and is governed by the weak nuclear force.

Beta-Minus ($\beta^−$) Decay

In an unstable, neutron-rich nucleus, a neutron can decay into a proton to increase stability. At the fundamental level, a **down quark** within the neutron (udd) transforms into an **up quark**. This changes the nucleon from a neutron (udd) to a proton (uud).

To conserve charge, lepton number, and other quantum properties, this transformation also creates and emits an electron ($e^−$) and an electron antineutrino ($\bar{\nu}_e$).

$$ d \rightarrow u + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e $$

Worked Example: Conservation in $\beta^−$ Decay

Show that charge, baryon number, and lepton number are conserved in the quark process for $\beta^−$ decay.

Decay: $d \rightarrow u + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$

1. Check Charge (Q):

$$ \text{Before: } -\frac{1}{3}e $$

$$ \text{After: } (+\frac{2}{3}e) + (-1e) + 0 = -\frac{1}{3}e $$

Charge is conserved.

2. Check Baryon Number (B):

$$ \text{Before: } +\frac{1}{3} $$

$$ \text{After: } (+\frac{1}{3}) + 0 + 0 = +\frac{1}{3} $$

Baryon number is conserved.

3. Check Lepton Number (L):

$$ \text{Before: } 0 $$

$$ \text{After: } 0 + (+1) + (-1) = 0 $$

Lepton number is conserved. The decay is permitted.

Beta-Plus ($\beta^+$) Decay

In an unstable, proton-rich nucleus, a proton can decay into a neutron. This happens when an **up quark** within the proton (uud) transforms into a **down quark**. This changes the nucleon from a proton (uud) to a neutron (udd).

To conserve charge and lepton number, this transformation also creates and emits a positron ($e^+$) and an electron neutrino ($\nu_e$).

$$ u \rightarrow d + e^+ + \nu_e $$

Worked Example: Conservation in $\beta^+$ Decay

Show that charge, baryon number, and lepton number are conserved in the quark process for $\beta^+$ decay.

Decay: $u \rightarrow d + e^+ + \nu_e$

1. Check Charge (Q):

$$ \text{Before: } +\frac{2}{3}e $$

$$ \text{After: } (-\frac{1}{3}e) + (+1e) + 0 = +\frac{2}{3}e $$

Charge is conserved.

2. Check Baryon Number (B):

$$ \text{Before: } +\frac{1}{3} $$

$$ \text{After: } (+\frac{1}{3}) + 0 + 0 = +\frac{1}{3} $$

Baryon number is conserved.

3. Check Lepton Number (L):

$$ \text{Before: } 0 $$

$$ \text{After: } 0 + (-1) + (+1) = 0 $$

Lepton number is conserved. The decay is permitted.
