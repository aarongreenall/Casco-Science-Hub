7.1 Progressive Waves

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe what is meant by wave motion as illustrated by vibration in ropes, springs and ripple tanks.
  2. Understand and use the terms displacement, amplitude, phase difference, period, frequency, wavelength and speed.
  3. Understand the use of the time-base and y-gain of a cathode-ray oscilloscope (CRO) to determine frequency and amplitude.
  4. Derive, using the definitions of speed, frequency and wavelength, the wave equation $v=f\lambda$.
  5. Recall and use $v=f\lambda$.
  6. Understand that energy is transferred by a progressive wave.
  7. Recall and use intensity = power/area and intensity $\propto$ (amplitude)² for a progressive wave.

LO1 & LO6: Wave Motion and Energy Transfer

A **progressive wave** is a disturbance that travels from one place to another, transferring energy through a medium or a vacuum. The key concept of wave motion is that it is the **energy that travels**, not the medium itself. The particles of the medium oscillate about their fixed equilibrium positions, but they do not have any net displacement in the direction of the wave's travel.

LO2, LO4 & LO5: Wave Parameters and The Wave Equation

Parameters of a Wave

Displacement (x): The distance of a particle on the wave from its equilibrium (undisturbed) position at any given instant. In other words: How far a point on the rope has moved up or down from its flat, resting position.

Amplitude (A): The maximum displacement from the equilibrium position. In other words: The height of a crest or the depth of a trough from the middle line.

Wavelength ($\lambda$): The shortest distance between two points on a wave that are in phase (e.g., from one crest to the next). In other words: The length of one complete "wave shape".

Period (T): The time taken for one complete oscillation of a particle in the medium. In other words: The time it takes for a floating cork to bob all the way down and back up to its highest point again.

Frequency (f): The number of complete oscillations per unit time. It is the reciprocal of the period ($f = 1/T$). In other words: How many full waves pass a point every second.

Phase Difference ($\phi$): The fraction of an oscillation by which one wave leads or lags another, measured in degrees or radians. In other words: How "out of sync" two waves are. If two waves have their crests and troughs at the same time, they are in phase ($\phi=0$). If one wave's crest aligns with the other's trough, they are in antiphase ($\phi=180^\circ$ or $\pi$ radians).

Detailed Derivation of the Wave Equation

1. Start with the basic definition of speed: $\text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}$

2. Consider a wave travelling. In the time it takes for one complete oscillation, the period ($T$), the wave travels a distance of exactly one wavelength ($\lambda$).

3. Substitute these specific quantities into the speed formula. The speed of the wave is $v$.

$$ v = \frac{\lambda}{T} $$

4. We know that frequency is the reciprocal of the period: $f = \frac{1}{T}$.

5. Substitute this expression for $\frac{1}{T}$ into the equation:

$$ v = \lambda \times \left(\frac{1}{T}\right) = \lambda \times f $$

6. This gives the final form of the wave equation:

$$ v = f\lambda $$

LO3: Using a Cathode-Ray Oscilloscope (CRO)

A CRO is a device that displays a voltage signal as a waveform on a screen. It can be used to analyse waves, particularly sound waves converted into electrical signals by a microphone.

  • The **Y-gain (or Volts/div)** setting controls the vertical scale. It is used to determine the amplitude of the signal (as a peak voltage).
  • The **Time-base (or Time/div)** setting controls the horizontal scale. It is used to determine the period of the signal.

Worked Example: Reading a CRO

The trace on a CRO screen shows a wave that has a vertical height of 4 divisions from crest to trough, and one complete wavelength occupies 5 horizontal divisions. The Y-gain is set to 0.5 V/div and the time-base is set to 2 ms/div. Find the amplitude and frequency of the signal.

1. Calculate the Amplitude:

The peak-to-peak height is 4 divisions. The amplitude is half of this, which is 2 divisions.

$$ \text{Amplitude (Voltage)} = \text{Vertical divisions} \times \text{Y-gain} $$

$$ = 2 \text{ div} \times 0.5 \text{ V/div} $$

$$ = 1.0 \text{ V} $$

2. Calculate the Period:

$$ \text{Period (T)} = \text{Horizontal divisions} \times \text{Time-base} $$

$$ = 5 \text{ div} \times 2 \text{ ms/div} $$

$$ = 10 \text{ ms} = 0.010 \text{ s} $$

3. Calculate the Frequency:

$$ f = \frac{1}{T} $$

$$ f = \frac{1}{0.010 \text{ s}} = 100 \text{ Hz} $$

LO7: Wave Intensity

**Intensity ($I$)** is a measure of the energy a wave carries. It is defined as the power transferred per unit area perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer.

$$ I = \frac{P}{\textit{Area}} $$

Derivation of the Intensity-Amplitude Relationship

The relationship between a wave's intensity and its amplitude is crucial.

1. The particles in a medium oscillate as a wave passes. If we model this as simple harmonic motion, the energy ($E$) of an oscillating particle is proportional to the square of its amplitude ($A$) and the square of its frequency ($f$). (This comes from $E = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2A^2$ where $\omega = 2\pi f$).

$$ E \propto f^2 A^2 $$

2. The power ($P$) of the wave is the energy transferred per unit time. Therefore, the power is also proportional to the square of the amplitude and the square of the frequency.

$$ P \propto f^2 A^2 $$

3. Intensity ($I$) is defined as power per unit *Area* ($I = P/\textit{Area}$). Since the *Area* is constant for a given wavefront, intensity is directly proportional to power.

$$ I \propto P $$

4. Combining these proportionalities, we find that intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude and the square of the frequency.

$$ I \propto f^2 A^2 $$

5. For most cases where we compare two waves, the frequency is constant. This simplifies to the key relationship:

$$ I \propto A^2 $$

Worked Example: Intensity and Amplitude

Wave A has an amplitude of 3.0 cm and an intensity of 12 W m⁻². Wave B has the same frequency but an amplitude of 6.0 cm. What is the intensity of Wave B?

1. State the relationship: Since frequency is constant, $I \propto A^2$.

2. Set up a ratio:

$$ \frac{I_B}{I_A} = \frac{kA_B^2}{kA_A^2} = \left(\frac{A_B}{A_A}\right)^2 $$

3. Substitute values and solve for $I_B$:

$$ \frac{I_B}{12} = \left(\frac{6.0}{3.0}\right)^2 $$

$$ \frac{I_B}{12} = (2)^2 = 4 $$

$$ I_B = 4 \times 12 = 48 \text{ W m}^{-2} $$
