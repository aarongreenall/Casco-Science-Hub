9.3.1 Waveforms and Sound Properties

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Draw and interpret waveforms, and recognise the link between loudness and amplitude, pitch and frequency.

What is a Wave?

In physics, a wave is a disturbance that transfers **energy** from one place to another. The most important thing to remember is that waves transfer energy **without transferring matter**. The particles of the substance the wave travels through (the medium) vibrate, but they don't travel along with the wave.

Analogy: A Stadium Wave

Imagine you're in a huge sports stadium and the crowd starts a Mexican wave. The wave shape travels all the way around the stadium, which is very energetic! But each person (the matter) simply stands up and sits down in their own seat. They don't run around the stadium with the wave. The energy moves, but the people stay in place. Sound waves transfer energy through vibrating particles in a similar way.

Visualising Sound: The Waveform

Sound travels through the air as a longitudinal wave (a series of squashes and stretches of air particles). This is hard to draw, so we often represent a sound wave using a **transverse waveform** on a graph. This graph is a picture of the sound, showing how the particles are displaced from their resting position over time.

Decoding the Waveform: Pitch and Loudness

By looking at the shape of a waveform, we can tell a lot about the sound it represents. The two most important properties are its loudness and its pitch.

Loudness and Amplitude

The **loudness** of a sound is determined by its **amplitude**. A wave with a larger amplitude carries more energy, so we hear it as a louder sound.

Pitch and Frequency

The **pitch** of a sound (how high or low it is) is determined by its **frequency**. Frequency is the number of complete waves that pass a point per second. High frequency means a high pitch.

At-Home Experiment: String Telephone

See for yourself how sound vibrations travel through a solid material.

You will need:

  • Two paper or plastic cups
  • A long piece of string (5-10 metres)
  • A sharp pencil or paperclip

Procedure:

  1. Ask an adult to help you poke a small hole in the bottom of each cup.
  2. Thread one end of the string through the hole of one cup from the outside in. Tie a large knot on the inside so it won't pull out.
  3. Do the same with the other end of the string and the second cup.
  4. You and a friend each take a cup and walk apart until the string is pulled tight (taut). It must not be touching anything else.
  5. One person talks into their cup while the other listens with their cup to their ear. Can you hear each other?

Scientific Explanation:

When you speak into the cup, the sound waves make the bottom of the cup vibrate. These vibrations travel as waves along the taut string to the other cup. The string makes the bottom of the second cup vibrate, which recreates the sound waves for your friend to hear. This shows that sound is a vibration that transfers energy!
