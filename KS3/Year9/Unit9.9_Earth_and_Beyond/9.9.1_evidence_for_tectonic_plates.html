9.9.1 Evidence for Tectonic Plates

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Explain why the jigsaw appearance of continental coasts, location of volcanoes and earthquakes, fossil record and alignment of magnetic materials in the Earth's crust are all evidence for tectonic plates.

The Jigsaw Puzzle Earth

The theory of **Plate Tectonics** states that the Earth's outer layer is broken into large, moving pieces called tectonic plates. This idea grew from an earlier theory called **Continental Drift**, proposed by a scientist named Alfred Wegener in 1912. His first and most intuitive piece of evidence was simple: the continents look like they could fit together like pieces of a giant jigsaw puzzle.

Analogy: A Torn Newspaper

If you tear a newspaper page in half, you can perfectly match the jagged edges. Not only that, but the lines of text will also line up across the tear. The matching coastlines and similar rock formations and mountain ranges found on different continents are like the torn edges and matching text, providing strong evidence that they were once connected.

Clues from the Past: The Fossil Record

Wegener's theory was strengthened by fossil evidence. Identical fossils of land-dwelling animals and plants have been found on continents that are now separated by thousands of kilometres of ocean.

For example, fossils of the land reptile *Lystrosaurus* have been found in Antarctica, India, and South Africa. This animal could not have swum across the vast oceans. The only logical explanation is that these continents were once joined together in a single supercontinent, which scientists call **Pangaea**, allowing the animals to walk from one area to the other.

The Earth's Fiery Footprint: Volcanoes and Earthquakes

If you plot the locations of the world's major volcanoes and earthquakes on a map, they don't appear randomly. They form distinct lines and clusters that perfectly outline the edges of the tectonic plates. These plate boundaries are where plates are colliding, pulling apart, or sliding past each other. This movement creates the immense stress and heat that leads to earthquakes and volcanic activity, providing a clear "footprint" of the plates.

A Magnetic Tape Recorder: Seafloor Spreading

The final, conclusive piece of evidence for plate tectonics came from the bottom of the ocean. Scientists discovered that the Earth's magnetic field has "flipped" its polarity many times throughout history.

At mid-ocean ridges, molten rock (magma) rises from the mantle to form new oceanic crust. As this rock cools, magnetic minerals inside it act like tiny compasses, aligning with the Earth's magnetic field at that time. This process has created a perfectly symmetrical pattern of magnetic "stripes" of normal and reversed polarity on either side of the ridges. This pattern is like a magnetic tape recording of the Earth's history, proving that the seafloor is spreading apart from the centre and pushing the continents with it.
