7.7.3 Tidal forces

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define tides as the regular rise and fall of the Earth's oceans.
  2. Explain that tides are caused mainly by the gravitational pull of the Moon.
  3. Describe how the alignment of the Sun and Moon causes Spring and Neap tides.

What are Tides?

**Tides** are the regular, predictable rise and fall of the sea level. They are caused by the force of gravity. While the Sun's gravity has an effect, the main cause of the Earth's tides is the gravitational pull of the **Moon**.

The Moon's gravity pulls on the entire Earth, but it pulls more strongly on the parts that are closer to it. This difference in gravitational pull is what creates the tides. The water on the side of the Earth nearest the Moon is pulled towards it, creating a bulge of water, or a **high tide**.

But this doesn't explain why there are two high tides every day. The Moon's gravity also pulls on the solid Earth more strongly than it pulls on the water on the far side. This effectively pulls the Earth away from the water on the far side, leaving a second bulge of water behind. This creates a second high tide on the opposite side of the Earth. As the Earth spins, any location on the coast passes through both of these bulges each day.

Spring and Neap Tides

The Sun's gravity also affects the tides, though less than the Moon's. When the Sun, Earth, and Moon are aligned, their gravitational pulls combine to create more extreme tides.

Spring Tides

This happens during a New Moon and a Full Moon, when the Sun, Earth, and Moon are in a straight line. The gravitational forces of the Sun and Moon work together, creating extra-high high tides and extra-low low tides.

Neap Tides

This happens during the first and third quarter moons, when the Sun and Moon are at right angles to each other relative to the Earth. Their gravitational forces partially cancel each other out, leading to less extreme tides with a smaller difference between high and low tide.
