B3.2 Osmosis

**Osmosis** is a special type of diffusion that involves the net movement of water molecules across a partially permeable membrane. It is a vital process in all living organisms, playing a crucial role in water uptake by plants, maintaining cell turgor, and regulating water balance in animal cells.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State that water diffuses through partially permeable membranes by osmosis.
  2. State that water moves into and out of cells by osmosis through the cell membrane.
  3. Investigate and describe the effects on plant tissues of immersing them in solutions of different concentrations.
  4. Describe osmosis as the net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential (dilute solution) to a region of lower water potential (concentrated solution), through a partially permeable membrane.
  5. Explain the effects on plant cells of immersing them in solutions of different concentrations by using the terms: turgid, turgor pressure, plasmolysis, flaccid.
  6. Explain the importance of water potential and osmosis in the uptake and loss of water by organisms.

Understanding Osmosis and Water Potential:

**Osmosis** is defined as the **net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential (a dilute solution) to a region of lower water potential (a concentrated solution), through a partially permeable membrane.**

  • **Partially Permeable Membrane:** A membrane that allows small molecules (like water) to pass through, but restricts the movement of larger solute molecules. Cell membranes are partially permeable.
  • **Water Potential:** A measure of the free energy of water molecules. Pure water has the highest water potential. Adding solutes lowers the water potential. Water moves from an area of high water potential to an area of low water potential.

Water constantly moves into and out of cells by osmosis through the cell membrane, which is a partially permeable membrane.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating osmosis, showing the net movement of water molecules across a partially permeable membrane.*

Effects of Osmosis on Plant Cells:

The rigid cell wall of plant cells gives them a different response to osmosis compared to animal cells.

  • **In dilute solutions (high water potential):** Water moves into the plant cell by osmosis. The vacuole swells and pushes the cell membrane against the cell wall. The cell becomes **turgid** (firm), and the outward pressure exerted by the cell contents on the cell wall is called **turgor pressure**. This turgor pressure helps support the plant.
  • **In concentrated solutions (low water potential):** Water moves out of the plant cell by osmosis. The vacuole shrinks, and the cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall. The cell becomes **plasmolysed** (flaccid, or limp), and the plant will wilt.

*Figure: The effects of different solutions on plant cells, showing turgid, flaccid, and plasmolysed states.*

Importance of Osmosis in Organisms:

Osmosis is vital for:

  • **Water Uptake by Plants:** Root hair cells absorb water from the soil by osmosis.
  • **Support in Plants:** Turgor pressure helps maintain the rigidity and upright posture of plant stems and leaves.
  • **Water Balance in Animals:** Animal cells (which lack a cell wall) can swell and burst if too much water enters by osmosis, or shrivel if too much water leaves. Organisms have mechanisms to regulate water potential (osmoregulation).
