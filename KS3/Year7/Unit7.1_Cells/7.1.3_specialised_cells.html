7.1.3 Specialised cells

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Explain what a specialised cell is.
  2. Describe how the structures of different animal and plant cells are adapted to their functions.
  3. Give examples of specialised cells in animals and plants.

What are Specialised Cells?

In large, multicellular organisms like humans and oak trees, not all cells are the same. Most cells develop in a way that makes them very good at one particular job. This process is called **specialisation** (or differentiation). A **specialised cell** is a cell whose shape and structure are adapted to carry out a specific function.

Analogy: A City of Specialists

If a basic cell is like a general-purpose building in our "Cell City," then a specialised cell is like a purpose-built structure. A city doesn't just have identical buildings; it has specially designed power plants, communication towers, factories, and delivery depots. In the same way, an organism has specialised cells to do all the different jobs it needs to survive.

Specialised Animal Cells

Nerve Cell (Neurone)

Function: To carry electrical signals (nerve impulses) around the body. It's the city's telephone network.
Adaptations: It is very long to carry signals over large distances, and has branched connections at its ends to connect to many other cells.

Muscle Cell

Function: To contract (shorten) to produce movement. It's the city's engines and cranes.
Adaptations: It contains special proteins that can slide over each other to make the cell shorter. It also has lots of mitochondria to provide the energy needed for contraction.

Red Blood Cell

Function: To carry oxygen from the lungs to the body's cells. It's the city's delivery van fleet.
Adaptations: It has no nucleus, providing more space for haemoglobin (the protein that carries oxygen). It has a biconcave shape to maximise its surface area for oxygen absorption.

Specialised Plant Cells

Root Hair Cell

Function: To absorb water and mineral ions from the soil. It's the city's water pipes extending into the ground.
Adaptations: It has a long, thin extension (the "hair") which provides a large surface area for efficient absorption.

Palisade Cell

Function: To carry out photosynthesis. It's the city's solar panel array.
Adaptations: It is packed full of chloroplasts (the site of photosynthesis) and is tall and thin, so lots of them can be packed together at the top of the leaf to absorb maximum sunlight.

In-School Experiment: Observing Specialised Cells

Using a microscope, you can observe the unique shapes of different specialised cells.

Equipment:
  • A light microscope
  • Prepared slides of specialised tissues (e.g., blood smear, nerve tissue, plant leaf cross-section)
Method:
  1. Observe each prepared slide under the microscope, starting with the lowest power objective lens.
  2. Focus the image and then switch to a higher power to see more detail.
  3. For each slide, make a careful drawing of one or two cells.
  4. Label any parts you can recognise and try to identify how the cell's shape is adapted for its function.
