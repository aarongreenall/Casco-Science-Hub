B17.2 Selection

**Selection** is a key mechanism driving evolution, explaining how populations of organisms change over generations in response to their environment. It acts upon the variation present within a species, favoring individuals with advantageous traits that enhance their survival and reproduction. This section explores both natural selection and artificial selection, highlighting their differences and impacts.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe natural selection as the survival of the fittest in terms of: variation, competition, adaptation, survival, reproduction.
  2. Explain the role of natural selection in the development of antibiotic-resistant bacteria.
  3. Describe artificial selection as a process in which humans choose organisms with desirable traits and breed them to produce offspring with those traits.
  4. Describe examples of artificial selection, limited to: selective breeding of crops and farm animals.

Natural Selection: Survival of the Fittest:

**Natural selection** is the process by which organisms that are better adapted to their environment tend to survive and produce more offspring. It is often summarized as "survival of the fittest" and drives evolution. The process can be described in terms of:

  • **Variation:** Individuals within a species show **variation** in their characteristics (due to mutations and sexual reproduction).
  • **Competition:** Organisms produce more offspring than can survive, leading to **competition** for resources (food, water, shelter, mates).
  • **Adaptation:** Individuals with characteristics that are better **adapted** to their environment are more likely to **survive** this competition.
  • **Reproduction:** These "fitter" individuals are more likely to **reproduce** and pass on their advantageous alleles to their offspring.
  • Over many generations, the frequency of these advantageous alleles increases in the population, leading to evolutionary change.

Natural Selection and Antibiotic Resistance:

The development of **antibiotic-resistant bacteria** is a prime example of natural selection in action:

  1. Within a population of bacteria, there is **variation** (e.g., some bacteria might have a random mutation that makes them resistant to an antibiotic).
  2. When antibiotics are used, they create a strong selective pressure. Most non-resistant bacteria are killed.
  3. The resistant bacteria **survive** (are "fitter" in the presence of the antibiotic).
  4. These resistant bacteria then **reproduce** rapidly (as there is less competition from non-resistant bacteria).
  5. Over time, the population becomes dominated by antibiotic-resistant bacteria, making the antibiotic ineffective.

*Figure: Illustration showing the development of antibiotic resistance through natural selection.*

Artificial Selection (Selective Breeding):

**Artificial selection**, also known as **selective breeding**, is a process in which **humans choose organisms with desirable traits** and breed them to produce offspring with those enhanced traits. Unlike natural selection, where the environment is the selective agent, in artificial selection, humans decide which individuals reproduce.

Examples of artificial selection:

  • **Selective breeding of crops:** Farmers breed plants for increased yield, disease resistance, larger fruits, or better nutritional content (e.g., high-yielding wheat, disease-resistant potatoes).
  • **Selective breeding of farm animals:** Breeders select animals for desirable traits like faster growth, higher milk/meat production, or specific wool/fur quality (e.g., cows bred for high milk yield, chickens bred for fast growth).
  • **Dog breeds:** All dog breeds originated from wolves through thousands of years of artificial selection for various traits (e.g., hunting ability, companionship, appearance).
