8.3.1 Respiration and energy release

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define aerobic respiration as the chemical reaction that releases energy from food.
  2. State the word and chemical equations for aerobic respiration.
  3. Identify the mitochondria as the site of respiration in both plant and animal cells.

The Energy of Life

Every living thing needs a constant supply of energy to carry out the seven life processes (MRS GREN). This energy comes from a chemical reaction called **aerobic respiration**. It's important not to confuse this with breathing, which is the physical process of moving air in and out of the lungs. Respiration is the chemical reaction that happens inside every living cell.

**Aerobic respiration** is the chemical reaction that uses oxygen to release energy from glucose (a sugar from food).

This reaction happens inside tiny organelles called **mitochondria**, which are found in both plant and animal cells.

Analogy: A Car Engine

You can think of respiration like a car engine. **Glucose** is the **fuel** (petrol), and **oxygen** is needed for the fuel to burn. **Respiration** is the **engine** burning the fuel in a slow, controlled way to release its energy. The **energy released** is what powers the car's movement (just like it powers our muscles). **Carbon dioxide** and **water** are the **exhaust fumes** (waste products).

The Equations for Respiration

We can represent this chemical reaction with both a word equation and a balanced chemical symbol equation.

Word Equation:

Glucose + Oxygen $\rightarrow$ Carbon Dioxide + Water (+ Energy)

Chemical Equation:

$$ C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O $$

Explanation: Aerobic Respiration

In-School Experiment: The Heat of Respiration

One of the ways energy is released during respiration is as heat. You can detect this heat by comparing living, respiring seeds with dead, non-respiring seeds.

Equipment:
  • Two vacuum flasks (Thermos flasks)
  • Two thermometers
  • Cotton wool
  • Living, germinating seeds (e.g., cress or mustard seeds)
  • Dead seeds (the same type, but boiled and cooled)
Method:
  1. Place the living, germinating seeds into one vacuum flask.
  2. Place the dead, boiled seeds into the second vacuum flask. This is your control.
  3. Insert a thermometer into each flask, with the bulb surrounded by the seeds. Use cotton wool to plug the top of each flask.
  4. Record the starting temperature in both flasks.
  5. Leave the flasks for a day or two, and then record the final temperature in both.
Expected Result:

You should find that the temperature in the flask with the living, respiring seeds has increased, while the temperature in the flask with the dead seeds has not. This shows that respiration releases energy as heat.
