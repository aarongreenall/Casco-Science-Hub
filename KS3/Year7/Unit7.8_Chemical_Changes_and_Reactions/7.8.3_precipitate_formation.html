7.8.3 Precipitate formation

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define a precipitate as an insoluble solid formed from a solution.
  2. Explain that the formation of a precipitate is a sign of a chemical reaction.
  3. Give examples of precipitation reactions.

What is a Precipitate?

Sometimes, when you mix two clear solutions together, something amazing happens: a solid suddenly appears out of nowhere! This solid is called a **precipitate**, and the reaction that forms it is a **precipitation reaction**.

A **precipitate** is an insoluble solid that is formed when two solutions containing soluble substances are mixed together. "Insoluble" means it cannot dissolve in the liquid.

The formation of a precipitate is one of the clearest signs that a chemical reaction has taken place, because a completely new substance (the solid) has been created from two liquids.

Analogy: The Magic Trick

Think of a precipitation reaction like a magician's trick. The magician takes two bottles of what looks like clear water (the two solutions). They pour them into the same glass, and instantly, a cloud of solid colour appears in the liquid. This "magic" is a precipitation reaction. The solid that appears is the precipitate, and its sudden appearance is proof that a new substance has been formed.

In-School Experiment: The "Golden Rain" Reaction

This is one of the most beautiful and impressive chemical reactions. You will mix two clear, colourless solutions to create a vibrant yellow solid.

Safety First!

This experiment must be done under teacher supervision. Wear safety goggles at all times. Lead nitrate is toxic, so handle it with care and wash your hands thoroughly afterwards.

Equipment:
  • A solution of lead(II) nitrate
  • A solution of potassium iodide
  • Two test tubes and a test tube rack
  • A beaker
Method:
  1. Pour a small amount of lead(II) nitrate solution into one test tube.
  2. Pour a similar amount of potassium iodide solution into the other test tube. Observe that both are clear and colourless.
  3. Carefully pour the contents of one test tube into the other.
  4. Observe the immediate formation of a bright yellow solid. This is the precipitate, called lead(II) iodide.
  5. If you gently heat the mixture in a hot water bath, the precipitate will dissolve. As it cools down slowly, the lead(II) iodide will re-form as beautiful, shimmering golden crystals, which is why it's called the "golden rain" experiment!
