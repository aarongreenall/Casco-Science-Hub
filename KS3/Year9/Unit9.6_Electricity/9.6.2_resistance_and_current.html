9.6.2 Resistance and Current

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explore the relationship between resistance and current in a circuit.

Resistance (R):

  • A measure of how difficult it is for electric current to flow through a component.
  • It is caused by collisions between the moving charge carriers (electrons) and the fixed positive ions in the material. These collisions transfer energy, often as heat.
  • Measured in Ohms (Ω).

Relationship between Resistance and Current:

  • For a given voltage, the current is inversely proportional to the resistance.
  • Higher Resistance → Lower Current
  • Lower Resistance → Higher Current

Factors Affecting Resistance in a Wire:

  • Length: A longer wire has more resistance than a shorter wire (electrons have to travel further and collide more).
  • Cross-Sectional Area (Thickness): A thicker wire has less resistance than a thinner wire (there are more paths for the electrons to flow through).
  • Material: Different materials have different resistivities. Conductors like copper have low resistivity, while insulators like plastic have very high resistivity.
  • Temperature: For most metallic conductors, resistance increases as temperature increases (the ions vibrate more, causing more collisions with electrons).
