7.5.3 Indicators

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define an indicator as a substance that changes colour to show pH.
  2. Give examples of common indicators and their colour changes.
  3. Understand that different indicators have different colour schemes.

What is an Indicator?

How can you tell if a clear, colourless liquid is a dangerous acid or just plain water? You can't know just by looking. To find out the hidden chemical property of acidity or alkalinity (its pH), scientists use a special chemical called an **indicator**.

An **indicator** is a substance that changes colour when it is added to an acidic or alkaline solution.

Analogy: Goldilocks and the Thermal Camera

Imagine a modern-day Goldilocks who is also a strong, independent scientist. Before she tastes any porridge, she checks it with her special thermal camera. The camera's screen is an **indicator** of the porridge's temperature. If the porridge is too hot, the screen flashes **red**. If it's too cold, it turns **blue**. If it's just right, it shows **green**. The colour change gives her a visual signal about the hidden property of temperature.

Different Indicators, Different Colours

The Goldilocks analogy is just a way to understand the idea. In a real science lab, we use chemical indicators, and it's very important to know that **different indicators have different colour schemes**. You must know what the colours mean for the specific indicator you are using.

  • Universal Indicator: This is a mixture of different indicators and gives a whole rainbow of colours, from red for strong acids to dark purple for strong alkalis, with green for neutral.
  • Litmus Paper: This is a simpler indicator. It turns **red** in acid and **blue** in alkali.
  • Phenolphthalein: This indicator is interesting because it is **colourless** in acidic and neutral solutions, but turns a bright **pink** in alkaline solutions.

At-Home Experiment: Making a Red Cabbage pH Indicator

You can make your own pH indicator at home using a red cabbage! The purple pigment in the cabbage leaves changes colour at different pH levels. Before you start, here are the colours you should expect:

  • In **strong acids** (like lemon juice), it will turn **red/pink**.
  • In **neutral** solutions (like pure water), it will stay **purplish**.
  • In **alkalis** (like baking soda solution), it will turn **blue/green**.
Equipment:
  • A few leaves of red cabbage
  • A bowl and hot water (ask an adult for help)
  • A sieve or coffee filter
  • Several clear glasses or jars
  • Household substances to test (e.g., lemon juice, vinegar, tap water, baking soda mixed with water, soap solution).
Method:
  1. Tear the cabbage leaves into small pieces and place them in the bowl.
  2. Ask an adult to carefully pour hot water over the leaves until they are covered.
  3. Leave the cabbage to soak for about 15-20 minutes. The water will turn a deep purple colour.
  4. Carefully strain the purple liquid into a clean jar. This is your indicator solution!
  5. Pour a small amount of your indicator into several clean glasses.
  6. One by one, add a few drops of your test substances to the indicator and watch the colour change.
  7. Can you use your results to classify your household substances as acidic, alkaline, or neutral?
