8.6.4 Bioaccumulation

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain the process of bioaccumulation and its impact on food chains.

Bioaccumulation:

  • The build-up of a substance (usually a toxin or pesticide) in a living organism.
  • This occurs when an organism absorbs a substance at a rate faster than that at which the substance is lost by catabolism and excretion.

Biomagnification:

  • The increasing concentration of a substance in organisms at successively higher levels in a food chain.
  • How it works:
    1. A persistent toxin (one that is not easily broken down), such as DDT or mercury, is introduced into the environment.
    2. It is absorbed by producers (e.g., algae, plants).
    3. Primary consumers eat many producers, accumulating all the toxin from those producers in their own bodies.
    4. Secondary consumers eat many primary consumers, accumulating the toxin from all of them.
    5. This process continues up the food chain, so the top predators accumulate the highest and most dangerous concentration of the toxin.
  • Example: DDT
    • DDT was a widely used pesticide.
    • It washed into waterways and was absorbed by small aquatic organisms.
    • Small fish ate these organisms, accumulating DDT.
    • Large fish ate the small fish.
    • Birds of prey (like eagles) ate the large fish, accumulating very high levels of DDT.
    • This caused the shells of their eggs to become thin and break easily, leading to a massive decline in their populations.
