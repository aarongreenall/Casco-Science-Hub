20.3 Force on a moving charge

Learning Objectives:

  1. Determine the direction of the force on a charge moving in a magnetic field.
  2. Recall and use $F = BQv \sin \theta$.
  3. Understand the origin of the Hall voltage and derive and use the expression $V_H = BI / (ntq)$, where t = thickness.
  4. Understand the use of a Hall probe to measure magnetic flux density.
  5. Describe the motion of a charged particle moving in a uniform magnetic field perpendicular to the direction of motion of the particle.
  6. Explain how electric and magnetic fields can be used in velocity selection.

LO1 & LO2: Force on a Moving Charge

Since an electric current is a flow of charge carriers, the motor effect can be understood at the level of a single charge. A magnetic field exerts a force on an individual moving charge.

$$ F = BQv \sin \theta $$

  • $F$ is the force on the charge in newtons (N).
  • $B$ is the magnetic flux density in tesla (T).
  • $Q$ is the magnitude of the charge in coulombs (C).
  • $v$ is the speed of the charge in metres per second (m s⁻¹).
  • $\theta$ is the angle between the velocity vector of the charge and the magnetic field lines.

The direction of this force is given by **Fleming's Left-Hand Rule**, where the second finger (Current) now represents the direction of motion of a **positive charge**. If the moving charge is negative (like an electron), the force is in the **opposite direction** to that predicted by the rule.

LO5: Circular Motion in a Magnetic Field

Consider a charged particle moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field ($\theta = 90^\circ$, so $\sin\theta = 1$). The magnetic force is $F = BQv$. According to Fleming's Left-Hand Rule, this force is always perpendicular to the particle's velocity.

A force that is always perpendicular to the velocity will change the direction of the motion but not the speed. This is the exact condition required for **uniform circular motion**. The magnetic force provides the centripetal force.

Worked Example: Radius of Path

An electron is accelerated through a potential difference of 500 V and then enters a uniform magnetic field of flux density 2.0 mT, perpendicular to its path. Calculate the radius of the circular path it follows.

1. Find the speed of the electron ($v$): The work done by the electric field equals the kinetic energy gained.

$$ QV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{2QV}{m}} $$

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{2(1.60 \times 10^{-19})(500)}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}}} = 1.33 \times 10^7 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

2. Equate magnetic force and centripetal force:

$$ BQv = \frac{mv^2}{r} $$

3. Rearrange for radius ($r$):

$$ r = \frac{mv}{BQ} $$

4. Substitute values and solve:

$$ r = \frac{(9.11 \times 10^{-31})(1.33 \times 10^7)}{(2.0 \times 10^{-3})(1.60 \times 10^{-19})} = 0.0378 \text{ m} \approx 3.8 \text{ cm} $$

LO6: Velocity Selection

By applying both a uniform electric field and a uniform magnetic field, perpendicular to each other and to the initial velocity of a charged particle, we can create a **velocity selector**.

The electric field exerts a force $F_E = QE$ in one direction (e.g., downwards). The magnetic field exerts a force $F_B = BQv$ in the opposite direction (e.g., upwards). For a specific speed, these two forces will be equal in magnitude, resulting in a zero net force. Particles travelling at this specific speed will pass through undeflected, while faster or slower particles will be deflected.

$$ F_E = F_B \implies QE = BQv $$

$$ v = \frac{E}{B} $$

LO3 & LO4: The Hall Effect and Hall Probes

The **Hall effect** is the production of a potential difference (the **Hall voltage**) across an electrical conductor, transverse to an electric current in the conductor and a magnetic field perpendicular to the current.

When a current flows through a thin slice of semiconductor material in a magnetic field, the magnetic force ($F_B = BQv$) pushes the charge carriers (e.g., electrons) to one side of the slice. This accumulation of charge creates a transverse electric field, $E_H$. This field exerts an opposing electric force, $F_E = QE_H$, on the charge carriers. Equilibrium is reached when the two forces balance.

Derivation of the Hall Voltage Equation

1. At equilibrium, the magnetic force equals the electric force: $F_B = F_E$.

$$ Bqv = qE_H \implies Bv = E_H $$

2. The Hall voltage $V_H$ across the slice of width $w$ (often written as $d$ in other contexts) is related to the electric field by $E_H = V_H/w$.

$$ Bv = \frac{V_H}{w} \implies V_H = Bvw $$

3. From the drift velocity equation, we know $I=Anvq$. For a slice of thickness $t$ and width $w$, the area is $A=wt$. So, $v = \frac{I}{n(wt)q}$.

4. Substitute this expression for $v$ into the equation for $V_H$:

$$ V_H = B \left( \frac{I}{nwtq} \right) w $$

5. The width $w$ cancels, giving the final Hall voltage equation:

$$ V_H = \frac{BI}{ntq} $$

A **Hall probe** is a device that uses this principle to measure magnetic flux density. Since $V_H \propto B$ (for constant $I, n, t, q$), the probe can be calibrated so that its voltage output gives a direct reading of the magnetic field strength.
