7.3.4 Solar and Lunar Eclipses

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define a solar eclipse and a lunar eclipse.
  2. Explain how the alignment of the Sun, Earth, and Moon causes eclipses.
  3. Describe the difference between a total and partial eclipse using the terms umbra and penumbra.

What is an Eclipse?

An **eclipse** is a special event that happens when one object in space blocks the light from another object, casting a shadow. The two types of eclipses we can see from Earth involve the Sun, the Earth, and the Moon lining up in a perfectly straight line.

Analogy: Shadow Puppets

You can think of eclipses like making shadow puppets. The **Sun** is your **flashlight**. The **Earth** and the **Moon** are your **hands**. A **solar eclipse** happens when your "Moon" hand moves in front of the flashlight, casting a shadow on your "Earth" hand. A **lunar eclipse** happens when your "Earth" hand moves in front of the flashlight, casting a shadow on your "Moon" hand.

Solar Eclipse

A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes directly between the Sun and the Earth, and the Moon's shadow is cast onto the Earth. This can only happen during a New Moon.

Lunar Eclipse

A lunar eclipse occurs when the Earth passes directly between the Sun and the Moon, and the Earth's shadow is cast onto the Moon. This alignment (**Sun --- Earth --- Moon**) is also the exact same alignment that allows us to see a **Full Moon**. We see a Full Moon when we are looking at the side of the Moon that is fully lit up by the Sun, which only happens when the Moon is on the opposite side of the Earth from the Sun. Therefore, a lunar eclipse can only happen during a Full Moon, because that is the only time the Moon is in the correct position to pass into the Earth's shadow.

Total vs. Partial Eclipses

Shadows cast by large objects like the Earth and Moon have two parts: a dark, central part and a lighter, outer part.

  • The **Umbra** is the darkest, central part of the shadow, where the light source is completely blocked.
  • The **Penumbra** is the lighter, outer part of the shadow, where the light source is only partially blocked.

If you are in the umbra of the Moon's shadow during a solar eclipse, you will see a **total solar eclipse**. If you are in the penumbra, you will only see a **partial solar eclipse**.

At-Home Experiment: Modeling an Eclipse with a Pinhole Camera

Important Safety Warning!

You must **NEVER** look directly at the real Sun. Doing so can cause permanent eye damage and blindness. This experiment should only be performed with a desk lamp, or by projecting the Sun's image. Never look at the Sun through your pinhole camera.

Part 1: Build a Pinhole Camera
  1. Take a cardboard box (like a shoebox) and make a small, neat pinhole in the centre of one of the smaller ends.
  2. Tape a piece of white paper to the inside of the opposite end. This is your screen.
  3. Cut a large viewing hole in the side of the box so you can see the screen inside.
Part 2: Model an Eclipse
  1. In a darkened room, use a desk lamp with the lampshade removed as your "Sun".
  2. Cut a small circle (about 2-3 cm in diameter) out of another piece of cardboard and tape it to a stick or pencil. This is your "Moon".
  3. Point the pinhole of your camera at the lamp. You should see a small, inverted image of the bright lamp filament on your screen.
  4. Now, slowly move your cardboard "Moon" between the lamp and the pinhole. Watch the screen of your pinhole camera.
  5. Observe how you can create a **partial eclipse** (where the Moon covers part of the Sun) and a **total eclipse** (where the Moon covers the entire Sun).
  6. **Challenge:** Film your model eclipse using a phone. Provide a commentary explaining what is happening, using the words Sun, Earth (your camera), Moon, partial eclipse, and total eclipse.
