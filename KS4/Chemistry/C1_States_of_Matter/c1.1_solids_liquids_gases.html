C1.1 Solids, liquids and gases

Matter exists in different states, primarily solid, liquid, and gas. Understanding the distinguishing properties of these states and their underlying particle arrangements is fundamental to chemistry.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State the distinguishing properties of solids, liquids and gases.
  2. Describe the structure of solids, liquids and gases in terms of particle separation, arrangement and motion.
  3. Explain that scientists often use the particle model of matter to explain the observable macroscopic properties.

Distinguishing Properties and Particle Model:

The three common states of matter—solids, liquids, and gases—each exhibit unique macroscopic properties.

  • **Solids:** Have a fixed shape and a fixed volume. They are not easily compressed.
  • **Liquids:** Have a fixed volume but take the shape of their container. They are not easily compressed.
  • **Gases:** Have no fixed shape or volume, expanding to fill their container. They are easily compressed.

*Figure: Diagrams illustrating the particle arrangement, separation, and motion in solids, liquids, and gases.*

Scientists use the **kinetic particle model of matter** to explain these observable properties. This model posits that all matter is made up of tiny particles (atoms or molecules) that are in constant, random motion. The differences in state arise from the varying arrangements, separations, and motions of these particles, as well as the forces between them.

  • **Solids:** Particles are closely packed in a fixed, regular arrangement (a lattice). They vibrate about fixed positions but do not move past each other. Strong forces of attraction exist between particles.
  • **Liquids:** Particles are closely packed but in a random arrangement. They can slide past each other, allowing liquids to flow. Forces of attraction are weaker than in solids but still significant.
  • **Gases:** Particles are widely separated and arranged randomly. They move rapidly and randomly, colliding with each other and the container walls. Forces of attraction between particles are negligible.
