8.8.2 Ecosystems and Invasive Species

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will define ecosystems and explain the threat posed by invasive species.

Ecosystems:

  • An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (biotic components) interacting with their non-living physical environment (abiotic components).
  • Ecosystems have a delicate balance, with complex food webs and nutrient cycles.

Invasive Species:

  • An invasive species is an organism that is not native to a specific location and has a tendency to spread to a degree believed to cause damage to the environment, economy, or human health.
  • Characteristics of Invasive Species:
    • Fast growth and reproduction.
    • Few or no natural predators or diseases in the new environment.
    • Ability to outcompete native species for resources like food, water, and space.
  • Impact on Ecosystems:
    • Competition: They compete with native species for resources, often leading to a decline in native populations.
    • Predation: They may prey on native species that have not evolved defenses against them.
    • Habitat Alteration: They can change the physical environment, making it unsuitable for native species.
    • Disease: They can introduce new diseases to which native species have no immunity.
  • Examples: Cane toads in Australia, zebra mussels in the Great Lakes, grey squirrels in the UK (outcompeting red squirrels).
