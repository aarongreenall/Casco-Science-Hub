21.2 Rectification and smoothing

Learning Objectives:

  1. Distinguish graphically between half-wave and full-wave rectification.
  2. Explain the use of a single diode for the half-wave rectification of an alternating current.
  3. Explain the use of four diodes (bridge rectifier) for the full-wave rectification of an alternating current.
  4. Analyse the effect of a single capacitor in smoothing, including the effect of the values of capacitance and the load resistance.

LO1: Graphical Representation of Rectification

**Rectification** is the process of converting an alternating current (a.c.), which periodically reverses direction, into a direct current (d.c.), which flows in only one direction. Most electronic devices require a steady d.c. supply to function, but mains electricity is supplied as a.c.

The difference between the two main types of rectification is best understood graphically:

  • Half-wave rectification blocks one half of the a.c. cycle (usually the negative half), resulting in a pulsating d.c. output with gaps.
  • Full-wave rectification inverts the negative half of the a.c. cycle, converting it to positive. This results in a more continuous, pulsating d.c. output without gaps.

LO2: Half-Wave Rectification with a Single Diode

Half-wave rectification can be achieved using a single semiconductor diode. A diode acts as a one-way valve for current, allowing it to pass when it is **forward-biased** and blocking it when it is **reverse-biased**.

Positive Half-Cycle: When the top of the a.c. supply is positive, the diode is forward-biased. It has a very low resistance, allowing current to flow through the load resistor $R$. A potential difference develops across $R$.

Negative Half-Cycle: When the top of the a.c. supply is negative, the diode is reverse-biased. It has a very high resistance, blocking the flow of current. No current flows through $R$, and the output voltage is zero.

LO3: Full-Wave Rectification with a Bridge Rectifier

To achieve full-wave rectification, a configuration of four diodes known as a **bridge rectifier** is used. This clever arrangement ensures that the current always flows through the load resistor in the same direction, regardless of the polarity of the a.c. supply.

Positive Half-Cycle: Current flows from the positive terminal, through diode D1, down through the load resistor $R$, and back to the negative terminal through diode D4.

Negative Half-Cycle: The polarity of the supply reverses. Current now flows from the new positive terminal (bottom wire), through diode D2, down through the load resistor $R$ (in the same direction as before), and back to the new negative terminal through diode D3.

LO4: Smoothing with a Capacitor

The output from a rectifier is a pulsating d.c. voltage. To create a steady d.c. voltage suitable for electronic devices, we need to **smooth** this output. This is achieved by connecting a capacitor in parallel with the load resistor.

The capacitor charges up as the rectified voltage rises. As the voltage begins to fall, the capacitor starts to discharge through the load resistor, supplying current and keeping the voltage high. The result is a much smoother d.c. voltage with a small variation called a **ripple**.

Analogy: The Water Tank Dam

Imagine your rectified voltage is like a river fed by a pulsing, irregular source of rain. The flow (current) comes in big gushes. A **capacitor** is like a large reservoir or dam built on this river. When the rain is heavy (voltage peak), the reservoir fills up, storing water (charge). When the rain stops (voltage trough), the dam releases its stored water, maintaining a steady, continuous flow downstream. The **load resistor** is like the town downstream that needs this steady flow. A large dam with a small town downstream (large time constant) will provide a very smooth, almost constant water level. A small dam with a large town (small time constant) will empty quickly, resulting in a much more noticeable drop in flow between rainfalls (a large ripple).

The effectiveness of the smoothing depends on the **time constant ($\tau = RC$)** of the capacitor-resistor combination.

  • A **large time constant** (large C and/or R) means the capacitor discharges slowly. This results in a very smooth output with a small ripple.
  • A **small time constant** means the capacitor discharges quickly, resulting in less effective smoothing and a large ripple.
