20.2 Force on a current-carrying conductor

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that a force might act on a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field.
  2. Recall and use the equation $F = BIL \sin \theta$, with directions as interpreted by Fleming’s left-hand rule.
  3. Define magnetic flux density as the force acting per unit current per unit length on a wire placed at right-angles to the magnetic field.

LO1: The Motor Effect

We know that moving charges create a magnetic field. It follows that a magnetic field will exert a force on a moving charge. An electric current is a flow of moving charge carriers (electrons). Therefore, when a wire carrying an electric current is placed in an external magnetic field, it will experience a force. This phenomenon is known as the **motor effect**.

The force arises from the interaction between the magnetic field of the permanent magnet and the magnetic field created by the current in the wire. The two fields superpose, creating a stronger field on one side of the wire and a weaker field on the other. This results in a net force pushing the wire from the region of stronger field to the region of weaker field.

LO2: Calculating the Magnetic Force

The magnitude of the force on a current-carrying wire in a magnetic field depends on the strength of the field, the size of the current, the length of the wire in the field, and the angle between the wire and the field lines.

$$ F = BIL \sin \theta $$

  • $F$ is the force on the conductor in newtons (N).
  • $B$ is the magnetic flux density of the external field in tesla (T).
  • $I$ is the current in the conductor in amperes (A).
  • $L$ is the length of the conductor within the magnetic field in metres (m).
  • $\theta$ is the angle between the conductor and the magnetic field lines.

The direction of the force is always perpendicular to both the current and the magnetic field, and is given by **Fleming's Left-Hand Rule**.

  • Thumb represents the direction of the **Th**rust or **F**orce.
  • First finger represents the direction of the magnetic **F**ield (North to South).
  • se**C**ond finger represents the direction of the conventional **C**urrent (Positive to Negative).

Worked Example: Force on a Wire

A straight wire of length 5.0 cm is placed in a uniform magnetic field of flux density 0.040 T. The wire carries a current of 2.0 A and makes an angle of 30° with the field lines. Calculate the magnitude of the force on the wire.

1. Identify variables and convert to SI units:

$$ B = 0.040 \text{ T}, \quad I = 2.0 \text{ A}, \quad L = 5.0 \text{ cm} = 0.050 \text{ m}, \quad \theta = 30^\circ $$

2. State the formula and substitute values:

$$ F = BIL \sin \theta $$

$$ F = (0.040) \times (2.0) \times (0.050) \times \sin(30^\circ) $$

$$ F = (0.004) \times (0.5) $$

$$ F = 0.0020 \text{ N} \text{ (or 2.0 mN)} $$

LO3: Defining Magnetic Flux Density ($B$)

The quantity $B$, **magnetic flux density**, is a measure of the strength of a magnetic field. We can define it formally by rearranging the motor effect equation for the case of maximum force, which occurs when the wire is perpendicular to the field ($\theta = 90^\circ$, so $\sin\theta = 1$).

Magnetic flux density is the force acting per unit current per unit length on a wire placed at right-angles to the magnetic field.

$$ B = \frac{F}{IL} $$

The SI unit for magnetic flux density is the **tesla (T)**. From the equation, we can see that $1 \text{ T} = 1 \text{ N A}^{-1} \text{m}^{-1}$. One tesla is a very strong magnetic field; the Earth's magnetic field is about 50 $\mu$T.
