10.3 Potential dividers

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand the principle of a potential divider circuit.
  2. Derive and use the potential divider formula.
  3. Understand the use of a potential divider circuit with a sensor (thermistor or LDR).
  4. Understand the use of a potentiometer as a variable potential divider.

LO1: The Principle of a Potential Divider

Why Use a Potential Divider? Use Cases

In simple terms, a potential divider is a basic electronic circuit that takes a larger voltage and splits it into a smaller one. Imagine you have a power source with a certain voltage, but a part of your circuit needs a lower voltage to work correctly. A potential divider allows you to tap off a fraction of the total voltage.

Potential dividers are incredibly common and have a wide range of applications in electronics. Here are a few examples:

  • Providing a Reference Voltage: Many electronic circuits, such as comparators and analog-to-digital converters, need a stable reference voltage to function correctly. A potential divider is a simple way to create this.
  • Sensor Circuits: Many sensors are essentially variable resistors, meaning their resistance changes in response to a physical quantity like light (LDRs) or temperature (thermistors). By using such a sensor as one of the resistors in a potential divider, you can convert the change in resistance into a change in voltage, which is easier for a microcontroller to measure.
  • Volume Controls: In audio equipment, a potentiometer (a type of variable resistor) is often used as a potential divider to adjust the amplitude of the audio signal, which we perceive as a change in volume.

Circuit Principle and Structure

The most basic potential divider is made by connecting two resistors, $R_1$ and $R_2$, in series. The input voltage ($V_{in}$) is applied across the pair of resistors, and the output voltage ($V_{out}$) is taken from the point between them (in parallel with the second resistor, $R_2$).

The principle is based on the rules of series circuits: the input voltage is shared between the resistors ($R_1$ and $R_2$) in proportion to their resistance. The resistor with the larger resistance will have a larger share of the potential difference across it. By choosing the ratio of the resistances, we can precisely control the output voltage.

LO2: The Potential Divider Formula

Detailed Derivation of the Formula

1. Apply Kirchhoff's Second Law to the closed loop. The sum of p.d.s across the resistors must equal the e.m.f. of the source (assuming an ideal source, $V_{in} = \mathcal{E}$).

$$ V_{in} = V_1 + V_2 $$

2. Apply Ohm's Law ($V=IR$) to each resistor in the series branch. The current $I$ is the same for both.

$$ V_{in} = (I R_1) + (I R_2) $$

3. Factor out the common current, $I$:

$$ V_{in} = I (R_1 + R_2) $$

4. Rearrange to find an expression for the current flowing through the series branch:

$$ I = \frac{V_{in}}{R_1 + R_2} $$

5. The output voltage, $V_{out}$, is the potential difference across resistor $R_2$ only. Applying Ohm's Law to $R_2$:

$$ V_{out} = I \times R_2 $$

6. Substitute the expression for the current $I$ from step 4 into the equation from step 5:

$$ V_{out} = \left( \frac{V_{in}}{R_1 + R_2} \right) \times R_2 $$

7. Rearranging gives the final potential divider formula:

$$ V_{out} = V_{in} \left( \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \right) $$

The Loading Effect: A Detailed Explanation

The simple formula above is only valid when **no current is drawn** from the output. This is called an **unloaded** divider. In practice, when you connect a component (a "load", with resistance $R_L$) across $R_2$ to use the output voltage, you create a parallel branch.

This **loading effect** changes the circuit dynamics, causing the output voltage to drop. Here is why:

  1. Parallel Resistance Decreases: When you connect the load $R_L$ in parallel with $R_2$, you create a new combined resistance for the lower branch, $R_p$. The formula for parallel resistors is $\frac{1}{R_p} = \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_L}$. A key consequence is that the combined resistance of a parallel branch is **always less** than the smallest individual resistance in that branch. Therefore, $R_p < R_2$.
  2. Ratio of Resistances Changes: The potential divider now splits the input voltage between $R_1$ and the new, smaller resistance $R_p$. The ratio of the lower resistance to the total resistance, $\frac{R_p}{R_1 + R_p}$, is now smaller than the original ratio, $\frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$.
  3. Output Voltage Drops: Since the output voltage is this new, smaller fraction of the input voltage, the value of $V_{out}$ decreases when the load is connected.

Worked Example: The Loading Effect

A potential divider is made from a 12 V supply connected to a 4 k$\Omega$ and a 2 k$\Omega$ resistor in series. Calculate (a) the unloaded output voltage across the 2 k$\Omega$ resistor, and (b) the output voltage when a voltmeter with a resistance of 10 k$\Omega$ is connected across the 2 k$\Omega$ resistor.

(a) Unloaded Output Voltage:

Here, $R_1 = 4$ k$\Omega$ and $R_2 = 2$ k$\Omega$.

$$ V_{out} = V_{in} \left( \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \right) = 12 \left( \frac{2}{4 + 2} \right) = 12 \left( \frac{2}{6} \right) = 4.0 \text{ V} $$

(b) Loaded Output Voltage:

1. First, calculate the combined resistance, $R_p$, of the parallel branch containing $R_2$ and the voltmeter ($R_L = 10$ k$\Omega$).

$$ \frac{1}{R_p} = \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_L} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{10} = \frac{5+1}{10} = \frac{6}{10} $$

$$ R_p = \frac{10}{6} = 1.67 \text{ k}\Omega $$

2. Now, use the potential divider formula with this new combined resistance in place of $R_2$.

$$ V_{out} = V_{in} \left( \frac{R_p}{R_1 + R_p} \right) = 12 \left( \frac{1.67}{4 + 1.67} \right) = 12 \left( \frac{1.67}{5.67} \right) = 3.53 \text{ V} $$

As predicted, connecting the load caused the output voltage to drop from 4.0 V to 3.5 V.

LO3: Sensor Circuits

Potential dividers are extremely useful in sensor circuits. By replacing one of the fixed resistors with a sensor whose resistance changes with physical conditions (like a thermistor or an LDR), the output voltage will change in response to the environment.

LO4: The Potentiometer

A **potentiometer** is a variable resistor with three terminals that is specifically designed to be used as a continuously variable potential divider. It is a fundamental component for applications where a user needs to adjust a voltage, such as in a volume control or a dimmer switch.

Construction and Principle

A potentiometer consists of a fixed resistive track (e.g., a strip of carbon or a coil of wire) with a terminal at each end (A and B). A third terminal (C) is connected to a sliding contact (or "wiper") that can be moved along the entire length of the track.

In the circuit diagram, the resistive track is the total resistance, $R_{total}$. The slider splits this track into two resistances, $R_{AC}$ and $R_{CB}$. The total resistance of the track is constant ($R_{total} = R_{AC} + R_{CB}$).

The slider C "taps off" the voltage across the portion of the track between C and B. Therefore, the resistance $R_{CB}$ acts as $R_2$ in our potential divider formula, and the resistance $R_{AC}$ acts as $R_1$.

By moving the slider, the ratio of $R_{AC}$ to $R_{CB}$ is changed. This, in turn, changes the output voltage, $V_{out}$, which can be varied smoothly from:

  • 0 V (when the slider is at terminal B, making the resistance of $R_{CB}$ zero).
  • The full input voltage, $V_{in}$ (when the slider is at terminal A, making the resistance of $R_{CB}$ equal to the total resistance).

Worked Example: Potentiometer Output

A 12 V supply is connected across a 10 k$\Omega$ potentiometer. What is the output voltage when the slider is set to one quarter of the way up from the bottom (terminal B)?

1. Determine the resistances $R_1$ and $R_2$:

The total resistance is 10 k$\Omega$. The slider is 1/4 of the way up from B, so the resistance of the lower part ($R_2 = R_{CB}$) is 1/4 of the total.

$$R_2 = \frac{1}{4} \times 10 \text{ k}\Omega = 2.5 \text{ k}\Omega$$

The resistance of the upper part ($R_1 = R_{AC}$) is the remainder:

$$R_1 = 10 \text{ k}\Omega - 2.5 \text{ k}\Omega = 7.5 \text{ k}\Omega$$

2. Apply the potential divider formula:

$$V_{out} = V_{in} \left( \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \right) = V_{in} \left( \frac{R_2}{R_{total}} \right)$$

$$V_{out} = 12 \left( \frac{2.5}{10} \right)$$

$$V_{out} = 12 \times 0.25 = 3.0 \text{ V}$$
