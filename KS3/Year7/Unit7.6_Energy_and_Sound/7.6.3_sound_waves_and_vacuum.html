7.6.3 Sound waves and the vacuum

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Explain that sound is a mechanical wave that requires a medium to travel.
  2. Define a vacuum as a space with no particles.
  3. Explain why sound cannot travel through a vacuum.

Why Sound Needs to Travel Through Something

We know that sound is caused by vibrations. For the sound to travel from the source to our ears, those vibrations need something to travel through. This "something" is called a **medium**. A medium can be a solid, a liquid, or a gas.

The vibrations from the source bump into the particles of the medium, which then bump into their neighbours, and so on. This chain reaction carries the sound energy from one place to another.

Analogy: The Domino Rally

Imagine a long line of dominoes. The dominoes are the **particles of the medium**. When you push the first domino (the **vibrating source**), it passes its energy to the next, which passes it to the next, creating a wave that travels all the way down the line. The dominoes themselves only move a little bit, but the wave of energy travels a long way. Now, what happens if you leave a gap in the line of dominoes? The wave stops! That gap is like a **vacuum**.

A **vacuum** is a space with no particles in it. Because there are no particles to pass the vibrations along, **sound cannot travel through a vacuum**. This is why there is no sound in outer space.

At-Home Experiment: Making a String Telephone

This classic experiment is a fun and simple way to prove that sound travels through a solid medium (the string) much better than it does through a gas (the air).

Equipment:
  • Two paper or plastic cups
  • A long piece of string (5-10 metres is great)
  • A sharp pencil or a pin
  • A friend to help you!
Method:
  1. Carefully poke a small hole in the centre of the bottom of each cup.
  2. Thread one end of the string through the hole in one cup, from the outside in. Tie a large knot on the inside of the cup so the string can't pull out.
  3. Do the same with the other end of the string and the second cup.
  4. You and your friend should each take a cup and walk apart until the string is pulled tight. **The string must be taut for this to work!**
  5. One person should put the cup to their ear (the receiver) while the other person speaks into their cup (the transmitter).
  6. Now, try talking to your friend from the same distance without the string telephone. Which is louder and clearer?
What's Happening?

When you speak into the cup, the sound waves make the bottom of the cup vibrate. These vibrations travel along the taut string to the other cup, making its bottom vibrate too. This second cup then turns the vibrations back into sound waves that your friend can hear. The sound is much clearer than through the air because the solid string is a better medium for transmitting the vibrations.
