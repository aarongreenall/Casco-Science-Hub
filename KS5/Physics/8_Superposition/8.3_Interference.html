8.3 Interference

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand the terms interference and coherence.
  2. Show an understanding of experiments that demonstrate two-source interference using water waves in a ripple tank, sound, light and microwaves.
  3. Understand the conditions required if two-source interference fringes are to be observed.
  4. Recall and use $\lambda=ax/D$ for double-slit interference using light.

LO1: Interference and Coherence

**Interference** is the phenomenon that occurs when two or more waves superpose to form a resultant wave of greater, lower, or the same amplitude. This effect is a direct consequence of the principle of superposition.

For a stable and observable interference pattern to be formed, the wave sources must be **coherent**.

Two sources are **coherent** if they emit waves with a **constant phase difference** between them.

This also implies that the sources must have the same frequency. If the phase difference between the sources were to change randomly, the positions of maximum and minimum intensity would also change randomly, and a stable interference pattern would not be seen.

LO2 & LO3: Demonstrating Two-Source Interference

Two-source interference can be demonstrated with various types of waves. The key is to create two coherent sources. This is often achieved by splitting a single wave source into two.

  • Water Waves: In a ripple tank, a single vibrating dipper can be replaced by a double dipper to create two coherent circular wave sources.
  • Sound Waves: Two loudspeakers connected to the same signal generator will act as two coherent sources.
  • Light Waves (Young's Double-Slit Experiment): Light from a single source (like a laser) is passed through two very narrow, closely spaced slits. Diffraction at the slits causes the light to spread out, and the two slits act as two coherent point sources.

Conditions for Observable Interference

To observe a clear, stable two-source interference pattern (e.g., bright and dark fringes for light), the following conditions are required:

  1. The sources must be **coherent** (constant phase difference).
  2. The waves must have approximately the **same amplitude** for the destructive interference to be effective (to produce points of minimum intensity).
  3. For transverse waves like light, the waves must be polarised in the **same plane**.

LO4: The Double-Slit Formula

For Young's double-slit experiment, we can derive an equation that relates the wavelength of light to the geometry of the setup.

Derivation of $\lambda = \frac{ax}{D}$

Consider two slits, S₁ and S₂, separated by a distance $a$. A screen is placed at a large distance $D$ from the slits. For a bright fringe (constructive interference) to form at a point P, the path difference between the waves from S₁ and S₂ must be a whole number of wavelengths ($n\lambda$).

1. From the diagram, the path difference is the extra distance travelled by the wave from S₂, which is $a \sin\theta$. For a bright fringe: $a \sin\theta = n\lambda$.

2. The distance $D$ is much larger than $a$ ($D \gg a$), so the angle $\theta$ is very small. For small angles measured in radians, we can use the approximation:

$$ \sin\theta \approx \tan\theta $$

3. From the large triangle in the diagram, we can see that:

$$ \tan\theta = \frac{x}{D} $$

where $x$ is the distance from the central maximum to the bright fringe at P.

4. Combining these gives: $a \frac{x}{D} \approx n\lambda$.

5. The distance between adjacent bright fringes (the fringe spacing) occurs when the change in $n$ is 1. If we let $x$ be this fringe spacing, then $n=1$.

$$ a \frac{x}{D} = \lambda \implies \lambda = \frac{ax}{D} $$

Worked Example: Double-Slit Experiment

In a Young's double-slit experiment, light passes through two slits separated by 0.50 mm. An interference pattern is observed on a screen 2.0 m away. The distance between 10 consecutive bright fringes is measured to be 24 mm. Calculate the wavelength of the light.

1. Identify variables and convert to SI units:

$$ a = 0.50 \text{ mm} = 0.50 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m} $$

$$ D = 2.0 \text{ m} $$

2. Calculate the fringe spacing ($x$):

The distance for 10 fringes is 24 mm, which is 9 fringe spacings.

$$ 9x = 24 \text{ mm} \implies x = \frac{24}{9} = 2.67 \text{ mm} = 2.67 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m} $$

3. Apply the double-slit formula:

$$ \lambda = \frac{ax}{D} $$

$$ \lambda = \frac{(0.50 \times 10^{-3}) \times (2.67 \times 10^{-3})}{2.0} $$

$$ \lambda = 6.675 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} = 668 \text{ nm} $$
