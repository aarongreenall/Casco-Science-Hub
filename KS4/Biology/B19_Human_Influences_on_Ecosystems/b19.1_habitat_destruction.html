B19.1 Food supply

Ensuring a sustainable and sufficient **food supply** for a growing global population is one of the most critical challenges facing humanity. Modern agriculture employs various methods to increase food yield, but these often come with environmental consequences. Understanding these methods and their impacts is essential for addressing global food security and environmental sustainability.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the effects of deforestation, limited to: loss of habitats; soil erosion; flooding; increase of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere.
  2. Describe the effects of overfishing, limited to: extinction of species; loss of food source.
  3. Describe the effects of eutrophication caused by leached fertilisers, limited to: increased growth of producers, e.g. algae; death of producers due to competition for light; increased decomposition by bacteria; increased respiration by decomposers; reduction in oxygen; death of aquatic organisms, e.g. fish.
  4. Describe the effects of pollution by: sewage; industrial waste; plastics.
  5. Describe the effects of pesticides, limited to: bioaccumulation; reduction in biodiversity.

Impacts on Terrestrial Ecosystems:

Deforestation:

**Deforestation** is the clearing of forests for other land uses (e.g., agriculture, logging). It has severe environmental consequences:

  • **Loss of habitats:** Destroys the homes of countless species, leading to reduced biodiversity and potential extinctions.
  • **Soil erosion:** Tree roots hold soil in place. Without trees, soil is easily washed away by rain or blown away by wind, leading to loss of fertile land.
  • **Flooding:** Forests absorb large amounts of rainwater. Deforestation increases surface runoff, leading to increased risk and severity of floods.
  • **Increase of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere:** Trees absorb CO$_2$ during photosynthesis. When forests are cut down and burned or decompose, the stored carbon is released as CO$_2$, contributing to global warming and climate change.

Impacts on Aquatic Ecosystems:

Overfishing:

**Overfishing** occurs when fish are caught faster than they can reproduce, leading to a decline in fish populations. Its effects include:

  • **Extinction of species:** Some fish species may be driven to extinction, disrupting marine food webs.
  • **Loss of food source:** Depletion of fish stocks leads to a loss of a vital food source for human populations and other marine animals.
  • **Damage to marine ecosystems:** Fishing practices can damage habitats (e.g., coral reefs) and catch non-target species (bycatch).

Eutrophication (from Leached Fertilisers):

**Eutrophication** is the enrichment of water bodies with nutrients, often caused by excess fertilizers leaching from agricultural land into rivers and lakes. This process has several adverse effects:

  1. **Increased growth of producers:** The excess nutrients (nitrates, phosphates) cause a rapid increase in the growth of aquatic producers, especially algae, leading to an **algal bloom**.
  2. **Death of producers:** The dense layer of algae at the surface blocks sunlight, causing plants and algae deeper in the water to die due to **competition for light**.
  3. **Increased decomposition by bacteria:** Dead producers sink to the bottom, providing a large food source for decomposers (bacteria). This leads to **increased decomposition by bacteria**.
  4. **Increased respiration by decomposers:** The bacteria respire aerobically, consuming large amounts of dissolved oxygen in the water. This leads to a **reduction in oxygen** levels.
  5. **Death of aquatic organisms:** The lack of oxygen causes the **death of aquatic organisms** like fish and other invertebrates, leading to a "dead zone."

Pollution by Sewage, Industrial Waste, and Plastics:

Pollution from human activities severely impacts ecosystems:

  • **Sewage:** Untreated sewage contains organic matter and pathogens. When released into water bodies, decomposers break down the organic matter, consuming oxygen and leading to the death of aquatic life. Pathogens can also spread diseases.
  • **Industrial Waste:** Can contain a variety of harmful chemicals (e.g., heavy metals, toxic organic compounds) that can poison aquatic organisms, accumulate in food chains, and render water unsafe.
  • **Plastics:** Plastic waste (e.g., bags, bottles, microplastics) pollutes oceans and land. Animals can become entangled in larger plastics or ingest microplastics, leading to injury, starvation, and death. Plastics persist in the environment for very long periods.

Effects of Pesticides:

**Pesticides** are chemicals used to kill pests (e.g., insects, weeds). While useful in agriculture, they can have adverse environmental effects:

  • **Bioaccumulation:** Pesticides can accumulate in organisms over time, especially if they are not easily broken down. This is called **bioaccumulation**.
  • **Biomagnification:** As these contaminated organisms are eaten by predators, the concentration of the pesticide increases at successively higher trophic levels in a food chain (biomagnification), potentially reaching toxic levels in top predators.
  • **Reduction in biodiversity:** Pesticides can kill non-target species (beneficial insects, birds, mammals), leading to a reduction in biodiversity.
