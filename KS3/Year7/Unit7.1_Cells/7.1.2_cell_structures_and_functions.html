7.1.2 Cell structures and functions

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Identify the main parts of an animal cell.
  2. Identify the extra parts found in a plant cell.
  3. Describe the function (the "job") of each part.
  4. Explain the differences between animal and plant cells.

Inside an Animal Cell

Just like our bodies have organs (like the heart and lungs) to do different jobs, cells have tiny structures inside them called **organelles**. Each organelle has a specific function that helps keep the cell alive.

Analogy: The Cell as a City

Imagine a cell is like a busy city. Each part of the city has a special job to do to keep the whole city running smoothly. We can use this analogy to understand the job of each organelle.

Nucleus: The "City Hall" or control centre. It contains the cell's DNA (the master instructions) and controls all the cell's activities.

Cytoplasm: The jelly-like substance that fills the cell. It's like the ground and air of the city, where all the chemical reactions happen.

Cell Membrane: The "City Border." It controls what substances can enter and leave the cell.

Mitochondria: The "Power Plants." This is where respiration happens, releasing energy to power the cell's activities.

Ribosomes: Tiny "Factories" that build proteins, which are essential for the cell to grow and repair itself.

Inside a Plant Cell

Plant cells have all the same organelles as animal cells, but they also have three extra parts that animal cells do not have.

Cell Wall: The "Strong Outer City Wall." Made of tough cellulose, it provides strength and support to the plant cell and helps it keep a fixed shape.

Permanent Vacuole: The "Large Water Reservoir." This is a large, central sac filled with cell sap (a watery solution of sugars and salts). It helps to keep the cell firm and rigid.

Chloroplasts: The "Solar-Powered Food Factories." These contain a green substance called chlorophyll, which absorbs sunlight for photosynthesis, the process plants use to make their own food.

In-School Experiment: Observing Cheek Cells

In this experiment, you will prepare a slide of your own cheek cells to see a real animal cell under the microscope.

Equipment:
  • Clean cotton bud
  • Clean microscope slide and coverslip
  • Dropper bottle of methylene blue stain
  • Light microscope
Method:
  1. Gently rub the inside of your cheek with the clean cotton bud.
  2. Smear the cotton bud onto the centre of the clean microscope slide.
  3. Add one drop of methylene blue stain to the smear. This will make the nucleus visible.
  4. Carefully lower a coverslip onto the drop at an angle to avoid trapping air bubbles.
  5. Place the slide on the microscope stage and observe it, starting with the lowest power objective lens.
  6. Try to find a single, clear cell and draw it in your notebook. Can you identify the nucleus, cytoplasm, and cell membrane?
