9.5.1 The Excretory (Renal) System

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the structure of the human excretory (renal) system and its function (limited to kidneys filtering blood to remove urea, which is excreted in urine).

Your Body's Ultimate Cleaning Crew

Your body's cells are constantly carrying out chemical reactions to keep you alive. These reactions, known as metabolism, produce useful substances but also create waste products that can be toxic if they build up. The **excretory system** is the body's vital cleaning service, responsible for removing these waste products from the blood.

Excretion vs. Egestion

It's important not to confuse these two terms!
• Excretion is the removal of metabolic waste products made by your body's cells (like urea).
• Egestion is the removal of undigested food from the gut as feces. Feces are not a product of your cells, so this is not excretion.

Analogy: A City's Waste Management

Think of your body as a busy city. The cells are the houses and factories, constantly producing waste. The bloodstream is the road network where bin lorries collect this waste. The **kidneys** are the city's sophisticated waste treatment plant, filtering the waste out of the lorries and keeping the city clean and running smoothly.

Meet the Renal System

The main part of the excretory system is the **renal system** (also called the urinary system). It has four main parts:

  1. Kidneys: You have two bean-shaped kidneys. These are the master filters that clean the blood.
  2. Ureters: Two tubes that carry the filtered waste, now called urine, from the kidneys down to the bladder.
  3. Bladder: A muscular, stretchy bag that stores urine until you are ready to go to the toilet.
  4. Urethra: A tube that carries urine from the bladder out of the body.

The Kidneys: Master Filters

The real work of the excretory system happens in the kidneys. Your entire blood supply passes through your kidneys many times each day. Their main job is to filter a toxic waste product called **urea** from the blood. Urea is produced in the liver when it breaks down excess proteins that your body doesn't need.

Inside the kidneys, millions of tiny filtering units remove urea, excess water, and excess mineral salts from the blood. These substances combine to form **urine**. The newly cleaned blood then leaves the kidneys to continue circulating around the body.

Analogy: A Coffee Filter

The filtering process in the kidneys is like making coffee with a filter paper. You pour a mixture of coffee grounds and hot water into the filter. The filter paper has tiny holes that are large enough to let the water and dissolved coffee flavours (like urine) pass through, but small enough to trap the solid coffee grounds (like blood cells and large proteins). The useful parts stay in the filter, while the waste is removed.

In-School Activity: Modelling Filtration

You can model the basic principle of kidney filtration with simple lab equipment.

You will need:

  • A beaker, filter funnel, and filter paper
  • A mixture made of water, a little sand, some salt, and a drop of food colouring

Procedure:

  1. Set up the filter funnel over the beaker with the filter paper inside.
  2. Slowly pour the mixture into the filter paper.
  3. Observe what passes through the filter paper (the filtrate) and what is left behind.

Explanation:

The water, dissolved salt (like urea), and food colouring pass through the filter paper. The sand particles (like large blood cells) are too big and get trapped. This is a simple model of how kidneys separate small, dissolved waste products from the larger, essential components of blood.
