17.1 Simple harmonic motion

  4. Use the equations $v = v_0 \cos \omega t$ and $v = \pm \omega \sqrt{(x_0^2 - x^2)}$.
  5. Analyse and interpret graphical representations of the variations of displacement, velocity and acceleration for simple harmonic motion.

LO1: Terminology of Oscillations

An **oscillation** is a repeating, periodic motion about a central equilibrium position. The terms used to describe oscillations are very similar to those used for waves, as waves are generated by oscillating sources.

  • Displacement ($x$): The instantaneous distance of the oscillating object from its equilibrium position.
  • Amplitude ($x_0$): The maximum displacement from the equilibrium position.
  • Period ($T$): The time taken for one complete oscillation.
  • Frequency ($f$): The number of complete oscillations per unit time. $f = 1/T$.
  • Angular Frequency ($\omega$): A measure of the rate of oscillation in radians per second. It is directly related to frequency and period:

$$ \omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T} $$

Phase Difference ($\phi$): Describes how "out of sync" two oscillating objects with the same frequency are. It is the fraction of an oscillation by which one leads or lags the other, measured in radians or degrees.

LO2 & LO3: The Defining Equation of SHM

**Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)** is a special type of oscillation that occurs under specific conditions.

An object undergoes simple harmonic motion if its acceleration is directly proportional to its displacement from a fixed equilibrium point, and is always directed towards that point.

This means the acceleration vector always points in the opposite direction to the displacement vector. Mathematically, this is expressed as:

$$ a \propto -x $$

The constant of proportionality is the square of the angular frequency, $\omega^2$. This gives the **defining equation of SHM**:

$$ a = -\omega^2x $$

The solution to this second-order differential equation describes the displacement $x$ at any time $t$. For an object starting at the equilibrium position ($x=0$ at $t=0$), the solution is:

$$ x = x_0 \sin(\omega t) $$

LO4: Velocity in SHM

The velocity of an object in SHM is not constant. It is maximum as the object passes through the equilibrium position ($x=0$) and is momentarily zero at the points of maximum displacement ($x = \pm x_0$).

The equation for velocity as a function of time is:

$$ v = x_0\omega \cos(\omega t) $$

Since the maximum value of $\cos(\omega t)$ is 1, the maximum velocity, $v_0$, is:

$$ v_0 = x_0\omega $$

We can also find an equation for velocity as a function of displacement, $x$.

Derivation of $v = \pm \omega \sqrt{x_0^2 - x^2}$

1. Start with the equations for displacement and velocity:

$$ x = x_0 \sin(\omega t) \quad \text{and} \quad v = x_0\omega \cos(\omega t) $$

2. Rearrange them to make the trigonometric terms the subject:

$$ \sin(\omega t) = \frac{x}{x_0} \quad \text{and} \quad \cos(\omega t) = \frac{v}{x_0\omega} $$

3. Use the fundamental trigonometric identity:

$$ \sin^2(\omega t) + \cos^2(\omega t) = 1 $$

4. Substitute the expressions from step 2 into the identity:

$$ \left(\frac{x}{x_0}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{v}{x_0\omega}\right)^2 = 1 $$

5. Rearrange to make $v^2$ the subject:

$$ \frac{v^2}{(x_0\omega)^2} = 1 - \frac{x^2}{x_0^2} = \frac{x_0^2 - x^2}{x_0^2} $$

$$ v^2 = (x_0\omega)^2 \left( \frac{x_0^2 - x^2}{x_0^2} \right) = \omega^2 (x_0^2 - x^2) $$

6. Take the square root of both sides:

$$ v = \pm \omega \sqrt{x_0^2 - x^2} $$

LO5: Graphical Representations of SHM

Plotting displacement, velocity, and acceleration against time reveals the phase relationships between them.

  • The acceleration graph is a negative sine curve. It is in **antiphase** with the displacement (a phase difference of $\pi$ radians or 180Â°), which is the defining condition of SHM.
