9.7.2 Conservation of Mass and Energy

In this lesson, you will learn that:

  1. In chemical reactions mass and energy are conserved.

The Two Golden Rules of a Chemical Reaction

All chemical reactions, from a simple campfire to the complex reactions in our bodies, are governed by two fundamental laws of the universe. These laws tell us that nothing is ever truly lost during a reaction; it just gets rearranged or transferred.

The Law of Conservation of Mass

This law states that **mass cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction**. This means that the total mass of all the reactants at the start must be exactly equal to the total mass of all the products at the end. The atoms are simply rearranged to form new substances, but no atoms are ever lost or gained.

Analogy: LEGO® Bricks

Imagine you have a pile of red and blue LEGO® bricks with a total mass of 500g. You use these bricks to build a house. If you weigh the finished house, its mass will be exactly 500g. No bricks were created or destroyed; they were just rearranged into a new structure. A chemical reaction does the same with atoms.

Misconception Alert: "Lost" Mass

When a log burns, the ash left behind has much less mass than the original log. It seems like mass has been lost, but it hasn't! The reaction happened in an **open system**, meaning substances could escape. The "missing" mass has turned into gases (carbon dioxide and water vapour) and escaped into the air. If you could perform the reaction in a sealed container (a **closed system**) and weigh everything, including the gases, the total mass would be exactly the same before and after.

The Law of Conservation of Energy

This is another golden rule: **energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred from one store to another**. In chemical reactions, we are mainly concerned with the transfer of energy from the **chemical energy store** (in the bonds of the reactants) to other stores, usually heat and light.

Exothermic Reactions

These reactions **release** energy into the surroundings, which get hotter. The products have less chemical energy than the reactants. Combustion (burning) is a classic example.

Endothermic Reactions

These reactions **absorb** energy from the surroundings, which get colder. The products have more chemical energy than the reactants. Sports injury cold packs are a good example.

In-School Investigation: Proving Conservation of Mass

This experiment demonstrates that even when a gas is produced, no mass is lost in a closed system.

You will need:

  • A conical flask
  • A small amount of vinegar (acetic acid)
  • A teaspoon of baking soda (sodium bicarbonate)
  • A balloon
  • A sensitive mass balance

Procedure:

  1. Pour the vinegar into the conical flask.
  2. Carefully put the baking soda into the balloon.
  3. Stretch the neck of the balloon over the neck of the flask, being careful not to let the baking soda fall in yet.
  4. Place the whole setup on the mass balance and record the starting mass.
  5. Lift the balloon to tip the baking soda into the vinegar. The mixture will fizz and the balloon will inflate.
  6. Once the reaction stops, place the flask with the inflated balloon back on the mass balance. Record the final mass.

Observation:

You will see that the mass reading on the balance **does not change**. The gas produced (carbon dioxide) is trapped in the balloon, so its mass is still part of the system. This proves that mass is conserved in the reaction.
