7.6.2 Energy dissipation

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define energy dissipation.
  2. Explain why sounds get quieter as they travel away from a source.
  3. Relate the loudness of a sound to the amplitude of the sound wave.

Why Do Sounds Fade Away?

If you shout to a friend who is far away, they might not hear you. This is because the sound gets quieter as it travels. The scientific term for this process of energy spreading out and being lost is **energy dissipation**.

There are two main reasons why sound dissipates:

  1. Spreading Out: Sound waves travel outwards from a source in all directions, like an expanding sphere. The total energy of the wave is spread over a larger and larger area, so the energy at any single point becomes less. This means the **amplitude** of the wave (how big the vibrations are) gets smaller, and we hear a quieter sound.
  2. Absorption: As the sound wave travels through a medium (like air or a wall), the particles of the medium vibrate. A small amount of this vibration energy is converted into thermal energy (heat) due to friction between the particles. This energy is lost from the sound wave, also making it quieter.

Analogy: Ripples in a Pond

Let's go back to the analogy of dropping a stone in a pond. The ripples (sound waves) are biggest and most powerful right where the stone was dropped (the source). As the ripples spread out in a bigger and bigger circle, the energy is shared over a much larger area. This causes the ripples to get smaller and smaller (a decrease in amplitude) until they eventually fade away. This is exactly how the energy of a sound wave dissipates as it travels through the air.
