7.1.1 Understanding Cells and Microorganisms

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define a cell as the basic unit of life.
  2. Define a microorganism as a living thing that is too small to be seen with the naked eye.
  3. Give examples of cells and microorganisms.
  4. Calculate magnification from diagrams.

What is a Cell?

Every living thing, from the smallest ant to the largest blue whale, is made of **cells**. A cell is the smallest, most basic "building block of life." It's the smallest part of an organism that can be considered alive. Most cells are so tiny that we can only see them with a microscope.

Analogy: LEGO Bricks

Think of a giant LEGO model of a castle. The entire castle is the living organism. If you look closely, you'll see it's built from thousands of individual LEGO bricks. Each single brick is like a **cell**. You need lots of them working together to build the whole castle, just like you need lots of cells working together to make a plant or an animal.

What are Microorganisms?

Some living things are made of just one single cell! These are called **microorganisms** (or microbes). They are living organisms that are too small to be seen without a microscope. They are found everywhere on Earth: in the soil, in the air, in water, and even inside our bodies.

Some common examples of microorganisms include:

  • Bacteria: Single-celled organisms that can be helpful (like those in yogurt) or harmful (like those that cause illness).
  • Yeast: A type of single-celled fungus used to make bread rise and to brew drinks.
  • Amoeba: A single-celled organism that lives in pond water and moves by changing its shape.

Calculating Magnification

Because cells are so small, we draw them much larger than they really are. **Magnification** tells us how many times larger a drawing or image is compared to the real object. We can calculate it using a simple formula:

$$ \text{Magnification} = \frac{\text{Image Size}}{\text{Actual Size}} $$

Worked Example: Magnification

A student draws a diagram of a cheek cell. The actual size of the cell is 0.05 mm wide. The drawing is 5.0 cm wide. Calculate the magnification of the drawing.

1. Make sure the units are the same. It's easiest to convert everything to millimetres (mm).

$$ \text{Image Size} = 5.0 \text{ cm} = 50 \text{ mm} $$

$$ \text{Actual Size} = 0.05 \text{ mm} $$

2. Use the formula:

$$ \text{Magnification} = \frac{50 \text{ mm}}{0.05 \text{ mm}} $$

3. Calculate the answer:

$$ \text{Magnification} = 1000\text{x} $$

The drawing is 1000 times larger than the real cell.

In-School Experiment: Using a Light Microscope

The best way to understand cells is to see them for yourself! In this experiment, you will learn the basic parts of a microscope and use it to observe some prepared slides.

Equipment:
  • A light microscope
  • Prepared slides of simple specimens (e.g., insect wing, plant stem)
Method:
  1. Place the microscope on the bench and plug it in. Turn on the light source (the illuminator).
  2. Place a prepared slide on the stage and secure it with the clips.
  3. Always start with the lowest power objective lens (usually 4x).
  4. Look through the eyepiece and turn the coarse focusing knob until the image comes into focus.
  5. Use the fine focusing knob to get a sharp, clear image.
  6. To see more detail, carefully switch to a higher power objective lens (e.g., 10x or 40x). You may need to adjust the fine focus again slightly.
  7. Draw what you see in your notebook, making sure to label your drawing and note the magnification used.
