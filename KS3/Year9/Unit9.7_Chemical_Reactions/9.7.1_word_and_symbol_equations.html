9.7.1 Word and Symbol Equations

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain how to represent chemical reactions using both word equations and balanced symbol equations.

Word Equations:

  • Show the names of the reactants and products.
  • Reactants → Products
  • Example: Methane + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water

Symbol Equations:

  • Use chemical formulas to represent the reactants and products.
  • Provide more detail than word equations, showing the number of atoms of each element involved.
  • Example: $CH_4 + 2O_2 → CO_2 + 2H_2O$

Balancing Symbol Equations:

  • The law of conservation of mass states that atoms are not created or destroyed in a chemical reaction.
  • Therefore, a symbol equation must be balanced – it must have the same number of atoms of each element on both sides of the arrow.
  • We balance equations by placing large numbers (coefficients) in front of the chemical formulas. We cannot change the small numbers (subscripts) within a formula.
  • Example: Balancing $H_2 + O_2 → H_2O$
    1. Count atoms on each side: Left has 2 H, 2 O. Right has 2 H, 1 O.
    2. The oxygen atoms are unbalanced. Place a 2 in front of $H_2O$ to balance the oxygen: $H_2 + O_2 → 2H_2O$.
    3. Recount atoms: Left has 2 H, 2 O. Right has 4 H, 2 O.
    4. The hydrogen atoms are now unbalanced. Place a 2 in front of $H_2$: $2H_2 + O_2 → 2H_2O$.
    5. Final count: Left has 4 H, 2 O. Right has 4 H, 2 O. The equation is now balanced.
