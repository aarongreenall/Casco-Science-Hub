9.1.2 Ions and Ionic Bonds

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe an ion as an atom which has gained at least one electron to be negatively charged or lost at least one electron to be positively charged.
  2. Describe an ionic bond as an attraction between a positively charged ion and a negatively charged ion.

From Atoms to Ions: Gaining and Losing Electrons

As we know, atoms are normally electrically neutral. This is because they have the same number of positive protons in their nucleus as they have negative electrons in their shells. However, atoms can gain or lose electrons to achieve a stable, full outer shell. When this happens, an **ion** is formed. An ion is an atom (or group of atoms) that has an overall electrical charge.

Analogy: Trading Cards

Imagine an atom is a collector trying to get a complete, stable set of trading cards (a full outer electron shell). A **metal atom** might have one or two spare cards it doesn't need for its set. By giving away these spare cards (losing electrons), it achieves a complete set and feels 'positive' about it. A **non-metal atom** might be missing one or two cards. By taking the spare cards (gaining electrons), it completes its set, but now feels like it 'owes' something, giving it a 'negative' status.

Making Positive Ions (Cations)

**Metal** atoms tend to **lose** the electrons in their outer shell. When an atom loses negative electrons, it has more positive protons than electrons, so it becomes a **positively charged ion**, also known as a **cation**.

A sodium atom (Na) has 11 protons (+) and 11 electrons (-). It loses its one outer electron to become stable.

The resulting sodium ion ($Na^+$) now has 11 protons (+) but only 10 electrons (-). The overall charge is +1.

Making Negative Ions (Anions)

**Non-metal** atoms tend to **gain** electrons to fill their outer shell. When an atom gains negative electrons, it has more electrons than protons, so it becomes a **negatively charged ion**, also known as an **anion**.

A chlorine atom (Cl) has 17 protons (+) and 17 electrons (-). It gains one electron to complete its outer shell.

The resulting chloride ion ($Cl^-$) now has 17 protons (+) but 18 electrons (-). The overall charge is -1.

The Ionic Bond: An Attraction of Opposites

When a metal and a non-metal react, the metal atom transfers its outer electrons to the non-metal atom. This creates a positive ion and a negative ion. Because opposites attract, these newly formed ions are powerfully drawn to each other. This strong force of attraction between oppositely charged ions is called an **ionic bond**.

Analogy: Magnets

Think of a positive ion as the North pole of a magnet and a negative ion as the South pole. When you bring them close, they don't physically touch with hooks or glue, but a powerful, invisible force snaps them together. That invisible force is like the **ionic bond**.

Formation of Sodium Chloride (NaCl)

1. Sodium (a metal) transfers its outer electron to Chlorine (a non-metal).
2. Sodium becomes a positive ion ($Na^+$) and Chlorine becomes a negative ion ($Cl^-$).
3. The oppositely charged ions are strongly attracted to each other, forming an ionic bond.

At-Home Experiment: Grow Your Own Salt Crystals

See a real-life ionic compound form before your eyes! Table salt (sodium chloride) is held together by ionic bonds. By growing crystals, you are creating a giant, organised structure of ions called a lattice.

You will need:

  • Table salt
  • Warm water
  • A clean glass jar
  • A pencil or stick
  • A piece of string
  • A paperclip or small weight

Procedure:

  1. Adult supervision required. Ask an adult to help you with the warm water.
  2. Pour warm water into the jar until it's about three-quarters full.
  3. Stir in salt, one teaspoon at a time, until no more salt will dissolve. You've now made a supersaturated solution!
  4. Tie one end of the string to the middle of the pencil and the other end to the paperclip.
  5. Rest the pencil across the top of the jar so the string hangs down into the salt solution.
  6. Place the jar in a safe, sunny spot where it won't be disturbed.
  7. Observe the jar every day. After a few days, you should see crystals begin to grow on the string!

Scientific Explanation:

As the water slowly evaporates, there is less space for the dissolved sodium ($Na^+$) and chloride ($Cl^-$) ions. They start to bond together, arranging themselves into a perfect, repeating cubic pattern called a **giant ionic lattice**. The beautiful crystals you see are made of billions of ions held together by ionic bonds.
