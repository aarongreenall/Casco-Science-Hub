P4.1 Simple phenomena of magnetism

Magnetism is a fascinating force that plays a crucial role in many aspects of our daily lives, from compasses guiding navigation to electric motors powering devices. This section introduces the fundamental concepts of magnetism, including the forces between magnetic poles, magnetic materials, and the nature of magnetic fields.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the forces between magnetic poles and between magnets and magnetic materials, including the use of the terms north pole (N pole), south pole (S pole), attraction and repulsion, magnetised and unmagnetised.
  2. State the difference between magnetic and non-magnetic materials.
  3. Describe induced magnetism.
  4. Describe a magnetic field as a region in which a magnetic pole experiences a force.
  5. State that the direction of a magnetic field at a point is the direction of the force on the N pole of a magnet at that point.
  6. State the differences between the properties of temporary magnets (made of soft iron) and the properties of permanent magnets (made of steel).
  7. Describe how a permanent magnet differs from an electromagnet.
  8. Describe the pattern and direction of the magnetic field due to currents in straight wires and in solenoids.
  9. Describe the effect on the magnetic field around straight wires and solenoids of changing the magnitude and direction of current.

Magnetic Poles and Materials:

Every magnet has two poles: a **north pole (N pole)** and a **south pole (S pole)**. The forces between magnetic poles follow a simple rule:

  • **Like poles repel:** North repels North, and South repels South.
  • **Unlike poles attract:** North attracts South.

Magnets also attract **magnetic materials**. These are materials that can be magnetized themselves. Common magnetic materials include iron, nickel, and cobalt. **Non-magnetic materials** (like wood, plastic, copper, or aluminum) are not attracted to magnets.

*Figure: Illustration of magnetic poles showing attraction between unlike poles and repulsion between like poles.*

Induced Magnetism:

When a magnetic material is brought near a magnet, it can become temporarily magnetized. This is called **induced magnetism**. The end of the magnetic material closer to the magnet will develop an opposite pole, causing attraction. For example, if you bring the North pole of a magnet near an iron nail, the end of the nail closest to the magnet will become a South pole, and the other end will become a North pole.

Magnetic Fields:

A **magnetic field** is the region around a magnet where a magnetic force can be detected. It is a region where a magnetic pole would experience a force. Magnetic field lines are used to represent the shape and direction of a magnetic field. The direction of a magnetic field at any point is defined as the direction of the force that would be exerted on a hypothetical isolated North pole placed at that point. Field lines always point from North to South.

*Figure: Magnetic field lines around a bar magnet, showing lines emerging from the North pole and entering the South pole.*

Permanent vs. Temporary Magnets and Electromagnets:

**Permanent magnets** are made from hard magnetic materials (like steel) that retain their magnetism even after the magnetizing force is removed. They produce their own persistent magnetic field.

**Temporary magnets** are made from soft magnetic materials (like soft iron) that are easily magnetized but lose their magnetism quickly once the magnetizing force is removed.

An **electromagnet** is a type of temporary magnet where the magnetic field is produced by an electric current. Unlike permanent magnets, the strength and direction of an electromagnet's field can be controlled by changing the current. An electromagnet typically consists of a coil of wire (solenoid) wrapped around a soft iron core.

Magnetic Fields from Currents:

An electric current flowing through a wire creates a magnetic field around it.

  • **Straight Wires:** The magnetic field lines around a straight current-carrying wire form concentric circles, with the direction given by the right-hand grip rule (if you point your right thumb in the direction of conventional current, your curled fingers indicate the direction of the magnetic field lines).
  • **Solenoids (Coils):** When a wire is coiled into a solenoid, the magnetic fields from individual loops combine to form a stronger, more uniform magnetic field inside the coil, similar to that of a bar magnet. The strength of this field is affected by the magnitude of the current, the number of turns in the coil, and the presence of an iron core. The direction of the field can be reversed by reversing the direction of the current.

*Figure: Magnetic field lines around a current-carrying loop, showing how the field is concentrated inside the loop.*
