9.7.3 Displacement Reactions

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Identify examples of displacement reactions and predict products (limited to reactions involving calcium, magnesium, zinc, iron, copper, gold and silver salts).

The Reactivity Series: A Chemical League Table

Not all metals are created equalâ€”some are much more reactive than others. Chemists have arranged the metals in order of their reactivity in a list called the **reactivity series**. This series is a powerful tool that allows us to predict how metals will behave in certain reactions.

What is a Displacement Reaction?

A **displacement reaction** is a reaction where a **more reactive metal** takes the place of a **less reactive metal** in a compound. The more reactive metal essentially "pushes out" or displaces the less reactive one.

The Golden Rule: A displacement reaction will only happen if the pure metal is HIGHER in the reactivity series than the metal in the compound.

Analogy: A Sports Team Substitution

Imagine a football match. You have a star player (a more reactive metal) on the bench and an average player (a less reactive metal) on the field. To improve the team, the coach substitutes the star player onto the field, sending the average player to the bench. The star player has **displaced** the other player. A more reactive metal does exactly the same thing to a less reactive metal in a compound.

Predicting Reactions

By using the reactivity series, we can easily predict whether a reaction will happen.

Example 1: Reaction Happens

Consider adding zinc to copper sulfate solution.

  1. Check the series: Zinc is HIGHER than Copper.
  2. Prediction: A reaction will happen. Zinc will displace copper.

Word Equation:

Zinc + Copper Sulfate $\rightarrow$ Zinc Sulfate + Copper

Symbol Equation:

$Zn(s) + CuSO_4(aq) \rightarrow ZnSO_4(aq) + Cu(s)$

Example 2: No Reaction

Consider adding copper to zinc sulfate solution.

  1. Check the series: Copper is LOWER than Zinc.
  2. Prediction: No reaction will happen.

Word Equation:

Copper + Zinc Sulfate $\rightarrow$ No Reaction

Symbol Equation:

$Cu(s) + ZnSO_4(aq) \rightarrow No Reaction$

In-School Investigation: Seeing Displacement

You can easily observe a displacement reaction in the lab. When a piece of zinc metal is placed in blue copper sulfate solution, two things happen:

  • The blue colour of the solution slowly fades as the copper sulfate is used up.
  • A reddish-brown solid layer of pure copper metal forms on the surface of the zinc.

The reaction is:

Zinc + Copper Sulfate $\rightarrow$ Zinc Sulfate + Copper

$Zn(s) + CuSO_4(aq) \rightarrow ZnSO_4(aq) + Cu(s)$
