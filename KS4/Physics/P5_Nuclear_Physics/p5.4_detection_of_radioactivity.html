P5.4 Detection of radioactivity

Radioactive emissions (alpha, beta, and gamma) are invisible and cannot be detected by our senses. Therefore, specialized detectors are essential to measure their presence and intensity. Understanding how these detectors work is crucial for safe handling of radioactive materials and for various applications in science, medicine, and industry.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Know what is meant by the terms ionising nuclear radiation and background radiation.
  2. Know the sources that make a significant contribution to background radiation including: radon gas (in the air), rocks and buildings, food and drink, cosmic rays.
  3. Know that ionising nuclear radiation can be measured using a detector connected to a counter.
  4. Use count rate measured in counts/s or counts/minute.

Ionising Radiation and Background Radiation:

**Ionising nuclear radiation** is radiation that carries enough energy to remove electrons from atoms, creating ions. This process can damage living cells and is the basis of both the hazards and some applications of radioactivity.

We are constantly exposed to **background radiation**, which is naturally occurring ionizing radiation from various sources. Key sources include:

  • **Radon gas:** A radioactive gas naturally released from rocks and soil, which can accumulate in buildings.
  • **Rocks and buildings:** Naturally occurring radioactive materials in the Earth's crust and building materials.
  • **Food and drink:** Trace amounts of radioactive isotopes are naturally present in many foods and water.
  • **Cosmic rays:** High-energy radiation from space that constantly bombards Earth.

*Figure: Common sources contributing to natural background radiation.*

Detecting Radioactivity:

Ionising nuclear radiation cannot be seen, smelled, or felt. Therefore, it is measured using specialized detectors connected to a counter. The most common detector is a **Geiger-Müller (GM) tube**, which produces a click or a pulse for each particle or photon of radiation it detects.

The rate at which radiation is detected is called the **count rate**, typically measured in **counts per second (counts/s)** or **counts per minute (counts/minute)**. When measuring a radioactive source, it's important to account for background radiation by taking a background count and subtracting it from the source's reading.
