7.3.2 Planet Formation

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe how a solar system is formed from a nebula.
  2. Explain the role of gravity in the process of accretion.
  3. Distinguish between a solar nebula and a galactic nebula.

From Clouds to Planets

Our solar system, with the Sun and all its planets, began its life about 4.6 billion years ago as a giant, swirling cloud of gas and dust. This cloud is called a **nebula**.

It's important to know that there are different sizes of nebulae. The cloud that formed our solar system is called a **solar nebula**. There are other, much, much larger nebulae in the universe. These are so vast that when they collapse under their own gravity, they don't just form one star system; they form an entire **galaxy** containing billions of stars.

The process of forming planets from the gas and dust in a solar nebula is driven by one key force: **gravity**.

  1. Collapse: Gravity slowly pulled the gas and dust in the solar nebula together. As it collapsed, it began to spin faster and flatten into a disk, like a spinning pizza base. Most of the material (over 99%) collapsed into the centre, becoming so hot and dense that it ignited to form our Sun.
  2. Accretion: The remaining dust and gas in the spinning disk started to stick together. Tiny dust grains collided to form small rocks. These rocks then collided to form bigger rocks. This process of growing by clumping together is called **accretion**.
  3. Planet Formation: Over millions of years, accretion continued. The rocks grew into huge bodies called **planetesimals**. These planetesimals continued to collide and merge, and their gravity became strong enough to pull in even more gas and dust. Eventually, these grew into the planets we know today.

Analogy: The Snowball Effect

You can think of accretion like a tiny snowflake rolling down a huge, snowy hill. As it rolls, it picks up more and more snow, getting bigger and bigger. The force of gravity pulling the snowflake down the hill is like the gravitational force in the nebula pulling dust and rocks together. After rolling for a long time, the tiny snowflake becomes a giant snowball, just like a tiny dust grain can eventually grow into a massive planet.
