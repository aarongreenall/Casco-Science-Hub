24.2 Production and use of X-rays

Learning Objectives:

  1. Explain that X-rays are produced by electron bombardment of a metal target and calculate the minimum wavelength of X-rays produced from the accelerating p.d.
  2. Understand the use of X-rays in imaging internal body structures, including an understanding of the term contrast in X-ray imaging.
  3. Recall and use $I = I_0e^{–\mu x}$ for the attenuation of X-rays in matter.
  4. Understand that computed tomography (CT) scanning produces a 3D image of an internal structure by first combining multiple X-ray images taken in the same section from different angles to obtain a 2D image of the section, then repeating this process along an axis and combining 2D images of multiple sections.

LO1: Production of X-rays

X-rays are produced in an X-ray tube when fast-moving electrons bombard a heavy metal target. The process involves several key steps:

  1. Thermionic Emission: A filament (cathode) is heated by a low-voltage supply, causing it to release electrons.
  2. Acceleration: A very high potential difference (the accelerating p.d., $V$) is applied between the cathode and a metal target (the anode). This accelerates the electrons to very high speeds and kinetic energies.
  3. Bombardment: The high-energy electrons collide with the metal target.
  4. Energy Conversion: Upon impact, the kinetic energy of the electrons is converted into other forms. Most of it (~99%) is converted into thermal energy, making the target extremely hot. A small fraction (~1%) is converted into X-ray photons.

The highest energy (and therefore shortest wavelength) X-ray photon is produced when a single electron loses all of its kinetic energy in a single interaction.

Derivation of Minimum Wavelength ($\lambda_{min}$)

1. The kinetic energy of an electron accelerated through a p.d. of $V$ is: $E_k = eV$.

2. The maximum energy of a single X-ray photon, $E_{max}$, occurs when all of this kinetic energy is converted in one go: $E_{max} = eV$.

3. The energy of a photon is also given by $E = hc/\lambda$. The maximum photon energy corresponds to the minimum possible wavelength, $\lambda_{min}$.

4. Equating the two expressions for maximum energy:

$$ eV = \frac{hc}{\lambda_{min}} $$

5. Rearranging gives the formula for the minimum wavelength:

$$ \lambda_{min} = \frac{hc}{eV} $$

Worked Example: Minimum X-ray Wavelength

An X-ray tube operates with an accelerating potential difference of 80 kV. Calculate the minimum wavelength of the X-rays produced.

1. Identify variables and convert to SI units:

$$ V = 80 \text{ kV} = 80 \times 10^3 \text{ V} $$

2. State the formula and substitute values:

$$ \lambda_{min} = \frac{hc}{eV} $$

$$ \lambda_{min} = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)}{(1.60 \times 10^{-19}) \times (80 \times 10^3)} $$

3. Calculate the result:

$$ \lambda_{min} = \frac{1.989 \times 10^{-25}}{1.28 \times 10^{-14}} = 1.55 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m} $$

LO2 & LO3: X-ray Imaging and Attenuation

X-ray imaging works by passing a beam of X-rays through the body onto a detector. The resulting image is essentially a shadowgraph. The key principle is **differential absorption**.

As X-rays pass through matter, their intensity decreases. This is called **attenuation**. The amount of attenuation depends on the thickness, density, and atomic number of the material. This exponential decrease is described by:

$$ I = I_0 e^{-\mu x} $$

Where $I_0$ is the initial intensity, $I$ is the transmitted intensity after passing through a thickness $x$, and $\mu$ is the linear attenuation coefficient of the material.

**Contrast** in an X-ray image is the difference in darkness between different areas. Good contrast is achieved because different tissues have different attenuation coefficients. Bone, being dense and containing elements with high atomic numbers (like calcium), has a high $\mu$ and absorbs X-rays strongly, appearing white on the film. Soft tissues have a much lower $\mu$, allowing more X-rays to pass through, and appear as shades of grey.

Worked Example: Attenuation in Bone

An X-ray beam with an initial intensity of $I_0$ passes through 2.5 cm of bone. The linear attenuation coefficient for bone for this X-ray energy is 0.35 cm⁻¹. Calculate the percentage of the intensity that is transmitted.

$$ I = I_0 e^{-\mu x} $$

$$ I = I_0 e^{-(0.35 \times 2.5)} = I_0 e^{-0.875} $$

$$ I = I_0 \times 0.417 $$

The ratio of transmitted to incident intensity is $I/I_0 = 0.417$. Therefore, 41.7% of the intensity is transmitted.

Conclusion for Observation:

This means that over 58% of the X-ray intensity is absorbed by the bone. This significant reduction in transmitted intensity is why bone appears much whiter on an X-ray image compared to surrounding soft tissue, which attenuates the beam far less. This difference in attenuation creates good contrast and makes the bone clearly visible.

LO4: Computed Tomography (CT) Scanning

A standard X-ray is a 2D projection, where 3D structures are superimposed on top of each other. **Computed Tomography (CT)** is an advanced imaging technique that overcomes this limitation to produce detailed 3D images.

  1. An X-ray tube and a ring of detectors are mounted on a gantry that rotates around the patient.
  2. Multiple X-ray images are taken of a single "slice" of the body from many different angles as the gantry rotates.
  3. A powerful computer processes all these 2D projections and reconstructs a detailed 2D cross-sectional image of that slice.
  4. The patient's bed is then moved a small distance along the axis of the scanner, and the process is repeated for the next slice.
  5. Finally, the computer stacks all the individual 2D slices together to build up a full 3D image of the internal structure, which can be viewed from any angle.
