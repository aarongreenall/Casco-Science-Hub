8.9.2 Exothermic and endothermic reactions

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define exothermic and endothermic reactions.
  2. Identify a reaction as exothermic or endothermic based on temperature changes.
  3. Give examples of useful exothermic and endothermic reactions.

Hot and Cold Reactions

All chemical reactions involve energy changes. One of the most common ways we observe this is as a change in temperature. We can classify reactions into two groups based on whether they give out heat or take it in.

Exothermic Reactions

An **exothermic** reaction is one that **releases energy** into the surroundings, usually as heat and light. This causes the temperature of the surroundings to increase. The prefix "exo-" means "out," and "thermic" relates to heat.

Example: A bonfire is a powerful exothermic reaction. The combustion of wood releases a huge amount of heat and light energy, making the surroundings much warmer.

Endothermic Reactions

An **endothermic** reaction is one that **takes in energy** from the surroundings, usually as heat. This causes the temperature of the surroundings to decrease. The prefix "endo-" means "in."

Example: An instant cold pack for sports injuries is a great example. When you activate it, a chemical reaction inside takes in heat from its surroundings, making the pack feel very cold.

In-School Experiment: Hot and Cold Reactions

You can observe exothermic and endothermic reactions in the lab by measuring the temperature change when different substances are mixed.

Safety First!

Always wear safety goggles when handling chemicals. Some of these reactions can produce heat quickly.

Equipment:
  • Insulated cups (like polystyrene cups)
  • A thermometer
  • A measuring cylinder
  • Various solutions (e.g., sodium hydroxide and hydrochloric acid; citric acid and sodium hydrogen carbonate)
Method:
  1. Measure a set volume of the first solution (e.g., 25 ml of sodium hydroxide) and pour it into an insulated cup. Record the starting temperature.
  2. Measure an equal volume of the second solution (e.g., 25 ml of hydrochloric acid).
  3. Quickly add the second solution to the cup, stir gently with the thermometer, and record the highest or lowest temperature reached.
  4. Calculate the temperature change. If the temperature increased, the reaction was exothermic. If it decreased, it was endothermic.
  5. Repeat the experiment with other pairs of solutions, like citric acid and sodium hydrogen carbonate.
