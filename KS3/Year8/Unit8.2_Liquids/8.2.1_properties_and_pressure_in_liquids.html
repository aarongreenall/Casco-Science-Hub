8.2.1 Properties and pressure in liquids

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the arrangement and movement of particles in a liquid.
  2. Explain why liquids can flow but are incompressible.
  3. Explain that pressure in a liquid acts in all directions and increases with depth.

Properties of Liquids

Like gases, liquids are made of particles that are constantly moving. However, in a liquid, the particles are packed much more closely together. This unique arrangement gives liquids their characteristic properties.

  • Flow: The particles in a liquid are touching but are not in fixed positions. They can slide and tumble over each other, which allows the liquid to flow and take the shape of its container.
  • Incompressible: Because the particles are already packed closely together, there are no large gaps between them. This means you cannot easily squash a liquid into a smaller volume. Liquids are said to be **incompressible**.

Analogy: A Ball Pit

A good way to picture the particles in a liquid is to think of a **ball pit**. The balls are the **liquid particles**. They are all jumbled up and touching, but they can still slide and tumble over each other, allowing you to "flow" through them. If you tried to squash the whole ball pit into a smaller box, you couldn't, because the balls are already packed tightly together. This is why liquids are incompressible.

Pressure in Liquids

Just like in a gas, the moving particles in a liquid collide with the walls of their container, creating pressure. This pressure acts in all directions. A key property of liquid pressure is that it **increases with depth**.

The deeper you go in a liquid, the more particles there are above you. The weight of all these particles pushes down, creating a higher pressure at the bottom than at the top. This is why your ears might feel a popping sensation when you swim to the bottom of a deep swimming pool.

At-Home Experiment: Building a Cartesian Diver

This classic experiment is a fun, almost magical way to demonstrate the properties of pressure in liquids and the compressibility of gases.

Equipment:
  • A clear plastic bottle with a lid, filled almost to the top with water
  • A pen cap (one with a clip that seals the top end)
  • Modelling clay or plasticine
Method:
  1. Take a small piece of modelling clay and wrap it around the bottom of the pen cap. This is your "diver".
  2. Test your diver in a glass of water. You need to add or remove clay until the diver *just barely* floats, with only the very top of the cap sticking out of the water. This is the trickiest part!
  3. Once your diver is perfectly balanced, drop it into your plastic bottle of water and screw the lid on tightly. The diver should be floating at the top.
  4. Now, squeeze the sides of the bottle firmly. What happens to the diver? It should sink to the bottom!
  5. Release the pressure on the bottle. The diver should rise back to the top.
What's Happening?

The pen cap has a small bubble of air trapped inside it, which is what makes it float. When you squeeze the bottle, you increase the pressure throughout the water (as pressure in a liquid acts in all directions). This pressure is transferred to the air bubble inside the diver. Because air is a gas, it is **compressible**, so the air bubble gets squashed into a smaller volume. This allows more water to enter the cap, making the diver denser and causing it to sink. When you release the pressure, the air bubble expands again, pushing the water out and making the diver float back up.
