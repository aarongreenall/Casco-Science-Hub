P6.1 The Solar System

Our **Solar System** is a vast and intricate cosmic neighborhood, consisting of one star, eight named planets, numerous minor planets, and countless moons, all bound together by gravity. Understanding its components and dynamics is fundamental to comprehending our place in the universe.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the Solar System as containing: one star, the Sun; the eight named planets and know their order from the Sun; minor planets that orbit the Sun, including dwarf planets such as Pluto and asteroids in the asteroid belt; moons, that orbit the planets.
  2. Know that the Sun contains most of the mass of the Solar System and this explains why the planets orbit the Sun.
  3. Know that the force that keeps an object in orbit around the Sun is due to the gravitational attraction of the Sun.
  4. Define orbital speed from the equation $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$, where $r$ is the radius of the orbit and $T$ is the orbital period; recall and use this equation.
  5. Know that the strength of the Sun’s gravitational field decreases and that the orbital speeds of the planets decrease as the distance from the Sun increases.

Components of Our Solar System:

The Solar System is dominated by the **Sun**, our star, which accounts for over 99.8% of the total mass of the system. Its immense mass generates a powerful gravitational field that holds all other objects in orbit around it.

Orbiting the Sun are eight named planets, in order from the Sun: **Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune**. Beyond these, the Solar System also includes:

  • **Minor planets:** These include **dwarf planets** like Pluto (which, while once considered a planet, is now classified as a dwarf planet) and countless **asteroids**, many of which reside in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.
  • **Moons:** Natural satellites that orbit planets (e.g., Earth's Moon, Jupiter's numerous moons).

*Figure: A diagram illustrating the components of our Solar System, showing the Sun and the eight planets in order.*

Orbits and Gravitational Attraction:

The force that keeps all objects in orbit around the Sun is the **gravitational attraction of the Sun**. This force pulls objects towards the Sun, balancing their tendency to move in a straight line (due to inertia).

The **orbital speed ($v$)** of a planet or any object orbiting the Sun can be calculated using the equation:

$v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$

Where $r$ is the radius of the orbit (average distance from the Sun) and $T$ is the orbital period (the time it takes for one complete orbit).

As the distance from the Sun increases, the strength of the Sun’s gravitational field **decreases**. Consequently, the **orbital speeds of the planets also decrease** as their distance from the Sun increases. Planets further away move slower in their orbits.
