22.2 The photoelectric effect

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that photoelectrons may be emitted from a metal surface when it is illuminated by electromagnetic radiation.
  2. Understand and use the terms threshold frequency and threshold wavelength.
  3. Explain photoelectric emission in terms of photon energy and work function energy.
  4. Recall and use $hf = \Phi + \frac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2$.
  5. Explain why the maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons is independent of intensity, whereas the photoelectric current is proportional to intensity.

LO1: Photoelectric Emission

The Historical Context: A Scientific Reversal

For centuries, the nature of light was a central question in physics. Isaac Newton's highly influential 'corpuscular' theory proposed that light was composed of tiny particles. This view was the prevailing consensus for over a century.

However, in the early 1800s, Thomas Young's double-slit experiment provided definitive proof of light's wave-like properties, showing that it could diffract and create interference patterns. This discovery caused a major shift in scientific thought, and the wave model became the universally accepted explanation for the nature of light.

Einstein's "Miracle Year" (1905)

The story might have ended there, but then came Albert Einstein. In 1905, while working as a patent clerk after a less-than-stellar university career, he had what is now known as his *annus mirabilis*, or "miracle year." In a single year, he published three revolutionary papers that would change the course of science forever:

  • Brownian Motion: He mathematically proved the existence of atoms by explaining that the random, jiggling motion of pollen grains in water was caused by them being bombarded by unseen, randomly moving water molecules.
  • Special Relativity: He introduced the world to the iconic equation $E=mc^2$ and completely redefined our understanding of space and time.
  • The Photoelectric Effect: This was the paper that would eventually win him the Nobel Prize. He explained that certain experimental results, which the wave theory of light couldn't account for, made perfect sense if you assumed light was not a continuous wave but a stream of discrete energy packets, or "quanta."

In a stunning turn of events, Einstein had brought back Newton's particle idea, but in a new, quantum form. He proposed that light was both a wave *and* a particle, a concept now known as wave-particle duality. These particles of light are called **photons**, and they are the key to understanding the photoelectric effect.

The **photoelectric effect** is the emission of electrons from the surface of a material (usually a metal) when electromagnetic radiation of a high enough frequency is shone on it. The emitted electrons are called **photoelectrons**.

LO2 & LO3: Threshold Frequency and the Work Function

Albert Einstein explained the photoelectric effect by proposing that light consists of photons. The key idea is that each electron on the metal surface can absorb the energy of only a **single photon**.

The electrons in a metal are held in place by electrostatic forces. To be liberated from the surface, an electron must be given a certain minimum amount of energy.

The **work function ($\Phi$)** is the minimum energy required to remove a single electron from the surface of a particular metal.

Since an electron can only absorb one photon, it will only be emitted if the energy of that single photon ($E=hf$) is greater than or equal to the work function. This leads to the concept of a **threshold frequency ($f_0$)**.

The **threshold frequency** is the minimum frequency of electromagnetic radiation required to cause photoelectric emission from the surface of a particular metal.

$$ hf_0 = \Phi $$

LO4: Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

Einstein's photoelectric equation is a simple statement of the conservation of energy for the one-photon, one-electron interaction.

Energy of incident photon = Work Function + Maximum KE of photoelectron

$$ hf = \Phi + E_{k(max)} $$

$E_{k(max)}$ is the maximum kinetic energy because the work function, $\Phi$, is the *minimum* energy required to escape. Some electrons deeper within the metal may require more energy to escape, and so will be emitted with less kinetic energy.

Graphical Analysis of the Photoelectric Equation

If we rearrange the photoelectric equation into the form of a straight-line graph ($y=mx+c$), we get:

$$ E_{k(max)} = hf - \Phi $$

Plotting a graph of $E_{k(max)}$ (y-axis) against frequency $f$ (x-axis) provides powerful experimental verification of the photon model.

  • The **gradient** of the graph is equal to the **Planck constant ($h$)**.
  • The **x-intercept** gives the **threshold frequency ($f_0$)**.
  • The **y-intercept** is equal to the **negative of the work function ($-\Phi$)**.

LO5: The Role of Intensity

The photon model perfectly explains the experimental observations related to the intensity of the incident light.

  • Maximum KE is independent of intensity: In the photon model, the intensity of light is proportional to the number of photons arriving per second. Increasing the intensity means more photons arrive, but the energy of each individual photon ($E=hf$) remains the same. Since each electron absorbs only one photon, the maximum kinetic energy it can be emitted with is fixed by the light's frequency, not its intensity.
  • Photoelectric current is proportional to intensity: A higher intensity means more photons are incident on the surface per second. This leads to more one-to-one interactions with electrons per second, so more photoelectrons are emitted per second. A greater rate of flow of charge results in a larger photoelectric current.
