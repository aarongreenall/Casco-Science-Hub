LO1 & LO2: Energy Stored in a Capacitor

Charging a capacitor involves moving charge from one plate to the other against the repulsive force of the charge already on the plates. This requires work to be done. The work done in charging the capacitor is stored as **electric potential energy ($W$ or $E_P$)** in the electric field between the plates.

Graphical Determination of Energy Stored

From the definition of capacitance, $C = Q/V$, we can see that for a given capacitor, the potential difference $V$ is directly proportional to the charge $Q$ stored on it ($V = \frac{1}{C}Q$). This means a graph of potential difference against charge is a straight line through the origin.

The work done to add a small amount of charge, $\Delta Q$, to the plates at a potential difference $V$ is given by $\Delta W = V\Delta Q$. The total work done to charge the capacitor to a final charge $Q$ and potential difference $V$ is therefore the sum of all these small amounts of work, which is equivalent to the **area under the potential-charge graph**.

Detailed Derivation of Energy Formulae

1. The energy stored, $W$, is the area of the triangle under the V-Q graph.

$$ W = \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} $$

2. The base of the triangle is the final charge, $Q$, and the height is the final potential difference, $V$.

$$ W = \frac{1}{2}QV $$

3. We can derive two other useful forms of this equation using the definition of capacitance, $C=Q/V$.

To get the formula in terms of C and V: Substitute $Q=CV$ into the equation above.

$$ W = \frac{1}{2}(CV)V $$

$$ W = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 $$

To get the formula in terms of Q and C: Rearrange $C=Q/V$ to get $V=Q/C$ and substitute this into the first energy equation.

$$ W = \frac{1}{2}Q\left(\frac{Q}{C}\right) $$

$$ W = \frac{Q^2}{2C} $$

Worked Example: Energy Stored

A 50 $\mu$F capacitor is charged by a 12 V supply. Calculate the energy stored in the capacitor.

1. Identify the known variables and convert to SI units:

$$ C = 50 \, \mu\text{F} = 50 \times 10^{-6} \text{ F} $$

$$ V = 12 \text{ V} $$

2. Choose the appropriate formula: We know C and V, so we use $W = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$.

$$ W = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ W = \frac{1}{2} \times (50 \times 10^{-6}) \times (12)^2 $$

$$ W = \frac{1}{2} \times (50 \times 10^{-6}) \times 144 $$

$$ W = 3.6 \times 10^{-3} \text{ J} \text{ (or 3.6 mJ)} $$
