B8.2 Water uptake

Plants require a continuous supply of water and mineral ions from the soil for photosynthesis, structural support, and various metabolic processes. This section explores how plants, particularly through their root systems, are adapted for efficient water uptake and the pathway water takes through the plant.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Identify in diagrams and images root hair cells and state their functions.
  2. State that the large surface area of root hairs increases the uptake of water and mineral ions.
  3. Outline the pathway taken by water through the root, stem and leaf as: root hair cells, root cortex cells, xylem, mesophyll cells.

Root Hair Cells: Adaptations for Uptake:

The primary site of water and mineral ion uptake in plants is the **root hair cell**. These specialized epidermal cells, found on the surface of plant roots, are highly adapted for absorption:

  • **Long, thin extension (root hair):** This greatly increases the **surface area** of the root, maximizing the contact with soil water and mineral ions. A large surface area is essential for efficient absorption.
  • **Thin cell wall and cell membrane:** Provide a short diffusion distance for water and ions to enter the cell.
  • **High concentration of solutes in vacuole:** Creates a lower water potential inside the root hair cell compared to the soil water, facilitating water uptake by osmosis.
  • **Mitochondria:** Abundant to provide energy for active transport of mineral ions (which move against their concentration gradient).

*Figure: Diagram of a root hair cell, showing its long extension for increased surface area.*

Pathway of Water Through the Plant:

Water absorbed by the roots travels through a continuous pathway from the soil to the leaves:

  1. **Root hair cells:** Absorb water from the soil by osmosis.
  2. **Root cortex cells:** Water moves across these cells (by osmosis and through cell walls) towards the center of the root.
  3. **Xylem:** Water then enters the xylem vessels in the center of the root. Xylem forms a continuous tube system that extends through the stem and into the leaves.
  4. **Mesophyll cells (in leaves):** Water is transported up the xylem vessels to the leaves, where it moves out of the xylem and into the mesophyll cells (palisade and spongy mesophyll). From here, it evaporates into the air spaces within the leaf, leading to transpiration.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the pathway of water from the root to the leaf.*
