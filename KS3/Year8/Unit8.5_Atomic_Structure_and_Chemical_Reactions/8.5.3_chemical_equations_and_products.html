8.5.3 Chemical equations and products

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Use word and symbol equations to describe chemical reactions.
  2. Understand that equations must be balanced to conserve mass.
  3. Predict the products of reactions between metals and acids.

Writing Down Reactions

Scientists use **word equations** to describe what happens in a chemical reaction. An even more detailed way is to use a **symbol equation**, which uses the chemical symbols for the elements and compounds involved.

Analogy: A Recipe Card for a Reaction

A word equation is just like a recipe card. The **reactants** on the left of the arrow are the **ingredients list**. The **arrow** is the **method** (what you do, e.g., "mix and heat"). The **products** on the right of the arrow are the **final dish** you have made.

Balancing Equations

We know that in a chemical reaction, atoms are just rearranged—none are created or destroyed. This means a symbol equation must be **balanced**. There must be the exact same number of each type of atom on both sides of the arrow.

Analogy: The LEGO Rule

An unbalanced equation is like a bad LEGO recipe. If the recipe says "1 Car + 1 Plane -> 1 House," but when you take the car and plane apart you have leftover bricks, the recipe is wrong. A balanced equation is a correct recipe, like "2 Cars + 1 Plane -> 1 House + 1 Boat," where all the bricks are used up perfectly. To balance an equation, we add large numbers in front of the chemical formulas to make sure the number of "bricks" (atoms) on both sides matches.

Worked Example 1: Balancing the formation of water

Balance the equation for hydrogen reacting with oxygen to form water: $H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow H_2O$.

1. Count the atoms on both sides:

Reactants: 2 Hydrogen, 2 Oxygen

Products: 2 Hydrogen, 1 Oxygen

The oxygen atoms are unbalanced.

2. Add a number in front of a formula to balance an element. We need 2 oxygen atoms on the right, so we place a 2 in front of $H_2O$:

$$ H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O $$

3. Recount the atoms:

Reactants: 2 Hydrogen, 2 Oxygen

Products: 4 Hydrogen (2 x 2), 2 Oxygen (2 x 1)

Now the hydrogen atoms are unbalanced.

4. Add another number to balance the other element. We need 4 hydrogen atoms on the left, so we place a 2 in front of $H_2$:

$$ 2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O $$

5. Final check:

Reactants: 4 Hydrogen, 2 Oxygen

Products: 4 Hydrogen, 2 Oxygen

The equation is now balanced!

At-Home Experiment: Investigating Reaction Speed

You can investigate two factors that affect the speed of a chemical reaction using a simple fizzing tablet.

Equipment:
  • Three clear glasses
  • Hot and cold tap water
  • Two effervescent (fizzing) tablets (e.g., Vitamin C or Alka-Seltzer)
  • A stopwatch or phone timer
Method Part 1: Temperature
  1. Fill one glass with cold water and another with hot water (not boiling!).
  2. Break one fizzing tablet in half.
  3. At the same time, drop one half of the tablet into the cold water and the other half into the hot water.
  4. Observe which tablet fizzes faster and dissolves completely first. You should see that the reaction is much faster in the hot water.
Method Part 2: Surface Area
  1. Fill two glasses with the same amount of water at the same temperature.
  2. Take the second fizzing tablet and carefully crush one half into a powder (you can do this inside a folded piece of paper).
  3. At the same time, drop the whole half-tablet into one glass and the crushed powder into the other.
  4. Observe which one reacts faster. The powder has a much larger surface area, so the reaction happens much more quickly!
