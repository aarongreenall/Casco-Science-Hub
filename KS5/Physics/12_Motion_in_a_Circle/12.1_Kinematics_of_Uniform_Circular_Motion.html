12.1 Kinematics of uniform circular motion

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand and use the radian as a measure of angle, and recall and use $s = r\theta$.
  2. Define and use angular speed $\omega = \theta/t = 2\pi/T = 2\pi f$.
  3. Recall and use $v = r\omega$.

LO1: Radian Measure

In the physics of rotation, it is often more convenient to measure angles in **radians** rather than degrees.

One radian is defined as the angle subtended at the centre of a circle by an arc that is equal in length to the radius of the circle.

Since the circumference of a full circle is $2\pi r$, there are $2\pi$ arcs of length $r$ around the circle. This gives the fundamental conversion factor:

$$ 360^\circ = 2\pi \text{ radians} \quad \implies \quad 180^\circ = \pi \text{ radians} $$

The definition of the radian leads to a simple formula for the arc length, $s$, subtended by an angle $\theta$ (in radians) in a circle of radius $r$:

$$ s = r\theta $$

LO2 & LO3: Angular and Linear Speed

For an object in circular motion, we can describe its speed in two ways.

  • Angular speed ($\omega$): The rate of change of angular displacement. It measures how quickly the angle is swept out. Its SI unit is radians per second (rad s⁻¹).

    $$ \omega = \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta t} $$

  • Linear speed ($v$): The instantaneous speed of the object along its circular path (i.e., the magnitude of its tangential velocity). Its SI unit is metres per second (m s⁻¹).

Relationships for Angular Speed

The concepts of Period ($T$) and Frequency ($f$) are the same as those you encountered in the study of waves (Topic 7). Circular motion is a periodic phenomenon, so the time for one complete revolution is the period, and the number of revolutions per second is the frequency.

For one complete revolution, the angle swept is $\Delta\theta = 2\pi$ radians and the time taken is the period, $T$. Therefore:

$$ \omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} $$

Since frequency $f = 1/T$, we can also write:

$$ \omega = 2\pi f $$

This means that the **angular speed ($\omega$)** for circular motion is mathematically identical to the **angular frequency ($\omega$)** used in describing oscillations and waves.

Detailed Derivation of $v = r\omega$

1. Start with the definition of linear speed, $v$, as the distance travelled per unit time. The distance is the arc length, $s$.

$$ v = \frac{s}{t} $$

2. We know the formula for arc length in radians is $s = r\theta$. Substitute this into the speed equation:

$$ v = \frac{r\theta}{t} $$

3. Rearrange the terms:

$$ v = r \left( \frac{\theta}{t} \right) $$

4. We recognise that the term $\frac{\theta}{t}$ is the definition of angular speed, $\omega$.

5. This gives the final relationship between linear and angular speed:

$$ v = r\omega $$

Worked Example: A Spinning Disk

A hard disk in a computer rotates at 7200 revolutions per minute (rpm). A point on the disk is located 4.0 cm from the centre. Calculate (a) its angular speed, and (b) its linear speed.

(a) Calculate the angular speed ($\omega$):

1. Convert the frequency to SI units (Hz, or revolutions per second).

$$ f = \frac{7200}{60} = 120 \text{ Hz} $$

2. Use the formula $\omega = 2\pi f$.

$$ \omega = 2\pi \times 120 = 240\pi \approx 754 \text{ rad s}^{-1} $$

(b) Calculate the linear speed ($v$):

1. Convert the radius to SI units: $r = 4.0 \text{ cm} = 0.040 \text{ m}$.

2. Use the formula $v = r\omega$.

$$ v = 0.040 \times 754 = 30.16 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

$$ v \approx 30 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$
