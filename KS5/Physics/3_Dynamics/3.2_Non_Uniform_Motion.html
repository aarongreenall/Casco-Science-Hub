3.2 Non-Uniform Motion

While uniform motion (constant velocity or constant acceleration) is a simplified model, real-world motion often involves changing speeds and directions due to varying forces. This is particularly true when considering **resistive forces** like friction and air resistance, which lead to **non-uniform motion** and phenomena like terminal velocity.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Show a qualitative understanding of frictional forces and viscous/drag forces including air resistance.
  2. Describe and explain qualitatively the motion of objects in a uniform gravitational field with air resistance.
  3. Understand that objects moving against a resistive force may reach a terminal (constant) velocity.

Resistive Forces: Friction and Drag:

**Resistive forces** are forces that oppose the motion of an object. They always act in the opposite direction to the object's motion.

  • **Frictional Forces:** Occur when two surfaces rub against each other. They depend on the nature of the surfaces and the normal force pressing them together.
  • **Viscous/Drag Forces (Fluid Resistance):** Occur when an object moves through a fluid (liquid or gas). These forces are often called **air resistance** when the fluid is air. Drag forces depend on factors like the object's speed, shape, surface area, and the density/viscosity of the fluid.

Unlike gravitational force, which is generally constant near Earth's surface, resistive forces often change with the speed of the object. For example, air resistance increases as an object's speed increases.

Motion with Air Resistance and Terminal Velocity:

When an object falls in a uniform gravitational field (like near Earth's surface), it is subject to two main forces:

  • **Weight:** Constant force acting downwards.
  • **Air Resistance (Drag):** Force acting upwards, opposing motion. This force increases as the object's speed increases.

Initially, when an object begins to fall, its speed is low, so air resistance is small. The resultant force is large and downwards, causing the object to accelerate. As the object speeds up, air resistance increases. This reduces the resultant downward force, and thus the acceleration decreases.

Eventually, the air resistance becomes equal in magnitude to the object's weight. At this point, the resultant force on the object is zero, and its acceleration becomes zero. The object then falls at a constant speed called its **terminal velocity**.

*Figure: Forces acting on a falling object, leading to terminal velocity.*

Different objects will have different terminal velocities depending on their weight, shape, and surface area (which affect the drag force). For example, a feather has a much lower terminal velocity than a stone due to its larger surface area-to-mass ratio.
