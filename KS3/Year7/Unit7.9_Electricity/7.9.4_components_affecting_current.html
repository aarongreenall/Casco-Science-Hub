7.9.4 Components affecting current

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define resistance as the opposition to the flow of current.
  2. Explain how adding more components in a series circuit affects the total resistance.
  3. Predict how adding more bulbs to a series circuit will affect their brightness.

Resistance: Opposing the Flow

In any circuit, the components (like bulbs, motors, and even the wires themselves) oppose the flow of the electric current. This opposition is called **resistance**. The more resistance a circuit has, the harder it is for the battery to push the electrons around, and so the smaller the current will be.

When you add more components to a series circuit, you are adding more obstacles to the path of the electricity. This means that the **total resistance of the circuit increases**.

Analogy: Water in Pipes

Let's go back to our "Water in Pipes" analogy. A bulb is like a narrow section of pipe or a small water wheel that the water has to push through. This is the **resistance**. If you add a second bulb in series, it's like adding a second water wheel in the same pipe. The pump (battery) now has to push the water (electrons) through two obstacles, which is much harder. This increased total opposition (resistance) means the overall flow rate of the water (current) will decrease.

Because adding a second bulb increases the total resistance, the current flowing through the circuit decreases. Since the brightness of a bulb depends on the current flowing through it, **both bulbs will be dimmer** than the single bulb was on its own.

In-School Experiment: Adding Bulbs

You can investigate how adding bulbs in series affects the current and the brightness.

Equipment:
  • A low-voltage power supply or battery
  • Two bulbs in holders
  • An ammeter
  • Connecting wires
Method:
  1. Build a simple series circuit with the power supply, one bulb, and the ammeter.
  2. Close the switch and observe the brightness of the bulb. Record the reading on the ammeter.
  3. Now, add the second bulb to the circuit, in series with the first one.
  4. Observe the brightness of both bulbs. How does it compare to the brightness of the single bulb?
  5. Record the new reading on the ammeter. How has the current changed?

Interactive Simulation: Circuit Construction Kit

In this PhET simulation, you can build the circuits from the experiment. First, build a circuit with one bulb and measure the current. Then, add a second bulb in series and measure the new, lower current. You can see the "rays" coming from the bulbs get smaller, showing that they have become dimmer.
