8.4.4 Colours of light

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Identify the primary and secondary colours of light.
  2. Explain how colours of light can be added together.
  3. Explain that the colour of an object depends on which colours of light it reflects and absorbs.

Adding Colours of Light

The colours we see on screens (like your phone or a TV) are made by adding different colours of light together. The **primary colours of light** are **Red**, **Green**, and **Blue**. By mixing these three colours in different amounts, we can create all other colours.

  • Red + Green = **Yellow**
  • Red + Blue = **Magenta**
  • Green + Blue = **Cyan**
  • Red + Green + Blue = **White**

Yellow, magenta, and cyan are known as the **secondary colours of light**.

The Colour of Objects

Why does a banana look yellow? Why does a leaf look green? The colour of an object is not a property of the object itself. It depends on the colours of light that the object **reflects** into our eyes. When white light hits an object, some colours are **absorbed** by the object, and some are reflected.

Analogy: The Bouncy Ball Catcher

Imagine white light is like someone throwing a red ball, a green ball, and a blue ball at you all at once. If you are wearing a **red jumper**, your jumper is a special material that only bounces the **red ball** back (reflection) but "catches" and holds onto the green and blue balls (absorption). An observer only sees the red ball coming from you, so they see your jumper as red. A **white jumper** is like a super bouncy trampoline that reflects all three coloured balls. When an observer's eyes see red, green, and blue light all at once, their brain mixes them to see white. A **black jumper** is like a patch of sticky mud that catches and holds all three coloured balls. None of them bounce back to an observer's eyes, so they see black.

Explanation: Colour

In-School Experiment: Coloured Filters

You can investigate how coloured objects appear under different coloured lights using filters. A filter is a piece of transparent material that only lets its own colour of light pass through, absorbing all other colours.

Equipment:
  • A ray box (producing white light)
  • Red, green, and blue coloured filters
  • A piece of white paper
  • A piece of red paper and a piece of blue paper
Method:
  1. In a darkened room, shine the white light from the ray box onto the white paper. Observe its colour.
  2. Now, place the red filter in front of the light. What colour does the white paper appear?
  3. Shine the red light onto the red paper. What colour does it appear?
  4. Shine the red light onto the blue paper. What colour does it appear? Why do you think this is? (Hint: The blue paper can only reflect blue light. What happens when only red light hits it?)
  5. Repeat steps 2-4 using the blue filter and then the green filter, and record your observations in a table.

Interactive Simulation: Color Vision

This PhET simulation is a great way to explore colour. You can shine different coloured lights on a person's shirt and see what colour of light is reflected to the observer's eye. Try shining a red light on a yellow shirt!
