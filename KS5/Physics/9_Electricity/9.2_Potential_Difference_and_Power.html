9.2 Potential Difference and Power

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define potential difference and the volt.
  2. Recall and use $V = W/Q$.
  3. Recall and use $P = VI$ and $P = I^2R$.
  4. Derive, using $V = W/Q$ and $I = Q/t$, the formula $P = VI$.
  5. Derive the formula $P = I^2R$ and $P=V^2/R$ using $V=IR$.

LO1 & LO2: Potential Difference

**Potential difference (p.d.)**, often called voltage, is the work done per unit charge in moving a charge between two points in a circuit. It is a measure of the energy transferred *from* the charge carriers to a component in the circuit.

$$ V = \frac{W}{Q} $$

The SI unit of potential difference is the **volt (V)**.

One volt is the potential difference between two points when one joule of work is done in moving one coulomb of charge from one point to the other ($1 \text{ V} = 1 \text{ J C}^{-1}$).

Worked Example: Work Done by a Component

A lamp has a potential difference of 12 V across it. In a certain time, a total charge of 150 C flows through it. Calculate the energy transferred by the lamp.

1. State the formula for potential difference:

$$ V = \frac{W}{Q} $$

2. Rearrange the formula to make work done ($W$) the subject:

$$ W = V \times Q $$

3. Substitute the values and solve:

$$ W = 12 \text{ V} \times 150 \text{ C} $$

$$ W = 1800 \text{ J} \text{ (or 1.8 kJ)} $$

LO3, LO4 & LO5: Electric Power

**Electric power ($P$)** is the rate at which electrical energy is transferred by a component. Its SI unit is the watt (W).

Detailed Derivations of Power Formulae

1. Deriving $P = VI$:

We start with the general definition of power as the rate of energy transfer (work done):

$$ P = \frac{W}{t} $$

From the definition of potential difference, we know $W = VQ$. Substitute this into the power equation:

$$ P = \frac{VQ}{t} $$

We also know that current is the rate of flow of charge, $I = \frac{Q}{t}$. We can see this term in our equation:

$$ P = V \left( \frac{Q}{t} \right) = VI $$

$$ P = VI $$

2. Deriving $P = I^2R$:

This formula is useful when we know the current and resistance. We start with $P=VI$ and use Ohm's Law, $V=IR$.

$$ P = VI $$

Substitute $V=IR$ into the equation:

$$ P = (IR)I $$

$$ P = I^2R $$

3. Deriving $P = \frac{V^2}{R}$:

This formula is useful when we know the potential difference and resistance. We start with $P=VI$ and use Ohm's Law, $V=IR$, rearranged for I.

$$ I = \frac{V}{R} $$

Substitute this expression for $I$ into $P=VI$:

$$ P = V \left( \frac{V}{R} \right) $$

$$ P = \frac{V^2}{R} $$

Worked Example: Power of a Resistor

A potential difference of 12 V is applied across a 48 $\Omega$ resistor. Calculate the power dissipated.

1. Identify the known variables: $V = 12$ V, $R = 48 \, \Omega$.

2. Choose the appropriate formula: We know V and R, so $P=V^2/R$ is the most direct formula.

$$ P = \frac{V^2}{R} $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ P = \frac{(12)^2}{48} $$

$$ P = \frac{144}{48} = 3.0 \text{ W} $$
