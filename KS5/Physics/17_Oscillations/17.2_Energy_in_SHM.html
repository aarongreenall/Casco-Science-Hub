17.2 Energy in simple harmonic motion

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the interchange between kinetic and potential energy during simple harmonic motion.
  2. Recall and use $E = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2x_0^2$ for the total energy of a system undergoing simple harmonic motion.

LO1: Energy Interchange in SHM

For a system undergoing simple harmonic motion (assuming no damping), the total mechanical energy remains constant. However, there is a continuous interchange between kinetic energy ($E_k$) and potential energy ($E_p$).

  • At maximum displacement (amplitude, $x = \pm x_0$): The object is momentarily stationary, so its velocity is zero and its kinetic energy is zero. The restoring force is at its maximum, so the potential energy (e.g., elastic potential energy in a spring) is at its maximum.
  • At the equilibrium position ($x = 0$): The object is moving at its maximum speed, so its kinetic energy is maximum. The restoring force is zero, so its potential energy is zero (relative to the equilibrium position).
  • At any other point: The total energy is a combination of both kinetic and potential energy. As the object moves away from the centre, its speed decreases (KE is converted to PE). As it moves towards the centre, its speed increases (PE is converted to KE).

LO2: Total Energy in SHM

Since the total energy of the system is constant, we can find an expression for it by considering the point where the energy is easiest to calculate: at maximum displacement.

Detailed Derivation of $E = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2x_0^2$

1. The total energy ($E$) of the system is constant and equal to the maximum potential energy, which occurs at the amplitude ($x=x_0$).

$$ E = E_{p(max)} $$

2. For a mass-spring system, the potential energy stored is the elastic potential energy, given by $E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$. At maximum displacement, this is:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}kx_0^2 $$

3. We need to relate the spring constant, $k$, to the properties of SHM. We know two expressions for the restoring force: Hooke's Law ($F=-kx$) and the SHM definition ($F=ma = -m\omega^2x$).

$$ -kx = -m\omega^2x $$

4. By comparing these two expressions, we can see that:

$$ k = m\omega^2 $$

5. Substitute this expression for $k$ back into the total energy equation from step 2:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}(m\omega^2)x_0^2 $$

6. This gives the final formula for the total energy of any system undergoing SHM:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2x_0^2 $$

Worked Example: Energy of an Oscillator

A 250 g mass is attached to a spring and oscillates with SHM. The frequency of the oscillation is 5.0 Hz and the amplitude is 8.0 cm. Calculate the total energy of the oscillator.

1. List variables and convert to SI units:

$$ m = 250 \text{ g} = 0.250 \text{ kg} $$

$$ f = 5.0 \text{ Hz} $$

$$ x_0 = 8.0 \text{ cm} = 0.080 \text{ m} $$

2. Calculate the angular frequency ($\omega$):

$$ \omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi \times 5.0 = 10\pi \text{ rad s}^{-1} $$

3. State the formula for total energy and solve:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2x_0^2 $$

$$ E = \frac{1}{2} \times (0.250) \times (10\pi)^2 \times (0.080)^2 $$

$$ E = 0.125 \times 986.96 \times 0.0064 $$

$$ E = 0.79 \text{ J} $$
