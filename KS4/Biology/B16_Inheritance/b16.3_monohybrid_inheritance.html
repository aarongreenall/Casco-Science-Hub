B16.3 Monohybrid inheritance

**Monohybrid inheritance** is the study of how a single gene, with its different alleles, is passed from parents to offspring. It forms the basis of Mendelian genetics and helps explain patterns of inheritance for simple traits. Understanding monohybrid crosses involves using Punnett squares and interpreting genetic ratios.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Predict the results of monohybrid crosses using: a Punnett square diagram; the ratios of genotypes and phenotypes.
  2. Describe the inheritance of sex in humans as XX and XY chromosomes.
  3. Describe the inheritance of the ABO blood groups as an example of codominance.
  4. Describe the inheritance of sickle cell anaemia as an example of codominance.
  5. Explain how a change in the nucleotide sequence of a gene can result in a different protein and so a different characteristic.

Predicting Monohybrid Crosses (Punnett Squares):

A **monohybrid cross** involves tracking the inheritance of a single gene with two alleles (one dominant, one recessive). **Punnett squares** are diagrams used to predict the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross.

**Steps to use a Punnett square:**

  1. Determine the genotypes of the parents.
  2. Determine the possible gametes (alleles) that each parent can produce through meiosis.
  3. Draw a Punnett square, placing the gametes from one parent along the top and the gametes from the other parent along the side.
  4. Fill in the squares by combining the alleles from the top and side, representing the possible genotypes of the offspring.
  5. Determine the ratios of the genotypes and phenotypes among the offspring.

For example, a cross between two heterozygous parents (Bb x Bb) for a trait where B is dominant (e.g., brown eyes) and b is recessive (blue eyes) would result in:

  • **Genotypic ratio:** 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb (or 1 homozygous dominant : 2 heterozygous : 1 homozygous recessive)
  • **Phenotypic ratio:** 3 Brown eyes : 1 Blue eyes (or 3 dominant phenotype : 1 recessive phenotype)

Inheritance of Sex in Humans:

In humans, sex is determined by the sex chromosomes:

  • **Females:** Have two X chromosomes (XX).
  • **Males:** Have one X and one Y chromosome (XY).

During meiosis, egg cells always carry an X chromosome. Sperm cells can carry either an X chromosome or a Y chromosome.

  • If an egg is fertilized by an X-carrying sperm, the offspring will be female (XX).
  • If an egg is fertilized by a Y-carrying sperm, the offspring will be male (XY).

This results in a 50% chance of having a male or female offspring.

Codominance: ABO Blood Groups and Sickle Cell Anaemia:

**Codominance** is a pattern of inheritance where both alleles for a gene are fully expressed in the phenotype of a heterozygous individual. Neither allele is dominant or recessive over the other; instead, both contribute to the phenotype.

ABO Blood Groups:

The **ABO blood groups** in humans are an example of codominance (as well as multiple alleles). The gene for blood type has three alleles: $I^A$, $I^B$, and $i$.

  • $I^A$ and $I^B$ are codominant. If both are present (genotype $I^A I^B$), both A and B antigens are expressed on red blood cells, resulting in blood type AB.
  • Allele $i$ is recessive to both $I^A$ and $I^B$.

This leads to four main blood phenotypes: A, B, AB, and O.

Sickle Cell Anaemia:

**Sickle cell anaemia** is a genetic disorder that affects red blood cells. It is an example of codominance at the molecular level. The gene involved codes for haemoglobin.

  • **Allele $H^A$:** Codes for normal haemoglobin.
  • **Allele $H^S$:** Codes for abnormal haemoglobin (sickle haemoglobin).

Individuals with genotype $H^A H^A$ have normal red blood cells. Individuals with genotype $H^S H^S$ have sickle cell anaemia (severe symptoms, sickle-shaped red blood cells). Individuals who are **heterozygous ($H^A H^S$)** have both normal and sickle haemoglobin in their red blood cells. They usually have mild or no symptoms but are resistant to malaria, demonstrating codominance.

*Figure: Comparison of normal red blood cells (round) and sickle-shaped red blood cells.*

Gene Mutation and Characteristic Change:

A **gene** is a sequence of nucleotides in DNA that codes for a specific protein. A **change in the nucleotide sequence of a gene** is called a **mutation**.

Such a change can result in:

  • A different sequence of amino acids in the protein.
  • This can lead to a different three-dimensional shape of the protein.
  • A change in the protein's function or even a non-functional protein.
  • Ultimately, this can result in a **different characteristic** (phenotype) in the organism.

For example, in sickle cell anaemia, a single nucleotide change in the haemoglobin gene leads to a faulty haemoglobin protein, causing red blood cells to become sickle-shaped.
