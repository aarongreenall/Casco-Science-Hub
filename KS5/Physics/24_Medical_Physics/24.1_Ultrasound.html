24.1 Production and use of ultrasound

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that a piezo-electric crystal changes shape when a p.d. is applied across it and that the crystal generates an e.m.f. when its shape changes.
  2. Understand how ultrasound waves are generated and detected by a piezoelectric transducer.
  3. Understand how the reflection of pulses of ultrasound at boundaries between tissues can be used to obtain diagnostic information about internal structures.
  4. Define the specific acoustic impedance of a medium as $Z = \rho c$, where c is the speed of sound in the medium.
  5. Use $I_R / I_0 = (Z_2 – Z_1)^2 / (Z_2 + Z_1)^2$ for the intensity reflection coefficient of a boundary between two media.
  6. Recall and use $I = I_0e^{–\mu x}$ for the attenuation of ultrasound in matter.

LO1 & LO2: The Piezoelectric Effect and Transducers

Ultrasound imaging relies on a remarkable property of certain crystals, like quartz, known as the **piezoelectric effect**. This effect is a two-way street between mechanical stress and electricity.

  • Generating an e.m.f.: When a piezoelectric crystal is compressed or stretched, its shape changes. This deformation causes a separation of charge within the crystal, generating a small electromotive force (e.m.f.) across it. (Mechanical energy $\rightarrow$ Electrical energy).
  • Changing Shape: Conversely, if a potential difference is applied across the crystal, it changes shape. If an alternating p.d. is applied, the crystal will oscillate, vibrating back and forth. (Electrical energy $\rightarrow$ Mechanical energy).

An **ultrasound transducer** is a device that cleverly uses both aspects of this effect. It contains a piezoelectric crystal that acts as both a transmitter and a receiver.

  1. Generation: A high-frequency alternating voltage is applied to the crystal, causing it to vibrate rapidly. These vibrations produce high-frequency sound waves—ultrasound—which are transmitted into the body.
  2. Detection: The transducer then switches to receiving mode. When the reflected ultrasound waves (echoes) return from the body, they hit the crystal and cause it to vibrate. These mechanical vibrations generate an e.m.f., which is converted into an electrical signal that can be processed to form an image.

LO3, LO4 & LO5: Ultrasound Imaging and Acoustic Impedance

Ultrasound imaging works by sending short pulses of ultrasound into the body. These pulses travel through different tissues and are partially reflected at the boundaries between them (e.g., between muscle and bone). The time taken for these echoes to return to the transducer is used to determine the depth of the boundary, while the intensity of the echo provides information about the type of tissue.

The amount of reflection that occurs at a boundary depends on a property of the medium called **specific acoustic impedance ($Z$)**.

$$ Z = \rho c $$

where $\rho$ is the density of the medium and $c$ is the speed of sound in the medium.

Derivation of the Intensity Reflection Coefficient

1. The reflection of waves at a boundary is determined by the amplitudes of the waves. We define:

  • Incident Amplitude ($A_0$): The amplitude of the original wave pulse travelling towards the boundary.
  • Reflected Amplitude ($A_r$): The amplitude of the wave pulse that reflects off the boundary.

The ratio of these amplitudes is given by the acoustic impedances of the two media ($Z_1$ and $Z_2$):

$$ \frac{A_r}{A_0} = \frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1} $$

2. We know that the intensity of a wave is proportional to the square of its amplitude ($I \propto A^2$).

3. Therefore, the ratio of the reflected intensity ($I_R$) to the incident intensity ($I_0$) is the square of the ratio of their amplitudes:

$$ \frac{I_R}{I_0} = \left(\frac{A_r}{A_0}\right)^2 $$

4. Substituting the expression from step 1 gives the final formula:

$$ \frac{I_R}{I_0} = \left(\frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}\right)^2 = \frac{(Z_2 - Z_1)^2}{(Z_2 + Z_1)^2} $$

Worked Example 1: Reflection at a Boundary (Calculating Z)

Calculate the intensity reflection coefficient for an ultrasound wave at the boundary between soft tissue and bone.
Soft tissue: density $\rho_1 = 1060$ kg m⁻³, speed of sound $c_1 = 1540$ m s⁻¹.
Bone: density $\rho_2 = 1900$ kg m⁻³, speed of sound $c_2 = 4080$ m s⁻¹.

1. Calculate the acoustic impedance for each medium:

$$ Z_1 = \rho_1 c_1 = 1060 \times 1540 = 1.63 \times 10^6 \text{ kg m}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1} $$

$$ Z_2 = \rho_2 c_2 = 1900 \times 4080 = 7.75 \times 10^6 \text{ kg m}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1} $$

2. Calculate the intensity reflection coefficient:

$$ \frac{I_R}{I_0} = \frac{(Z_2 - Z_1)^2}{(Z_2 + Z_1)^2} = \frac{(7.75 \times 10^6 - 1.63 \times 10^6)^2}{(7.75 \times 10^6 + 1.63 \times 10^6)^2} $$

$$ \frac{I_R}{I_0} = \frac{(6.12 \times 10^6)^2}{(9.38 \times 10^6)^2} = \frac{37.45 \times 10^{12}}{87.98 \times 10^{12}} = 0.426 $$

Approximately 43% of the intensity is reflected.

Interpretation of the Result

The 43% reflection calculated in the example is a **strong reflection**. On an ultrasound machine's display, the intensity of the reflected echo is represented by brightness. A strong echo, like the one from the boundary between soft tissue and bone, is detected by the transducer and displayed as a **bright white line** on the screen. This is why the outlines of bones and organs are so clear and white in an ultrasound image. In contrast, a boundary between two similar soft tissues would produce a very weak echo, appearing as a shade of grey, allowing sonographers to see the subtle textures within the body.

LO6: Attenuation

As an ultrasound wave travels through a medium, its intensity decreases. This is called **attenuation**. It is caused by the absorption of energy by the tissues and the scattering of the wave. This decrease is exponential.

$$ I = I_0 e^{-\mu x} $$

Where $I_0$ is the initial intensity, $I$ is the intensity after travelling a distance $x$ through the medium, and $\mu$ is the **linear attenuation coefficient** of the medium.

Worked Example: Attenuation in Tissue

An ultrasound pulse with an initial intensity of 5.0 W cm⁻² travels through 4.0 cm of soft tissue. The linear attenuation coefficient for the tissue is 0.23 cm⁻¹. Calculate the intensity of the pulse after it has travelled through the tissue.

1. State the formula and identify variables:

$$ I = I_0 e^{-\mu x} $$

$I_0 = 5.0$ W cm⁻²; $\mu = 0.23$ cm⁻¹; $x = 4.0$ cm.

(Note: As long as $\mu$ and $x$ are in consistent units, they don't need to be converted to SI units).

2. Substitute values and solve:

$$ I = 5.0 \times e^{-(0.23 \times 4.0)} $$

$$ I = 5.0 \times e^{-0.92} $$

$$ I = 5.0 \times 0.3985 = 1.99 \text{ W cm}^{-2} $$
