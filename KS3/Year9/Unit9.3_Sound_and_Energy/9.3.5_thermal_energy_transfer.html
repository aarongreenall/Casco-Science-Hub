9.3.5 Thermal Energy Transfer

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will describe the three ways thermal energy (heat) can be transferred: conduction, convection, and radiation.

1. Conduction:

  • The transfer of heat through a substance by direct contact of particles, without the substance itself moving.
  • Mainly occurs in solids.
  • Mechanism: When one part of a solid is heated, its particles gain kinetic energy and vibrate more vigorously. They collide with neighboring particles, transferring the energy along the substance.
  • Conductors vs. Insulators: Metals are good conductors of heat because they have free electrons that can move and transfer energy quickly. Materials like wood, plastic, and air are poor conductors (insulators).

2. Convection:

  • The transfer of heat through fluids (liquids and gases) by the movement of the fluid itself.
  • Mechanism: When a part of a fluid is heated, it expands, becomes less dense, and rises. Cooler, denser fluid sinks to take its place, gets heated, and rises. This creates a circular flow called a convection current.
  • Examples: Heating water in a kettle, a radiator heating a room, sea breezes.

3. Radiation:

  • The transfer of heat as electromagnetic waves (specifically infrared radiation).
  • It does not require a medium and can travel through a vacuum.
  • Mechanism: All objects above absolute zero emit thermal radiation. Hotter objects emit more radiation.
  • Surface Properties:
    • Dull, black surfaces are good absorbers and good emitters of thermal radiation.
    • Shiny, light-colored surfaces are poor absorbers and poor emitters (they are good reflectors).
  • Examples: Heat from the Sun reaching Earth, heat felt from a fire, a vacuum flask (thermos) using shiny surfaces to reduce heat transfer by radiation.
