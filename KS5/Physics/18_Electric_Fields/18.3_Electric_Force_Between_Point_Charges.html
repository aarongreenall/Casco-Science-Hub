18.3 Electric force between point charges

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that, for a point outside a spherical conductor, the charge on the sphere may be considered to be a point charge at its centre.
  2. Recall and use Coulomb’s law $F = \frac{Q_1Q_2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2}$ for the force between two point charges in free space.

LO1 & LO2: Coulomb's Law

**Coulomb's Law** describes the magnitude of the electrostatic force between two point charges. It is the electrical analogue of Newton's Law of Gravitation.

Similar to gravity, a crucial simplification applies: for any point outside a uniformly charged sphere, the electrostatic force exerted by the sphere is identical to what it would be if all the sphere's charge were concentrated into a single **point charge** at its centre. This allows us to apply the law to spherical objects like charged metal spheres.

The electrostatic force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

$$ F = \frac{Q_1Q_2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} $$

  • $F$ is the magnitude of the electrostatic force between the two charges.
  • $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ are the magnitudes of the two charges.
  • $r$ is the distance between the centres of the two charges.
  • $\epsilon_0$ is the **permittivity of free space**, a fundamental constant with a value of $8.85 \times 10^{-12}$ F m⁻¹. It describes how easily an electric field can be established in a vacuum.

Comparison: Coulomb's Law vs. Newton's Law of Gravitation

Newton's Law of Gravitation

$$ F = \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2} $$

  • Depends on the product of **masses**.
  • Force is **always attractive**.
  • $G$ is the gravitational constant.
Coulomb's Law

$$ F = \frac{Q_1Q_2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} $$

  • Depends on the product of **charges**.
  • Force can be **attractive or repulsive**.
  • $1/(4\pi\epsilon_0)$ is the electrostatic constant.

Worked Example: Force between Protons

Calculate the magnitude of the electrostatic force between two protons separated by a distance of $5.0 \times 10^{-15}$ m in a nucleus.

1. Identify the variables:

The charge on a proton is the elementary charge, $Q_1 = Q_2 = e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C.

$$ r = 5.0 \times 10^{-15} \text{ m} $$

2. State the formula and substitute values:

$$ F = \frac{Q_1Q_2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} $$

$$ F = \frac{(1.60 \times 10^{-19}) \times (1.60 \times 10^{-19})}{4\pi(8.85 \times 10^{-12})(5.0 \times 10^{-15})^2} $$

3. Calculate the result:

$$ F = \frac{2.56 \times 10^{-38}}{2.78 \times 10^{-39}} $$

$$ F = 9.21 \text{ N} $$

This is a huge repulsive force on a nuclear scale, highlighting the need for the strong nuclear force to hold the nucleus together.
