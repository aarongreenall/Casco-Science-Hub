15.3 Kinetic theory of gases

Learning Objectives:

  1. State the basic assumptions of the kinetic theory of gases.
  2. Explain how molecular movement causes the pressure exerted by a gas and derive and use the relationship $pV = \frac{1}{3}Nm\langle c^2 \rangle$, where $\langle c^2 \rangle$ is the mean-square speed.
  3. Understand that the root-mean-square speed $c_{r.m.s.}$ is given by $\sqrt{\langle c^2 \rangle}$.
  4. Compare $pV = \frac{1}{3}Nm\langle c^2 \rangle$ with $pV = NkT$ to deduce that the average translational kinetic energy of a molecule is $\frac{3}{2}kT$, and recall and use this expression.

LO1: Assumptions of the Kinetic Theory

The kinetic theory of gases is a model that explains the macroscopic properties of an ideal gas (pressure, volume, temperature) in terms of the microscopic motion of its atoms or molecules. The model is based on a set of simplifying assumptions:

  1. A gas consists of a very large number of identical molecules that are in continuous, random motion.
  2. The volume of the molecules themselves is negligible compared to the volume occupied by the gas.
  3. There are no intermolecular forces between the molecules, except during collisions.
  4. The time taken for a collision is negligible compared to the time between collisions.
  5. All collisions, both between molecules and between molecules and the walls of the container, are perfectly elastic (i.e., no kinetic energy is lost).

LO2: Deriving the Pressure Equation

The pressure exerted by a gas is caused by the collisions of its molecules with the walls of the container. Each collision exerts a small force on the wall. The average of these forces over the entire area of the container walls results in the gas pressure.

Detailed Derivation of $pV = \frac{1}{3}Nm\langle c^2 \rangle$

1. Consider one molecule in a 1D box: Imagine a single molecule of mass $m$ moving with velocity $c_x$ in a cubic box of side length $L$. It collides elastically with one wall (area $A=L^2$).

$$ \text{Initial momentum} = mc_x \quad \text{Final momentum} = -mc_x $$

$$ \text{Change in momentum, } \Delta p = -mc_x - mc_x = -2mc_x $$

By Newton's third law, the change in momentum of the wall is $+2mc_x$.

2. Time between collisions: The molecule travels a distance of $2L$ (to the opposite wall and back) between successive collisions with the same wall. The time taken is $\Delta t = \frac{2L}{c_x}$.

3. Force exerted by one molecule: By Newton's second law, the average force on the wall is the rate of change of momentum.

$$ F_1 = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{2mc_x}{2L/c_x} = \frac{mc_x^2}{L} $$

4. Pressure exerted by N molecules in 1D: For $N$ molecules, the total force is the sum of the individual forces. We must use the mean-square speed in the x-direction, $\langle c_x^2 \rangle$, as molecules have different speeds.

$$ F_{total} = \frac{Nm\langle c_x^2 \rangle}{L} \quad \implies \quad p = \frac{F_{total}}{A} = \frac{Nm\langle c_x^2 \rangle}{LA} = \frac{Nm\langle c_x^2 \rangle}{V} $$

5. Extend to 3D: Molecules move randomly in three dimensions. The total speed squared, $c^2$, is related to its components by Pythagoras: $c^2 = c_x^2 + c_y^2 + c_z^2$. For a large number of molecules, the average motion is the same in all directions, so $\langle c_x^2 \rangle = \langle c_y^2 \rangle = \langle c_z^2 \rangle$.

$$ \langle c^2 \rangle = \langle c_x^2 \rangle + \langle c_y^2 \rangle + \langle c_z^2 \rangle = 3\langle c_x^2 \rangle \implies \langle c_x^2 \rangle = \frac{1}{3}\langle c^2 \rangle $$

6. Final Equation: Substitute this 3D relationship back into the pressure equation:

$$ pV = Nm \left( \frac{1}{3}\langle c^2 \rangle \right) \implies pV = \frac{1}{3}Nm\langle c^2 \rangle $$

LO3: Mean-Square and Root-Mean-Square Speed

The molecules in a gas move at a wide range of speeds. The **mean-square speed, $\langle c^2 \rangle$**, is the average of the squared speeds of all the molecules. It is important because the kinetic energy of the gas is related to this value, not the mean speed.

To get a value for a typical speed, we take the square root of the mean-square speed. This is called the **root-mean-square speed ($c_{r.m.s.}$) **.

$$ c_{r.m.s.} = \sqrt{\langle c^2 \rangle} $$

LO4: Kinetic Energy of a Molecule

We can now link the microscopic kinetic energy of gas molecules to the macroscopic property of temperature.

Detailed Derivation of Average KE = $\frac{3}{2}kT$

1. We have two equations for $pV$:

$$ pV = \frac{1}{3}Nm\langle c^2 \rangle \quad \text{(from kinetic theory)} $$

$$ pV = NkT \quad \text{(from the ideal gas equation)} $$

2. Equate these two expressions:

$$ \frac{1}{3}Nm\langle c^2 \rangle = NkT $$

3. Cancel the number of molecules, $N$, from both sides:

$$ \frac{1}{3}m\langle c^2 \rangle = kT $$

4. The average translational kinetic energy of a molecule is given by $E_k = \frac{1}{2}m\langle c^2 \rangle$. We can see this term in our equation if we manipulate it. Multiply both sides by 3 and divide by 2:

$$ \frac{3}{2} \times \frac{1}{3}m\langle c^2 \rangle = \frac{3}{2}kT $$

5. This simplifies to the final, important result:

$$ \frac{1}{2}m\langle c^2 \rangle = \frac{3}{2}kT $$

This shows that the **thermodynamic temperature of an ideal gas is directly proportional to the average translational kinetic energy of its molecules.**

Worked Example: r.m.s. Speed of Helium Atoms

Calculate the r.m.s. speed of helium atoms at a temperature of 20 °C. (Mass of a helium atom = $6.6 \times 10^{-27}$ kg).

1. Convert temperature to Kelvin:

$$ T = 20 + 273.15 = 293.15 \text{ K} $$

2. Start with the kinetic energy formula:

$$ \frac{1}{2}m\langle c^2 \rangle = \frac{3}{2}kT $$

3. Rearrange for mean-square speed, $\langle c^2 \rangle$:

$$ \langle c^2 \rangle = \frac{3kT}{m} $$

4. Substitute values:

$$ \langle c^2 \rangle = \frac{3 \times (1.38 \times 10^{-23}) \times (293.15)}{6.6 \times 10^{-27}} = 1.835 \times 10^6 \text{ m}^2\text{s}^{-2} $$

5. Find the r.m.s. speed by taking the square root:

$$ c_{r.m.s.} = \sqrt{\langle c^2 \rangle} = \sqrt{1.835 \times 10^6} = 1355 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

$$ c_{r.m.s.} \approx 1400 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

Temperature and Average Kinetic Energy

The Problem with the Celsius Scale

We know that the temperature of a substance is a measure of the average random kinetic energy of its particles. The Celsius scale, which sets the freezing point of water as its zero point (0 °C), creates a logical problem. It implies that at 0 °C, particles have zero kinetic energy, and at negative temperatures, they would have negative kinetic energy. This is physically impossible, as kinetic energy ($E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$) can never be negative. We know many substances are liquids or gases well below 0 °C, meaning their particles are still moving and have kinetic energy. This shows that the Celsius scale's zero point is arbitrary and not a true zero of energy.

Discovering Absolute Zero

In the 19th century, scientists investigated the relationship between the temperature of a gas and its pressure or volume. When they plotted graphs of pressure or volume against temperature in degrees Celsius, they found a straight-line relationship. When they extrapolated these graphs backward, they all intersected the temperature axis at the same point: **-273.15 °C**.

This suggested that -273.15 °C was a fundamental, absolute minimum temperature, where the gas would theoretically have zero volume or pressure, and its particles would have zero kinetic energy. This point is called **absolute zero**.

The Kelvin Scale

To create a temperature scale that directly reflects the kinetic energy of particles, the **thermodynamic (or Kelvin) scale** was created. This scale simply shifts the zero point of the Celsius scale to absolute zero.

  • The zero point (0 K) is absolute zero.
  • The size of one kelvin (1 K) is the same as the size of one degree Celsius (1 °C).

On this scale, the average kinetic energy of particles is directly proportional to the thermodynamic temperature.
