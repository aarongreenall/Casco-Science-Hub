7.7.6 Microorganisms as decomposers

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define a decomposer.
  2. Identify bacteria and fungi as the main types of decomposer.
  3. Explain the crucial role of decomposers in recycling nutrients in an ecosystem.

What are Decomposers?

In any ecosystem, organisms are born, they live, and eventually they die. What happens to all the dead material, like fallen leaves, dead trees, and the bodies of animals? If it just piled up, the world would be a very messy place! This is where decomposers come in.

A **decomposer** is a living organism that breaks down dead organic material.

The most important decomposers in any ecosystem are **microorganisms**, specifically **bacteria** and **fungi** (like moulds and mushrooms). They perform a vital role that no other organisms can.

Analogy: The Ultimate Recycling Crew

Think of decomposers as the **"Ultimate Recycling Crew"** of an ecosystem. When a tree falls or an animal dies, it's like putting a complex piece of rubbish out for collection. The decomposers (the bacteria and fungi) are the recycling workers who come and break down this complex waste into simple, useful raw materials, like minerals and nutrients. These raw materials are then put back into the soil, ready for the producers (plants) to use again to grow. Without this recycling crew, the world would be buried in dead stuff, and the raw materials needed for new life would quickly run out.
