25.1 Luminosity and radiant flux intensity

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand the term luminosity as the total power of radiation emitted by a star.
  2. Recall and use the inverse square law for radiant flux intensity F in terms of the luminosity L of the source $F = L / (4\pi d^2)$.
  3. Understand that an object of known luminosity is called a standard candle.
  4. Understand the use of standard candles to determine distances to galaxies.

LO1: Luminosity

**Luminosity ($L$)** is the total power of electromagnetic radiation emitted by a star. It is a measure of the star's intrinsic brightness—how much energy it radiates into space every second.

Luminosity is the total power radiated by a star.

The SI unit for luminosity is the **watt (W)**. A star's luminosity depends on its temperature and its size (surface area).

LO2: Radiant Flux Intensity and the Inverse Square Law

While luminosity tells us the total power a star emits, what we measure on Earth is its apparent brightness. This is quantified as **radiant flux intensity ($F$)**, which is the power received per unit area.

As the radiation from a star travels out into space, it spreads out over the surface of an ever-expanding sphere. This means the energy per unit area decreases with distance. This relationship is known as the **inverse square law**.

Detailed Derivation of $F = L / (4\pi d^2)$

1. Start with the definition of intensity: Intensity = Power / Area.

2. For a star, the total power radiated is its luminosity, $L$. The radiant flux intensity, $F$, is the intensity we observe.

3. At a distance $d$ from the star, the total power $L$ has spread out over the surface of a sphere of radius $d$. The surface area of this sphere is $A = 4\pi d^2$.

4. Substitute these terms into the intensity definition:

$$ F = \frac{L}{4\pi d^2} $$

LO3 & LO4: Standard Candles and Measuring Cosmic Distances

Measuring the vast distances to other galaxies is one of the greatest challenges in astronomy. A key tool for this is the **standard candle**.

A **standard candle** is an astronomical object that has a known, predictable luminosity.

The logic is simple but powerful: if you know how bright an object truly is (its luminosity, $L$), and you can measure how bright it appears to be from Earth (its radiant flux intensity, $F$), you can calculate its distance ($d$) by rearranging the inverse square law.

$$ d = \sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi F}} $$

Two of the most important types of standard candles are:

  • Cepheid Variables: These are pulsating stars whose luminosity is directly related to the period of their pulsation. By measuring the period, astronomers can determine the star's true luminosity.
  • Type Ia Supernovae: These are powerful stellar explosions that occur when a white dwarf star in a binary system accretes too much mass. Because they all happen in a very similar way, their peak luminosity is incredibly consistent, making them excellent standard candles for measuring the distances to the most remote galaxies.

Worked Example: Distance to a Supernova

A Type Ia supernova is observed in a distant galaxy. At its brightest, it has a peak luminosity of $2.0 \times 10^{36}$ W. The radiant flux intensity measured on Earth from this supernova is $4.5 \times 10^{-15}$ W m⁻². Calculate the distance to the galaxy.

1. State the inverse square law, rearranged for distance:

$$ d = \sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi F}} $$

2. Substitute the values:

$$ d = \sqrt{\frac{2.0 \times 10^{36}}{4\pi (4.5 \times 10^{-15})}} $$

3. Calculate the result:

$$ d = \sqrt{\frac{2.0 \times 10^{36}}{5.65 \times 10^{-14}}} = \sqrt{3.54 \times 10^{49}} $$

$$ d = 1.88 \times 10^{24} \text{ m} $$
