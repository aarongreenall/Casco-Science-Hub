8.5.4 Inert Substances

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain what makes some substances, like the noble gases, chemically inert (unreactive).

Reactivity and Electron Structure:

  • The chemical reactivity of an element is determined by the arrangement of electrons in its outermost shell (the valence shell).
  • Atoms react in order to achieve a stable electron arrangement, which usually means having a full outer shell of electrons.
  • Atoms can achieve a full outer shell by losing, gaining, or sharing electrons.

Inert Substances - The Noble Gases:

  • The elements in Group 0 (or Group 18) of the periodic table are called the noble gases (Helium, Neon, Argon, Krypton, Xenon, Radon).
  • They are called "noble" or "inert" because they are extremely unreactive.
  • Reason for Unreactivity: The noble gases have a full outer shell of electrons.
    • Helium has 2 electrons in its first (and only) shell, which is full.
    • All other noble gases have 8 electrons in their outermost shell, which is a stable arrangement.
  • Since they already have a stable electron configuration, they have no tendency to lose, gain, or share electrons to form chemical bonds with other atoms.
  • This makes them very useful for applications where non-reactivity is required, such as in light bulbs (argon) and balloons (helium).
