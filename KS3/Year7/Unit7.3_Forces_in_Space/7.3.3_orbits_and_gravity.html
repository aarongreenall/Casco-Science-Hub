7.3.3 Orbits and Gravity

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define an orbit as the curved path of an object around a larger object due to gravity.
  2. Explain how an object stays in orbit.
  3. Describe how the strength of gravity affects an orbit.

What is an Orbit?

An **orbit** is the curved path that an object, like a planet or a satellite, takes around a much larger object, like a star. This path is caused by the force of **gravity**. The Moon orbits the Earth, and the Earth orbits the Sun. In every case, the smaller object is held in its path by the gravitational pull of the larger object.

But if gravity is constantly pulling the Earth towards the Sun, why doesn't it just fall in and crash? The answer is that the Earth is also moving sideways at a very high speed.

Analogy: The Cannonball on the Tower

Imagine standing on a very tall tower and firing a cannonball horizontally. This famous thought experiment helps to explain how orbits work:

  • If you fire it slowly, gravity pulls it down to the ground quickly.
  • If you fire it faster, it travels further before gravity pulls it to the ground.
  • If you could fire it at exactly the right, incredibly high speed, the cannonball would travel so far that as it falls, the curved surface of the Earth drops away beneath it at the same rate. The cannonball is now in orbitâ€”it is constantly falling towards the Earth, but it's moving so fast sideways that it keeps "missing" the ground!

At-Home Experiment: The Stretched Sheet Model of Gravity

You can build a simple model to see how gravity creates orbits. In this model, the stretchy sheet represents the "fabric of spacetime," and heavy objects create a dip, or "gravity well," that lighter objects can orbit.

Equipment:
  • A large, stretchy piece of fabric (like a bedsheet, spandex, or lycra)
  • Four chairs or helpers to hold the fabric taut and flat
  • A heavy, round object (like a basketball or a melon) to be the "Sun"
  • Several smaller, lighter marbles or ball bearings to be the "planets"
  • A phone or tablet to film your experiment
Method:
  1. Stretch the fabric between the four chairs to create a flat, trampoline-like surface. Make sure it is as tight as possible.
  2. Place the heavy basketball in the centre of the sheet. Observe how it creates a dip or a "well" in the fabric. This represents the Sun's gravitational field.
  3. Now, gently roll a marble across the sheet, aiming near the basketball but not directly at it. Try to give it some sideways speed.
  4. Observe the path of the marble. If you get the speed and angle right, you should be able to make the marble orbit the basketball several times before it spirals in.
  5. **Film your best orbit!** Record a short video of your model in action. While you film, provide a commentary explaining what each part represents (the sheet, the basketball, the marble) and how your model shows that an object needs both the force of gravity and sideways motion to stay in orbit.
