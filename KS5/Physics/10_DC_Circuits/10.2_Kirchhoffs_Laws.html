10.2 Kirchhoff's Laws

Learning Objectives:

  1. Recall and use Kirchhoff's first law.
  2. Recall and use Kirchhoff's second law.
  3. Solve problems involving Kirchhoff's laws.

LO1: Kirchhoff's First Law (The Junction Rule)

Kirchhoff's First Law deals with the flow of current at any junction in a circuit. It is a direct consequence of the **conservation of charge**. Since charge cannot be created or destroyed, the rate at which charge flows into a junction must equal the rate at which it flows out.

The sum of the currents entering any junction in a circuit is equal to the sum of the currents leaving that junction.

$$ \sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out} $$

Shopping Mall Analogy: The Food Court Entrance

Imagine the shoppers (current) walking through the mall. A junction in a circuit is like the entrance to a food court. The number of shoppers walking into the food court per minute must be equal to the number of shoppers walking out of its various exits per minute. Shoppers can't just appear or disappear inside the food court.

Worked Example: Finding an Unknown Current

For the junction shown in the diagram above, if $I_1 = 5$ A, $I_2 = 2$ A, and $I_4 = 1$ A, calculate the current $I_3$.

1. Apply Kirchhoff's First Law:

$$ \sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out} $$

2. Identify currents in and out:

$$ I_1 = I_2 + I_3 + I_4 $$

3. Substitute known values:

$$ 5 = 2 + I_3 + 1 $$

$$ 5 = 3 + I_3 $$

4. Solve for $I_3$:

$$ I_3 = 5 - 3 = 2 \text{ A} $$

LO2: Kirchhoff's Second Law (The Loop Rule)

Kirchhoff's Second Law deals with the potential differences in any closed loop of a circuit. It is a direct consequence of the **conservation of energy**. The total energy supplied to the charges in a loop must equal the total energy transferred by them.

In any closed loop in a circuit, the sum of the electromotive forces (e.m.f.s) is equal to the sum of the potential differences (p.d.s).

$$ \sum \mathcal{E} = \sum V = \sum (IR) $$

Shopping Mall Analogy: A Full Lap of the Mall

A closed loop is like a shopper starting at the ATM, walking a full lap of the mall through various shops, and returning to the ATM. The total money given by the ATM (sum of e.m.f.s) must be equal to the total money spent in all the shops along that specific lap (sum of p.d.s). You can't return to the start having spent more or less money than you were given.

LO3: Solving Problems with Kirchhoff's Laws

For complex circuits with multiple loops and sources, we must apply both of Kirchhoff's laws simultaneously to find unknown currents and voltages. This often involves setting up and solving simultaneous equations.

Worked Example: Multi-loop Circuit with Internal Resistance

For the circuit shown, calculate the currents $I_1$, $I_2$, and $I_3$.

1. Apply the First Law at junction A:

$$ I_1 = I_2 + I_3 \quad \cdots(1) $$

2. Apply the Second Law to the left loop (clockwise):

The sum of e.m.f.s is 12 V. The sum of p.d.s includes the external resistor and the internal resistor of the 12V cell.

$$ \sum \mathcal{E} = \sum (IR) $$

$$ 12 = (I_1 \times 2) + (I_2 \times 6) + (I_1 \times 1) $$

$$ 12 = 3I_1 + 6I_2 \quad \cdots(2) $$

3. Apply the Second Law to the right loop (anticlockwise):

The sum of e.m.f.s is 9 V. The sum of p.d.s includes the external resistor and the internal resistor of the 9V cell. We traverse the 6$\Omega$ resistor against the direction of $I_2$, so its p.d. is negative.

$$ \sum \mathcal{E} = \sum (IR) $$

$$ 9 = (I_3 \times 3) - (I_2 \times 6) + (I_3 \times 0.5) $$

$$ 9 = 3.5I_3 - 6I_2 \quad \cdots(3) $$

4. Solve the simultaneous equations. Substitute (1) into (2):

$$ 12 = 3(I_2 + I_3) + 6I_2 = 3I_2 + 3I_3 + 6I_2 = 9I_2 + 3I_3 \quad \cdots(4) $$

5. Rearrange (3) for $6I_2$ and substitute into (4):

From (3): $6I_2 = 3.5I_3 - 9$. Multiply (4) by 2 to get a term of $18I_2$ and (3) by 3 to get a term of $18I_2$.

From (4): $24 = 18I_2 + 6I_3$

From (3): $27 = 10.5I_3 - 18I_2 \implies 18I_2 = 10.5I_3 - 27$

Substitute $18I_2$ into the modified equation (4):

$$ 24 = (10.5I_3 - 27) + 6I_3 $$

$$ 51 = 16.5I_3 \implies I_3 = \frac{51}{16.5} = 3.09 \text{ A} \approx 3.1 \text{ A} $$

6. Find $I_2$ and $I_1$:

$$ 6I_2 = 3.5(3.09) - 9 = 10.815 - 9 = 1.815 \implies I_2 = 0.30 \text{ A} $$

$$ I_1 = I_2 + I_3 = 0.30 + 3.09 = 3.39 \text{ A} \approx 3.4 \text{ A} $$
