9.2.2 Photosynthesis Process

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Know that photosynthesis occurs in chloroplasts and is the process by which plants make carbohydrates, using the energy from light.
  2. Know and use the summary word equation for photosynthesis (carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen, in the presence of light and chlorophyll).

The Ultimate Food Factory

**Photosynthesis** is the amazing chemical process that plants use to make their own food. This process doesn't just happen anywhere in the plant; it takes place inside tiny, specialised structures within the plant's leaf cells called **chloroplasts**. These chloroplasts contain a green pigment called **chlorophyll**, which is the key to the whole process.

Analogy: A Solar-Powered Factory

Think of a single chloroplast as a tiny, high-tech, solar-powered factory.

  • Energy Source: The factory is powered by sunlight.
  • Raw Materials: It takes in **water** (delivered from the roots) and **carbon dioxide** (from the air).
  • Machinery: The green **chlorophyll** inside acts like solar panels, capturing the sun's energy.
  • Main Product: The factory produces **glucose** (a type of sugar, which is a carbohydrate) that the plant uses for food and energy.
  • Waste Product: As a by-product, it releases **oxygen** back into the air.

The Recipe for Photosynthesis

Like any good recipe, photosynthesis has ingredients (reactants), conditions, and finished products. We can summarise the entire process with a simple word equation.

The Word Equation

Carbon Dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen

(in the presence of Light Energy & Chlorophyll)

Chemists also use a balanced symbol equation to show exactly how many atoms are involved. This equation shows that nothing is lost; the atoms are just rearranged to form new substances.

The Balanced Symbol Equation

$6CO_2 + 6H_2O \xrightarrow{\text{}} C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$

(in the presence of Light Energy & Chlorophyll)

What Does the Plant Do with the Glucose?

The glucose produced during photosynthesis is incredibly useful. It's the plant's main source of energy and a versatile building block. The plant has three main uses for it:

  1. Immediate Energy: The plant can use the glucose straight away in **respiration** to release energy for its life processes, like growing.
  2. Storage: If the plant makes more glucose than it needs, it can convert it into insoluble **starch** and store it in its leaves, stem, or roots for later use. This is like packing away food for the winter.
  3. Building Materials: The plant can use glucose to build other essential substances. For example, by combining it with minerals like nitrates, it can make proteins for building new cells.

Analogy: Pocket Money

Think of glucose as pocket money for the plant. It can **spend it immediately** on things it needs right now (energy for respiration). It can **save it for later** by putting it in a piggy bank (storing it as starch). Or, it can use the money to **buy other things** it needs to build a better life (making proteins and other substances).
