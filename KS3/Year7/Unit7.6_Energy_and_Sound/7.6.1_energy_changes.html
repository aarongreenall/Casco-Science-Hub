7.6.1 Energy changes and Sound

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define sound as a form of energy produced by vibrations.
  2. Describe the three things needed for a sound to be heard.
  3. Explain that sound waves transfer energy.

What is Sound?

**Sound** is a form of energy that we can hear. It is created when an object **vibrates**â€”moves back and forth very quickly. These vibrations travel through a substance as a wave, carrying energy from the source to our ears.

For a sound to be heard, three things are needed:

  1. A **Source:** Something that vibrates to create the sound (e.g., a guitar string, your vocal cords).
  2. A **Medium:** A substance for the vibrations to travel through (e.g., air, water, a solid wall).
  3. A **Detector:** Something to detect the vibrations (e.g., your ear, a microphone).

Analogy: Ripples in a Pond

A great way to understand how sound travels is to think about dropping a stone into a calm pond. The **stone hitting the water** is like the **vibrating source**. The **water** is the **medium**. The **ripples** that spread out are like the **sound waves**. If you watch a leaf floating on the water, you will see it bob up and down as the ripples pass, but it doesn't travel across the pond. This shows that **energy** has been transferred by the wave, but the medium (the water) has stayed in the same place. Sound works in exactly the same way.

In-School Activity: Feeling the Vibrations

You can directly feel the link between sound and vibrations with some simple activities.

Equipment:
  • A plastic ruler
  • A tuning fork and a block to strike it on
  • A rubber band
Method:
  1. The Ruler: Hold the ruler firmly on the edge of a desk with about half of it sticking out. Pluck the end of the ruler to make it twang. You can see it vibrating and hear the sound. Now, place your other hand on the ruler to stop the vibrations. What happens to the sound?
  2. The Tuning Fork: Strike the tuning fork on the block to make it ring. Gently touch the prongs with your fingertips. What do you feel? Now touch the prongs to the surface of some water in a beaker. What do you see?
  3. The Rubber Band: Stretch a rubber band between your thumb and forefinger. Pluck it like a guitar string. Can you see it vibrating and hear the sound?
