C2.6 Giant covalent structures

Beyond simple molecular compounds, carbon atoms can form vast, continuous networks through covalent bonds, creating **giant covalent structures**. These structures, exemplified by diamond and graphite, exhibit unique properties that are a direct consequence of their extensive bonding arrangements.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the giant covalent structures of graphite and diamond.
  2. Relate the structures and bonding of graphite and diamond to their uses, limited to: graphite as a lubricant and as an electrode; diamond in cutting tools.

Diamond: A Hard, Non-Conducting Network:

In **diamond**, each carbon atom is covalently bonded to **four other carbon atoms** in a rigid, tetrahedral arrangement. This forms a vast, continuous 3D network of strong covalent bonds.

The properties of diamond are a direct result of this structure:

  • **Extremely Hard:** The strong covalent bonds extending in all three dimensions make diamond the hardest known natural substance. This property makes it ideal for use in **cutting tools** (e.g., drills, saws) and as an abrasive.
  • **High Melting/Boiling Point:** A huge amount of energy is required to break the extensive network of strong covalent bonds, giving diamond a very high melting and boiling point.
  • **Does Not Conduct Electricity:** All outer shell electrons in diamond are involved in covalent bonding, so there are no delocalised (free) electrons to carry charge. This makes diamond an electrical insulator.

*Figure: The giant covalent structure of diamond, showing each carbon atom bonded to four others.*

Graphite: A Layered Conductor:

**Graphite** also consists only of carbon atoms, but its structure is very different from diamond. In graphite, each carbon atom is covalently bonded to **three other carbon atoms** in flat, hexagonal layers. These layers are held together by weak intermolecular forces.

The properties of graphite are also a direct result of its unique structure:

  • **Soft and Slippery:** The weak forces between the layers allow the layers to slide easily over each other. This makes graphite soft and slippery, ideal for use as a **lubricant** (e.g., in pencil lead, or as a dry lubricant in machinery).
  • **Good Electrical Conductor:** Each carbon atom in graphite has one outer electron that is not involved in bonding within its layer. These electrons become **delocalised** between the layers and are free to move, allowing graphite to conduct electricity. This property makes it suitable for use as **electrodes** in electrolysis.
  • **High Melting/Boiling Point:** Although the forces between layers are weak, the covalent bonds *within* each layer are very strong, requiring a lot of energy to break the entire structure.

*Figure: The giant covalent structure of graphite, showing carbon atoms bonded in layers.*
