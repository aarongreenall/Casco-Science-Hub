B18.3 Nutrient cycles

In ecosystems, essential chemical elements are constantly recycled between living organisms (biotic components) and the non-living environment (abiotic components). These processes are known as **nutrient cycles**. Understanding the carbon cycle and the nitrogen cycle is crucial for comprehending how ecosystems maintain balance and how human activities can impact these vital processes.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the carbon cycle, limited to: photosynthesis; respiration; feeding; decomposition; combustion.
  2. Describe the nitrogen cycle, limited to: decomposition; nitrification; nitrogen fixation by lightning and bacteria; absorption of nitrate ions by plants; ingestion and assimilation; excretion; denitrification.
  3. State the roles of: decomposers in the carbon and nitrogen cycles; nitrifying bacteria; nitrogen-fixing bacteria (in root nodules and free-living in soil); denitrifying bacteria.

The Carbon Cycle:

The **carbon cycle** describes the movement of carbon atoms through the Earth's atmosphere, oceans, land, and living organisms. Key processes in the carbon cycle include:

  • **Photosynthesis:** Plants and algae absorb carbon dioxide (CO$_2$) from the atmosphere (or dissolved in water) and convert it into organic compounds (glucose) using light energy. This removes CO$_2$ from the atmosphere.
  • **Respiration:** All living organisms (plants, animals, decomposers) release carbon dioxide into the atmosphere as they break down organic compounds to release energy.
  • **Feeding:** Carbon compounds are transferred from one organism to another along food chains when organisms feed.
  • **Decomposition:** When organisms die, **decomposers** (bacteria and fungi) break down their complex organic carbon compounds into simpler substances, including carbon dioxide, which is released back into the atmosphere.
  • **Combustion:** The burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) and biomass (e.g., wood) releases large amounts of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. This is a major human impact on the carbon cycle.

The Nitrogen Cycle:

The **nitrogen cycle** describes the movement of nitrogen atoms through the atmosphere, soil, and living organisms. Nitrogen is essential for making proteins and nucleic acids (DNA), but atmospheric nitrogen (N$_2$) is unreactive and cannot be directly used by most organisms. Various bacteria play crucial roles in converting nitrogen into usable forms.

*Figure: A diagram illustrating the main processes of the nitrogen cycle.*

  • **Nitrogen Fixation:** * **By lightning:** High energy from lightning can cause atmospheric nitrogen (N$_2$) to react with oxygen, forming nitrogen oxides that dissolve in rain and fall to the soil as nitrates. * **By bacteria:** * **Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (free-living in soil):** Convert atmospheric nitrogen (N$_2$) into ammonia (NH$_3$). * **Nitrogen-fixing bacteria (in root nodules of legumes):** Live symbiotically in the root nodules of leguminous plants (e.g., peas, beans) and convert atmospheric nitrogen into ammonia.
  • **Absorption of Nitrate Ions by Plants:** Plants absorb nitrate ions (NO$_3^-$) from the soil through their roots. These nitrates are then used to make amino acids and proteins.
  • **Ingestion and Assimilation:** Animals obtain nitrogen by eating plants or other animals. The nitrogen compounds are then assimilated into their own proteins and other organic molecules.
  • **Decomposition:** When plants and animals die, **decomposers** (bacteria and fungi) break down their nitrogen-containing organic compounds (e.g., proteins, urea) into ammonia. This process is called ammonification.
  • **Nitrification:** **Nitrifying bacteria** convert ammonia (or ammonium ions) in the soil into nitrites (NO$_2^-$) and then into nitrates (NO$_3^-$). This makes nitrogen available for plants.
  • **Excretion:** Animals excrete nitrogenous waste products (e.g., urea, ammonia), which are then broken down by decomposers.
  • **Denitrification:** **Denitrifying bacteria** convert nitrates in the soil back into atmospheric nitrogen gas (N$_2$) under anaerobic (low oxygen) conditions. This returns nitrogen to the atmosphere.
