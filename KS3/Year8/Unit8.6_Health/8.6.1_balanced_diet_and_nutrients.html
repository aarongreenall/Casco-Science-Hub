8.6.1 Balanced diet and nutrients

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define a balanced diet.
  2. Identify the seven main nutrient groups.
  3. Describe the function of each nutrient group in the body.

What is a Balanced Diet?

The food we eat is our body's fuel and building material. To stay healthy, we need to eat a **balanced diet**. This doesn't mean eating equal amounts of everything. It means eating the **right proportions** of different types of food, called **nutrients**, to provide our bodies with everything they need to work properly.

There are seven main groups of nutrients that our bodies need. A good way to visualize a balanced diet is using the **Eatwell Plate**, which shows how much of your diet should come from each of the main food groups.

The Seven Nutrients

Each nutrient has a specific and vital job to do in the body.

Analogy: Building and Running a Car

You can think of the seven nutrients as the different things a car needs to be built and to run properly.

  • Carbohydrates are the **petrol** – the main fuel for everyday energy.
  • Fats are the **reserve fuel tank** – a concentrated store of energy.
  • Proteins are the **engine parts and bodywork** – essential for building and repairing the car (the body).
  • Vitamins & Minerals are the **oil and spark plugs** – you only need tiny amounts, but without them, the engine won't run smoothly.
  • Water is the **coolant** – needed for all the systems to work correctly.
  • Fibre is the **exhaust pipe cleaner** – it doesn't provide energy but is vital for keeping the digestive system clean and running well.

At-Home Experiment: Testing Foods for Fats

You can find out which foods contain fats using this simple test. Fats leave a permanent, translucent (see-through) spot on paper.

Equipment:
  • A piece of brown paper or a paper bag
  • A variety of small food samples to test (e.g., a crisp, a piece of apple, a nut, a small piece of cheese, a drop of cooking oil, a drop of water)
Method:
  1. Draw circles on the brown paper and label each one with the name of a food you are going to test.
  2. In each circle, firmly rub a small sample of the corresponding food. Use a drop of oil and a drop of water as your test samples.
  3. Leave the paper in a warm place to dry completely for about 10-15 minutes.
  4. Once dry, hold the paper up to a light source, like a window or a lamp.
  5. Observe the spots. The spot made by water will have disappeared. Any food that contains fat will have left a permanent, translucent (greasy) spot that lets light pass through it.
Conclusion:

Based on your results, which of your food samples contained fat?
