4.3 Density and Pressure

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define and use density.
  2. Define and use pressure.
  3. Derive, from the definitions of pressure and density, the equation for hydrostatic pressure $\Delta p=\rho g\Delta h$.
  4. Use the equation $\Delta p=\rho g\Delta h$.
  5. Understand that the upthrust acting on an object in a fluid is due to a difference in hydrostatic pressure.
  6. Calculate the upthrust acting on an object in a fluid using the equation $F=\rho gV$ (Archimedes' principle).

LO1: Density

Density ($\rho$) is an intrinsic property of a substance that describes how much mass is packed into a given volume. It is defined as the mass per unit volume.

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} $$

The SI unit for density is kilograms per cubic metre (kg m⁻³). It is a scalar quantity.

Worked Example: Calculating Density

A solid cube of aluminium has a side length of 5.0 cm and a mass of 337.5 g. Calculate the density of aluminium in SI units.

1. Convert all measurements to SI units:

$$ m = 337.5 \text{ g} = 0.3375 \text{ kg} $$

$$ L = 5.0 \text{ cm} = 0.050 \text{ m} $$

2. Calculate the volume of the cube:

$$ V = L^3 = (0.050 \text{ m})^3 = 0.000125 \text{ m}^3 $$

3. Use the density formula:

$$ \rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{0.3375 \text{ kg}}{0.000125 \text{ m}^3} = 2700 \text{ kg m}^{-3} $$

LO2: Pressure

Pressure ($p$) is defined as the normal (perpendicular) force exerted per unit area.

$$ p = \frac{F}{A} $$

The SI unit for pressure is the **pascal (Pa)**, where 1 Pa = 1 N m⁻². Pressure is a scalar quantity.

LO3 & LO4: Hydrostatic Pressure

Within a fluid (a liquid or gas), pressure increases with depth. This is because the deeper you go, the greater the weight of the fluid column above you. This pressure exerted by a fluid at rest is known as **hydrostatic pressure**.

Derivation of $\Delta p = \rho g \Delta h$

1. Consider a cylindrical column of fluid of height $\Delta h$ and cross-sectional area $A$.

2. The volume of this column is $V = A \times \Delta h$.

3. The mass of the fluid in this column is $m = \rho V = \rho A \Delta h$.

4. The weight of this fluid column is the force it exerts on the area $A$ at the bottom: $F = W = mg = (\rho A \Delta h)g$.

5. The pressure difference, $\Delta p$, due to this column of fluid is this force divided by the area:

$$ \Delta p = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{\rho A g \Delta h}{A} $$

6. Cancelling the area $A$ from the numerator and denominator gives the final equation:

$$ \Delta p = \rho g \Delta h $$

Worked Example: Pressure in the Ocean

Calculate the pressure due to the seawater at a depth of 1.5 km in the ocean. The density of seawater is 1030 kg m⁻³.

1. Identify variables and convert to SI units:

$$ \rho = 1030 \text{ kg m}^{-3}, \quad g = 9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}, \quad \Delta h = 1.5 \text{ km} = 1500 \text{ m} $$

2. Apply the hydrostatic pressure formula:

$$ \Delta p = \rho g \Delta h = 1030 \times 9.81 \times 1500 $$

$$ \Delta p = 15,156,450 \text{ Pa} \approx 1.5 \times 10^7 \text{ Pa} \text{ (or 15 MPa)} $$

LO5 & LO6: Upthrust and Archimedes' Principle

When an object is submerged in a fluid, the pressure on its bottom surface is greater than the pressure on its top surface. This pressure difference results in a net upward force on the object, known as **upthrust**.

This is formally stated by **Archimedes' Principle**:

The upthrust exerted on a body immersed in a fluid, whether fully or partially, is equal to the weight of the fluid that the body displaces.

$$ F_{upthrust} = \rho_{fluid} g V_{displaced} $$
