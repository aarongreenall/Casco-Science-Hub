B7.3 Digestion

**Digestion** is the complex process of breaking down large, insoluble food molecules into small, soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the bloodstream and used by the body. This process involves both physical (mechanical) and chemical digestion, with enzymes playing a crucial role in the latter.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe physical digestion as the breakdown of food into smaller pieces without chemical change to the food molecules.
  2. State that physical digestion increases the surface area of food for the action of enzymes in chemical digestion.
  3. Describe chemical digestion as the break down of large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules.
  4. State the role of chemical digestion in producing small soluble molecules that can be absorbed.
  5. Describe the functions of enzymes as follows: amylase breaks down starch to simple reducing sugars; proteases break down protein to amino acids; lipase breaks down fats and oils to fatty acids and glycerol.
  6. State where, in the digestive system, amylase, protease and lipase are secreted and where they act.
  7. Describe the functions of hydrochloric acid in gastric juice, limited to killing harmful microorganisms in food and providing an acidic pH for optimum enzyme activity of proteases in the stomach.
  8. Explain that bile is an alkaline mixture that neutralises the acidic mixture of food and gastric juices entering the duodenum from the stomach, to provide a suitable pH for enzyme action in the small intestine.
  9. Outline the role of bile in emulsifying fats and oils to increase the surface area for chemical digestion.

Physical vs. Chemical Digestion:

Digestion involves two main types of breakdown:

  • **Physical Digestion (Mechanical Digestion):** The breakdown of food into smaller pieces without any chemical change to the food molecules. This increases the **surface area** of the food, which is crucial for the efficient action of enzymes in chemical digestion. * Examples: Chewing in the mouth, churning in the stomach, emulsification of fats by bile.
  • **Chemical Digestion:** The breakdown of large, insoluble food molecules into small, soluble molecules using digestive enzymes. These small, soluble molecules can then be absorbed into the bloodstream.

Role of Enzymes in Chemical Digestion:

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical digestion. Different enzymes are specific to different types of food molecules:

  • **Amylase:** Breaks down **starch** into simple reducing sugars (like maltose). * **Secreted:** Salivary glands (in mouth), pancreas (into duodenum). * **Acts in:** Mouth, small intestine.
  • **Proteases:** Break down **proteins** into amino acids. * **Secreted:** Stomach wall, pancreas (into duodenum), small intestine wall. * **Acts in:** Stomach, small intestine.
  • **Lipase:** Breaks down **fats and oils** (lipids) into fatty acids and glycerol. * **Secreted:** Pancreas (into duodenum), small intestine wall. * **Acts in:** Small intestine.

Role of Hydrochloric Acid and Bile:

Two other important substances aid digestion:

  • **Hydrochloric Acid (HCl) in Gastric Juice:** * **Function:** Produced by the stomach lining. * Kills harmful microorganisms (bacteria) present in food. * Provides an **acidic pH** (around pH 1.5-3.5) which is the optimum pH for the activity of proteases (like pepsin) in the stomach.
  • **Bile:** * **Nature:** An alkaline mixture produced by the liver and stored in the gall bladder. * **Function:** Released into the duodenum. * **Neutralizes** the acidic mixture of food and gastric juices entering from the stomach, providing a suitable alkaline pH (around pH 7-8) for enzyme action (amylase, proteases, lipase) in the small intestine. * **Emulsifies fats and oils:** Breaks down large fat globules into smaller fat droplets. This is a physical process that increases the surface area of fats, making them more accessible for digestion by lipase.
