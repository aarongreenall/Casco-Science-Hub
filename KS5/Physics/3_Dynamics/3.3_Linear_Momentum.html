3.3 Linear Momentum and its Conservation

**Linear momentum** is a crucial concept in physics for analyzing collisions and interactions between objects. It is a measure of an object's "quantity of motion." The **principle of conservation of momentum** is a fundamental law that governs how momentum is transferred in isolated systems.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State the principle of conservation of momentum.
  2. Apply the principle of conservation of momentum to solve simple problems, including elastic and inelastic interactions between objects in both one and two dimensions.
  3. Recall that, for a perfectly elastic collision, the relative speed of approach is equal to the relative speed of separation.
  4. Understand that, while momentum of a system is always conserved in interactions between objects, some change in kinetic energy may take place.

Linear Momentum:

**Linear momentum (p)** is a vector quantity defined as the product of an object's **mass (m)** and its **velocity (v)**. It has the same direction as the velocity.

$p = mv$

Where: * $p$ is momentum in kilogram meters per second (kg m/s) or Newton seconds (N s). * $m$ is mass in kilograms (kg). * $v$ is velocity in meters per second (m/s).

The greater an object's mass or velocity, the greater its momentum. A large truck moving slowly can have the same momentum as a small car moving quickly.

*Figure: Illustration of momentum as a product of mass and velocity.*

Principle of Conservation of Momentum:

The **principle of conservation of momentum** states that for an isolated system (a system where no external forces act), the total linear momentum remains constant. In other words, the total momentum before a collision or interaction is equal to the total momentum after the collision or interaction.

$m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$

Where: * $m_1, m_2$ are the masses of object 1 and object 2. * $u_1, u_2$ are their initial velocities. * $v_1, v_2$ are their final velocities.

This principle applies to all types of interactions, including collisions (elastic and inelastic) and explosions.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the conservation of momentum in a collision.*

Elastic and Inelastic Interactions:

While momentum is always conserved in interactions within an isolated system, **kinetic energy** may or may not be conserved.

  • **Elastic Collision:** A collision in which **both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved**. In a perfectly elastic collision, the relative speed of approach between the objects is equal to the relative speed of separation. These are rare in macroscopic systems but common at the atomic level (e.g., gas molecules colliding).
  • **Inelastic Collision:** A collision in which **momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is NOT conserved**. Some kinetic energy is converted into other forms of energy, such as heat, sound, or deformation energy. Most real-world collisions (e.g., car crashes, a ball hitting the ground) are inelastic.

In inelastic collisions, the objects may stick together after impact, or deform significantly.
