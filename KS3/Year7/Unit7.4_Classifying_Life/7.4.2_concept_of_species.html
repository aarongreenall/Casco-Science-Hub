7.4.2 Concept of species

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define the term 'species'.
  2. Explain the importance of 'fertile offspring' in the definition of a species.
  3. Use examples to explain why some similar-looking animals are different species.

What is a Species?

Scientists have identified millions of different types of living things on Earth. To organise them, they group similar organisms together. The most fundamental group is a **species**.

A **species** is a group of living organisms that are able to reproduce with each other to produce **fertile offspring**.

The key word here is **fertile**. Fertile means that the offspring (the babies) can grow up and have their own babies. If the offspring are infertile (sterile), then the two original parents must belong to different species.

Example: Horses and Donkeys

A horse and a donkey are similar enough that they can reproduce. Their baby is called a mule. However, all mules are infertileâ€”they cannot have their own babies. This is the scientific proof that horses and donkeys are two different species.

In contrast, a Golden Retriever and a Poodle are very different breeds of dog, but they can have puppies (often called Labradoodles). These puppies can then grow up and have their own puppies. This proves that all dog breeds belong to the same species (*Canis familiaris*).

Scientific Names

To avoid confusion with common names (for example, a "robin" in America is a very different bird from a "robin" in the UK), scientists give every species a unique, two-part scientific name. This system is called the **binomial system**. For example, the scientific name for humans is *Homo sapiens*, and for a lion it is *Panthera leo*.

In-School Activity: Investigating Hybrids

Sometimes, animals from two different species can have offspring. These are called hybrids. Your task is to research some famous hybrids and find out if their offspring are fertile. This will help you confirm if their parents are the same or different species.

Research Questions:
  1. A male lion (*Panthera leo*) and a female tiger (*Panthera tigris*) can produce a **liger**. Are ligers fertile? What does this tell you about lions and tigers?
  2. A grizzly bear (*Ursus arctos horribilis*) and a polar bear (*Ursus maritimus*) can produce a **grolar bear** (or pizzly bear). Are these hybrids fertile? What does this tell you about grizzly and polar bears?
