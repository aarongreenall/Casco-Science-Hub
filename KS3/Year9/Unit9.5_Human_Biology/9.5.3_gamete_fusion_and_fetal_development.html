9.5.3 Gamete Fusion and Fetal Development

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will describe sexual reproduction in humans, from the fusion of gametes to the development of a fetus.

Gametes and Fertilisation:

  • Gametes: Sex cells (sperm and egg). They are haploid, meaning they contain only one set of chromosomes (23 in humans).
  • Fertilisation: The fusion of the male gamete (sperm) and the female gamete (egg) to form a zygote.
  • Zygote: The first cell of a new organism. It is diploid, containing two sets of chromosomes (46 in humans), one set from each parent.

Fetal Development:

  • The zygote undergoes repeated cell division (mitosis) and develops into an embryo.
  • The embryo implants in the wall of the uterus.
  • The placenta develops, which is an organ that allows for the exchange of materials between the mother's blood and the fetal blood without them mixing.
    • Oxygen and nutrients pass from mother to fetus.
    • Carbon dioxide and waste products pass from fetus to mother.
  • The fetus is surrounded by amniotic fluid in the amniotic sac, which protects it from physical shock.
  • The umbilical cord connects the fetus to the placenta.
  • After about 8 weeks, the embryo is referred to as a fetus as all the major organs have formed. The fetus continues to grow and develop until birth (around 40 weeks).
