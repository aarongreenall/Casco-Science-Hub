25.2 Stellar radii

Learning Objectives:

  1. Recall and use Wien’s displacement law $\lambda_{max} \propto 1 / T$ to estimate the peak surface temperature of a star.
  2. Use the Stefan–Boltzmann law $L = 4\pi\sigma r^2 T^4$.
  3. Use Wien’s displacement law and the Stefan–Boltzmann law to estimate the radius of a star.

LO1: Wien's Displacement Law

Stars behave approximately as **black-body radiators**, meaning they emit a continuous spectrum of electromagnetic radiation. The peak wavelength ($\lambda_{max}$) of this spectrum—the wavelength at which the star is most luminous—is determined by its surface temperature ($T$).

**Wien's Displacement Law** states that the peak wavelength of the emitted radiation is inversely proportional to the thermodynamic temperature of the black body.

$$ \lambda_{max} T = \text{constant} = 2.90 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K} $$

This means hotter stars have a shorter peak wavelength and appear blueish-white, while cooler stars have a longer peak wavelength and appear reddish-orange.

Worked Example: Temperature of the Sun

The Sun's spectrum peaks at a wavelength of approximately 500 nm. Estimate the surface temperature of the Sun.

1. State Wien's Displacement Law and rearrange for T:

$$ \lambda_{max} T = 2.90 \times 10^{-3} \implies T = \frac{2.90 \times 10^{-3}}{\lambda_{max}} $$

2. Substitute values in SI units: $\lambda_{max} = 500 \text{ nm} = 500 \times 10^{-9}$ m.

$$ T = \frac{2.90 \times 10^{-3}}{500 \times 10^{-9}} $$

$$ T = 5800 \text{ K} $$

LO2: The Stefan-Boltzmann Law

The **Stefan-Boltzmann Law** relates a star's luminosity ($L$) to its surface area and temperature. It states that the total power radiated per unit surface area of a black body is directly proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature.

For a spherical star of radius $r$, the surface area is $4\pi r^2$. The law is therefore expressed as:

$$ L = 4\pi\sigma r^2 T^4 $$

Where $\sigma$ is the **Stefan-Boltzmann constant**:

$\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8}$ W m⁻² K⁻⁴

The strong dependence on temperature ($T^4$) means that a small increase in a star's temperature leads to a very large increase in its luminosity.

LO3: Determining the Radius of a Star

By combining Wien's Law, the Stefan-Boltzmann Law, and the inverse square law for radiant flux intensity, we can estimate the radius of a distant star. This is a powerful application that connects several key concepts.

Worked Example: Radius of the Star Betelgeuse

The star Betelgeuse has a peak emission wavelength of 855 nm and a radiant flux intensity on Earth of $2.9 \times 10^{-8}$ W m⁻². Its distance from Earth is $6.1 \times 10^{18}$ m. Estimate the radius of Betelgeuse.

Step 1: Find the surface temperature (T) using Wien's Law.

$$ T = \frac{2.90 \times 10^{-3}}{\lambda_{max}} = \frac{2.90 \times 10^{-3}}{855 \times 10^{-9}} = 3392 \text{ K} $$

Step 2: Find the luminosity (L) using the inverse square law.

$$ F = \frac{L}{4\pi d^2} \implies L = 4\pi d^2 F $$

$$ L = 4\pi (6.1 \times 10^{18})^2 (2.9 \times 10^{-8}) = 1.35 \times 10^{31} \text{ W} $$

Step 3: Find the radius (r) using the Stefan-Boltzmann Law.

$$ L = 4\pi\sigma r^2 T^4 \implies r = \sqrt{\frac{L}{4\pi\sigma T^4}} $$

$$ r = \sqrt{\frac{1.35 \times 10^{31}}{4\pi(5.67 \times 10^{-8})(3392)^4}} $$

$$ r = \sqrt{\frac{1.35 \times 10^{31}}{9.45 \times 10^{16}}} = \sqrt{1.43 \times 10^{14}} $$

$$ r = 3.78 \times 10^{11} \text{ m} $$

(This is enormous - about 540 times the radius of our Sun, confirming Betelgeuse is a red supergiant).
