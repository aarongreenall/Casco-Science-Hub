C12.5 Identification of ions and gases

In chemistry, identifying unknown substances is a crucial skill. This often involves performing qualitative tests to detect the presence of specific ions (cations and anions) and gases. These tests rely on characteristic observable changes, such as precipitate formation, color changes, or gas properties.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe tests to identify the anions: carbonate, CO$_3^{2-}$, by reaction with dilute acid and then testing for carbon dioxide gas; chloride, Cl$^-$, bromide, Br$^-$, and iodide, I$^-$, by acidifying with dilute nitric acid then adding aqueous silver nitrate; nitrate, NO$_3^-$, by reduction with aluminium foil and aqueous sodium hydroxide and then testing for ammonia gas; sulfate, SO$_4^{2-}$, by acidifying with dilute nitric acid then adding aqueous barium nitrate.
  2. Describe the use of a flame test to identify the cations: lithium, Li$^+$; sodium, Na$^+$; potassium, K$^+$; copper(II), Cu$^{2+}$.
  3. Describe tests using aqueous sodium hydroxide and aqueous ammonia to identify the aqueous cations: ammonium, NH$_4^+$; calcium, Ca$^{2+}$; copper(II), Cu$^{2+}$; iron(II), Fe$^{2+}$; iron(III), Fe$^{3+}$; zinc, Zn$^{2+}$ (formulas of complex ions are not required).
  4. Describe tests to identify the gases: ammonia, NH$_3$, using damp red litmus paper; carbon dioxide, CO$_2$, using limewater; chlorine, Cl$_2$, using damp litmus paper; hydrogen, H$_2$, using a lighted splint; oxygen, O$_2$, using a glowing splint.
  5. Describe chemical tests for the presence of water using anhydrous cobalt(II) chloride and anhydrous copper(II) sulfate.

Tests for Anions (Negative Ions):

Anions are identified using specific chemical tests that often involve forming a precipitate or a gas.

  • **Carbonate (CO$_3^{2-}$):** * **Test:** Add dilute acid (e.g., dilute hydrochloric acid). * **Observation:** Effervescence (fizzing) of a gas. Test the gas with limewater (calcium hydroxide solution); it turns cloudy/milky, confirming carbon dioxide. * `CO3^{2-}(aq) + 2H^+(aq) -> H2O(l) + CO2(g)`
  • **Chloride (Cl$^-$), Bromide (Br$^-$), Iodide (I$^-$):** * **Test:** Acidify with dilute nitric acid (to remove any carbonate ions that would interfere), then add aqueous silver nitrate solution. * **Observation:** * Cl$^-$: White precipitate of silver chloride (AgCl). * Br$^-$: Cream precipitate of silver bromide (AgBr). * I$^-$: Yellow precipitate of silver iodide (AgI). * `Ag^+(aq) + Cl^-(aq) -> AgCl(s)`
  • **Nitrate (NO$_3^-$):** * **Test:** Add aluminium foil and aqueous sodium hydroxide, then warm gently. Test any gas produced with damp red litmus paper. * **Observation:** Damp red litmus paper turns blue, indicating ammonia gas (NH$_3$). * `8Al(s) + 3NO3^-(aq) + 5OH^-(aq) + 2H2O(l) -> 8AlO2^-(aq) + 3NH3(g)`
  • **Sulfate (SO$_4^{2-}$):** * **Test:** Acidify with dilute nitric acid, then add aqueous barium nitrate solution. * **Observation:** White precipitate of barium sulfate (BaSO$_4$). * `Ba^{2+}(aq) + SO4^{2-}(aq) -> BaSO4(s)`

Tests for Cations (Positive Ions):

Cations are identified using flame tests or by adding aqueous sodium hydroxide and aqueous ammonia.

1. Flame Tests:

Flame tests are used to identify certain metal ions based on the characteristic color they produce when heated in a hot Bunsen flame.

  • **Lithium (Li$^+$):** Crimson red flame.
  • **Sodium (Na$^+$):** Orange-yellow flame.
  • **Potassium (K$^+$):** Lilac (purple) flame.
  • **Copper(II) (Cu$^{2+}$):** Blue-green flame.

*Figure: Various metal ions producing characteristic colors in a flame test.*

2. Tests with Aqueous Sodium Hydroxide and Aqueous Ammonia:

These reagents are used to identify various aqueous cations based on the color and solubility of the precipitates formed.

  • **Ammonium (NH$_4^+$):** * **Test:** Warm with aqueous sodium hydroxide. * **Observation:** Ammonia gas produced (damp red litmus turns blue).
  • **Calcium (Ca$^{2+}$):** * **Test:** Add aqueous sodium hydroxide. * **Observation:** White precipitate, insoluble in excess NaOH.
  • **Copper(II) (Cu$^{2+}$):** * **Test:** Add aqueous sodium hydroxide. * **Observation:** Light blue precipitate, insoluble in excess NaOH. * **Test:** Add aqueous ammonia. * **Observation:** Light blue precipitate, soluble in excess ammonia to form a deep blue solution.
  • **Iron(II) (Fe$^{2+}$):** * **Test:** Add aqueous sodium hydroxide. * **Observation:** Green precipitate, insoluble in excess NaOH.
  • **Iron(III) (Fe$^{3+}$):** * **Test:** Add aqueous sodium hydroxide. * **Observation:** Red-brown precipitate, insoluble in excess NaOH.
  • **Zinc (Zn$^{2+}$):** * **Test:** Add aqueous sodium hydroxide. * **Observation:** White precipitate, soluble in excess NaOH (forms a colorless solution). * **Test:** Add aqueous ammonia. * **Observation:** White precipitate, soluble in excess ammonia (forms a colorless solution).

Tests for Gases:

Gases produced in reactions can be identified by their characteristic properties or reactions with specific test reagents.

  • **Ammonia (NH$_3$):** * **Test:** Damp red litmus paper. * **Observation:** Turns blue (alkaline gas).
  • **Carbon Dioxide (CO$_2$):** * **Test:** Bubble through limewater (calcium hydroxide solution). * **Observation:** Turns limewater cloudy/milky.
  • **Chlorine (Cl$_2$):** * **Test:** Damp litmus paper. * **Observation:** Bleaches litmus paper (turns it white).
  • **Hydrogen (H$_2$):** * **Test:** Lighted splint. * **Observation:** Produces a 'pop' sound.
  • **Oxygen (O$_2$):** * **Test:** Glowing splint. * **Observation:** Relights the glowing splint.

Tests for Water:

The presence of water can be confirmed using anhydrous compounds:

  • **Anhydrous Cobalt(II) Chloride:** * **Test:** White anhydrous cobalt(II) chloride paper. * **Observation:** Turns blue in the presence of water.
  • **Anhydrous Copper(II) Sulfate:** * **Test:** White anhydrous copper(II) sulfate powder. * **Observation:** Turns blue in the presence of water.
