P4.4 Voltage (electromotive force and potential difference)

Voltage is a crucial concept in electricity, representing the "push" or "electrical pressure" that drives electric current through a circuit. It is often described using terms like electromotive force (e.m.f.) and potential difference (p.d.), which, while related, have distinct meanings. Understanding voltage is essential for designing and analyzing electrical circuits.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the voltage of the source as the cause of current in the circuit.
  2. Know that the voltage of the source is shared between the components in a series circuit.
  3. Describe the use of voltmeters (analogue and digital) with different ranges.
  4. Define electromotive force (e.m.f) as the electrical work done by a source in moving a unit charge around a complete circuit.
  5. Know that e.m.f is measured in volts (V).
  6. Define potential difference (p.d) as the work done by a unit charge passing between two points in a circuit.
  7. Know that the p.d between two points is measured in volts (V).
  8. Recall and use in calculations, the fact that: the total p.d across the components in a series circuit is equal to the sum of the individual p.d.s across each component.
  9. Recall and use in calculations, the fact that: the p.d across each branch of a parallel arrangement of components is the p.d. across the whole arrangement.

Understanding Voltage, e.m.f., and p.d.:

**Voltage** is the electrical potential energy difference between two points in a circuit. It is the driving force that pushes electrons around a circuit, causing a current to flow. The standard SI unit for voltage is the **Volt (V)**.

**Electromotive force (e.m.f.)** is the total electrical work done by a source (like a battery or power supply) in moving a unit charge around a complete circuit. It represents the energy supplied by the source.

**Potential difference (p.d.)** is the work done by a unit charge as it passes between two points in a circuit. It represents the energy converted from electrical energy to other forms (like light or heat) by a component. In a simple circuit, the p.d. across a component is often referred to as the voltage across that component.

Measuring Voltage (Voltmeters):

Voltage is measured using a **voltmeter**. Voltmeters must always be connected in **parallel** across the component or section of the circuit where the voltage is being measured. They have a very high resistance to ensure that very little current flows through the voltmeter itself, thus not affecting the circuit's operation. Voltmeters can be analogue or digital and come with different ranges for various measurement needs.

*Figure: Standard circuit symbol for a voltmeter.*

Voltage in Series and Parallel Circuits:

The behavior of voltage differs in series and parallel circuits:

  • **Series Circuits:** The voltage of the source (e.m.f.) is **shared** among the components in a series circuit. The sum of the individual potential differences across each component in a series circuit is equal to the total voltage of the source.
  • **Parallel Circuits:** The potential difference (voltage) across each branch of a parallel arrangement of components is **the same**, and it is equal to the voltage across the entire parallel arrangement (and often the source voltage).
