3.2 Non-uniform motion

Learning Objectives:

  1. Show a qualitative understanding of frictional forces and viscous/drag forces including air resistance.
  2. Describe and explain qualitatively the motion of objects in a uniform gravitational field with air resistance.
  3. Understand that objects moving against a resistive force may reach a terminal (constant) velocity.

LO1: Frictional and Viscous/Drag Forces

In real-world scenarios, motion is almost always opposed by resistive forces. These forces act to slow down moving objects. The two main types are frictional forces and viscous/drag forces.

  • Frictional Forces: Occur when two solid surfaces slide, or attempt to slide, across one another. Friction always acts parallel to the surfaces and opposes the direction of motion or intended motion.
  • Viscous/Drag Forces: These are resistive forces that an object experiences when it moves through a fluid (a liquid or a gas). Air resistance is the most common example of a drag force.

A key characteristic of drag forces is that their magnitude is **not constant**. It depends on several factors, but most importantly for A-Level Physics:

The magnitude of the drag force increases as the speed of the object increases.

LO2 & LO3: Motion with Air Resistance & Terminal Velocity

When an object falls through the air, it is subject to two main forces: its weight (acting downwards) and air resistance (acting upwards). Unlike the idealised model of free fall, the acceleration is not constant. This leads to the concept of **terminal velocity**.

Let's analyse the motion of a skydiver qualitatively, step-by-step:

1. Instant of Release ($t=0$): The skydiver's initial velocity is zero. As drag increases with speed, the air resistance is zero. The only force acting is weight ($W$). The resultant force is $F_{net} = W$, so the initial acceleration is $a = g$.

2. Accelerating Phase: As the skydiver's speed increases, the upward force of air resistance ($D$) also increases. The resultant downward force is now $F_{net} = W - D$. Since $F_{net}$ is decreasing, the acceleration ($a = F_{net}/m$) also decreases. The skydiver is still speeding up, but at a slower rate.

3. Reaching Terminal Velocity: The skydiver continues to accelerate until their speed is high enough that the force of air resistance becomes equal in magnitude to their weight ($D = W$). At this point, the resultant force on the skydiver is zero ($F_{net} = W - D = 0$). According to Newton's First Law, with zero resultant force, the acceleration is zero. The skydiver stops accelerating and falls at a constant, maximum velocity. This is called **terminal velocity**.

Graphical Analysis

The velocity-time graph for an object reaching terminal velocity is a characteristic curve. The gradient, which represents acceleration, starts at a maximum value ($g$) and gradually decreases to zero as the velocity becomes constant.

Worked Example: Forces at Terminal Velocity

A raindrop of mass $5.0 \times 10^{-5}$ kg falls at its terminal velocity. What is the magnitude of the drag force acting on it? (Take $g = 9.81$ m s⁻²).

1. Identify the condition for terminal velocity: At terminal velocity, the net force is zero, which means the upward drag force ($D$) must be equal in magnitude to the downward weight ($W$).

$$ D = W $$

2. Calculate the weight of the raindrop:

$$ W = mg = (5.0 \times 10^{-5} \text{ kg}) \times (9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}) $$

$$ W = 4.905 \times 10^{-4} \text{ N} $$

3. State the drag force:

Since $D=W$, the drag force is $4.9 \times 10^{-4}$ N (to 2 s.f.).
