B8.3 Transpiration

**Transpiration** is the process by which plants lose water vapor, primarily from the surfaces of their leaves. This continuous loss of water creates a transpiration stream that pulls water up from the roots, playing a crucial role in water transport throughout the plant. Understanding transpiration and the factors that affect its rate is essential for comprehending plant physiology.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe transpiration as the loss of water vapour from leaves.
  2. State that water evaporates from the surfaces of the mesophyll cells into the air spaces and then diffuses out of the leaves through the stomata as water vapour.
  3. Investigate and describe the effects of variation of temperature and wind speed on transpiration rate.
  4. Explain the effects on the rate of transpiration of varying the following: temperature, wind speed and humidity.
  5. Explain how and why wilting occurs.

The Process of Transpiration:

Transpiration is essentially the evaporation of water from the plant surface, mainly from the leaves. The process occurs in several steps:

  1. Water is absorbed by the roots and transported up the xylem vessels to the leaves.
  2. In the leaves, water moves from the xylem into the mesophyll cells.
  3. Water then evaporates from the moist surfaces of the **mesophyll cells** into the large **air spaces** within the spongy mesophyll layer of the leaf.
  4. The water vapor then **diffuses out of the leaves** through small pores called **stomata** (singular: stoma), which are mainly located on the lower epidermis.

This continuous loss of water vapor from the leaves creates a "pull" (transpiration pull) that draws water up the xylem from the roots, forming the **transpiration stream**.

Factors Affecting Transpiration Rate:

The rate of transpiration is influenced by several environmental factors:

  • **Temperature:** As temperature increases, water molecules gain more kinetic energy, leading to faster evaporation from mesophyll cell surfaces and faster diffusion out of the stomata. This increases the rate of transpiration.
  • **Wind Speed (Air Movement):** Wind blows away the water vapor that accumulates around the stomata. This maintains a steep concentration gradient of water vapor between the leaf's air spaces and the outside air, increasing the rate of diffusion and thus transpiration.
  • **Humidity:** Humidity refers to the amount of water vapor in the air. * **High humidity:** Means the air is already saturated with water vapor, reducing the concentration gradient between the leaf and the atmosphere. This slows down the rate of diffusion of water vapor out of the stomata, decreasing transpiration. * **Low humidity:** Means the air is dry, creating a steep concentration gradient, which increases the rate of transpiration.
  • **Light Intensity:** (Indirectly) High light intensity increases the rate of photosynthesis, which usually causes stomata to open wider to allow more carbon dioxide in. Wider stomata mean more water vapor can escape, increasing transpiration.

Wilting:

**Wilting** occurs when a plant loses water by transpiration faster than it can absorb water from the soil. This leads to a loss of turgor pressure in the plant cells, particularly in the leaves and stem.

**Why wilting occurs:**

  • When water loss exceeds water uptake, the plant cells become **flaccid** (limp) as their vacuoles lose water and the cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall (plasmolysis).
  • The loss of turgor pressure means the cells no longer push against the cell walls, and the plant loses its rigidity, causing leaves and stems to droop.

Wilting is a protective mechanism; it reduces the surface area exposed to sunlight and wind, thereby reducing the rate of transpiration and conserving water. If water is supplied promptly, the plant can regain turgor and recover.
