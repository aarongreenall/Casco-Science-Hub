C9.3 Alloys and their properties

While pure metals possess many useful properties, their applications can be limited by their softness or reactivity. To overcome these limitations, metals are often mixed with other elements to form **alloys**. Alloys are mixtures that combine the properties of their constituent elements, often resulting in materials that are harder, stronger, or more resistant to corrosion than the pure metals.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe alloys as mixtures of a metal with other elements, including: brass as a mixture of copper and zinc; stainless steel as a mixture of iron and other elements such as chromium, nickel and carbon.
  2. State that alloys can be harder and stronger than the pure metals and are more useful.
  3. Describe the use of alloys in terms of their physical properties, including stainless steel in cutlery because of its hardness and resistance to rusting.
  4. Identify representations of alloys from diagrams of structure.
  5. Explain in terms of structure how alloys can be harder and stronger than the pure metals because the different sized atoms in alloys mean the layers can no longer slide over each other.

What are Alloys?

An **alloy** is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements. These other elements can be other metals or non-metals. The components of an alloy are physically mixed, not chemically bonded in a fixed ratio like in a compound.

Common examples of alloys include:

  • **Brass:** A mixture of copper (metal) and zinc (metal).
  • **Stainless Steel:** A mixture of iron (metal) with other elements like chromium (metal), nickel (metal), and carbon (non-metal).

*Figure: Samples of brass (an alloy) and pure copper, showing their distinct appearances.*

Why are Alloys Harder and Stronger?

Alloys are typically **harder and stronger** than the pure metals from which they are made, making them more useful for many applications. This improved property is explained by their atomic structure:

  • In a pure metal, the atoms are all of the same size and are arranged in regular, orderly layers. These layers can easily slide over one another when a force is applied, which explains why pure metals are malleable and ductile.
  • In an alloy, the added elements usually have **different sized atoms** compared to the main metal. When these different sized atoms are introduced into the metallic lattice, they disrupt the regular arrangement of the layers. This disruption makes it much more difficult for the layers to slide over each other when a force is applied.
  • Consequently, alloys are harder and stronger because the presence of different sized atoms means the layers can no longer slide over each other easily.

*Figure: Comparison of a pure metal's orderly atomic structure (left) versus an alloy's disrupted structure (right), explaining increased hardness.*

Uses of Alloys:

Alloys are extensively used due to their enhanced physical properties:

  • **Stainless Steel in Cutlery:** Stainless steel is widely used for cutlery (knives, forks, spoons) because of its exceptional **hardness** (making it durable and able to hold a sharp edge) and its excellent **resistance to rusting** (corrosion). The chromium in stainless steel forms a protective oxide layer that prevents corrosion.
  • **Bronze (Copper + Tin):** Used for statues, bells, and historical tools due to its hardness and resistance to corrosion.
  • **Solder (Tin + Lead/other metals):** Has a low melting point, making it suitable for joining electrical components.
