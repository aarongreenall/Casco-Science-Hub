C3.3 The mole and the Avogadro constant

In chemistry, atoms and molecules are incredibly small, making it impractical to count them individually. To work with quantities of substances in a practical way, chemists use a unit called the **mole**. The mole provides a bridge between the microscopic world of atoms and molecules and the macroscopic world of laboratory measurements.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State that the mole, mol, is the unit of amount of substance and that one mole contains $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ particles, e.g. atoms, ions, molecules: this number is the Avogadro constant.
  2. Use the relationship amount of substance (mol) = mass (g) / molar mass (g/mol) to calculate: amount of substance, mass, molar mass, relative atomic mass or relative molecular/formula mass.
  3. State that concentration can be measured in g/dm$^3$.
  4. Use the molar gas volume, taken as 24 dm$^3$ at room temperature and pressure, r.t.p. in calculations involving gases.
  5. Calculate stoichiometric reacting masses, limiting reactants, volumes of gases at r.t.p., including conversion between cm$^3$ and dm$^3$.

The Mole and Avogadro's Constant:

The **mole (mol)** is the SI unit for the **amount of substance**. It is defined as the amount of substance that contains exactly **$6.02 \times 10^{23}$ particles**. This enormous number is known as **Avogadro's constant ($N_A$)**. The particles can be atoms, ions, molecules, or any other specified elementary entities.

The mole allows chemists to count particles by weighing them. One mole of any substance has a mass in grams numerically equal to its relative atomic mass ($A_r$), relative molecular mass ($M_r$), or relative formula mass ($M_r$). This is called the **molar mass** (units: g/mol).

*Figure: Visual representation of the mole concept, showing how different substances have different masses for one mole.*

Calculations Involving the Mole:

The relationship between amount of substance (moles), mass, and molar mass is:

$Amount \ of \ substance \ (mol) = \frac{Mass \ (g)}{Molar \ mass \ (g/mol)}$

This equation can be rearranged to calculate mass, molar mass, or relative atomic/molecular/formula mass if the other values are known.

Molar Gas Volume:

For gases, there's a special relationship: one mole of any gas occupies the same volume under the same conditions of temperature and pressure. At **room temperature and pressure (r.t.p.)**, which is typically 20°C and 1 atmosphere, one mole of any gas occupies a volume of **24 dm$^3$**.

This relationship is used in calculations involving gases:

$Amount \ of \ substance \ (mol) = \frac{Volume \ of \ gas \ (dm^3)}{24 \ dm^3/mol}$

Remember that 1 dm$^3$ = 1000 cm$^3$.

Stoichiometric Reacting Masses and Limiting Reactants:

**Stoichiometry** involves using mole ratios from balanced chemical equations to calculate the amounts of reactants and products.

In a chemical reaction, the **limiting reactant** (or limiting reagent) is the reactant that is completely consumed first, thereby limiting the amount of product that can be formed. The other reactants are said to be in excess. Calculations can be performed to identify the limiting reactant and determine the theoretical yield of products.
