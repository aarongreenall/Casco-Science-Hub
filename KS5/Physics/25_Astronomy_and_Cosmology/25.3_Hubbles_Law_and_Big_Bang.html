25.3 Hubble's law and the Big Bang theory

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that the lines in the emission and absorption spectra from distant objects show an increase in wavelength from their known values.
  2. Use $\Delta\lambda / \lambda \approx \Delta f / f \approx v / c$ for the redshift of electromagnetic radiation from a source moving relative to an observer.
  3. Explain why redshift leads to the idea that the Universe is expanding.
  4. Recall and use Hubble’s law $v \approx H_0d$ and explain how this leads to the Big Bang theory (candidates will only be required to use SI units).

LO1 & LO2: Cosmological Redshift

When astronomers observe the light from distant galaxies, they analyse its spectrum. They observe the characteristic absorption or emission lines for known elements like hydrogen. However, they find that these lines are not at the wavelengths measured in a laboratory on Earth. For almost all distant galaxies, the spectral lines are shifted towards the longer-wavelength (red) end of the spectrum. This phenomenon is called **redshift**.

This redshift is caused by the Doppler effect applied to light. It indicates that these distant galaxies are moving away from us. The change in wavelength ($\Delta\lambda$) or frequency ($\Delta f$) is related to the recessional velocity ($v$) of the source by the Doppler equation for electromagnetic radiation:

$$ z = \frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda} \approx \frac{\Delta f}{f} \approx \frac{v}{c} $$

Where $z$ is the redshift (a dimensionless quantity), $\lambda$ is the source wavelength, $f$ is the source frequency, and $c$ is the speed of light. This approximation is valid for velocities much less than the speed of light.

Worked Example: Calculating Recessional Velocity

A specific spectral line for hydrogen has a wavelength of 656.3 nm when measured in a laboratory. When observing a distant galaxy, this same line is found to have a wavelength of 692.1 nm. Calculate the recessional velocity of the galaxy.

1. Calculate the change in wavelength ($\Delta\lambda$):

$$ \Delta\lambda = \lambda_{observed} - \lambda_{source} = 692.1 - 656.3 = 35.8 \text{ nm} $$

2. State the redshift formula and rearrange for $v$:

$$ \frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda} = \frac{v}{c} \implies v = c \left( \frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda} \right) $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ v = (3.00 \times 10^8) \times \left( \frac{35.8 \text{ nm}}{656.3 \text{ nm}} \right) $$

$$ v = (3.00 \times 10^8) \times 0.0545 $$

$$ v = 1.64 \times 10^7 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

LO3 & LO4: The Expanding Universe and the Big Bang Theory

The crucial discovery made by Edwin Hubble in the 1920s was that not only are distant galaxies moving away from us, but the further away a galaxy is, the faster it is moving away. This observation is the cornerstone of modern cosmology.

The redshift of distant galaxies is not because they are flying through space away from a central point. Instead, it is evidence that **space itself is expanding**. This is a difficult concept to visualize, but a common analogy is the surface of an expanding balloon with dots drawn on it. As the balloon inflates, every dot moves away from every other dot, and dots that are further apart move away from each other faster.

Hubble's Law

Hubble's observations are summarised in **Hubble's Law**:

$$ v \approx H_0 d $$

Where $v$ is the galaxy's recessional velocity, $d$ is its distance from us, and $H_0$ is the **Hubble constant**. The currently accepted value for $H_0$ is approximately $2.2 \times 10^{-18}$ s⁻¹.

Evidence for the Big Bang Theory

Hubble's Law provides the primary evidence for the **Big Bang theory**. If all galaxies are currently moving away from each other, it implies that in the past, they must have been much closer together. If we "rewind the clock" on the expansion, all the matter and energy in the universe must have originated from a single, incredibly hot and dense point. The expansion of space from this initial singularity is what we call the Big Bang. The age of the Universe can be estimated as the reciprocal of the Hubble constant ($t \approx 1/H_0$).

Worked Example: Age of the Universe

Use the Hubble constant, $H_0 = 2.2 \times 10^{-18}$ s⁻¹, to estimate the age of the Universe in years.

1. State the relationship between age and the Hubble constant:

$$ t \approx \frac{1}{H_0} $$

2. Calculate the age in seconds:

$$ t \approx \frac{1}{2.2 \times 10^{-18} \text{ s}^{-1}} = 4.55 \times 10^{17} \text{ s} $$

3. Convert the age from seconds to years:

Number of seconds in a year = $365 \times 24 \times 60 \times 60 = 3.15 \times 10^7$ s.

$$ t \approx \frac{4.55 \times 10^{17} \text{ s}}{3.15 \times 10^7 \text{ s/year}} = 1.44 \times 10^{10} \text{ years} $$

This is approximately 14.4 billion years, which is in good agreement with more precise modern measurements.
