B15.4 Sexual reproduction in humans

**Sexual reproduction in humans** is a complex biological process that involves the fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote, leading to the development of genetically unique offspring. This process ensures genetic variation within the human population, which is vital for adaptation and survival. Understanding the structures of the male and female reproductive systems, the processes of puberty, menstruation, fertilization, and fetal development is crucial for comprehending human life cycles.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Identify in diagrams and images the main parts of the male reproductive system, limited to: testes, scrotum, sperm ducts, prostate gland, urethra, penis.
  2. State the functions of the structures listed in 15.4.1.
  3. Identify in diagrams and images the main parts of the female reproductive system, limited to: ovaries, oviducts (fallopian tubes), uterus, cervix, vagina.
  4. State the functions of the structures listed in 15.4.3.
  5. Describe the roles of oestrogen and progesterone in the menstrual cycle, limited to: oestrogen – causes rebuilding of uterus lining and stimulates LH; progesterone – maintains uterus lining.

  8. Describe the functions of the placenta and umbilical cord.
  9. Describe the advantages and disadvantages of: breast-feeding; bottle-feeding.
  10. Describe the advantages and disadvantages of: natural methods of birth control; chemical methods of birth control, limited to oral contraceptives; mechanical methods of birth control, limited to condoms; surgical methods of birth control, limited to vasectomy and female sterilisation.

Human Reproductive Systems:

Male Reproductive System:

The male reproductive system is adapted for the production and delivery of sperm (male gametes).

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the main parts of the male reproductive system.*

  • **Testes (singular: testis):** Produce sperm and male hormones (e.g., testosterone).
  • **Scrotum:** Sac that holds the testes outside the body, keeping them at an optimal temperature for sperm production.
  • **Sperm ducts (vas deferens):** Tubes that carry sperm from the testes to the urethra.
  • **Prostate gland:** Secretes fluid that nourishes and protects sperm, forming semen.
  • **Urethra:** Tube that carries urine and semen out of the body through the penis.
  • **Penis:** External organ adapted for sexual intercourse and urination.

Female Reproductive System:

The female reproductive system is adapted for the production of egg cells (female gametes), fertilization, and supporting fetal development.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the main parts of the female reproductive system.*

  • **Ovaries:** Produce egg cells and female hormones (e.g., oestrogen, progesterone).
  • **Oviducts (Fallopian tubes):** Tubes that carry egg cells from the ovaries to the uterus. Site of fertilization.
  • **Uterus (womb):** Muscular organ where a fertilized egg implants and develops into a fetus.
  • **Cervix:** Narrow opening at the base of the uterus, leading to the vagina.
  • **Vagina:** Muscular tube connecting the cervix to the outside of the body; receives sperm during sexual intercourse.

Menstrual Cycle and Hormones:

The **menstrual cycle** is a monthly cycle of changes in the female reproductive system, preparing the uterus for a possible pregnancy. It is controlled by hormones, primarily **oestrogen** and **progesterone**.

  • **Oestrogen:** Produced by the ovaries. Causes the rebuilding of the uterus lining after menstruation and stimulates the release of luteinizing hormone (LH), which triggers ovulation.
  • **Progesterone:** Produced by the corpus luteum (in the ovary) after ovulation. Maintains the uterus lining, making it thick and rich in blood vessels, ready for implantation. If pregnancy does not occur, progesterone levels fall, and the lining breaks down (menstruation).

Fertilisation and Early Development:

**Fertilisation** is the **fusion of the nucleus of the sperm (male gamete) and the nucleus of the egg cell (female gamete)**, typically occurring in the oviduct. This forms a **zygote**.

The early development of the zygote involves rapid cell division:

`Zygote -> Embryo -> Fetus`

  • The **zygote** undergoes cell division (mitosis) to form a ball of cells.
  • This ball of cells develops into an **embryo** (from implantation until about 8 weeks).
  • After 8 weeks, the developing human is called a **fetus**.

Placenta and Umbilical Cord:

During pregnancy, the **placenta** and **umbilical cord** are vital structures:

  • **Placenta:** A specialized organ that develops in the uterus during pregnancy. * **Functions:** Allows for the exchange of oxygen, nutrients, antibodies, and waste products (like urea, carbon dioxide) between the mother's blood and the fetus's blood. It also produces hormones to maintain pregnancy. *Note: Mother's and fetus's blood do not mix directly.*
  • **Umbilical Cord:** Connects the fetus to the placenta. * **Functions:** Contains blood vessels that transport oxygen and nutrients from the placenta to the fetus, and waste products from the fetus back to the placenta.

Feeding Choices and Birth Control:

Breast-feeding vs. Bottle-feeding:

  • **Breast-feeding (Advantages):** Provides natural antibodies to the baby (passive immunity), ideal nutrient composition, promotes bonding, reduces risk of allergies, free, helps mother lose weight.
  • **Breast-feeding (Disadvantages):** Can be challenging for some mothers, requires mother's presence, difficult to measure intake precisely.
  • **Bottle-feeding (Advantages):** Allows others to feed the baby, easier to measure intake, allows mother to return to work.
  • **Bottle-feeding (Disadvantages):** No antibodies from mother, formula can be expensive, requires sterilization, less bonding (potentially).

Methods of Birth Control:

  • **Natural Methods:** (e.g., rhythm method, withdrawal) Rely on tracking fertility cycles or withdrawal before ejaculation. Less reliable.
  • **Chemical Methods (Oral Contraceptives - "The Pill"):** Contain hormones (oestrogen and/or progesterone) that prevent ovulation, thicken cervical mucus, and thin the uterus lining. Highly effective if used correctly, but do not protect against STIs.
  • **Mechanical Methods (Condoms):** Barrier methods that prevent sperm from reaching the egg. Condoms (male and female) are the only method that also protect against STIs.
  • **Surgical Methods:** Permanent methods. * **Vasectomy (male sterilisation):** Sperm ducts are cut and tied. * **Female sterilisation (tubal ligation):** Oviducts are cut and tied.
