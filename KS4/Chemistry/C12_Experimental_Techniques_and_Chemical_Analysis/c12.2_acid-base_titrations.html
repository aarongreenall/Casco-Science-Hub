C12.2 Acid-base titrations

**Acid-base titrations** are precise quantitative analytical techniques used to determine the unknown concentration of an acid or a base. This method relies on the neutralization reaction between an acid and a base, where a solution of known concentration (the titrant) is gradually added to a solution of unknown concentration (the analyte) until the reaction is complete.

Titration Apparatus and Procedure:

An acid-base titration typically involves the following apparatus:

  • **Burette:** A long, graduated glass tube with a tap at the bottom, used to deliver variable, precise volumes of the titrant (solution of known concentration).
  • **Volumetric Pipette:** A glass tube designed to deliver a single, fixed, very accurate volume of the analyte (solution of unknown concentration) into a conical flask.
  • **Conical Flask:** Used to hold the analyte and indicator.
  • **Retort Stand and Clamp:** To hold the burette vertically.
  • **White Tile:** Placed under the conical flask to make the color change of the indicator more visible.
  • **Suitable Indicator:** A substance that changes color sharply at or near the equivalence point (the point where the acid and base have completely reacted). Common indicators include phenolphthalein and methyl orange.

Identifying the End-Point:

The **end-point** of a titration is the point at which the indicator changes color, signaling that the reaction is complete (or very nearly complete). The choice of indicator depends on the strength of the acid and base being titrated to ensure its color change occurs as close as possible to the equivalence point.

**Procedure for a typical acid-base titration:**

  1. A known volume of the analyte (e.g., alkali) is accurately measured into a conical flask using a volumetric pipette.
  2. A few drops of a suitable indicator are added to the conical flask.
  3. The burette is filled with the titrant (e.g., acid) of known concentration, and the initial reading is recorded.
  4. The titrant is slowly added from the burette to the conical flask, with constant swirling, until the indicator just changes color. This is the end-point.
  5. The final burette reading is recorded, and the volume of titrant used (titer) is calculated.
  6. The titration is repeated multiple times (concordant results, typically within $\pm 0.1 \text{ cm}^3$) to ensure accuracy, and an average titer is calculated.
