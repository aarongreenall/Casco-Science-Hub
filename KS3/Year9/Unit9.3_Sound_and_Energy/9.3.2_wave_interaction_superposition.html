9.3.2 Wave Interaction (Superposition)

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain the principle of superposition and how it leads to constructive and destructive interference.

Principle of Superposition:

  • When two or more waves meet at a point, the resultant displacement at that point is equal to the vector sum of the displacements of the individual waves.
  • In simple terms, the waves add up or cancel each other out.

Types of Interference:

  • Constructive Interference:
    • Occurs when the crests (or troughs) of two waves align.
    • The amplitudes of the waves add together to produce a wave with a larger amplitude.
    • In sound, this results in a louder sound.
  • Destructive Interference:
    • Occurs when the crest of one wave aligns with the trough of another wave.
    • The amplitudes cancel each other out. If the original waves had equal amplitudes, the resultant displacement is zero.
    • In sound, this results in a quieter sound or silence.

Applications:

  • Noise-cancelling headphones: They detect the surrounding sound waves and produce an "anti-sound" wave that is perfectly out of phase. This creates destructive interference, cancelling out the external noise.
  • Musical instruments: The complex sounds produced are a result of the superposition of many different waves (harmonics).
