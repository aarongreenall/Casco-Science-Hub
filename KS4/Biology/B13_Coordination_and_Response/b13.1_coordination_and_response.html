B13.1 Coordination and response

Living organisms constantly interact with their environment and must be able to detect changes and respond appropriately to ensure their survival. This ability is facilitated by **coordination and response systems**, which involve communication between different parts of the organism. In humans, the nervous system and the endocrine (hormonal) system are the primary coordinating systems.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the nervous system in terms of: central nervous system (CNS) – brain and spinal cord; peripheral nervous system (PNS) – nerves.
  2. Describe the functions of: neurones – conduction of electrical impulses; sensory neurones – transmit impulses from receptors to the CNS; relay neurones – transmit impulses within the CNS; motor neurones – transmit impulses from the CNS to effectors.
  3. Describe the structure of a sensory neurone and a motor neurone, limited to: cell body, dendron, dendrites, axon, myelin sheath, Schwann cells, nodes of Ranvier, axon terminals.
  4. Describe the components of a reflex arc, limited to: receptor, sensory neurone, relay neurone, motor neurone, effector.
  5. Describe a reflex action as a means of automatically and rapidly integrating and coordinating stimuli with responses.
  6. State that the nervous system coordinates and regulates body functions.
  7. Describe the eye as a sense organ, limited to: cornea, iris, pupil, lens, retina, optic nerve, suspensory ligaments, ciliary bodies.
  8. Describe the functions of the structures listed in 13.1.7, limited to: cornea – refracts light; iris – controls pupil diameter and thus the amount of light entering the eye; pupil – hole in the centre of the iris through which light enters; lens – changes shape to refract light and focus it onto the retina; retina – contains light-sensitive cells; optic nerve – transmits electrical impulses from the retina to the brain; suspensory ligaments and ciliary bodies – control the shape of the lens.

The Nervous System:

The **nervous system** is a complex network of specialized cells (neurons) that transmits signals between different parts of the body. It is responsible for coordinating and regulating most body functions, enabling rapid responses to stimuli. It is divided into two main parts:

  • **Central Nervous System (CNS):** Consists of the **brain** and **spinal cord**. It is the processing center for all information.
  • **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):** Consists of **nerves** that extend from the CNS to all parts of the body, carrying signals to and from the CNS.

*Figure: Diagram of the human nervous system, showing the CNS and PNS.*

Neurones (Nerve Cells):

**Neurones** are specialized cells that transmit electrical impulses (nerve impulses) throughout the nervous system. There are three main types of neurones:

  • **Sensory Neurones:** Transmit impulses from **receptors** (e.g., in skin, eyes, ears) to the CNS. Their cell body is usually located off to the side of the main axon.
  • **Relay Neurones (Interneurones):** Transmit impulses **within the CNS** (between sensory and motor neurones).
  • **Motor Neurones:** Transmit impulses from the CNS to **effectors** (muscles or glands), causing a response. Their cell body is typically at one end, with a long axon extending to the effector.

Common structures of neurones include:

  • **Cell body:** Contains the nucleus and most of the cytoplasm.
  • **Dendron/Dendrites:** Branching extensions that receive impulses from other neurones.
  • **Axon:** A long extension that transmits impulses away from the cell body.
  • **Myelin sheath:** A fatty insulating layer around some axons, formed by **Schwann cells**, which speeds up impulse transmission. Gaps in the myelin sheath are called **nodes of Ranvier**.
  • **Axon terminals:** Endings of the axon that transmit impulses to other neurones or effectors.

*Figure: Diagrams of a sensory neurone and a motor neurone, showing their structures.*

Reflex Actions and Reflex Arcs:

A **reflex action** is an automatic, rapid, involuntary response to a stimulus that does not involve conscious thought. It is a means of automatically and rapidly integrating and coordinating stimuli with responses, providing quick protection from harm.

A **reflex arc** is the neural pathway that mediates a reflex action. It typically involves five components:

  1. **Receptor:** Detects the stimulus (e.g., pain receptors in the skin).
  2. **Sensory neurone:** Transmits the electrical impulse from the receptor to the CNS (spinal cord).
  3. **Relay neurone:** Located within the CNS, it transmits the impulse from the sensory neurone to the motor neurone.
  4. **Motor neurone:** Transmits the electrical impulse from the CNS to the effector.
  5. **Effector:** A muscle or gland that carries out the response (e.g., a muscle contracting to withdraw a hand from a hot object).

The Eye as a Sense Organ:

The **eye** is a specialized sense organ adapted to detect light and form images. Its main structures and their functions are:

*Figure: A diagram of the human eye, showing its main structures.*

  • **Cornea:** The transparent outer layer at the front of the eye. Its curved shape **refracts (bends) light** to focus it onto the retina.
  • **Iris:** The colored part of the eye. It is a muscular diaphragm that **controls the diameter of the pupil** and thus the amount of light entering the eye.
  • **Pupil:** The hole in the center of the iris through which light enters the eye. Its size changes to regulate light intensity.
  • **Lens:** A transparent, biconvex structure behind the iris. It changes shape (due to the action of suspensory ligaments and ciliary bodies) to **refract light** and **focus it onto the retina**.
  • **Retina:** The light-sensitive layer at the back of the eye. It contains photoreceptor cells (rods and cones) that detect light.
  • **Optic Nerve:** Transmits electrical impulses generated by the retina to the brain for interpretation.
  • **Suspensory Ligaments and Ciliary Bodies:** Structures that work together to **control the shape of the lens**, allowing the eye to focus on objects at different distances (accommodation).
