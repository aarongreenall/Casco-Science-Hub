7.5.2 Acidity and alkalinity (pH)

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define acids and alkalis based on their properties.
  2. Describe the pH scale as a measure of acidity and alkalinity.
  3. Give examples of common acidic, alkaline, and neutral substances.

Acids and Alkalis

Many common substances we use every day can be classified as either an **acid** or an **alkali**. An alkali is a base that can dissolve in water. Substances that are neither acidic nor alkaline are called **neutral**. We can identify them by their chemical properties:

  • Acids: These are substances that react with metals to produce hydrogen gas, and react with carbonates (like limestone) to produce carbon dioxide gas. Common examples include lemon juice (citric acid) and vinegar (acetic acid).
  • Alkalis: These are substances that can neutralise acids. Common examples include baking soda, soap, and bleach.

Analogy: A Chemical Tug-of-War

You can think of acidity and alkalinity as a kind of chemical tug-of-war. **Acids** are like a team pulling strongly in one direction. **Alkalis** are like a team pulling strongly in the *opposite* direction. A **neutral** substance like pure water is when the two teams are pulling with equal strength, resulting in a perfect balance.

The pH Scale

To measure how acidic or alkaline a substance is, scientists use the **pH scale**. The scale runs from 0 to 14.

  • A pH **less than 7** indicates an **acidic** substance. The lower the number, the stronger the acid.
  • A pH **of exactly 7** indicates a **neutral** substance.
  • A pH **greater than 7** indicates an **alkaline** substance. The higher the number, the stronger the alkali.

In-School Experiment: Measuring pH

You can measure the pH of different household liquids using a special chemical called a **universal indicator**. It changes colour depending on the pH of the substance it is mixed with.

Equipment:
  • A spotting tile or a set of test tubes
  • Samples of different safe liquids (e.g., lemon juice, vinegar, water, salt water, baking soda solution, soap solution)
  • Universal indicator solution (or paper) and a pH colour chart
  • Dropping pipettes
Method:
  1. Place a few drops of each liquid into a separate dimple of the spotting tile or a separate test tube.
  2. Add a drop of universal indicator solution to each liquid (or dip a piece of universal indicator paper in).
  3. Observe the colour change for each substance.
  4. Compare the colour to the pH colour chart to determine the approximate pH of each liquid.
  5. Record your results in a table and classify each substance as an acid, an alkali, or neutral.
