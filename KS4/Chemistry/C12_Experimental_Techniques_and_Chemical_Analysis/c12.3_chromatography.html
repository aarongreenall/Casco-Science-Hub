C12.3 Chromatography

**Chromatography** is a powerful analytical technique used to separate mixtures. It relies on the differential distribution of components between a stationary phase and a mobile phase. Paper chromatography is a simple and effective method for separating soluble colored substances, helping to identify unknown compounds and assess purity.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe how paper chromatography is used to separate mixtures of soluble coloured substances, using a suitable solvent.
  2. Interpret simple chromatograms to identify: unknown substances by comparison with known substances; pure and impure substances.
  3. State and use the equation for Rf: Rf = distance travelled by substance / distance travelled by solvent.

Principles of Paper Chromatography:

**Paper chromatography** is a separation technique used for mixtures of soluble colored substances (like inks or food colorings). It involves two phases:

  • **Stationary Phase:** A piece of absorbent paper (e.g., filter paper).
  • **Mobile Phase:** A suitable solvent (e.g., water, ethanol) that moves up the paper.

The process:

  1. A spot of the mixture is placed on a pencil line (baseline) near the bottom of the chromatography paper.
  2. The bottom edge of the paper is dipped into a small amount of solvent, ensuring the solvent level is below the baseline.
  3. The solvent (mobile phase) moves up the paper by capillary action.
  4. As the solvent passes the spot, the components of the mixture dissolve and are carried up the paper.
  5. Different components travel at different speeds depending on their solubility in the solvent and their attraction to the paper (stationary phase). Components that are more soluble in the solvent and less attracted to the paper will travel further up the paper.
  6. This separates the mixture into distinct spots on the chromatogram.

Interpreting Chromatograms and Rf Value:

A **chromatogram** is the result of a chromatography experiment (the developed paper with separated spots).

  • **Identifying Unknown Substances:** By running known substances alongside an unknown mixture, an unknown substance can be identified if its spot travels the same distance as a known substance.
  • **Pure vs. Impure Substances:** * A **pure substance** will produce only **one spot** on a chromatogram (assuming it's soluble in the solvent). * An **impure substance** (a mixture) will produce **multiple spots**.

The **retardation factor ($R_f$)** value is a ratio used to characterize and identify substances in chromatography. It is defined as:

$R_f = \frac{\text{distance travelled by substance}}{\text{distance travelled by solvent}}$

The $R_f$ value is always between 0 and 1 (as the substance cannot travel further than the solvent) and has no units. It is constant for a given substance and solvent under specific conditions.
