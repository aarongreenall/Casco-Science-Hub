C1.2 Diffusion

Diffusion is a fundamental process in chemistry and biology that describes the net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration. This movement occurs as a result of the random motion of particles and continues until the particles are evenly distributed, reaching a state of equilibrium. Understanding diffusion is crucial for explaining phenomena such as the spread of odors, the absorption of nutrients in living organisms, and gas exchange.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe diffusion as the net movement of particles from a region of their higher concentration to a region of their lower concentration (i.e. down a concentration gradient), as a result of their random movement.
  2. Describe and explain the effect of relative molecular mass on the rate of diffusion of gases.

Understanding Diffusion:

**Diffusion** is the process by which particles spread out from an area where they are in high concentration to an area where they are in low concentration. This movement is driven by the **random motion of particles**. All particles (atoms, molecules, ions) are in constant, random motion. Over time, these random movements lead to a net movement from areas of high concentration to areas of low concentration, until the particles are uniformly distributed throughout the available space. This is referred to as moving **down a concentration gradient**.

*Figure: An animation illustrating the process of diffusion, showing particles spreading out from a region of high concentration.*

Factors Affecting Diffusion Rate:

The rate of diffusion is influenced by several factors:

  • **Concentration Gradient:** A steeper concentration gradient (larger difference in concentration between two regions) leads to a faster rate of diffusion.
  • **Temperature:** Higher temperature means particles have more kinetic energy and move faster, leading to a faster rate of diffusion.
  • **Surface Area:** A larger surface area allows more particles to move across a boundary, increasing the rate of diffusion.
  • **Distance:** A shorter distance over which diffusion occurs leads to a faster rate.
  • **Relative Molecular Mass (for gases):** This is a particularly important factor for gases. Lighter gas particles (those with a smaller relative molecular mass) move faster on average than heavier gas particles at the same temperature. Therefore, lighter gases diffuse faster than heavier gases.

Experiment Demonstration: Diffusion of Gases (Ammonia and Hydrogen Chloride):

This classic experiment demonstrates the effect of relative molecular mass on the rate of diffusion. Ammonia gas (lighter) and hydrogen chloride gas (heavier) are released at opposite ends of a long tube. A white ring of ammonium chloride forms closer to the hydrogen chloride end, indicating that the lighter ammonia diffused faster.
