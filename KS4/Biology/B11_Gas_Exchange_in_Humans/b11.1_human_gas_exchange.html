B11.1 Human gas exchange

**Gas exchange** in humans is the vital process by which oxygen is taken into the body from the atmosphere and carbon dioxide is released from the body into the atmosphere. This occurs in the lungs, specifically in tiny air sacs called alveoli, which are highly adapted for efficient diffusion of gases.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Identify in diagrams and images the main structures of the human gas exchange system, limited to: trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, lungs, alveoli, diaphragm, ribs, intercostal muscles.
  2. Describe the functions of the structures listed in 11.1.1.
  3. Describe the process of gas exchange in the alveoli in terms of diffusion of oxygen and carbon dioxide between the alveoli and the capillaries.
  4. Explain the adaptations of the alveoli for efficient gas exchange, limited to: large total surface area; thin walls; rich blood supply; moist surfaces.
  5. Describe the effect of physical activity on the rate and depth of breathing.
  6. Explain the effect of physical activity on the rate and depth of breathing in terms of the body’s need for increased energy and the removal of carbon dioxide.
  7. Describe the effects of smoking on the gas exchange system, limited to: tar – causes cancer and increased mucus production; nicotine – addictive and causes increased heart rate and blood pressure; carbon monoxide – reduces oxygen transport by red blood cells.

Structures of the Human Gas Exchange System:

The human gas exchange system, also known as the respiratory system, includes:

*Figure: A diagram illustrating the main structures of the human gas exchange system.*

  • **Trachea (windpipe):** A tube supported by rings of cartilage, carrying air from the nose/mouth to the bronchi.
  • **Bronchi (singular: bronchus):** Two tubes branching off the trachea, one leading to each lung.
  • **Bronchioles:** Smaller tubes branching off the bronchi, leading to the alveoli.
  • **Lungs:** Main organs of gas exchange, containing millions of alveoli.
  • **Alveoli (singular: alveolus):** Tiny air sacs in the lungs where gas exchange occurs.
  • **Diaphragm:** A large, dome-shaped muscle at the base of the lungs, crucial for breathing.
  • **Ribs:** Bones that form the rib cage, protecting the lungs and aiding breathing movements.
  • **Intercostal muscles:** Muscles between the ribs that help with breathing movements.

Gas Exchange in the Alveoli:

Gas exchange occurs by **diffusion** in the alveoli.

  • **Oxygen:** Diffuses from the air in the alveoli (high oxygen concentration) into the blood in the surrounding capillaries (low oxygen concentration).
  • **Carbon Dioxide:** Diffuses from the blood in the capillaries (high carbon dioxide concentration) into the air in the alveoli (low carbon dioxide concentration), to be exhaled.

*Figure: Diagram showing gas exchange between an alveolus and a capillary.*

Adaptations of the Alveoli for Efficient Gas Exchange:

The alveoli are highly adapted for maximum efficiency of gas exchange:

  • **Large Total Surface Area:** Millions of alveoli provide a very large total surface area for diffusion (about the size of a tennis court).
  • **Thin Walls:** The walls of the alveoli and the capillaries are both only one cell thick, providing a very short diffusion distance for gases.
  • **Rich Blood Supply:** A dense network of capillaries surrounds each alveolus, maintaining a steep concentration gradient for both oxygen and carbon dioxide. Blood arriving at the lungs is deoxygenated, and blood leaving is oxygenated.
  • **Moist Surfaces:** The inner surfaces of the alveoli are covered in a thin layer of moisture, allowing gases to dissolve before diffusing across the membrane.

Effect of Physical Activity on Breathing:

During **physical activity**, muscles respire more rapidly to produce increased energy (ATP). This increased respiration requires more oxygen and produces more carbon dioxide.

To meet these demands, the **rate and depth of breathing increase**. This ensures:

  • More oxygen is taken into the blood (to supply respiring muscles).
  • More carbon dioxide (a waste product of respiration) is removed from the blood.

Effects of Smoking on the Gas Exchange System:

Smoking has severe adverse effects on the gas exchange system:

  • **Tar:** A sticky, black substance that: * Accumulates in the lungs, paralyzing and destroying cilia (leading to "smoker's cough"). * Causes cancer (carcinogenic). * Increases mucus production, leading to blockages and infections.
  • **Nicotine:** * An addictive drug, making it hard to stop smoking. * Increases heart rate and blood pressure, putting strain on the cardiovascular system.
  • **Carbon Monoxide (CO):** * A toxic gas that reduces oxygen transport by red blood cells. Carbon monoxide binds irreversibly to haemoglobin (forming carboxyhaemoglobin) much more readily than oxygen, reducing the blood's oxygen-carrying capacity. This puts strain on the heart and can lead to oxygen deprivation in tissues.
