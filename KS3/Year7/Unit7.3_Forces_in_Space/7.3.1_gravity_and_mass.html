7.3.1 Gravity and Mass

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define mass as the amount of matter in an object.
  2. Define weight as the force of gravity on an object.
  3. Explain that an object's mass is constant, but its weight can change.

What's the Difference Between Mass and Weight?

In everyday language, we often use the words "mass" and "weight" as if they mean the same thing, but in science, they are very different!

  • Mass is the amount of "stuff" or matter an object is made of. It is measured in kilograms (kg). Your mass is the same whether you are on Earth, on the Moon, or floating in space.
  • Weight is a force caused by gravity pulling on an object's mass. Because it's a force, it is measured in newtons (N). Your weight depends on how strong the gravity is, so you would weigh much less on the Moon than on Earth!

Analogy: The Bowling Ball and the Tennis Ball

Imagine you have a bowling ball and a tennis ball. On Earth, the bowling ball has a large mass and feels heavy (it has a large weight). The tennis ball has a small mass and feels light (it has a small weight). If you took both balls to the Moon, where gravity is much weaker, their mass (the amount of "stuff" they are made of) would be exactly the same. However, they would both feel much lighter because the Moon's gravity isn't pulling on them as strongly. Their weight would have changed, but their mass would not.

Calculating Weight

We can calculate the weight of an object using a simple formula. The strength of gravity in a particular location is called the **gravitational field strength (g)**. On Earth, g is approximately 10 N/kg.

$$ \text{Weight (N)} = \text{mass (kg)} \times \text{gravitational field strength (N/kg)} $$

$$ W = mg $$

Worked Example: Your Weight on Earth

A student has a mass of 50 kg. Calculate their weight on Earth, where the gravitational field strength is 9.8 N/kg.

1. State the formula:

$$ W = mg $$

2. Substitute the values:

$$ W = 50 \text{ kg} \times 9.8 \text{ N/kg} $$

3. Calculate the answer:

$$ W = 490 \text{ N} $$
