C7.1 The characteristic properties of acids and bases

Acids and bases are fundamental chemical substances with distinct properties that play crucial roles in countless chemical reactions, from industrial processes to biological systems. Understanding their characteristic behaviors and how to distinguish them is essential in chemistry.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the characteristic properties of acids in terms of their reactions with: metals, bases, carbonates.
  2. Describe the characteristic properties of bases in terms of their reactions with acids.
  3. State that bases are oxides or hydroxides of metals and that alkalis are soluble bases.
  4. Describe how to compare neutrality, relative acidity and relative alkalinity in terms of colour and pH using universal indicator.
  5. Describe the neutralisation reaction between an acid and an alkali to produce a salt and water (the ionic equation for this reaction is not required).
  6. Describe acids in terms of their effects on: litmus, methyl orange.
  7. Describe alkalis in terms of their effect on: litmus, methyl orange.

Properties of Acids:

Acids are substances that produce hydrogen ions (H$^+$) when dissolved in water. Their characteristic properties include:

  • **Taste:** Sour (e.g., lemon juice). *Note: Never taste chemicals in the lab.*
  • **Effect on Indicators:** Turn blue litmus paper red, turn methyl orange red.
  • **Reactions with Metals:** Acids react with reactive metals to produce a salt and hydrogen gas. * Example: `Magnesium + Hydrochloric Acid -> Magnesium Chloride + Hydrogen` * `Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)`
  • **Reactions with Bases (Neutralization):** Acids react with bases to produce a salt and water. This is a neutralization reaction. * Example: `Hydrochloric Acid + Sodium Hydroxide -> Sodium Chloride + Water` * `HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) -> NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)`
  • **Reactions with Carbonates:** Acids react with carbonates to produce a salt, water, and carbon dioxide gas. * Example: `Hydrochloric Acid + Calcium Carbonate -> Calcium Chloride + Water + Carbon Dioxide` * `2HCl(aq) + CaCO3(s) -> CaCl2(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g)`

*Figure: Diagram showing an acid reacting with a metal, producing hydrogen gas.*

Properties of Bases and Alkalis:

**Bases** are substances that can neutralize acids. They are typically metal oxides or metal hydroxides.

**Alkalis** are bases that are soluble in water. They produce hydroxide ions (OH$^-$) when dissolved in water. All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis.

*Figure: A Venn diagram illustrating the relationship between bases and alkalis.*

Characteristic properties of bases (and alkalis):

  • **Taste:** Bitter (e.g., soap). *Note: Never taste chemicals in the lab.*
  • **Feel:** Soapy or slippery.
  • **Effect on Indicators:** Turn red litmus paper blue, turn methyl orange yellow.
  • **Reactions with Acids (Neutralization):** Bases react with acids to produce a salt and water.

Neutrality, Acidity, Alkalinity, and Indicators:

The **pH scale** is used to measure the acidity or alkalinity of a solution. It ranges from 0 to 14:

  • **Acidic solutions:** pH less than 7.
  • **Neutral solutions:** pH exactly 7 (e.g., pure water).
  • **Alkaline (basic) solutions:** pH greater than 7.

**Universal indicator** is a mixture of dyes that changes color over a wide range of pH values, allowing us to estimate the pH of a solution and determine its relative acidity, neutrality, or alkalinity.

*Figure: The pH scale showing corresponding colors of universal indicator.*
