9.3.6 Cooling by Evaporation

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Explain cooling by evaporation.

What is Evaporation?

**Evaporation** is the process where a liquid turns into a gas (or vapour) at a temperature **below its boiling point**. You see it all the time when a puddle dries up on a sunny day. The water doesn't need to boil to disappear; it evaporates.

This happens because the particles in a liquid are all moving at different speeds. The fastest-moving particles, which have the most kinetic energy, can escape from the surface of the liquid and become gas particles.

Analogy: Energetic Kids on a Playground

Imagine the particles in a liquid are like a group of kids running around a playground. Most are just jogging, but a few are sprinting with lots of energy. These super-fast, energetic kids (the 'hottest' particles) might be able to jump right over the playground fence and escape. The kids left behind are, on average, the slower, less energetic ones.

The Cooling Effect Explained

Here is the crucial part: since it's the most energetic ("hottest") particles that escape during evaporation, the **average** kinetic energy of the particles left behind in the liquid decreases.

Remember that temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles. So, if the average energy drops, the **temperature of the remaining liquid also drops**. This is why **evaporation is a cooling process**. The liquid uses its own thermal energy to turn some of its particles into a gas, leaving the rest of the liquid cooler.

Real-World Examples of Evaporative Cooling

You experience this cooling effect every day!

Sweating

When you exercise, your body produces sweat. This liquid evaporates from your skin, taking heat energy from your body with it and helping you to cool down.

Leaving a Pool

You feel shivery and cold when you get out of a swimming pool, even on a hot day. This is because the layer of water on your skin is rapidly evaporating, drawing a lot of heat from your body.

Hand Sanitiser

Alcohol-based hand sanitiser feels very cold on your hands. Alcohol is more volatile than water (it evaporates more easily), so it removes heat from your skin much faster, creating a strong cooling sensation.

In-School Investigation: The Evaporation Race

You can feel the difference in evaporation rates for yourself.

You will need:

  • A small amount of water
  • A small amount of rubbing alcohol (or alcohol-based hand sanitiser)
  • Two droppers

Procedure:

  1. Place a single drop of water on the back of one hand.
  2. Place a single drop of rubbing alcohol on the back of your other hand.
  3. Gently blow on both hands at the same time. Which hand feels colder?

Observation and Explanation:
