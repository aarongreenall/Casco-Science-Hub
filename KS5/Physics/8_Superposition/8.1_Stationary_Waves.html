8.1 Stationary Waves

Learning Objectives:

  1. Explain and use the principle of superposition.
  2. Show an understanding of experiments that demonstrate stationary waves using microwaves, stretched strings and air columns.
  3. Explain the formation of a stationary wave using a graphical method, and identify nodes and antinodes.
  4. Understand how wavelength may be determined from the positions of nodes or antinodes of a stationary wave.

LO1: The Principle of Superposition

The **Principle of Superposition** states that when two or more waves of the same type meet at a point, the resultant displacement at that point is the vector sum of the individual displacements of the waves. This leads to constructive interference (displacements add up) and destructive interference (displacements cancel out).

LO3: Formation of Stationary Waves - A Graphical Method

A **stationary (or standing) wave** is formed from the superposition of two progressive waves of the same frequency, wavelength, and amplitude, travelling in opposite directions. Unlike a progressive wave, a stationary wave does not transfer energy. It has points of zero amplitude, called **nodes (N)**, and points of maximum amplitude, called **antinodes (A)**.

We can understand how this pattern forms by graphically adding the two waves at different moments in time. Let's consider a blue wave moving right and a red wave moving left. The purple wave is their resultant.

  • At time t=0: The waves are perfectly in phase. Crest meets crest, and trough meets trough. This creates antinodes with maximum positive and negative displacement.
  • At time t=T/4: Each wave has travelled a quarter of a wavelength. A crest from one wave now meets a trough from the other. They completely cancel out, resulting in zero displacement everywhere.
  • At time t=T/2: The waves are in phase again, but inverted compared to t=0. Trough meets trough and crest meets crest, creating antinodes with maximum negative and positive displacement.
  • At time t=3T/4: The waves again cancel out completely, resulting in zero displacement everywhere.

Notice that the points marked 'N' (nodes) never move, while the points marked 'A' (antinodes) oscillate between the maximum positive and negative amplitudes.

LO2 & LO4: Demonstrating and Measuring Stationary Waves

Stationary waves can be demonstrated in various systems. A key measurement is the distance between adjacent nodes (or antinodes), which is always equal to half a wavelength.

$$ \text{Distance between adjacent nodes} = \frac{\lambda}{2} $$

Stretched Strings, Microwaves, and Air Columns

Stationary waves are formed in stretched strings (musical instruments), with microwaves reflecting off a metal plate, and in air columns (wind instruments) where sound waves reflect from an end. In each case, a wave superposes with its reflection.

Worked Example: Wavelength from Nodes

In a microwave experiment, a detector is moved along the path of a stationary wave. It registers a minimum signal (a node) at a position of 12.5 cm and the next minimum at 15.3 cm. What is the wavelength of the microwaves?

1. Identify the relationship: The distance between two adjacent nodes is equal to half a wavelength.

$$ \text{Distance between nodes} = \frac{\lambda}{2} $$

2. Calculate the distance:

$$ \text{Distance} = 15.3 \text{ cm} - 12.5 \text{ cm} = 2.8 \text{ cm} $$

3. Solve for the wavelength ($\lambda$):

$$ \frac{\lambda}{2} = 2.8 \text{ cm} $$

$$ \lambda = 2 \times 2.8 \text{ cm} = 5.6 \text{ cm} $$
