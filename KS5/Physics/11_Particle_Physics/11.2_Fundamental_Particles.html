11.2 Fundamental particles

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that a proton and a neutron are not fundamental particles.
  2. Describe the simple quark model of the proton (up, up, down) and the neutron (up, down, down).
  3. Describe how the quark model can be used to explain the charges of the proton and the neutron.
  4. Understand that a beta ($\beta$) particle is either an electron or a positron.
  5. State that electrons and positrons are leptons.
  6. Describe $\beta^−$ and $\beta^+$ decay in terms of a simple quark model.
  7. Understand that neutrinos and antineutrinos are also leptons.

LO1, LO2 & LO3: The Quark Model

A **fundamental particle** is a particle that is not made up of any smaller components. For a long time, protons and neutrons were thought to be fundamental. However, experiments in the mid-20th century showed that they have an internal structure. Protons and neutrons are **not** fundamental; they are composite particles called **hadrons**.

Hadrons are made of fundamental particles called **quarks**. In the Standard Model of particle physics, there are six "flavours" of quarks, which are grouped into three generations of increasing mass:

  • **First generation:** up (u), down (d)
  • **Second generation:** charm (c), strange (s)
  • **Third generation:** top (t), bottom (b)

All everyday matter is made from the first-generation quarks. For the A-Level syllabus, we are concerned with the **up quark** and the **down quark**, which have fractional electric charges.

Worked Example: Verifying Proton and Neutron Charge

Use the quark model to show that the charge of a proton is $+e$ and the charge of a neutron is 0.

(a) Proton (uud):

$$ \text{Charge} = (\text{charge of u}) + (\text{charge of u}) + (\text{charge of d}) $$

$$ = \left(+\frac{2}{3}e\right) + \left(+\frac{2}{3}e\right) + \left(-\frac{1}{3}e\right) $$

$$ = \frac{2+2-1}{3}e = \frac{3}{3}e = +e $$

(b) Neutron (udd):

$$ \text{Charge} = (\text{charge of u}) + (\text{charge of d}) + (\text{charge of d}) $$

$$ = \left(+\frac{2}{3}e\right) + \left(-\frac{1}{3}e\right) + \left(-\frac{1}{3}e\right) $$

$$ = \frac{2-1-1}{3}e = \frac{0}{3}e = 0 $$

LO4, LO5 & LO7: Leptons

**Leptons** are the second family of fundamental particles. Unlike quarks, they do not experience the strong nuclear force. The most familiar lepton is the **electron ($e^-$)**.

Beta ($\beta$) particles are electrons or their antimatter equivalent, **positrons ($e^+$)**. A positron has the same mass as an electron but an opposite charge (+e).

Also in the lepton family are **neutrinos ($\nu$)** and their antimatter counterparts, **antineutrinos ($\bar{\nu}$)**. These are neutral particles with a very small mass that are produced during beta decay.

LO6: Beta Decay in the Quark Model

Beta decay is a nuclear process that involves a change in the quark composition of a nucleon.

Beta-minus ($\beta^−$) decay

In $\beta^−$ decay, a neutron in the nucleus turns into a proton. This happens when a **down quark** within the neutron transforms into an **up quark**. To conserve charge, an electron ($e^−$) is emitted. An antineutrino ($\bar{\nu}_e$) is also emitted to conserve other quantum numbers.

$$ d \rightarrow u + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e $$

Beta-plus ($\beta^+$) decay

In $\beta^+$ decay (which occurs in proton-rich nuclei), a proton in the nucleus turns into a neutron. This happens when an **up quark** within the proton transforms into a **down quark**. To conserve charge, a positron ($e^+$) is emitted. A neutrino ($\nu_e$) is also emitted.

$$ u \rightarrow d + e^+ + \nu_e $$
