9.1.2 Ions and Ionic Bonds

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain how ionic bonds form between metal and non-metal atoms through the transfer of electrons.

Ions:

  • An ion is an atom or molecule that has an overall electrical charge.
  • This happens when an atom loses or gains one or more electrons to achieve a full outer shell.
    • Cations: Positively charged ions formed when metal atoms lose electrons.
    • Anions: Negatively charged ions formed when non-metal atoms gain electrons.

Ionic Bonding:

  • The electrostatic force of attraction between oppositely charged ions.
  • It occurs between a metal and a non-metal.
  • The metal atom transfers its outer shell electron(s) to the non-metal atom.
  • This results in the metal atom becoming a positive ion and the non-metal atom becoming a negative ion, both with stable, full outer shells.
  • These oppositely charged ions are strongly attracted to each other.

Ionic Compounds:

  • Substances formed through ionic bonding.
  • They form giant, three-dimensional lattice structures of alternating positive and negative ions.
  • Ionic compounds have high melting and boiling points due to the strong electrostatic forces between the ions, which require a lot of energy to overcome.

Example (with dot-and-cross diagrams):

  • Sodium Chloride (NaCl): A sodium atom (metal) transfers its one outer electron to a chlorine atom (non-metal). Sodium becomes Na⁺ and chlorine becomes Cl⁻.
