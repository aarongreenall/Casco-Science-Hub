14.1 Thermal equilibrium

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that (thermal) energy is transferred from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature.
  2. Understand that regions of equal temperature are in thermal equilibrium.

LO1 & LO2: Temperature and Thermal Equilibrium

At a microscopic level, the particles (atoms and molecules) that make up an object are in continuous, random motion. The energy associated with this random motion is called **internal energy**. Temperature is a measure of the average random kinetic energy of these particles.

When two objects at different temperatures are placed in **thermal contact** (meaning energy can flow between them), a process of energy transfer occurs.

Thermal energy is transferred from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature.

This net flow of energy continues until the rate of energy transfer from the hotter object to the colder object is equal to the rate of energy transfer from the colder object to the hotter object. At this point, there is **no net flow of thermal energy** between them.

When this state is reached, the objects are said to be in **thermal equilibrium**. A fundamental consequence of this is that:

Objects in thermal equilibrium have the same temperature.

This concept is sometimes called the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics and is the principle upon which thermometers work. A thermometer reaches thermal equilibrium with the object it is measuring and gives a reading of its own temperature, which we can then infer is the temperature of the object.
