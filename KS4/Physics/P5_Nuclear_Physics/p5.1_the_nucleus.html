P5.1 The nucleus

At the heart of every atom lies the **nucleus**, a tiny, dense region that contains most of the atom's mass. Nuclear physics delves into the composition, properties, and behavior of these atomic nuclei, which are central to understanding radioactivity, nuclear energy, and the origins of elements.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the composition of the nucleus in terms of protons and neutrons.
  2. State the relative charges of protons, neutrons and electrons as +1, 0 and -1 respectively.
  3. Define the terms proton number (atomic number), $Z$ and nucleon number (mass number), $A$ and be able to calculate the number of neutrons in a nucleus.
  4. Use the nuclide notation $^A_ZX$.
  5. State that an element may have more than one isotope and know that some isotopes are radioactive.
  6. Know the relationship between the proton number and the relative charge on a nucleus.

Composition of the Nucleus and Atomic Notation:

The atomic nucleus is composed of two types of subatomic particles:

  • **Protons:** Positively charged particles with a relative charge of +1 and a relative mass of 1.
  • **Neutrons:** Neutral (no charge) particles with a relative charge of 0 and a relative mass of 1.

Electrons, which orbit the nucleus, have a relative charge of -1 and a negligible relative mass (approximately 1/1836 of a proton).

Key terms used to describe the composition of an atom are:

  • **Proton number ($Z$):** Also known as the **atomic number**, this is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. It uniquely identifies an element. The proton number is equal to the number of electrons in a neutral atom.
  • **Nucleon number ($A$):** Also known as the **mass number**, this is the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.

The number of neutrons in a nucleus can be calculated by:

Number of neutrons = Nucleon number ($A$) - Proton number ($Z$)

Atoms are often represented using **nuclide notation**: $^A_ZX$. For example, $^{12}_6C$ represents a Carbon atom with a nucleon number of 12 and a proton number of 6.

Isotopes and Nuclear Charge:

An element can have more than one **isotope**. Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons (and thus different nucleon numbers). For example, Carbon-12 ($^{12}_6C$) and Carbon-14 ($^{14}_6C$) are isotopes of carbon.

Some isotopes are **radioactive**, meaning their nuclei are unstable and will spontaneously decay, emitting radiation.

The **relative charge on a nucleus** is determined solely by its proton number ($Z$). Since each proton has a relative charge of +1, a nucleus with $Z$ protons will have a relative charge of $+Z$. Neutrons have no charge, so they do not contribute to the nuclear charge.
