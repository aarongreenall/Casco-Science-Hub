C11.1 Formulas and terminology

Organic chemistry is the study of carbon compounds. Carbon is unique in its ability to form stable bonds with itself and with many other elements, leading to a vast array of organic molecules. To navigate this diversity, chemists use specific formulas and terminology to describe organic compounds and classify them into homologous series.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Draw and interpret the displayed formula of a molecule to show all the atoms and all the bonds.
  2. State that a homologous series is a family of similar compounds with similar chemical properties.
  3. Describe the general characteristics of a homologous series as: having the same general formula; displaying a trend in physical properties.

Displayed Formulas:

A **displayed formula** (also known as a full structural formula) shows all the atoms in a molecule and all the bonds between them. This provides a clear visual representation of the molecule's structure.

For example, for methane (CH$_4$), the displayed formula shows the central carbon atom bonded to four hydrogen atoms:

*Figure: The displayed formula of methane, showing all atoms and bonds.*

In displayed formulas:

  • A single line represents a single covalent bond (sharing one pair of electrons).
  • A double line represents a double covalent bond (sharing two pairs of electrons).
  • A triple line represents a triple covalent bond (sharing three pairs of electrons).

Homologous Series:

A **homologous series** is a family of organic compounds that have similar chemical properties and a general formula. Members of a homologous series differ from each other by a repeating unit (typically -CH$_2$-).

General characteristics of a homologous series:

  • **Same General Formula:** All members can be represented by the same general formula (e.g., C$_n$H$_{2n+2}$ for alkanes).
  • **Similar Chemical Properties:** Due to having the same functional group (the part of the molecule responsible for its chemical reactions).
  • **Gradual Trend in Physical Properties:** Physical properties like melting point, boiling point, and density show a gradual change (trend) as the number of carbon atoms in the chain increases. For example, boiling points generally increase with increasing chain length.
  • **Successive members differ by -CH$_2$-:** Each member differs from the next by a -CH$_2$- unit.
