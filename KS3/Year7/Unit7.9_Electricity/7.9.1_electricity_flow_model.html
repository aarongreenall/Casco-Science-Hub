7.9.1 Electricity flow model

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe electricity as a flow of charged particles (electrons).
  2. Define a circuit as a complete loop.
  3. Understand the role of a battery (voltage) and a switch in a circuit.

What is Electricity?

**Electricity** is a form of energy that comes from the movement of tiny charged particles called **electrons**. To get these electrons to move and do useful work (like lighting a bulb), we need to create a complete path for them to travel around. This path is called a **circuit**.

A simple circuit has three main parts:

  1. A **power source**, like a battery, which provides the "push" to make the electrons move. This push is called **voltage**.
  2. A **component**, like a bulb or a motor, which uses the energy of the moving electrons.
  3. **Wires** to connect everything together and form a complete, unbroken loop.

Analogy: Water in Pipes

A great way to understand a simple circuit is to think of it like a central heating system with water flowing through pipes.

  • The **wires** are the **pipes**.
  • The **electrons** are the **water** flowing inside the pipes.
  • The **battery** is the **pump**. It doesn't make water, it just pushes the water that's already in the pipes.
  • A **bulb** is like a **radiator**. The moving water makes it heat up and glow.
  • A **switch** is like a **tap**. It can open or close the pipe to stop or start the flow of water.

At-Home Experiment: Investigating Static Electricity

Before we can understand electricity flowing in a circuit, it's fun to see what electric charge can do when it's standing still! This is called static electricity.

Equipment:
  • An inflated balloon
  • Your own hair (or a woolly jumper)
  • A small pile of tiny, torn-up pieces of paper
  • A tap that can produce a thin, steady stream of water
Method:
  1. First, try to pick up the pieces of paper with the balloon. Nothing happens.
  2. Now, charge the balloon by rubbing it vigorously on your hair or a woolly jumper for about 20 seconds. This transfers tiny electrons to the balloon, giving it a negative charge.
  3. Bring the charged part of the balloon close to (but not touching) the pieces of paper. What happens now? The balloon should be able to pick them up!
  4. Re-charge the balloon again. Turn on a tap so there is a very thin, steady stream of water.
  5. Slowly bring the charged balloon close to the stream of water. Watch carefully as the water stream bends towards the balloon without you even touching it!
What's Happening?

By rubbing the balloon, you have built up a static electric charge on its surface. This charge creates an electric field around the balloon that is strong enough to attract the light pieces of paper and the molecules in the stream of water. This shows that electric charge can exert a force at a distance.
