9.4.2 Group Trends (Group 1 Example)

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain how properties of elements change down a group, using the Group 1 alkali metals as an example.

Group 1: The Alkali Metals

  • Includes Lithium (Li), Sodium (Na), Potassium (K), etc.
  • They all have one electron in their outer shell.
  • This makes them very reactive, as they want to lose this one electron to achieve a stable, full outer shell.

Trend in Reactivity Down Group 1:

  • Reactivity increases as you go down the group.
  • Reason:
    1. As you go down the group, the number of electron shells increases.
    2. This means the outer electron is further away from the positive nucleus.
    3. The attraction between the nucleus and the outer electron is weakened by the increased distance and by the shielding effect of the inner electron shells.
    4. Therefore, less energy is needed to remove the outer electron, making the atom more reactive.
  • Evidence: The reactions with water become more vigorous as you go down the group:
    • Lithium fizzes steadily.
    • Sodium fizzes rapidly and melts into a ball.
    • Potassium reacts violently, producing a lilac flame.
