11.1 Atoms, nuclei and radiation

Learning Objectives:

  1. Infer from the results of the $\alpha$-particle scattering experiment the existence and small size of the nucleus.
  2. Describe a simple model for the nuclear atom that includes protons, neutrons and electrons.
  3. Understand the terms nucleon number (mass number) $A$, proton number (atomic number) $Z$ and nuclide.
  4. Use the nuclide notation $^{A}_{Z}X$.
  5. Understand the term isotope.
  6. Understand that nucleon number and proton number are conserved in nuclear processes.
  7. Describe the composition, mass and charge of $\alpha$-particles, $\beta$-particles and $\gamma$-rays.
  8. Represent $\alpha$- and $\beta$-decay by nuclear equations of the form $^{A}_{Z}X \rightarrow {^{A-4}_{Z-2}Y} + {^{4}_{2}\alpha}$ and $^{A}_{Z}X \rightarrow {^{A}_{Z+1}Y} + {^{0}_{-1}\beta} + \bar{\nu}$.

LO1: The $\alpha$-particle Scattering Experiment

In the early 20th century, the accepted model of the atom was J.J. Thomson's "plum pudding" model. This model envisioned the atom as a sphere of uniformly distributed positive charge (the "pudding"), with negatively charged electrons (the "plums") interspersed on the surface to make the atom electrically neutral.

Expectations from the Plum Pudding Model

Under the supervision of Ernest Rutherford, physicists Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden set out to test this model. They used gold as a target because it is extremely malleable and can be hammered into a foil only a few atoms thick. They then fired a beam of fast-moving, positively charged alpha ($\alpha$) particles at this foil.

Based on the plum pudding model, they expected the alpha particles to push through with only minor deflections, like a **bullet firing through a sheet of tissue paper**. This was because:

  1. They believed that the gold foil was mostly empty space *between* the atoms. Therefore, they expected the vast majority of alpha particles to fly straight through these gaps without hitting anything at all.
  2. For the few particles that did happen to hit an atom, they predicted only minor deflections. This was because the positive charge of the "pudding" was thought to be too spread out and weak to significantly repel the heavy, fast-moving alpha particle. Furthermore, the negative electrons on the surface would effectively neutralise the atom's charge, further reducing any potential deviation.

Experimental Observations and Inferences

The results of the Geiger-Marsden experiment were completely unexpected and revolutionary. Rutherford famously remarked, "It was almost as incredible as if you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you."

  1. Observation: The vast majority of $\alpha$-particles passed straight through the thin gold foil with little or no deflection.
    Inference: The atom is mostly empty space.
  2. Observation: A small number of $\alpha$-particles were deflected through large angles.
    Inference: There must be a region of concentrated positive charge within the atom, which repelled the positive $\alpha$-particles. This region was named the **nucleus**.
  3. Observation: A very small number of $\alpha$-particles (about 1 in 8000) were deflected by more than 90Â°, effectively bouncing back.
    Inference: The nucleus must be very small, dense, and contain most of the atom's mass. A head-on collision with this tiny, massive nucleus is rare but results in a very large electrostatic repulsion.

LO2, LO3, LO4 & LO5: The Nuclear Model and Isotopes

The results of the scattering experiment led to the nuclear model of the atom: a small, dense, positively charged nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by orbiting electrons.

  • Proton Number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus. It defines the element. Also known as the atomic number.
  • Nucleon Number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus. Also known as the mass number.
  • Nuclide: A specific type of nucleus, defined by its particular values of Z and A.

We represent a specific nuclide using the notation:

$$ ^{A}_{Z}X $$

Where X is the chemical symbol for the element.

**Isotopes** are nuclides of the same element that have the same number of protons (same Z) but different numbers of neutrons (different A). For example, Carbon-12 ($^{12}_{6}C$) and Carbon-14 ($^{14}_{6}C$) are isotopes of carbon.

LO6, LO7 & LO8: Nuclear Processes and Radiation

In all nuclear processes, such as radioactive decay, both the total nucleon number (A) and the total proton number (Z) are conserved. Unstable nuclei decay by emitting radiation to become more stable.

Worked Example: Nuclear Equations

Write the nuclear equations for (a) the alpha decay of Uranium-238 ($^{238}_{92}U$) and (b) the beta decay of Carbon-14 ($^{14}_{6}C$).

(a) Alpha Decay: An $\alpha$-particle ($^{4}_{2}\alpha$) is emitted. A decreases by 4, Z decreases by 2.

$$ ^{238}_{92}U \rightarrow {^{238-4}_{92-2}Y} + {^{4}_{2}\alpha} $$

The new element has Z = 90, which is Thorium (Th).

$$ ^{238}_{92}U \rightarrow {^{234}_{90}Th} + {^{4}_{2}\alpha} $$

(b) Beta Decay: A $\beta$-particle ($^{0}_{-1}\beta$, an electron) and an antineutrino ($\bar{\nu}$) are emitted. A is unchanged, Z increases by 1 (as a neutron turns into a proton).

$$ ^{14}_{6}C \rightarrow {^{14}_{6+1}Y} + {^{0}_{-1}\beta} + \bar{\nu} $$

The new element has Z = 7, which is Nitrogen (N).

$$ ^{14}_{6}C \rightarrow {^{14}_{7}N} + {^{0}_{-1}\beta} + \bar{\nu} $$
