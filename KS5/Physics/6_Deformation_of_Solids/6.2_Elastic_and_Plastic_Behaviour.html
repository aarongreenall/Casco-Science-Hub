6.2 Elastic and Plastic Behaviour

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand and use the terms elastic deformation, plastic deformation and elastic limit.
  2. Understand that the area under the force-extension graph represents the work done.
  3. Determine the elastic potential energy of a material deformed within its limit of proportionality from the area under the force-extension graph.
  4. Recall and use $E_{P}=\frac{1}{2}Fx=\frac{1}{2}kx^{2}$ for a material deformed within its limit of proportionality.

LO1: Elastic and Plastic Deformation

When a material is stretched or compressed, its response can be categorized into two types of deformation:

  • Elastic Deformation: This is a temporary change in shape. The material returns to its original dimensions once the deforming force (load) is removed. In this region, the atomic bonds are stretched but not broken.
  • Plastic Deformation: This is a permanent change in shape. The material does not return to its original dimensions after the load is removed. This occurs when the atoms in the material move past each other to new positions.

The **elastic limit** is the maximum force (or stress) that can be applied to an object without causing permanent (plastic) deformation. If you stretch a material beyond its elastic limit, it will be permanently deformed.

LO2, LO3 & LO4: Work Done and Energy Stored

When you stretch a material, you are doing work on it. This work is stored within the material as **elastic potential energy ($E_p$)**.

The work done in stretching a material is equal to the area under its force-extension graph.

For a material that obeys Hooke's Law (i.e., it is deformed within its limit of proportionality), the force-extension graph is a straight line through the origin, forming a right-angled triangle.

Derivation of Elastic Potential Energy Formula

1. The work done, $W$, is the area under the F-x graph. For a linear relationship, this area is a triangle.

$$ W = \text{Area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} $$

2. The base of the triangle is the extension, $x$, and the height is the final force, $F$.

$$ W = \frac{1}{2}Fx $$

3. The work done is stored as elastic potential energy, $E_p$.

$$ E_p = \frac{1}{2}Fx $$

4. We can also substitute Hooke's Law, $F=kx$, into this equation:

$$ E_p = \frac{1}{2}(kx)x $$

$$ E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 $$

Worked Example: Energy Stored in a Stretched Wire

A steel wire is stretched elastically by 2.0 mm when a force of 150 N is applied. Calculate the work done in stretching the wire.

1. Identify variables and convert to SI units:

$$ F = 150 \text{ N} $$

$$ x = 2.0 \text{ mm} = 2.0 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m} $$

2. State the formula for work done (elastic potential energy):

Since the deformation is elastic, we can assume it's within the limit of proportionality.

$$ E_p = \frac{1}{2}Fx $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ E_p = \frac{1}{2} \times (150 \text{ N}) \times (2.0 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}) = 0.15 \text{ J} $$
