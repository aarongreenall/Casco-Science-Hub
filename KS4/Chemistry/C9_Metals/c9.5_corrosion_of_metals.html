C9.5 Corrosion of metals

**Corrosion** is the process by which metals deteriorate due to chemical reactions with their environment. It is a significant problem, particularly for iron and steel, where it is commonly known as **rusting**. Understanding the conditions required for corrosion and methods to prevent it is crucial for protecting metal structures and products.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State the conditions required for the rusting of iron (presence of oxygen and water).
  2. Describe how barrier methods prevent rusting by excluding oxygen or water.
  3. State some common barrier methods, including painting, greasing and coating with plastic.
  4. Explain sacrificial protection in terms of the reactivity series and in terms of electron loss.
  5. Describe the use of zinc in galvanising as an example of a barrier method and sacrificial protection.

Rusting of Iron:

**Rusting** is the specific term for the corrosion of iron and steel. It is an oxidation reaction that requires the presence of both **oxygen** and **water**. If either oxygen or water is absent, rusting will not occur.

The chemical equation for the formation of rust (hydrated iron(III) oxide) is complex, but the overall process can be simplified as:

`Iron + Oxygen + Water -> Hydrated Iron(III) Oxide (Rust)`

Rust is a reddish-brown, flaky solid that weakens the iron structure.

*Figure: An example of rusted iron, showing the characteristic reddish-brown flaky appearance.*

Methods of Rust Prevention:

Several methods are used to prevent rusting, primarily by preventing oxygen and/or water from reaching the iron surface:

1. Barrier Methods:

These methods create a physical barrier between the iron and the oxygen and water in the environment. Common barrier methods include:

  • **Painting:** Provides a protective layer (e.g., on cars, bridges).
  • **Greasing/Oiling:** Used for moving parts of machinery or tools.
  • **Coating with Plastic:** Durable and long-lasting protection (e.g., on garden furniture, bicycle racks).
  • **Plating with a less reactive metal:** Coating iron with a thin layer of a less reactive metal like tin (e.g., tin cans) or chromium (e.g., chrome plating).

*Figure: A painted metal fence, where paint acts as a barrier to prevent rusting.*

2. Sacrificial Protection:

This method involves connecting the iron to a more reactive metal. The more reactive metal will preferentially corrode (oxidize) instead of the iron, thus "sacrificing" itself to protect the iron. This works because the more reactive metal has a greater tendency to lose electrons.

  • **Explanation in terms of reactivity series and electron loss:** In the reactivity series, a more reactive metal (e.g., zinc) will lose electrons more readily than iron. When both are in contact with water and oxygen, the more reactive metal will oxidize (lose electrons) instead of the iron, protecting the iron from rusting.
  • **Galvanising:** A common example is **galvanising**, where iron or steel is coated with a layer of zinc. Zinc acts as both a barrier method (preventing oxygen and water contact) and provides sacrificial protection. If the zinc coating is scratched, the zinc will still corrode in preference to the iron, protecting the underlying steel.
