7.9.3 Measuring current in series circuits

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define electric current and its unit, the ampere.
  2. Describe the rule for current in a series circuit.
  3. Explain how to use an ammeter to measure current.

What is Current?

**Electric current** is the rate of flow of electric charge. In simple terms, it's a measure of how much electricity is flowing through a wire. The unit of electric current is the **ampere**, which has the symbol **A**. We measure current using an instrument called an **ammeter**.

In a **series circuit**, the components are connected end-to-end, forming a single, unbroken loop. Because there is only one path for the electricity to take, the current must be the same at every point in the circuit.

Analogy: Water in Pipes

Imagine a series circuit is like a single, unbranching loop of garden hose. The **current** is like the **flow rate of the water** (e.g., litres per second). In a single loop, the flow rate must be the same everywhereâ€”you can't have water disappearing or appearing in the middle of the hose! To measure this flow, you would have to cut the hose and insert a **flow meter** (the ammeter) directly into the path of the water. This is why an ammeter must always be connected **in series**.

In-School Experiment: Measuring Current

You can build a simple series circuit to prove that the current is the same everywhere.

Equipment:
  • A low-voltage power supply or battery
  • Two bulbs in holders
  • Three ammeters
  • Connecting wires
Method:
  1. Build the series circuit as shown in the diagram above, with one ammeter placed before the first bulb.
  2. Close the switch and record the reading on the ammeter.
  3. Now, move the ammeter so it is between the two bulbs. Record the new reading.
  4. Finally, move the ammeter so it is after the second bulb. Record the reading again.
  5. What do you notice about the three readings you have taken? They should all be the same, proving that current is not "used up" as it flows around a series circuit.
