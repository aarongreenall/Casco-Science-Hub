7.5.4 Alloys and Their Properties

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will define alloys and explain why they are made and how their properties differ from those of pure metals.

Key concepts:

  • Definition: An alloy is a mixture of a metal with at least one other element (which can be a metal or a non-metal).
  • Purpose of alloying: To enhance the properties of the base metal, such as increasing strength, hardness, or resistance to corrosion.
  • How alloying works: The different sized atoms of the added element(s) distort the regular arrangement of atoms in the pure metal, making it harder for layers to slide over each other.
  • Common examples of alloys and their uses:
    • Steel: Iron and carbon (and other elements). Used in construction, cars, tools. (Stronger than pure iron).
    • Brass: Copper and zinc. Used for musical instruments, plumbing fittings. (Harder than copper, corrosion-resistant).
    • Bronze: Copper and tin. Used for statues, bells, bearings. (Harder than copper, casts well).
    • Solder: Tin and lead (or other metals). Used for joining electrical components. (Low melting point).
