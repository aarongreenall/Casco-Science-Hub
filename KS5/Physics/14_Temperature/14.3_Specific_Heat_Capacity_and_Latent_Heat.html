14.3 Specific heat capacity and specific latent heat

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define and use specific heat capacity.
  2. Define and use specific latent heat and distinguish between specific latent heat of fusion and specific latent heat of vaporisation.

LO1: Specific Heat Capacity

When thermal energy is supplied to an object, its temperature generally increases. The **heat capacity** of an object is the amount of energy required to raise its temperature by 1 K (or 1 °C). However, this property depends on the size of the object; a swimming pool has a much larger heat capacity than a cup of water.

To compare materials themselves, irrespective of their size, we use the **specific heat capacity ($c$)**. The word **"specific"** in physics means "per unit mass" (i.e., per kg). By defining the property per kilogram, we are standardizing it, allowing us to compare the intrinsic ability of different substances to store thermal energy. This is fundamentally related to the number of particles in that kilogram of substance, a concept that will be explored further in Topic 15.1 with the mole.

Analogy: Buckets and Water

Think of the "capacity" of a substance to hold heat like the capacity of a bucket to hold water. A substance with a high specific heat capacity is like a very wide bucket: you have to add a lot of water (thermal energy) just to raise the water level (temperature) by one centimetre. A substance with a low specific heat capacity is like a very narrow bucket: just a small amount of water (thermal energy) will cause the water level (temperature) to rise significantly.

The specific heat capacity of a substance is the thermal energy required per unit mass to produce a unit increase in temperature.

This definition leads to the formula for the thermal energy, $Q$, required to change the temperature of a mass $m$ by $\Delta\theta$:

$$ c = \frac{Q}{m\Delta\theta} \quad \implies \quad Q = mc\Delta\theta $$

Worked Example: Heating Water

An electric kettle has a power rating of 2.2 kW. It contains 1.5 kg of water at an initial temperature of 20 °C. Assuming all the electrical energy is transferred to the water, calculate the time it will take for the water to reach boiling point (100 °C). The specific heat capacity of water is 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹.

1. Calculate the required thermal energy ($Q$):

The temperature change is $\Delta\theta = 100 - 20 = 80$ °C.

$$ Q = mc\Delta\theta $$

$$ Q = (1.5 \text{ kg}) \times (4200 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{K}^{-1}) \times (80 \text{ K}) $$

$$ Q = 504000 \text{ J} $$

2. Relate energy, power, and time:

Historically, the study of heat (thermodynamics) and the study of mechanics and electricity developed separately. This led to different symbols being used for what we now understand as the same fundamental quantity: energy. We use $Q$ for thermal energy, $E$ for electrical energy, and $W$ (not to be confused with the unit W for watts, named after James Watt) for work done. By the principle of conservation of energy, the electrical energy supplied ($E$) is converted into thermal energy ($Q$), so $E=Q$.

Power is the rate of energy transfer, $P = E/t$. Since $E=Q$, we can write:

$$ P = \frac{Q}{t} \implies t = \frac{Q}{P} $$

3. Calculate the time ($t$):

Power $P = 2.2 \text{ kW} = 2200 \text{ W}$ (or J s⁻¹).

$$ t = \frac{504000 \text{ J}}{2200 \text{ W}} $$

$$ t = 229 \text{ s} \text{ (approx. 3.8 minutes)} $$

LO2: Specific Latent Heat

When a substance changes state, thermal energy is transferred, but the temperature of the substance **remains constant**. This energy is called **latent heat**.

The **specific latent heat ($L$)** of a substance is the thermal energy required to change the state of a unit mass of the substance without any change in temperature.

$$ Q = mL $$

There are two types, and the process is reversible:

  • Specific Latent Heat of Fusion ($L_f$): Energy is **absorbed** to melt a solid into a liquid. The same amount of energy is **released** when the liquid freezes back into a solid.
  • Specific Latent Heat of Vaporisation ($L_v$): Energy is **absorbed** to boil a liquid into a gas. The same amount of energy is **released** when the gas condenses back into a liquid.

Worked Example: Latent Heat of Fusion

How much thermal energy is required to melt a 300 g block of ice at 0 °C? The specific latent heat of fusion of ice is $3.3 \times 10^5$ J kg⁻¹.

1. Convert mass to SI units:

$$ m = 300 \text{ g} = 0.30 \text{ kg} $$

2. State the formula for latent heat of fusion:

$$ Q = mL_f $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ Q = (0.30 \text{ kg}) \times (3.3 \times 10^5 \text{ J kg}^{-1}) $$

$$ Q = 99000 \text{ J} \text{ (or 99 kJ)} $$

Summary Worked Example 1: Heating Ice to Steam

Calculate the total energy required to turn 0.50 kg of ice at -10 °C into steam at 100 °C.
(c_ice = 2100 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, c_water = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, L_f = 3.3 × 10⁵ J kg⁻¹, L_v = 2.3 × 10⁶ J kg⁻¹)

1. Energy to heat the ice from -10 °C to 0 °C ($Q_1$):

$$ Q_1 = mc_{ice}\Delta\theta = (0.50) \times (2100) \times (10) = 10500 \text{ J} $$

2. Energy to melt the ice at 0 °C ($Q_2$):

$$ Q_2 = mL_f = (0.50) \times (3.3 \times 10^5) = 165000 \text{ J} $$

3. Energy to heat the water from 0 °C to 100 °C ($Q_3$):

$$ Q_3 = mc_{water}\Delta\theta = (0.50) \times (4200) \times (100) = 210000 \text{ J} $$

4. Energy to turn the water into steam at 100 °C ($Q_4$):

$$ Q_4 = mL_v = (0.50) \times (2.3 \times 10^6) = 1150000 \text{ J} $$

5. Total Energy ($Q_{total}$):

$$ Q_{total} = Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_3 + Q_4 = 1,535,500 \text{ J} \approx 1.5 \text{ MJ} $$

Summary Worked Example 2: Mixing Ice and Water

A 50 g ice cube at 0 °C is placed in a thermally insulated beaker containing 200 g of water at 30 °C. After the ice has melted, a 2.0 kW heater is used to turn all the water into steam at 100 °C. Calculate (a) the final equilibrium temperature of the mixture after the ice melts, and (b) the total time the heater must be on.

Part (a): Find the final temperature, $\theta_f$

By conservation of energy, the thermal energy lost by the warm water equals the energy gained by the ice.

$$ \text{Energy lost by water} = \text{Energy to melt ice} + \text{Energy to heat melted ice} $$

$$ m_w c_w (30 - \theta_f) = m_{ice} L_f + m_{ice} c_w (\theta_f - 0) $$

$$ (0.200)(4200)(30 - \theta_f) = (0.050)(3.3 \times 10^5) + (0.050)(4200)(\theta_f) $$

$$ 840(30 - \theta_f) = 16500 + 210\theta_f $$

$$ 25200 - 840\theta_f = 16500 + 210\theta_f $$

$$ 8700 = 1050\theta_f $$

$$ \theta_f = \frac{8700}{1050} = 8.29 \, ^\circ\text{C} \approx 8.3 \, ^\circ\text{C} $$

Part (b): Find the time to turn the mixture into steam

The total mass of water is $m_{total} = 0.200 + 0.050 = 0.250$ kg. The initial temperature is 8.3 °C.

1. **Energy to heat all water from 8.3 °C to 100 °C ($Q_1$):**

$$ Q_1 = m_{total}c_w\Delta\theta = (0.250)(4200)(100 - 8.3) = 96285 \text{ J} $$

2. **Energy to turn all water into steam at 100 °C ($Q_2$):**

$$ Q_2 = m_{total}L_v = (0.250)(2.3 \times 10^6) = 575000 \text{ J} $$

3. **Total energy required from heater ($Q_{total}$):**

$$ Q_{total} = Q_1 + Q_2 = 96285 + 575000 = 671285 \text{ J} $$

4. **Calculate the time ($t$):** Power $P = 2.0 \text{ kW} = 2000$ W.

$$ t = \frac{Q_{total}}{P} = \frac{671285}{2000} = 335.6 \text{ s} \approx 340 \text{ s} $$
