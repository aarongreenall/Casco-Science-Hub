B7.2 Digestive system

The **digestive system** is a complex organ system responsible for breaking down food into smaller molecules that can be absorbed into the bloodstream and used by the body for energy, growth, and repair. This intricate system involves a long tube called the alimentary canal and several associated organs that secrete digestive juices.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Identify in diagrams and images the main organs of the digestive system, limited to: alimentary canal: mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine (duodenum and ileum) and large intestine (colon, rectum, anus); associated organs: salivary glands, pancreas, liver and gall bladder.
  2. Describe the functions of the organs of the digestive system listed in 7.2.1, in relation to: ingestion – the taking of substances, e.g. food and drink, into the body; digestion – the breakdown of food; absorption – the movement of nutrients from the intestines into the blood; assimilation – uptake and use of nutrients by cells; egestion – the removal of undigested food from the body as faeces.

Organs of the Digestive System:

The human digestive system consists of the **alimentary canal** (a long tube through which food passes) and several **associated organs** that produce digestive enzymes and other substances.

*Figure: A diagram illustrating the main organs of the human digestive system.*

Alimentary Canal:

  • **Mouth:** Where ingestion occurs; mechanical digestion (chewing) and chemical digestion (salivary amylase) begin.
  • **Oesophagus (gullet):** Muscular tube that transports food from the mouth to the stomach by peristalsis.
  • **Stomach:** Muscular bag that churns food (mechanical digestion) and mixes it with gastric juice (chemical digestion by proteases and hydrochloric acid).
  • **Small Intestine:** The primary site of chemical digestion and absorption. * **Duodenum:** First part, where most chemical digestion occurs with digestive juices from the pancreas and liver. * **Ileum:** Longer, highly folded part where most absorption of digested nutrients into the blood occurs.
  • **Large Intestine:** * **Colon:** Absorbs water from undigested food. * **Rectum:** Stores faeces temporarily. * **Anus:** Opening through which faeces are egested.

Associated Organs:

  • **Salivary Glands:** Secrete saliva into the mouth, containing amylase for starch digestion.
  • **Pancreas:** Produces pancreatic juice containing digestive enzymes (amylase, protease, lipase) and bicarbonate to neutralize stomach acid, released into the duodenum.
  • **Liver:** Produces bile, which emulsifies fats.
  • **Gall Bladder:** Stores and concentrates bile produced by the liver, releasing it into the duodenum.

Functions of the Digestive System:

The digestive system performs five key functions:

  • **Ingestion:** The process of taking food and drink into the body through the mouth.
  • **Digestion:** The breakdown of large, complex, insoluble food molecules into small, simple, soluble molecules that can be absorbed. This involves both mechanical digestion (physical breakdown, e.g., chewing, churning) and chemical digestion (breakdown by enzymes).
  • **Absorption:** The movement of digested nutrients (small, soluble molecules) from the intestines (mainly the ileum of the small intestine) into the bloodstream.
  • **Assimilation:** The uptake and use of absorbed nutrients by the body's cells for energy, growth, and repair.
  • **Egestion:** The removal of undigested food materials (faeces) from the body through the anus. This is distinct from excretion, which is the removal of metabolic waste.
