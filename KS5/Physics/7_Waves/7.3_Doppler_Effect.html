7.3 Doppler effect for sound waves

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that when a source of sound waves moves relative to a stationary observer, the observed frequency is different from the source frequency.
  2. Use the expression $f_{o}=f_{s}v/(v\pm v_{s})$ for the observed frequency when a source of sound waves moves relative to a stationary observer.

LO1: Qualitative Understanding of the Doppler Effect

The **Doppler effect** is the apparent change in the frequency (and wavelength) of a wave in relation to an observer who is moving relative to the wave source. For A-Level Physics, we focus on the case of a moving source and a stationary observer.

Source Moving Towards Observer

When a sound source moves towards a stationary observer, each successive wavefront is emitted from a position closer to the observer than the previous one. This causes the wavefronts to "bunch up" in front of the source.

  • The distance between wavefronts (the **wavelength**) decreases.
  • Since the speed of sound in the medium remains constant, and $v=f\lambda$, a decrease in wavelength must result in an **increase in the observed frequency**.
  • The observer hears a sound with a **higher pitch**.

Source Moving Away From Observer

Conversely, when the sound source moves away from the observer, each successive wavefront is emitted from a position further away. This causes the wavefronts to "spread out" behind the source.

  • The distance between wavefronts (the **wavelength**) increases.
  • Since the speed of sound is constant, an increase in wavelength must result in a **decrease in the observed frequency**.
  • The observer hears a sound with a **lower pitch**.

LO2: The Doppler Effect Equation

The relationship between the source frequency and the observed frequency can be described by the following equation:

$$ f_o = f_s \left( \frac{v}{v \pm v_s} \right) $$

Where:

  • $f_o$ is the observed frequency.
  • $f_s$ is the source frequency (the frequency emitted by the source).
  • $v$ is the speed of the sound waves in the medium.
  • $v_s$ is the speed of the source.

Sign Convention

The choice of plus or minus in the denominator is crucial and depends on the direction of the source's motion relative to the observer.

  • Use the **minus sign (-)** when the source is moving **towards** the observer. This makes the denominator smaller, resulting in a higher observed frequency ($f_o > f_s$).
  • Use the **plus sign (+)** when the source is moving **away** from the observer. This makes the denominator larger, resulting in a lower observed frequency ($f_o < f_s$).

Worked Example 1: Source Moving Towards

A police car with a 500 Hz siren moves at 30 m s⁻¹ towards a stationary observer. The speed of sound in air is 340 m s⁻¹. Calculate the frequency heard by the observer.

1. Identify the variables: $f_s = 500$ Hz, $v_s = 30$ m s⁻¹, $v = 340$ m s⁻¹.

2. Choose the correct sign: The source is moving towards the observer, so we use the minus sign.

$$ f_o = f_s \left( \frac{v}{v - v_s} \right) $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ f_o = 500 \left( \frac{340}{340 - 30} \right) $$

$$ f_o = 500 \left( \frac{340}{310} \right) $$

$$ f_o = 548.38... \approx 550 \text{ Hz} $$

Worked Example 2: Source Moving Away

The same police car now passes the observer and moves away at 30 m s⁻¹. Calculate the new frequency heard by the observer.

1. Identify the variables: $f_s = 500$ Hz, $v_s = 30$ m s⁻¹, $v = 340$ m s⁻¹.

2. Choose the correct sign: The source is moving away from the observer, so we use the plus sign.

$$ f_o = f_s \left( \frac{v}{v + v_s} \right) $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ f_o = 500 \left( \frac{340}{340 + 30} \right) $$

$$ f_o = 500 \left( \frac{340}{370} \right) $$

$$ f_o = 459.45... \approx 460 \text{ Hz} $$
