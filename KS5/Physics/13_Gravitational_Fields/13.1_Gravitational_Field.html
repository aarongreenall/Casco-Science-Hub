13.1 Gravitational field

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand a gravitational field as an example of a field of force and define gravitational field strength as force per unit mass.
  2. Represent a gravitational field by means of field lines.

LO1: The Concept of a Gravitational Field

Gravity is a "non-contact" force. The Sun pulls on the Earth without physically touching it. To explain this action at a distance, we use the concept of a **field of force**.

A **gravitational field** is a region of space where an object with mass experiences a gravitational force. Any object with mass creates a gravitational field around it. The field is the medium through which the force of gravity is exerted. We can visualize a field using **field lines**, where the direction of the arrow shows the direction of the force on a mass, and the density of the lines indicates the strength of the field.

Gravitational Field Strength ($g$)

The strength of a gravitational field at a point is defined as the gravitational force experienced per unit mass placed at that point.

$$ g = \frac{F}{m} $$

Gravitational field strength, $g$, is a vector quantity, and its SI unit is the newton per kilogram (N kg⁻¹). Near the Earth's surface, it has an approximate value of 9.81 N kg⁻¹. Note that the unit N kg⁻¹ is equivalent to m s⁻², so gravitational field strength is numerically equal to the acceleration of free fall.

Worked Example: Field Strength on Mars

An astronaut of mass 120 kg (including their suit) experiences a gravitational force of 444 N on the surface of Mars. Calculate the gravitational field strength on Mars.

1. State the formula:

$$ g = \frac{F}{m} $$

2. Substitute the values and solve:

$$ g = \frac{444 \text{ N}}{120 \text{ kg}} $$

$$ g = 3.7 \text{ N kg}^{-1} $$
