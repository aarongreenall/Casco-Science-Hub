8.5.2 Reactivity of metals

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define reactivity as how readily a substance takes part in a chemical reaction.
  2. Describe the properties of the alkali metals (Group 1).
  3. Explain the trend in reactivity down Group 1.

What is Reactivity?

Some metals, like gold and platinum, can last for thousands of years without changing. Other metals, like sodium, react so violently with water that they can explode! The **reactivity** of a metal is a measure of how readily it takes part in a chemical reaction.

A great way to see a clear pattern in reactivity is to look at the elements in **Group 1** of the periodic table, known as the **Alkali Metals**.

The alkali metals (Lithium, Sodium, Potassium, etc.) all share similar properties: they are soft, have low densities (they can float on water!), and are extremely reactive. They all have one electron in their outer shell, which they are very keen to give away in a chemical reaction.

The Trend in Reactivity

The most important pattern for the alkali metals is that their **reactivity increases as you go down the group**. Potassium is more reactive than Sodium, and Sodium is more reactive than Lithium.

The reason for this is atomic structure. As you go down the group, the atoms get bigger. This means the single outer electron is further away from the positive nucleus. The attraction between the nucleus and this outer electron is weaker, so the electron is lost much more easily, making the element more reactive.

In-School Demonstration: Alkali Metals in Water

The trend in reactivity of the alkali metals is best shown by observing their reaction with water. This is a teacher-led demonstration.

Safety First!

This is a dangerous experiment that must only be performed by a qualified teacher behind a safety screen. Alkali metals are highly corrosive and react violently with water.

Observations:
Metal Reaction with Water
Lithium (Li) Fizzes steadily on the surface, producing hydrogen gas.
Sodium (Na) Fizzes much more vigorously, melts into a silvery ball, and darts across the surface.
Potassium (K) Reacts instantly and very violently. The hydrogen gas produced immediately catches fire, burning with a lilac-coloured flame.

The increasing vigour of the reaction as you go down the group provides clear evidence that the reactivity of the alkali metals increases down the group.
