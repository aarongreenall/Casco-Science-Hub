P6.3 Life cycle of stars

Stars, including our Sun, undergo a fascinating life cycle, evolving through various stages from their birth in vast clouds of gas and dust to their eventual demise. The path a star takes through its life cycle is primarily determined by its initial mass.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Know that stable stars are formed as protostars from interstellar clouds of gas and dust due to gravitational attraction.
  2. Know that the next stages of the life cycle of a star depend on its mass, limited to: a small mass star (about the same mass as the Sun): red giant $\rightarrow$ white dwarf + planetary nebula; a large mass star: red supergiant $\rightarrow$ supernova $\rightarrow$ neutron star; a very large mass star: red supergiant $\rightarrow$ supernova $\rightarrow$ black hole.
  3. Know that the nebula from a supernova may form new stars with orbiting planets.

From Protostar to Stable Star:

The life cycle of a star begins in vast, cold, and dense regions of space known as **interstellar clouds of gas and dust** (also called nebulae). Within these clouds, gravity plays a crucial role. Due to **gravitational attraction**, clumps of gas and dust begin to pull together, slowly collapsing and increasing in density and temperature. As the clump continues to contract, it forms a **protostar**.

A protostar continues to contract and heat up until its core reaches a temperature and pressure high enough for **nuclear fusion** to begin. At this point, hydrogen nuclei start fusing into helium, releasing enormous amounts of energy. This outward pressure from fusion balances the inward pull of gravity, and the protostar becomes a **stable star**. Our Sun is currently in this stable phase.

*Figure: The Pillars of Creation, a region of star formation within the Eagle Nebula.*

Stellar Evolution Paths (Mass Dependent):

The subsequent stages of a star's life cycle depend critically on its initial mass:

  • **Small Mass Stars (like the Sun):** * After exhausting hydrogen fuel, the core contracts, and the outer layers expand and cool, forming a **red giant**. * The red giant eventually sheds its outer layers, forming a beautiful **planetary nebula**. * The remaining core collapses into a dense, hot **white dwarf**, which slowly cools over billions of years.
  • **Large Mass Stars:** * These stars are much hotter and brighter. After the main sequence, they expand into a **red supergiant**. * When their fuel runs out, their core collapses catastrophically, leading to a spectacular explosion called a **supernova**. * The remnant core can collapse further to form an incredibly dense **neutron star**.
  • **Very Large Mass Stars:** * Similar to large mass stars, they become **red supergiants** and then explode as a **supernova**. * However, if the remaining core is extremely massive (typically more than about three times the mass of the Sun), it collapses completely under its own gravity to form a **black hole**, a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape.

*Figure: A diagram illustrating the different life cycles of stars based on their initial mass.*

New Star and Planet Formation:

The immense explosion of a supernova disperses heavy elements (created within the star and during the explosion) into space, forming a new **nebula**. This enriched gas and dust can then, under gravitational attraction, collapse and form **new stars with orbiting planets**. This cycle of stellar birth, death, and rebirth is how the universe recycles matter and creates the building blocks for new solar systems.
