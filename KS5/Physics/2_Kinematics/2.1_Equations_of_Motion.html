2.1 Equations of Motion

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define and use distance, displacement, speed, velocity and acceleration.
  2. Use graphical methods to represent motion, including the determination of displacement from velocity-time graphs and velocity/acceleration from displacement-time/velocity-time graphs.
  3. Derive and use the equations for uniformly accelerated motion in a straight line.
  4. Describe an experiment to determine the acceleration of free fall, $g$.
  5. Describe and explain motion with uniform velocity in one direction and uniform acceleration in a perpendicular direction.

LO1: Defining Motion Quantities

Kinematics is built on a precise set of definitions. It is crucial to distinguish between scalar quantities (magnitude only) and vector quantities (magnitude and direction).

QuantityDefinitionTypeFormula
DistanceThe total path length covered.Scalar-
Displacement ($s$)The change in position in a specific direction.Vector-
SpeedThe rate of change of distance.Scalar$\text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}$
Velocity ($v$)The rate of change of displacement.Vector$v = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}$
Acceleration ($a$)The rate of change of velocity.Vector$a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$

LO2: Graphical Representation of Motion

Motion graphs are powerful tools for visualizing and analyzing motion. For a curve on a displacement-time graph, the gradient of the tangent at any point gives the instantaneous velocity. For a velocity-time graph, the gradient gives acceleration, and the area under the graph gives displacement.

LO3: The SUVAT Equations

For motion in a straight line with constant acceleration, we can derive and use a set of four key equations. These are known as the SUVAT equations.

Detailed Derivations of the SUVAT Equations

1. Deriving $v = u + at$:

We start from the definition of constant acceleration:

$$ a = \frac{\text{change in velocity}}{\text{time}} = \frac{v-u}{t} $$

Multiply both sides by $t$:

$$ at = v - u $$

Add $u$ to both sides to make $v$ the subject:

$$ v = u + at \quad \cdots(1) $$

2. Deriving $s = \frac{1}{2}(u+v)t$:

For an object moving with constant acceleration, its velocity changes linearly with time. Therefore, the average velocity, $\bar{v}$, is the arithmetic mean of the initial and final velocities.

$$ \bar{v} = \frac{u+v}{2} $$

The definition of average velocity is the total displacement divided by the total time:

$$ \bar{v} = \frac{s}{t} $$

By equating these two expressions for average velocity, we get:

$$ \frac{s}{t} = \frac{u+v}{2} $$

Multiplying both sides by $t$ gives the equation:

$$ s = \frac{1}{2}(u+v)t \quad \cdots(2) $$

3. Deriving $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$:

This equation is derived by eliminating the final velocity, $v$, from our first two equations. We substitute the expression for $v$ from equation (1) into equation (2):

$$ s = \frac{1}{2}(u + (u+at))t $$

Combine the terms inside the bracket:

$$ s = \frac{1}{2}(2u + at)t $$

Expand the bracket by multiplying by $t$:

$$ s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 $$

4. Deriving $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$:

This equation is derived by eliminating time, $t$. We rearrange equation (1) to make $t$ the subject:

$$ t = \frac{v-u}{a} $$

Now substitute this expression for $t$ into equation (2):

$$ s = \frac{1}{2}(u+v) \left( \frac{v-u}{a} \right) $$

Combine the terms in the numerator (this is a difference of two squares):

$$ s = \frac{(v+u)(v-u)}{2a} = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a} $$

Multiply both sides by $2a$ and rearrange to make $v^2$ the subject:

$$ v^2 = u^2 + 2as $$

LO4: Experiment to Determine 'g'

A common method to determine the acceleration of free fall, $g$, involves dropping an object and measuring the time it takes to fall a known distance. Using $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ with $u=0$ and $a=g$, we get $s = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$. By measuring $s$ and $t$, we can calculate $g$. To improve accuracy, a graph of $s$ against $t^2$ is plotted. The gradient will be $\frac{1}{2}g$, allowing for a more reliable value of $g$ to be determined.

LO5: Projectile Motion

Projectile motion is a key example of two-dimensional motion where the horizontal and vertical components are independent. The horizontal motion has constant velocity ($a_x=0$), while the vertical motion has constant downward acceleration ($a_y=-g$).

Worked Example: Projectile Motion

A projectile is fired with an initial velocity of 50 m s⁻¹ at an angle of 30° to the horizontal. Find the time of flight and the horizontal range.

1. Resolve initial velocity:

$u_x = u \cos\theta = 50 \cos(30^\circ) = 43.3$ m s⁻¹

$u_y = u \sin\theta = 50 \sin(30^\circ) = 25.0$ m s⁻¹

2. Analyse vertical motion to find time of flight ($t$):

The projectile lands when its displacement $s_y = 0$. Using $s_y = u_y t + \frac{1}{2}a_y t^2$:

$0 = 25t + \frac{1}{2}(-9.81)t^2 = t(25 - 4.905t)$.

This gives $t=0$ (start) or $25 - 4.905t = 0 \implies t = \frac{25}{4.905} = 5.10$ s.

3. Analyse horizontal motion to find range ($s_x$):

$s_x = u_x t = 43.3 \times 5.10 = 221$ m.
