13.2 Gravitational force between point masses

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that, for a point outside a uniform sphere, the mass of the sphere may be considered to be a point mass at its centre.
  2. Recall and use Newton’s law of gravitation $F = Gm_1m_2 / r^2$ for the force between two point masses.
  3. Analyse circular orbits in gravitational fields by relating the gravitational force to the centripetal acceleration it causes.
  4. Understand that a satellite in a geostationary orbit remains at the same point above the Earth’s surface, with an orbital period of 24 hours, orbiting from west to east, directly above the Equator.

LO1 & LO2: Newton's Law of Gravitation and Point Masses

Isaac Newton formulated a law to describe the magnitude of the gravitational force between any two point masses in the universe.

Any two point masses attract each other with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centres.

$$ F = \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2} $$

A crucial simplification, known as **Newton's Shell Theorem**, is that for any point outside a uniform spherical body (like a planet), the gravitational force exerted by the sphere is identical to what it would be if all the sphere's mass were concentrated into a single point at its centre. This allows us to use the equation above for large objects like planets and stars by measuring the distance $r$ from their centres.

Worked Example: Force between Earth and Moon

Calculate the magnitude of the gravitational force between the Earth and the Moon. (Mass of Earth = $5.97 \times 10^{24}$ kg; Mass of Moon = $7.35 \times 10^{22}$ kg; Mean distance between centres = $3.84 \times 10^8$ m).

$$ F = \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2} = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times (7.35 \times 10^{22})}{(3.84 \times 10^8)^2} $$

$$ F = \frac{2.926 \times 10^{37}}{1.475 \times 10^{17}} = 1.98 \times 10^{20} \text{ N} $$

LO3: Circular Orbits

For a satellite of mass $m$ to maintain a stable circular orbit of radius $r$ around a planet of mass $M$, there must be a resultant force acting on it. This force is the **centripetal force**, and it is provided entirely by the gravitational force between the planet and the satellite.

Derivation of Orbital Speed and Period

1. **Equate the forces:**

$$ \text{Gravitational Force} = \text{Centripetal Force} $$

$$ \frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{GMm}{r^2} = mr\omega^2 $$

2. **Derive Orbital Speed ($v$):** Using the first form, we can cancel $m$ and one factor of $r$.

$$ \frac{GM}{r} = v^2 \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}} $$

3. **Derive Orbital Period ($T$):** Using the second form, we can cancel $m$. We also know $\omega = 2\pi/T$.

$$ \frac{GM}{r^2} = r\omega^2 = r\left(\frac{2\pi}{T}\right)^2 = \frac{4\pi^2r}{T^2} $$

Rearranging for $T^2$ gives Kepler's Third Law for circular orbits:

$$ T^2 = \left(\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}\right)r^3 $$

LO4: Geostationary Orbits

A **geostationary orbit** is a special type of orbit in which a satellite appears to remain fixed in the same position in the sky when viewed from the ground. This is essential for communications and weather satellites. For this to be possible, three conditions must be met:

  1. The satellite must have an orbital **period of 24 hours** (or more precisely, one sidereal day: 86,164 seconds) to match the Earth's rotation.
  2. The satellite must orbit directly above the **Equator**.
  3. The satellite must orbit from **west to east**, the same direction as the Earth's rotation.

Worked Example: Radius of a Geostationary Orbit

Calculate the radius of the orbit for a geostationary satellite above the Earth. (Mass of Earth, $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24}$ kg).

1. State the required period in SI units:

$$ T = 24 \text{ hours} = 24 \times 60 \times 60 = 86400 \text{ s} $$

2. Start with the formula for orbital period:

$$ T^2 = \left(\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}\right)r^3 $$

3. Rearrange to make $r$ the subject:

$$ r^3 = \frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2} \implies r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}} $$

4. Substitute values and solve:

$$ r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})(86400)^2}{4\pi^2}} $$

$$ r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{2.97 \times 10^{42}}{39.48}} = \sqrt[3]{7.52 \times 10^{22}} $$

$$ r = 4.22 \times 10^7 \text{ m} \text{ (or 42,200 km)} $$

This is the radius from the centre of the Earth.
