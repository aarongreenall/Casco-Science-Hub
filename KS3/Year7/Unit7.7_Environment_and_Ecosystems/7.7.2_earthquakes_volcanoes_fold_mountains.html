7.7.2 Earthquakes, volcanoes, and fold mountains

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Explain that earthquakes and volcanoes are found at the boundaries between tectonic plates.
  2. Describe the three main types of plate boundary: convergent, divergent, and transform.
  3. Relate the formation of volcanoes and fold mountains to different types of plate boundary.

Where Plates Meet

The Earth's most dramatic geological events—massive earthquakes, explosive volcanoes, and the creation of huge mountain ranges—are not random. They happen in specific zones, which are the **boundaries** where the tectonic plates meet. The way the plates interact at these boundaries determines what kind of geological features are formed.

Convergent Boundaries (Plates Colliding)

At a convergent boundary, two tectonic plates are moving towards each other and colliding. What happens next depends on the type of plates. If two continental plates collide, they have a similar density, so neither can slide under the other. Instead, the immense pressure buckles and pushes the rock upwards, creating enormous **fold mountains**.

Real-World Example: The **Himalayan mountain range** was formed (and is still growing) due to the collision between the Indian Plate and the Eurasian Plate. If an oceanic plate collides with a continental plate, the denser oceanic plate is forced underneath in a process called subduction. This often creates a line of volcanoes, like those in the **Pacific Ring of Fire**.

Divergent Boundaries (Plates Separating)

At a divergent boundary, two tectonic plates are moving away from each other. This creates a gap between the plates, allowing hot magma from the mantle to rise to the surface. When the magma cools, it forms new crust.

Real-World Example: The **Mid-Atlantic Ridge** is a massive underwater mountain range in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean where the North American and Eurasian plates are pulling apart. This process, known as seafloor spreading, creates volcanoes and shallow earthquakes along the ridge.

Transform Boundaries (Plates Sliding)

At a transform boundary, two plates are sliding past each other horizontally. The edges of the plates are rough and can get stuck. As the rest of the plates continue to move, huge amounts of stress build up in the stuck sections. When the stress becomes too great, the rocks suddenly break or slip, releasing the stored energy as seismic waves, which we feel as an **earthquake**.

Real-World Example: The **San Andreas Fault** in California is a famous transform boundary between the Pacific Plate and the North American Plate. The city of San Francisco is located on this fault line and has a long history of powerful earthquakes, including the great earthquake of 1906.
