19.1 Capacitors and capacitance

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define capacitance, as applied to both isolated spherical conductors and to parallel plate capacitors.
  2. Recall and use $C = Q / V$.
  3. Derive, using $C = Q / V$, formulae for the combined capacitance of capacitors in series and in parallel.
  4. Use the capacitance formulae for capacitors in series and in parallel.

LO1 & LO2: The Concept of Capacitance

Why Use a Capacitor?

A **capacitor** is an electrical component that stores electric charge, and therefore electrical potential energy. While a battery also stores energy, a capacitor can release its energy much, much faster. This makes them essential for a wide range of applications:

  • Smoothing Voltage: In power supplies that convert AC to DC, capacitors are used to smooth out the rectified voltage, providing a steady DC output.
  • Timing Circuits: The predictable way a capacitor charges and discharges allows it to be used as the basis for timing circuits.
  • Energy Storage for Fast Discharge: Applications like a camera flash or a defibrillator require a large burst of energy in a very short time, which is a key function of a capacitor.
  • Tuning and Filtering: In radios and audio equipment, capacitors are used in circuits that select or filter out specific frequencies.

How a Parallel Plate Capacitor Works

The most common type of capacitor consists of two parallel conducting plates separated by an insulating material called a **dielectric**. When connected to a power source like a battery:

  1. The battery pulls electrons from the plate connected to its positive terminal, making that plate positively charged.
  2. The battery pushes these electrons onto the plate connected to its negative terminal, making that plate negatively charged.
  3. This process continues until the potential difference across the capacitor plates is equal to the e.m.f. of the battery. At this point, the capacitor is fully charged.
  4. An electric field is established in the dielectric between the two oppositely charged plates, and this field is where the electrical potential energy is stored.

Analogy: The Re-chargeable Battery

Think of a capacitor like a small, very fast re-chargeable battery. It can't hold nearly as much energy as a normal battery, but it can be charged up almost instantly and, more importantly, can release all of its stored energy in a sudden, powerful burst.

Real-World Capacitors

Defining Capacitance

The ability of a capacitor to store charge is known as its **capacitance ($C$)**.

Capacitance is defined as the ratio of the charge stored on an object to the potential difference across it.

$$ C = \frac{Q}{V} $$

The SI unit of capacitance is the **farad (F)**, where one farad is one coulomb per volt (1 F = 1 C V⁻¹). The farad is a very large unit, so capacitance is often measured in microfarads ($\mu$F, $10^{-6}$ F), nanofarads (nF, $10^{-9}$ F), or picofarads (pF, $10^{-12}$ F).

LO3 & LO4: Capacitors in Combination

Capacitors in Parallel

Derivation for Parallel Capacitors

1. In a parallel circuit, the potential difference $V$ across each capacitor is the same as the source voltage.

2. The total charge $Q_T$ stored is the sum of the charges stored on each capacitor: $Q_T = Q_1 + Q_2 + \dots$

3. From the definition of capacitance, $Q=CV$. Substitute this into the charge equation:

$$ C_T V = C_1 V + C_2 V + \dots $$

4. Since the voltage $V$ is common to all terms, it can be cancelled out:

$$ C_T = C_1 + C_2 + \dots $$

Capacitors in Series

Derivation for Series Capacitors

1. In a series circuit, the charge $Q$ stored on each capacitor is the same.

2. The total potential difference $V_T$ from the source is shared between the capacitors: $V_T = V_1 + V_2 + \dots$

3. From the definition of capacitance, $V=Q/C$. Substitute this into the voltage equation:

$$ \frac{Q}{C_T} = \frac{Q}{C_1} + \frac{Q}{C_2} + \dots $$

4. Since the charge $Q$ is common to all terms, it can be cancelled out:

$$ \frac{1}{C_T} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \dots $$

Worked Example: Combined Capacitance

A 10 $\mu$F capacitor and a 20 $\mu$F capacitor are connected to a 12 V supply. Calculate the total capacitance and the total charge stored if they are connected (a) in parallel, and (b) in series.

(a) In Parallel:

$$ C_T = C_1 + C_2 = 10 + 20 = 30 \, \mu\text{F} $$

$$ Q_T = C_T V = (30 \times 10^{-6} \text{ F}) \times (12 \text{ V}) = 3.6 \times 10^{-4} \text{ C} $$

(b) In Series:

$$ \frac{1}{C_T} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{20} = \frac{2+1}{20} = \frac{3}{20} $$

$$ C_T = \frac{20}{3} = 6.7 \, \mu\text{F} $$

$$ Q_T = C_T V = (6.7 \times 10^{-6} \text{ F}) \times (12 \text{ V}) = 8.0 \times 10^{-5} \text{ C} $$
