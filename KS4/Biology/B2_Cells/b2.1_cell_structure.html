B2.1 Cell structure

Cells are the fundamental building blocks of all living organisms. They are the smallest units of life that can replicate independently, and they carry out all the necessary functions for life. Understanding cell structure is crucial for comprehending how organisms are organized and how biological processes occur.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe and compare the structure of a plant cell with an animal cell, limited to: cell wall, cell membrane, nucleus, cytoplasm, chloroplasts, ribosomes, mitochondria, vacuoles.
  2. Describe the structure of a bacterial cell, limited to: cell wall, cell membrane, cytoplasm, ribosomes, circular DNA, plasmids.
  3. Identify the cell structures listed in 2.1.1 and 2.1.2 in diagrams and images of plant, animal and bacterial cells.
  4. Describe the functions of the structures listed in 2.1.1 and 2.1.2 in plant, animal and bacterial cells.
  5. State that new cells are produced by division of existing cells.
  6. State that specialised cells have specific functions, limited to: ciliated cells – movement of mucus in the trachea and bronchi; root hair cells – absorption; palisade mesophyll cells – photosynthesis; neurones – conduction of electrical impulses; red blood cells – transport of oxygen; sperm and egg cells (gametes) – reproduction.
  7. Describe the meaning of the terms: cell, tissue, organ, organ system and organism as illustrated by examples given in the syllabus.

Comparing Plant and Animal Cells:

Both plant and animal cells are eukaryotic, meaning they have a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. However, they have distinct structural differences that reflect their different functions.

*Figure: A diagram comparing the key structures of a plant cell and an animal cell.*

  • **Cell Wall:** (Plant cells only) A rigid outer layer made of cellulose, providing support and protection.
  • **Cell Membrane:** (Both) A partially permeable barrier that controls what enters and leaves the cell.
  • **Nucleus:** (Both) Contains the cell's genetic material (DNA) and controls cell activities.
  • **Cytoplasm:** (Both) Jelly-like substance filling the cell, where most chemical reactions occur.
  • **Chloroplasts:** (Plant cells only) Contain chlorophyll and are the site of photosynthesis.
  • **Ribosomes:** (Both) Small organelles responsible for protein synthesis.
  • **Mitochondria:** (Both) Site of aerobic respiration, where energy (ATP) is released.
  • **Vacuoles:** (Both, but much larger in plant cells) Plant cells have a large, permanent central vacuole filled with cell sap, which helps maintain turgor pressure. Animal cells may have small, temporary vacuoles.

Bacterial Cell Structure:

Bacterial cells are prokaryotic, meaning they do not have a true nucleus or membrane-bound organelles. Their structure is simpler than eukaryotic cells.

  • **Cell Wall:** (Bacterial cells) Rigid outer layer, different in composition from plant cell walls.
  • **Cell Membrane:** (Bacterial cells) Controls what enters and leaves the cell.
  • **Cytoplasm:** (Bacterial cells) Jelly-like substance filling the cell.
  • **Ribosomes:** (Bacterial cells) Responsible for protein synthesis.
  • **Circular DNA:** (Bacterial cells) Genetic material is a single, circular chromosome, not enclosed in a nucleus.
  • **Plasmids:** (Bacterial cells) Small, circular pieces of extra DNA that carry additional genes (e.g., for antibiotic resistance).

*Figure: A diagram of a bacterial cell, showing its key structures.*

Specialised Cells and Levels of Organization:

New cells are produced by the **division of existing cells** (e.g., through mitosis). In multicellular organisms, cells often become **specialised** to perform specific functions.

  • **Ciliated cells:** Have cilia (hair-like structures) to move mucus in the trachea and bronchi.
  • **Root hair cells:** Have a large surface area to absorb water and mineral ions from the soil.
  • **Palisade mesophyll cells:** Packed with chloroplasts for photosynthesis in leaves.
  • **Neurones (nerve cells):** Long and thin to conduct electrical impulses.
  • **Red blood cells:** Biconcave shape and no nucleus to maximize oxygen transport (contain haemoglobin).
  • **Sperm and egg cells (gametes):** Involved in reproduction. Sperm are motile, egg cells store energy.

Living organisms exhibit a hierarchy of organization:

  • **Cell:** The basic unit of life.
  • **Tissue:** A group of similar cells working together to perform a specific function (e.g., muscle tissue).
  • **Organ:** A group of different tissues working together to perform a specific function (e.g., heart, stomach).
  • **Organ System:** A group of different organs working together to perform a major function (e.g., digestive system, circulatory system).
  • **Organism:** A complete living being made up of various organ systems.
