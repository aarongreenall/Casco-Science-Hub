9.7.5 Rate of Reaction

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the effects of concentration, surface area, and temperature on the rate of reaction, and explain them using the particle model.

What is Rate of Reaction?

Some chemical reactions are incredibly fast, like an explosion, while others are extremely slow, like the rusting of iron. The **rate of reaction** is a measure of how quickly the reactants are converted into products. We can measure it by finding out how much product is made (or how much reactant is used up) in a certain period of time.

Analogy: Making Popcorn

Think of the reaction rate as how quickly your popcorn pops in the microwave. A **fast rate** means all the kernels pop within two minutes. A **slow rate** might mean it takes five minutes for them all to pop. You could measure the rate by counting how many kernels pop every 10 seconds.

Collision Theory: The Key to Speed

For a chemical reaction to happen, the reactant particles must physically collide with each other. But not just any collision will do! **Collision Theory** states that for a reaction to occur, particles must collide with **sufficient energy** (known as the activation energy).

The rate of reaction depends on the frequency of successful collisions between reactant particles.

Factors Affecting the Rate of Reaction

If we want to speed up a reaction, we need to increase the number of successful collisions per second. We can do this by changing three main factors.

1. Temperature

Increasing the temperature increases the reaction rate. This is because heating gives the particles more kinetic energy. They move faster, leading to **more frequent collisions**, and the collisions are **more energetic**, so they are more likely to be successful.

2. Concentration

Increasing the concentration of a reactant (or the pressure of a gas) increases the reaction rate. This is because there are more reactant particles packed into the same volume, so **collisions are more frequent**.

3. Surface Area

For a solid reactant, breaking it into smaller pieces (increasing its surface area) increases the reaction rate. This is because more particles of the solid are exposed and available for collisions, leading to **more frequent collisions**.

Summary of Factors

Factor How to Increase It Effect on Rate Explanation (in terms of collisions)
Temperature Heat the reactants Increases Particles move faster, leading to more frequent and more energetic collisions.
Concentration Add more reactant to the same volume Increases More particles in the same space leads to more frequent collisions.
Surface Area Crush a solid into a powder Increases More particles are exposed, leading to more frequent collisions.

At-Home Experiment: Fizzing Race

You can easily see the effect of temperature on reaction rate in your kitchen.

You will need:

  • Two identical glasses
  • Cold water and warm water
  • Two effervescent tablets (like Alka-Seltzer or vitamin C tablets)
  • A stopwatch or phone timer

Procedure:

  1. Fill one glass with cold water and the other with the same amount of warm water.
  2. Get your timer ready. Simultaneously drop one tablet into each glass and start the timer.
  3. Observe the fizzing reaction. Which tablet dissolves completely first? Record the times.

Scientific Explanation:

The tablet will dissolve much faster in the warm water. The higher temperature gives the water and tablet particles more energy, causing them to collide more frequently and with more force, speeding up the reaction rate.
