22.1 Energy and momentum of a photon

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that electromagnetic radiation has a particulate nature.
  2. Understand that a photon is a quantum of electromagnetic energy.
  3. Recall and use $E = hf$.
  4. Use the electronvolt (eV) as a unit of energy.
  5. Understand that a photon has momentum and that the momentum is given by $p = E / c$.

LO1 & LO2: The Particulate Nature of Light

For centuries, the nature of light was a central question in physics. Isaac Newton's highly influential 'corpuscular' theory proposed that light was composed of tiny particles. This view was the prevailing consensus for over a century.

However, in the early 1800s, Thomas Young's double-slit experiment provided definitive proof of light's wave-like properties, showing that it could diffract and create interference patterns. This discovery caused a major shift in scientific thought, and the wave model became the universally accepted explanation for the nature of light.

This consensus was challenged at the turn of the 20th century. New experimental results, most notably the photoelectric effect, could not be explained by the wave model. To account for these observations, Albert Einstein, building on the work of Max Planck, proposed that light energy was not a continuous wave but was quantised into discrete packets. This revived the particle-like view of light in a new form, leading to the modern understanding of wave-particle duality, where light exhibits both wave and particle properties. The fundamental particle of light is called the photon.

A **photon** is a quantum (a discrete packet) of electromagnetic energy.

Explanation: The Photon

LO3: The Photon Energy Equation

The energy of a single photon is directly proportional to the frequency of the electromagnetic radiation. This relationship is given by the Planck-Einstein relation:

$$ E = hf $$

  • $E$ is the energy of the photon in joules (J).
  • $h$ is the **Planck constant**, a fundamental constant with a value of $6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J s.
  • $f$ is the frequency of the electromagnetic radiation in hertz (Hz).

Since $c = f\lambda$, we can also express the photon energy in terms of its wavelength:

$$ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} $$

LO4: The Electronvolt (eV)

The energy of a single photon is extremely small when measured in joules. For convenience in atomic and particle physics, a smaller unit of energy called the **electronvolt (eV)** is used.

One electronvolt is the energy gained or lost by an electron when it moves through a potential difference of one volt.

From the definition of potential difference, $W=VQ$. The work done on an electron ($q=e$) moving through 1 V is:

$$ 1 \text{ eV} = (1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) \times (1 \text{ V}) = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} $$

Worked Example: Energy Conversion

A photon of blue light has a wavelength of 450 nm. Calculate its energy in (a) joules, and (b) electronvolts.

(a) Energy in Joules:

$$ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{450 \times 10^{-9}} $$

$$ E = 4.42 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} $$

(b) Energy in Electronvolts:

$$ E \text{ (in eV)} = \frac{E \text{ (in J)}}{1.60 \times 10^{-19}} = \frac{4.42 \times 10^{-19}}{1.60 \times 10^{-19}} $$

$$ E = 2.76 \text{ eV} $$

LO5: Photon Momentum

Although photons have zero rest mass, they possess momentum. This is a consequence of Einstein's theory of relativity, which shows that energy and momentum are related. For a particle with zero rest mass, like a photon, the relationship simplifies.

$$ p = \frac{E}{c} $$

We can combine this with the photon energy equation ($E=hf$) and the wave equation ($c=f\lambda$) to get another important relationship:

$$ p = \frac{hf}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda} $$

Worked Example: Photon Momentum

Calculate the momentum of a photon of the blue light from the previous example ($\lambda = 450$ nm).

Method 1: Using $p=E/c$

From the previous example, $E = 4.42 \times 10^{-19}$ J.

$$ p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{4.42 \times 10^{-19}}{3.00 \times 10^8} = 1.47 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg m s}^{-1} $$

Method 2: Using $p=h/\lambda$

$$ p = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}{450 \times 10^{-9}} = 1.47 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg m s}^{-1} $$
