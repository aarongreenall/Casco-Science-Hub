P1.7 Pressure

Pressure is a fundamental concept in physics that describes how a force is distributed over an area. Understanding pressure helps explain a wide range of phenomena, from the sharpness of a knife to the forces exerted by fluids.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define pressure as force per unit area; recall and use the equation $P = F/A$.
  2. Describe how pressure is exerted by solids and how it can be changed.
  3. Describe how the pressure in a liquid changes with depth and density.
  4. Recall and use the equation for pressure in a liquid $P = \rho gh$.
  5. Describe how the pressure in a gas changes with temperature and volume.
  6. Describe the action of a hydraulic system as a force multiplier.

Understanding Pressure:

**Pressure ($P$)** is defined as the force ($F$) applied perpendicular to a surface divided by the area ($A$) over which the force is distributed. The equation for pressure is:

$P = \frac{F}{A}$

The standard SI unit for pressure is the Pascal (Pa), which is equivalent to one Newton per square meter (N/m$^2$). This definition highlights that a given force can produce a very different pressure depending on the area it acts upon. For example, a sharp knife exerts a high pressure due to its small contact area, allowing it to cut easily.

Pressure in Liquids:

Liquids also exert pressure, and this pressure increases with depth and with the density of the liquid. The deeper you go in a liquid, the more liquid is above you, and thus the greater the weight of the liquid pressing down. Denser liquids will also exert more pressure at the same depth because they have more mass per unit volume. The equation for pressure in a liquid is:

$P = \rho gh$

Where $\rho$ (rho) is the density of the liquid, $g$ is the gravitational field strength, and $h$ is the depth.

Pressure in Gases:

Gases exert pressure due to the collisions of their particles with the walls of their container. The pressure of a gas is affected by its temperature and volume. Increasing the temperature of a gas (at constant volume) increases the kinetic energy of its particles, leading to more frequent and forceful collisions, thus increasing pressure. Decreasing the volume of a gas (at constant temperature) forces the particles into a smaller space, increasing the frequency of collisions and thus increasing pressure.

This video from **FreeScienceLessons** (2:37 min) explains how the pressure in a gas changes with temperature and volume.

Hydraulic Systems:

Hydraulic systems use liquids to transmit and multiply forces. They work on the principle that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every portion of the fluid and the walls of the containing vessel (Pascal's Principle). By applying a small force over a small area on one side, a much larger force can be generated over a larger area on the other side. This makes hydraulic systems effective force multipliers, commonly found in car brakes, hydraulic jacks, and construction machinery.
