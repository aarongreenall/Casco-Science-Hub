B13.2 Hormones

In addition to the nervous system, the body also uses the **endocrine system** to coordinate and regulate various functions. The endocrine system relies on **hormones**, chemical messengers produced by glands and transported by the blood to target organs, where they elicit specific responses. Understanding hormones is crucial for comprehending processes like blood glucose regulation and the 'fight or flight' response.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe hormones as chemical messengers, produced by glands, transported in the blood to target organs, where they alter the activity of one or more specific target organs.
  2. Describe the role of adrenaline in the chemical control of the heart rate.
  3. Describe the control of blood glucose concentration by the liver, pancreas and insulin.
  4. Describe the causes of Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes.

What are Hormones?

**Hormones** are **chemical messengers** produced by specialized organs called **glands** (endocrine glands). Once produced, hormones are secreted directly into the bloodstream and transported by the blood to specific **target organs** or cells throughout the body. At these target sites, hormones bind to specific receptors and **alter the activity** of the target organ, initiating a particular physiological response.

Hormonal responses are generally slower but longer-lasting than nervous responses.

*Figure: Diagram showing the major endocrine glands in the human body that produce hormones.*

Role of Adrenaline:

**Adrenaline** is a hormone produced by the adrenal glands (located above the kidneys). It plays a crucial role in the body's 'fight or flight' response, providing rapid chemical control of the heart rate and other physiological changes in response to stress, fear, or excitement.

When adrenaline is released into the bloodstream, it travels to target organs, including the heart. At the heart, adrenaline causes:

  • An **increase in heart rate**.
  • An **increase in the force of heart contractions**.

This increases blood flow to muscles, brain, and other vital organs, preparing the body for intense physical activity or rapid decision-making.

Control of Blood Glucose Concentration:

Maintaining a stable **blood glucose concentration** is critical for cell function, as glucose is the primary source of energy for respiration. This is a tightly regulated process involving the **pancreas** and the hormone **insulin**.

  • **Pancreas:** Contains specialized cells (islets of Langerhans) that monitor blood glucose levels and produce hormones.
  • **Insulin:** A hormone produced by the pancreas when blood glucose levels are too high (e.g., after a meal). * **Action:** Insulin travels in the blood to the **liver** and muscle cells, causing them to take up glucose from the blood and convert it into **glycogen** (a storage carbohydrate). This lowers blood glucose levels.
  • **Glucagon (not explicitly required, but for context):** Another hormone produced by the pancreas when blood glucose levels are too low. It causes the liver to convert stored glycogen back into glucose and release it into the blood, raising blood glucose levels.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the hormonal control of blood glucose concentration.*

Diabetes Mellitus:

**Diabetes mellitus** is a chronic condition characterized by persistently high blood glucose levels, resulting from problems with insulin production or action.

  • **Type 1 Diabetes:** * **Cause:** An autoimmune disease where the body's immune system attacks and destroys the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. * **Effect:** The body produces little or no insulin. Requires regular insulin injections.
  • **Type 2 Diabetes:** * **Cause:** Often develops later in life, linked to lifestyle factors (obesity, lack of exercise) and genetics. The body either does not produce enough insulin or the body's cells become resistant to insulin (insulin resistance). * **Effect:** Blood glucose levels remain high. Can often be managed through diet, exercise, and medication, but may eventually require insulin.
