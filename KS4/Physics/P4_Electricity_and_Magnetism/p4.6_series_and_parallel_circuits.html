P4.6 Series and parallel circuits

Electric circuits can be constructed in various configurations, with the most common being series and parallel arrangements. Understanding how components behave in these different setups is fundamental to designing and analyzing electrical systems, from household wiring to complex electronic devices.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Know how to construct and use series and parallel circuits.
  2. Know the advantages of connecting lamps in parallel in a circuit.

Series Circuits:

In a **series circuit**, components are connected end-to-end, forming a single path for the electric current to flow.

  • **Current:** The current is **the same at every point** in a series circuit. If one component breaks, the entire circuit is broken, and current stops flowing to all components.
  • **Voltage:** The voltage from the source is **shared** among the components. The sum of the potential differences across each component equals the total voltage of the source.
  • **Resistance:** The total resistance of resistors in series is the sum of their individual resistances ($R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + ...$).

*Figure: A simple series circuit diagram with two resistors.*

Parallel Circuits:

In a **parallel circuit**, components are connected across the same two points, creating multiple paths for the electric current to flow.

  • **Current:** The total current from the source is **divided** among the parallel branches. The sum of the currents in each branch equals the total current from the source.
  • **Voltage:** The voltage (potential difference) across each branch in a parallel circuit is **the same** and equal to the source voltage.
  • **Resistance:** The total resistance of resistors in parallel is always **less than the smallest individual resistance**. This is because adding more parallel paths provides more routes for current, reducing overall opposition.

*Figure: A simple parallel circuit diagram with two resistors.*

Advantages of Parallel Connections for Lamps:

Connecting lamps (or other household appliances) in parallel is advantageous for several reasons:

  • **Independent Operation:** Each lamp can be switched on or off independently without affecting the others.
  • **Constant Brightness:** Each lamp receives the full supply voltage, so they shine at their normal brightness.
  • **Circuit Continuity:** If one lamp breaks or burns out, the other lamps in the circuit continue to function, as there are alternative paths for the current. This is unlike a series circuit, where all lamps would go out.
