9.6.3 Current in Parallel Circuits

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain how current behaves in a parallel circuit.

Parallel Circuit:

  • A circuit where the components are connected in separate branches.
  • The current has more than one path to take.
  • If one component breaks, the other branches of the circuit will continue to work. This is why household lighting is wired in parallel.

Rules for Current in Parallel Circuits:

  • The total current flowing from the cell/battery is split between the different branches.
  • The current in each branch depends on the resistance of that branch.
  • The total current flowing into a junction (a point where the circuit splits) is equal to the total current flowing out of that junction. (This is known as Kirchhoff's First Law).
  • Formula: $I_{total} = I_1 + I_2 + ...$ (where $I_1$, $I_2$, etc., are the currents in the individual branches).

Comparison with Series Circuits:

  • Series: Current is the same everywhere.
  • Parallel: Current splits between the branches.
