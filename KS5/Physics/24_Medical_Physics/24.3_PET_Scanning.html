LO1 & LO2: Radiotracers for PET Scanning

**Positron Emission Tomography (PET)** is a medical imaging technique that provides information about the metabolic function of tissues, rather than just their structure. It relies on a substance called a **radiotracer**.

A tracer is a substance containing radioactive nuclei that can be introduced into the body and is then absorbed by the tissue being studied.

For PET scanning, the tracer is a biologically active molecule (like glucose) that has been tagged with a radioactive isotope that decays by **beta-plus ($\beta^+$) decay**. Beta-plus decay emits a **positron** (the antiparticle of an electron). A common tracer is Fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG), which contains the positron-emitting isotope Fluorine-18.

Metabolically active tissues, such as cancerous tumours or active parts of the brain, have a high uptake of glucose. Therefore, the FDG tracer will concentrate in these areas, allowing them to be imaged.

LO3, LO4 & LO5: Annihilation and Photon Energy

The positron emitted by the tracer is the key to PET imaging. After being emitted, it travels a very short distance (a few millimetres) through the tissue before it encounters an electron. Since the positron is the antiparticle of the electron, they undergo **annihilation**.

**Annihilation** is the process that occurs when a particle interacts with its corresponding antiparticle. The total mass of the particle-antiparticle pair is converted into energy in the form of two or more high-energy photons (gamma rays).

In this process, both **mass-energy** and **momentum** are conserved. To conserve momentum (assuming the electron and positron are almost stationary before annihilation), two identical gamma-ray photons must be produced, travelling in exactly opposite directions.

Worked Example: Energy of Gamma Photons

Calculate the energy of each gamma-ray photon produced during the annihilation of an electron-positron pair. (Mass of an electron, $m_e = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ kg).

1. Calculate the total mass converted to energy:

The total mass is the mass of the electron plus the mass of the positron (which is the same).

$$ \Delta m = 2 \times m_e = 2 \times (9.11 \times 10^{-31}) = 1.822 \times 10^{-30} \text{ kg} $$

2. Use Einstein's mass-energy equivalence to find the total energy released:

$$ E_{total} = \Delta m c^2 = (1.822 \times 10^{-30}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)^2 $$

$$ E_{total} = 1.64 \times 10^{-13} \text{ J} $$

3. Find the energy of a single photon:

This total energy is shared equally between the two gamma photons.

$$ E_{photon} = \frac{E_{total}}{2} = \frac{1.64 \times 10^{-13}}{2} = 8.2 \times 10^{-14} \text{ J} $$

4. Convert the photon energy to Mega-electronvolts (MeV):

First, convert from Joules to electronvolts ($1 \text{ eV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$).

$$ E_{photon} (\text{in eV}) = \frac{8.2 \times 10^{-14}}{1.60 \times 10^{-19}} = 512500 \text{ eV} $$

Then, convert from eV to MeV ($1 \text{ MeV} = 10^6 \text{ eV}$).

$$ E_{photon} (\text{in MeV}) = \frac{512500}{10^6} = 0.51 \text{ MeV} $$

LO6: Image Creation in PET

A PET scanner consists of a ring of gamma-ray detectors surrounding the patient. The gamma photons produced by annihilation travel out of the body and are detected by this ring.

The key to forming an image is **coincidence detection**. The scanner's electronics are programmed to only register an event if two photons are detected at opposite sides of the ring at almost exactly the same time. This indicates that an annihilation event must have occurred somewhere along the line connecting the two detectors (the "line of response").

By collecting data from millions of these annihilation events, a computer can use complex algorithms to reconstruct a 3D map of the tracer's concentration within the body. Areas with a high concentration of the tracer (high metabolic activity) will be the source of many annihilation events and will appear as bright "hotspots" on the final PET scan image.
