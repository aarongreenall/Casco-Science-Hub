P2.3 Conduction

Conduction is one of the primary methods by which thermal energy (heat) is transferred. It is particularly important in solids, where particles are closely packed, but it also occurs in liquids and gases to a lesser extent. Understanding conduction helps explain why some materials feel hot or cold to the touch, and how heat moves through various objects.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Identify and give examples of typical good thermal conductors and bad thermal conductors (thermal insulators).
  2. Describe thermal conduction in solids in terms of atomic or molecular lattice vibrations and also in terms of the movement of delocalised (mobile) electrons in metallic conductors.

Understanding Thermal Conduction:

**Thermal conduction** is the transfer of thermal energy through a material by the vibration and collision of particles, or by the movement of free electrons. Heat energy is passed from hotter parts of the material to colder parts without the overall movement of the material itself.

Conduction in Non-Metals (Lattice Vibrations):

In non-metallic solids, liquids, and gases, thermal energy is primarily transferred through **lattice vibrations** (or molecular collisions in fluids). When one part of the material is heated, its particles gain kinetic energy and vibrate more vigorously. These vibrating particles collide with neighboring particles, transferring some of their energy. This process continues, passing thermal energy through the material. Materials where particles are far apart (like gases) or have weaker bonds are generally poor conductors (insulators).

Conduction in Metals (Delocalised Electrons):

Metals are exceptionally good thermal conductors due to the presence of **delocalised (mobile) electrons**. In addition to lattice vibrations, these free electrons can move rapidly throughout the metallic lattice. When metals are heated, these delocalised electrons gain kinetic energy and move faster. They then collide with other electrons and positive metal ions, transferring their energy quickly across the material. This efficient transfer by mobile electrons makes metals much better conductors than non-metals.

Good Conductors vs. Insulators:

**Good thermal conductors** are materials that allow heat to transfer through them easily. Examples include most metals (e.g., copper, aluminum, iron).

**Bad thermal conductors** (or **thermal insulators**) are materials that resist the transfer of heat. Examples include wood, plastic, air, and polystyrene. These materials are often used to prevent heat loss or gain.
