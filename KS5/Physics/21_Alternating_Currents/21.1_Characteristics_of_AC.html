21.1 Characteristics of a.c.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand and use the terms period, frequency and peak value as applied to an alternating current or voltage.
  2. Use equations of the form $x = x_0 \sin \omega t$ representing a sinusoidally alternating current or voltage.
  3. Recall and use the fact that the mean power in a resistive load is half the maximum power for a sinusoidal alternating current.
  4. Distinguish between root-mean-square (r.m.s.) and peak values and recall and use $I_{r.m.s.} = I_0 / \sqrt{2}$ and $V_{r.m.s.} = V_0 / \sqrt{2}$ for a sinusoidal alternating current.

LO1 & LO2: Describing Alternating Currents

An **alternating current (a.c.)** is an electric current that periodically reverses direction, in contrast to direct current (d.c.), which flows in a single direction. The most common form of a.c. is a **sinusoidal** waveform, where the current and voltage vary as a sine or cosine function of time.

  • Period (T): The time taken for one complete cycle of the alternating current.
  • Frequency (f): The number of complete cycles per second ($f=1/T$).
  • Peak Value ($I_0$ or $V_0$): The maximum value of the current or voltage in a cycle. This is the amplitude of the sine wave.

The instantaneous value of a sinusoidal current ($I$) or voltage ($V$) at time $t$ can be described by the equations:

$$ I = I_0 \sin(\omega t) \quad \text{and} \quad V = V_0 \sin(\omega t) $$

where $\omega$ is the angular frequency ($\omega = 2\pi f$).

LO3 & LO4: Power and r.m.s. Values

Since the current and voltage in an a.c. circuit are constantly changing, the instantaneous power dissipated in a resistor ($P=I^2R$) also changes. The average current over a full cycle is zero, but the average power is not, because the power is proportional to $I^2$, which is always positive.

The **mean power ($\bar{P}$)** dissipated in a resistor in an a.c. circuit is exactly **half the maximum power ($P_0$)**.

$$ \bar{P} = \frac{1}{2}P_0 = \frac{1}{2}I_0^2R $$

Root-Mean-Square (r.m.s.) Values

To make a.c. calculations more convenient, we use an "effective" value for current and voltage called the **root-mean-square (r.m.s.) value**.

The r.m.s. value of an alternating current is the value of the direct current that would dissipate thermal energy at the same rate (i.e., produce the same power) in a given resistor.

Detailed Derivation of r.m.s. Values

1. The power dissipated by a direct current $I_{DC}$ in a resistor $R$ is $P_{DC} = I_{DC}^2R$.

2. The mean power dissipated by an alternating current with peak value $I_0$ is $\bar{P}_{AC} = \frac{1}{2}I_0^2R$.

3. By definition, the r.m.s. current ($I_{r.m.s.}$) is the value of $I_{DC}$ that produces the same power. So, we set $P_{DC} = \bar{P}_{AC}$ and $I_{DC} = I_{r.m.s.}$.

$$ (I_{r.m.s.})^2 R = \frac{1}{2}I_0^2R $$

4. The resistance $R$ cancels from both sides:

$$ (I_{r.m.s.})^2 = \frac{I_0^2}{2} $$

5. Taking the square root of both sides gives the final relationship:

$$ I_{r.m.s.} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}} $$

6. Since $V=IR$, the same relationship holds for voltage:

$$ V_{r.m.s.} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}} $$

Worked Example: r.m.s. Values and Power

The mains electricity supply in the UK is quoted as 230 V. This is the r.m.s. value. For this supply, calculate (a) the peak voltage, and (b) the mean power dissipated by a 100 $\Omega$ resistor connected to it.

(a) Calculate the peak voltage ($V_0$):

$$ V_{r.m.s.} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}} \implies V_0 = V_{r.m.s.} \times \sqrt{2} $$

$$ V_0 = 230 \times \sqrt{2} = 325.27... \approx 330 \text{ V} $$

(b) Calculate the mean power ($\bar{P}$):

We can use the standard power formulas with r.m.s. values to find the mean power directly.

$$ \bar{P} = \frac{V_{r.m.s.}^2}{R} $$

$$ \bar{P} = \frac{(230)^2}{100} $$

$$ \bar{P} = \frac{52900}{100} = 529 \text{ W} \approx 530 \text{ W} $$
