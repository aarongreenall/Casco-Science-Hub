8.7.3 Turning forces (moments)

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define a moment as the turning effect of a force.
  2. Explain that the size of a moment depends on the force and the distance from the pivot.
  3. Calculate moments using the formula: moment = force Ã— perpendicular distance.
  4. Use the principle of moments to solve balancing problems.

What is a Moment?

When a force makes an object turn, we say it has a **turning effect**. The scientific name for this turning effect is a **moment**. A moment is created when a force is applied to an object that can rotate around a fixed point called a **pivot** or **fulcrum**.

The size of the moment depends on two things:

  1. The **size of the force** applied.
  2. The **perpendicular distance** from the pivot to the line of action of the force.

$$ \text{Moment} = \text{Force} \times \text{Perpendicular Distance from Pivot} $$

$$ M = F \times d $$

The unit for a moment is the newton-metre (Nm).

Analogy: Using a Spanner

The best way to understand moments is to think about tightening a nut with a spanner. The **nut** is the **pivot**. The **push or pull** on the spanner is the **force**. The **moment** is the **turning effect** that tightens the nut. It's much easier to turn a tight nut with a long spanner than a short one. This is because the same force applied at a greater distance creates a larger moment.

The Principle of Moments

When an object like a seesaw is balanced, it is not moving. This means the total turning effect in the clockwise direction is exactly equal to the total turning effect in the anticlockwise direction. This is known as the **Principle of Moments**.

For an object to be in rotational equilibrium (balanced), the sum of the clockwise moments about any pivot must equal the sum of the anticlockwise moments about the same pivot.

Worked Example 1: The Balanced Seesaw

A child with a weight of 300 N sits on a seesaw at a distance of 1.5 m from the pivot. Where must a child with a weight of 450 N sit to balance the seesaw?

$$ \text{Anticlockwise} \quad \circlearrowleft \quad |\quad \blacktriangle \quad | \quad \circlearrowright \quad \text{Clockwise} $$ $$ \downarrow F_1 = 300 \text{ N} \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \downarrow F_2 = 450 \text{ N} $$ $$ \leftarrow d_1 = 1.5 \text{ m} \rightarrow \quad \quad \leftarrow d_2 = ? \rightarrow $$

1. Identify the moments:

The 300 N child creates an anticlockwise moment. The 450 N child must create an equal clockwise moment.

2. Apply the Principle of Moments:

$$ \text{Clockwise Moment} = \text{Anticlockwise Moment} $$

$$ F_2 \times d_2 = F_1 \times d_1 $$

$$ (450 \text{ N} \times d_2) = (300 \text{ N} \times 1.5 \text{ m}) $$

3. Solve for the unknown distance, $d_2$:

$$ 450 d_2 = 450 $$

$$ d_2 = \frac{450}{450} = 1.0 \text{ m} $$

The second child must sit 1.0 m from the pivot on the opposite side.

Worked Example 2: Net Moment on a Beam

A wooden beam is pivoted at one end. A downward force of 20 N is applied 0.5 m from the pivot, and an upward force of 15 N is applied 1.0 m from the pivot. Calculate the net moment and state the direction of rotation.

$$ \blacktriangle \text{ Pivot} \quad \quad \quad \downarrow F_1 = 20 \text{ N} \quad \quad \quad \uparrow F_2 = 15 \text{ N} $$ $$ \quad \quad \quad \leftarrow d_1 = 0.5 \text{ m} \rightarrow $$ $$ \quad \quad \quad \leftarrow \quad \quad d_2 = 1.0 \text{ m} \quad \quad \rightarrow $$

1. Calculate the clockwise moment:

$$ M_{cw} = F_1 \times d_1 = 20 \text{ N} \times 0.5 \text{ m} = 10 \text{ Nm} $$

2. Calculate the anticlockwise moment:

$$ M_{acw} = F_2 \times d_2 = 15 \text{ N} \times 1.0 \text{ m} = 15 \text{ Nm} $$

3. Find the net moment:

The anticlockwise moment is larger than the clockwise moment.

$$ \text{Net Moment} = M_{acw} - M_{cw} = 15 - 10 = 5 \text{ Nm} $$

The net moment is 5 Nm in the **anticlockwise** direction, so the beam will rotate anticlockwise.

In-School Experiment: Balancing a Ruler

You can investigate the principle of moments using a ruler, a pivot, and some weights.

Equipment:
  • A 30 cm or 1 m ruler
  • A triangular pivot
  • A set of slotted masses (or identical coins and tape)
Method:
  1. Find the centre of the ruler and place it on the pivot so that it balances.
  2. Place a known weight (e.g., 100 g, which is about 1 N) at a certain distance from the pivot on one side. This creates a known moment.
  3. Now, take a different weight (e.g., 50 g, about 0.5 N) and try to find the position on the other side of the pivot where it will make the ruler balance again.
  4. Calculate the clockwise moment and the anticlockwise moment. Are they equal?
  5. Repeat with different weights and distances to confirm the principle of moments.
