8.3.2 Gas exchange and the respiratory system

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Identify the main organs of the human respiratory system.
  2. Describe the structure of the lungs, including the alveoli.
  3. Explain how the alveoli are adapted for efficient gas exchange.

The Breathing System

The **respiratory system** is the group of organs responsible for taking in oxygen and getting rid of carbon dioxide. This process is called **breathing** or ventilation. Air enters your body through your nose and mouth, travels down the **trachea** (windpipe), which then splits into two tubes called **bronchi** (one for each lung). The bronchi continue to branch into smaller and smaller tubes called **bronchioles**.

Analogy: A Bunch of Grapes

A good way to picture the structure inside your lungs is to think of a bunch of grapes. The **main stalk** is like the **trachea**. The **stems that branch off** are like the **bronchi and bronchioles**. Each individual **grape** at the end of a stem is like one of the tiny air sacs in your lungs, called an **alveolus**.

A Key Difference:

It's important to remember that this is an analogy for the *shape*. The stalks of the grapes carry food and water to the fruit, whereas the tubes in your lungs carry air to the alveoli.

Gas Exchange in the Alveoli

The real work of the lungs happens in the **alveoli**. These are millions of tiny, stretchy air sacs at the end of the bronchioles. This is where **gas exchange** takes place: oxygen from the air you breathe in passes into your blood, and carbon dioxide from your blood passes out into the air you breathe out. The alveoli are perfectly adapted for this job:

  • Huge Surface Area: There are millions of them, giving the lungs a total surface area as big as a tennis court!
  • Thin Walls: The walls of the alveoli (and the capillaries surrounding them) are only one cell thick, providing a very short distance for the gases to diffuse across.
  • Good Blood Supply: Each alveolus is covered in a fine mesh of tiny blood vessels called capillaries, which constantly transport blood to and from the lungs.

At-Home Experiment: Measuring Your Lung Capacity

You can get an estimate of your **vital lung capacity**—the maximum amount of air you can breathe out after taking the deepest possible breath—with this simple experiment.

Equipment:
  • A large plastic bottle (e.g., a 3-5 litre water bottle)
  • A large basin or bucket
  • A flexible tube (like from a science lab or a piece of garden hose)
  • A measuring jug
  • Water
Method:
  1. Fill the large plastic bottle completely to the brim with water.
  2. Fill the basin about halfway with water.
  3. Carefully, place your hand over the top of the bottle, turn it upside down, and place the opening under the water in the basin before removing your hand. The water should stay inside the bottle.
  4. Push one end of the flexible tube into the neck of the bottle, under the water.
  5. Take the deepest breath you possibly can.
  6. Exhale as much air as you can through the tube, bubbling it into the bottle. The air you breathe out will push the water out of the bottle.
  7. Once you have breathed out completely, carefully lift the bottle out of the basin, keeping it upside down so the remaining water doesn't spill.
  8. Turn the bottle the right way up and use the measuring jug to measure how much water is needed to fill it back to the top. This volume is your vital lung capacity!
