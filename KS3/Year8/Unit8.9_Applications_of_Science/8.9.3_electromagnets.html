8.9.3 Electromagnets

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain what electromagnets are, how they work, and their applications.

What is an Electromagnet?

  • An electromagnet is a type of magnet in which the magnetic field is produced by an electric current.
  • It consists of a coil of wire (called a solenoid) wrapped around a soft iron core.
  • The magnetic field is only present when the electric current is flowing. It can be switched on and off.

How it Works:

  • When an electric current flows through a wire, it creates a magnetic field around the wire.
  • Coiling the wire into a solenoid concentrates the magnetic field inside the coil.
  • Placing a soft iron core inside the solenoid greatly increases the strength of the magnetic field.

Controlling the Strength of an Electromagnet:

The strength can be increased by:

  • Increasing the number of coils (turns) in the solenoid.
  • Increasing the size of the electric current flowing through the wire.
  • Using a larger iron core.

Applications of Electromagnets:

  • Scrap Yard Cranes: Large, powerful electromagnets are used to pick up and move scrap metal (like iron and steel). The magnet can be switched off to release the metal.
  • Electric Bells: An electromagnet is used to attract a metal arm, which strikes a bell. This breaks the circuit, turning the magnet off, and the arm springs back. The process repeats rapidly, causing the bell to ring continuously.
  • Relays: An electromagnet is used as a switch in a high-power circuit, controlled by a small current in a separate, low-power circuit. This is a safety feature.
  • Loudspeakers, MRI machines, and many other devices.
