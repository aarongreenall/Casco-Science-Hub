2.1 Equations of Motion

**Kinematics** is the branch of mechanics that describes the motion of points, bodies, and systems of bodies without considering the forces that cause them to move. Understanding key definitions like distance, displacement, speed, velocity, and acceleration, along with their graphical representations and mathematical relationships, is fundamental to analyzing motion.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define and use distance, displacement, speed, velocity and acceleration.
  2. Use graphical methods to represent distance, displacement, speed, velocity and acceleration.
  3. Determine displacement from the area under a velocity–time graph.
  4. Determine velocity using the gradient of a displacement–time graph.
  5. Determine acceleration using the gradient of a velocity–time graph.
  6. Derive, from the definitions of velocity and acceleration, equations that represent uniformly accelerated motion in a straight line.
  7. Solve problems using equations that represent uniformly accelerated motion in a straight line, including the motion of bodies falling in a uniform gravitational field without air resistance.
  8. Describe an experiment to determine the acceleration of free fall using a falling object.
  9. Describe and explain motion due to a uniform velocity in one direction and a uniform acceleration in a perpendicular direction.

Key Definitions in Motion:

  • **Distance:** A scalar quantity representing the total path length covered by an object. It only has magnitude.
  • **Displacement:** A vector quantity representing the change in position of an object from its starting point. It has both magnitude and direction.
  • **Speed:** A scalar quantity representing the rate at which an object covers distance. $Speed = \frac{Distance}{Time}$.
  • **Velocity:** A vector quantity representing the rate at which an object changes its displacement. $Velocity = \frac{Displacement}{Time}$.
  • **Acceleration:** A vector quantity representing the rate of change of velocity. $Acceleration = \frac{Change \ in \ Velocity}{Time}$.

Graphical Representation of Motion:

Motion can be effectively analyzed using graphs:

  • **Displacement-Time Graphs:** * The **gradient (slope)** of a displacement-time graph gives the **velocity** of the object. * A straight line indicates constant velocity. * A curved line indicates changing velocity (acceleration).
  • **Velocity-Time Graphs:** * The **gradient (slope)** of a velocity-time graph gives the **acceleration** of the object. * The **area under** a velocity-time graph gives the **displacement** of the object. * A straight horizontal line means constant velocity (zero acceleration). * A straight sloping line means constant acceleration.

Equations of Uniformly Accelerated Motion (SUVAT Equations):

For motion in a straight line with **uniform (constant) acceleration**, a set of equations (often called SUVAT equations) can be derived from the definitions of velocity and acceleration. These equations relate: * $s$ = displacement * $u$ = initial velocity * $v$ = final velocity * $a$ = acceleration * $t$ = time

  1. $v = u + at$
  2. $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$
  3. $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$
  4. $s = \frac{(u+v)}{2}t$

These equations are used to solve problems involving uniformly accelerated motion, including objects falling in a uniform gravitational field (where $a = g \approx 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$, ignoring air resistance).

Acceleration of Free Fall Experiment:

The acceleration of free fall ($g$) can be determined experimentally using a falling object. A common method involves:

  • Dropping an object from a known height ($s$).
  • Measuring the time ($t$) it takes to fall.
  • Using the equation $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$. Since the object starts from rest, $u=0$, so $s = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$.
  • Rearranging to find $g = \frac{2s}{t^2}$.

More accurate methods use light gates connected to a data logger to measure time intervals precisely over specific distances.

Projectile Motion:

**Projectile motion** describes the motion of an object thrown or projected into the air, subject only to the acceleration of gravity. It is a combination of:

  • **Uniform velocity in the horizontal direction:** Assuming no air resistance, there are no horizontal forces, so horizontal velocity remains constant.
  • **Uniform acceleration in the vertical direction:** Due to gravity ($g$), acting downwards.

The horizontal and vertical components of motion are independent of each other. This means you can analyze them separately using the appropriate kinematic equations.
