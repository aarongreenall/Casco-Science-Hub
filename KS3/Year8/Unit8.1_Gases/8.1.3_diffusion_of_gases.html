8.1.3 Diffusion of gases

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define diffusion as the movement of particles from a high to a low concentration.
  2. Explain that diffusion is caused by the random motion of particles.
  3. Give everyday examples of diffusion in gases.

What is Diffusion?

If someone opens a bottle of perfume at the front of a room, it doesn't take long for people at the back of the room to smell it. This spreading out of particles is called **diffusion**.

**Diffusion** is the net movement of particles from a region of their higher concentration to a region of their lower concentration.

The most important thing to understand is that this happens because the gas particles are in continuous, **random motion**. They are constantly moving and bumping into each other and the air particles, which causes them to spread out. It is not because they "want" to move or because of wind or fans.

Analogy: The Smell of Baking Cookies

Imagine someone is baking cookies in the kitchen. The smell particles are highly concentrated in the kitchen (the area of high concentration). These smell particles are constantly moving and bouncing off the air particles in the room. Over time, this random movement causes them to spread out from the kitchen into the rest of the house, until you can eventually smell them everywhere (the area of low concentration). This happens all by itself, just through the random motion of the particles.

In-School Experiment: The Smell Race

You can observe diffusion in action in the classroom.

Equipment:
  • A bottle of a strong-smelling but safe substance (e.g., perfume, vanilla extract)
  • A paper towel
  • A stopwatch
Method:
  1. Make sure the classroom is as still as possible, with no fans on and windows closed.
  2. Your teacher will place a few drops of the perfume onto a paper towel at the front of the classroom and start the stopwatch.
  3. Raise your hand as soon as you can smell the perfume.
  4. Observe how the smell gradually spreads from the front of the room to the back. This is diffusion in action!
