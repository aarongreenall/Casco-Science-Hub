C11.5 Alkenes

**Alkenes** are a homologous series of unsaturated hydrocarbons, characterized by the presence of at least one carbon-carbon double bond (C=C). This double bond makes them more reactive than alkanes and allows them to undergo addition reactions. Understanding alkenes is crucial for the production of polymers and other organic compounds.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State that the bonding in alkenes includes a double carbon–carbon covalent bond and that alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons.
  2. State that an unsaturated compound has molecules in which one or more carbon–carbon bonds are not single bonds.
  3. Name and draw the displayed formulas of: ethene; propene; but-1-ene and but-2-ene (not cis/trans), containing up to four carbon atoms per molecule.
  4. Describe the manufacture of alkenes and hydrogen by the cracking of larger alkane molecules using a high temperature and a catalyst.
  5. Describe the test to distinguish between saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons by their reaction with aqueous bromine.
  6. Describe the properties of alkenes in terms of addition reactions with: bromine; hydrogen in the presence of a nickel catalyst; steam in the presence of an acid catalyst.

Structure and Naming of Alkenes:

Alkenes are **unsaturated hydrocarbons** because they contain at least one **carbon-carbon double covalent bond (C=C)**. The presence of this double bond means the molecule is not "saturated" with hydrogen atoms, unlike alkanes.

Alkenes form a homologous series with the general formula **C$_n$H$_{2n}$**, where 'n' is the number of carbon atoms (n $\ge$ 2).

The names of unbranched alkenes containing up to four carbon atoms are:

  • **Ethene (C$_2$H$_4$):** 2 carbon atoms, with a C=C double bond.
  • **Propene (C$_3$H$_6$):** 3 carbon atoms, with a C=C double bond.
  • **But-1-ene (C$_4$H$_8$):** 4 carbon atoms, with the C=C double bond between the first and second carbon atoms.
  • **But-2-ene (C$_4$H$_8$):** 4 carbon atoms, with the C=C double bond between the second and third carbon atoms. (You do not need to distinguish between cis/trans isomers).

You should be able to draw their **displayed formulas**, showing all atoms and all bonds.

*Figure: Displayed formulas for ethene, propene, but-1-ene, and but-2-ene.*

Manufacture by Cracking:

Alkenes are primarily manufactured by the **cracking** of larger alkane molecules, typically obtained from the fractional distillation of petroleum. Cracking involves breaking down long-chain alkanes into smaller, more useful alkane molecules and alkenes. This process requires **high temperatures** (e.g., 400-700°C) and/or a **catalyst**.

Example of cracking: `C10H22 (decane) -> C8H18 (octane) + C2H4 (ethene)`

Properties and Reactions of Alkenes:

The presence of the C=C double bond makes alkenes much more reactive than alkanes. They readily undergo **addition reactions**, where atoms are added across the double bond, breaking it and forming a single bond.

1. Test for Unsaturation (Reaction with Aqueous Bromine):

This is a key test to distinguish between saturated (alkanes) and unsaturated (alkenes) hydrocarbons.

  • When **aqueous bromine** (which is orange-brown) is added to an **alkene**, the bromine adds across the double bond, and the orange-brown color of the bromine water **decolorizes** (turns colorless).
  • When aqueous bromine is added to an **alkane**, no reaction occurs, and the orange-brown color persists.

`C2H4(g) + Br2(aq) -> C2H4Br2(aq)`
`(Ethene + Orange-brown bromine water -> 1,2-dibromoethane (colorless))`

*Figure: The bromine water test. Bromine water (orange-brown) becomes colorless in the presence of an alkene.*

2. Reaction with Hydrogen (Hydrogenation):

Alkenes react with hydrogen gas in the presence of a **nickel catalyst** (typically at 150°C) to form alkanes. This is an addition reaction where hydrogen atoms add across the double bond.

`C2H4(g) + H2(g) --(Ni catalyst)--> C2H6(g)`
`(Ethene + Hydrogen -> Ethane)`

3. Reaction with Steam (Hydration):

Alkenes react with steam (water vapor) in the presence of an **acid catalyst** (e.g., concentrated phosphoric acid) at high temperature and pressure to form alcohols. This is also an addition reaction.

`C2H4(g) + H2O(g) --(Acid catalyst)--> C2H5OH(g)`
`(Ethene + Steam -> Ethanol)`
