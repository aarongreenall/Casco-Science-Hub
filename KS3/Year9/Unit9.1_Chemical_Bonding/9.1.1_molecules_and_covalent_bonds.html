9.1.1 Molecules and Covalent Bonds

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Understand that a molecule is formed when two or more atoms join together chemically, through a covalent bond.
  2. Describe a covalent bond as a bond made when a pair of electrons is shared by two atoms (limited to single bonds).

What is a Molecule?

In chemistry, a **molecule** is a particle made of two or more atoms that are chemically bonded together. Think of atoms as the basic building blocks of everything. When these blocks join together in a specific way, they form a molecule.

Analogy: LEGO® Bricks

Imagine atoms are individual LEGO® bricks. You can have a pile of single red bricks, single blue bricks, and so on. A **molecule** is what you get when you click two or more of these bricks together. The strong connection that holds them is the **chemical bond**. You can have a molecule made of two red bricks clicked together, or one made of a red brick and a blue brick clicked together.

Misconception Alert!

A common mistake is thinking that molecules must be made of different types of atoms. This is not true! A molecule of oxygen gas ($O_2$) is a pure substance because it only contains oxygen atoms, but it's still a molecule because two oxygen atoms are chemically bonded together. The key is the **chemical bond**.

Pure Element (Atoms)

e.g., Helium (He). All particles are separate, individual atoms.

Pure Element (Molecules)

e.g., Oxygen ($O_2$). All particles are identical molecules made of two of the same atom bonded together.

Pure Compound (Molecules)

e.g., Water ($H_2O$). All particles are identical molecules made of different atoms bonded together.

The Covalent Bond: A Sharing Agreement

So how do atoms join together? One of the most common ways is by forming a **covalent bond**. This type of bond typically forms between **non-metal atoms**. A covalent bond is formed when two atoms **share** one or more pairs of electrons. By sharing electrons, each atom can achieve a full outer shell, which is a very stable arrangement.

Analogy: Sharing a Video Game Controller

Imagine two friends (atoms) both want to play a two-player video game, but they each only have one controller. The game needs two controllers to work. If they sit close together and **share** their controllers, they can both play the game. This sharing creates a strong link holding them together so they can play. The shared pair of controllers is like the shared pair of electrons in a covalent bond.

We can show this sharing using **dot and cross diagrams**. Let's look at how two hydrogen atoms form a covalent bond to make a hydrogen molecule ($H_2$). Each hydrogen atom has one electron. They can share this electron with another hydrogen atom to get a full outer shell of two electrons.

Formation of a Hydrogen Molecule ($H_2$)

The electron shells overlap, and the two atoms share a pair of electrons, forming a stable molecule.

Methane Molecule ($CH_4$)

A central carbon atom shares electrons with four hydrogen atoms, forming four single covalent bonds.

In-School Activity: Build-a-Molecule

Using a molecular modelling kit (or marshmallows for atoms and toothpicks for bonds), work in groups to build the following molecules:

  1. Water ($H_2O$)
  2. Ammonia ($NH_3$)
  3. Methane ($CH_4$)
  4. Hydrogen ($H_2$)

Discussion Questions:

  • What does each part of your model represent (the balls and the sticks)?
  • What are the strengths of using a model like this to understand molecules?
  • What are the limitations? (e.g., Do electrons really look like sticks?)
