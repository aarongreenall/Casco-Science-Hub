P5.5 Half-life

Radioactive decay is a fundamental process in nuclear physics where unstable atomic nuclei spontaneously transform into more stable configurations by emitting radiation. This process is inherently random and cannot be predicted for individual nuclei, but the rate of decay for a large sample can be described by a characteristic property called half-life.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define the half-life of a particular isotope as the time taken for half the nuclei of that isotope in any sample to decay; recall and use this definition in simple calculations, which might involve information in tables or decay curves (calculations will not include background radiation).

Understanding Half-Life:

The **half-life** of a radioactive isotope is defined as the **time taken for half the nuclei of that isotope in any given sample to decay**. It is a constant value for a particular isotope and is not affected by external factors like temperature, pressure, or chemical reactions. This makes half-life a very useful property for dating archaeological artifacts and geological formations, as well as for medical applications.

During each half-life period, the number of undecayed radioactive nuclei (and thus the activity of the sample) reduces by half. For example, if a sample has a half-life of 10 days, after 10 days, half of its original nuclei will have decayed. After another 10 days (total 20 days), half of the *remaining* nuclei will have decayed, leaving one-quarter of the original amount.

Calculating Half-Life from Data:

Half-life can be determined from experimental data presented in tables or decay curves:

  • **From Tables:** Look at the time intervals required for the activity or number of undecayed nuclei to halve.
  • **From Decay Curves:** Plot a graph of activity (or number of undecayed nuclei) against time. The half-life is the time interval corresponding to a halving of the activity.
