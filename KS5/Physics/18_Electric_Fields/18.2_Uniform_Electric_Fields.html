18.2 Uniform electric fields

Learning Objectives:

  1. Recall and use $E = \Delta V / \Delta d$ to calculate the field strength of the uniform field between charged parallel plates.
  2. Describe the effect of a uniform electric field on the motion of charged particles.

LO1: Field Strength in a Uniform Field

A **uniform electric field** is a field in which the electric field strength is the same at all points in magnitude and direction. Such a field is created in the region between two large, parallel metal plates that are oppositely charged. The field lines are parallel, equally spaced, and point from the positive plate to the negative plate.

Detailed Derivation of $E = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta d}$

1. Consider a positive charge $q$ being moved by an external agent from the negative plate to the positive plate, against the electric field. The distance between the plates is $d$.

2. The electric field exerts a force $F$ on the charge, given by $F=qE$. To move the charge at a constant velocity, the external agent must apply an equal and opposite force.

3. The work done ($W$) by the external agent in moving the charge a distance $d$ against the field is:

$$ W = \text{Force} \times \text{distance} = Fd = (qE)d $$

4. From the definition of potential difference, the work done in moving a charge $q$ through a p.d. of $\Delta V$ is $W = q\Delta V$.

5. By equating these two expressions for the work done, we get:

$$ q\Delta V = (qE)d $$

6. The charge $q$ cancels from both sides, leaving the relationship between field strength, potential difference, and distance:

$$ \Delta V = Ed \quad \implies \quad E = \frac{\Delta V}{d} $$

This shows that the unit for electric field strength, N C⁻¹, is equivalent to the volt per metre (V m⁻¹).

Worked Example: Field Between Plates

Two parallel metal plates are separated by a distance of 5.0 cm and have a potential difference of 3.0 kV between them. Calculate the electric field strength in the region between the plates.

1. Identify variables and convert to SI units:

$$ \Delta V = 3.0 \text{ kV} = 3000 \text{ V} $$

$$ d = 5.0 \text{ cm} = 0.050 \text{ m} $$

2. State the formula and solve:

$$ E = \frac{\Delta V}{d} = \frac{3000}{0.050} = 60000 \text{ V m}^{-1} $$

$$ E = 6.0 \times 10^4 \text{ N C}^{-1} $$

LO2: Motion of Charged Particles in a Uniform Field

Since the electric field $E$ between two parallel plates is uniform, the force on a charge $q$ within that field ($F=qE$) is also **constant**. According to Newton's Second Law, if the force is constant, the acceleration of the charge ($a=F/m = qE/m$) is also **constant**.

This is directly analogous to the motion of a mass in the uniform gravitational field near the Earth's surface, where the acceleration is always $g$. Because the acceleration is constant, we can use the **SUVAT equations of motion** to analyse the particle's trajectory.

Worked Example: Deflection of an Electron Beam

An electron enters a uniform electric field midway between two plates with an initial horizontal velocity of $2.0 \times 10^7$ m s⁻¹. The field has a strength of 1500 N C⁻¹ and is directed downwards. The plates are 8.0 cm long. Calculate the vertical deflection of the electron as it leaves the plates. (Mass of electron = $9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ kg; Charge of electron = $-1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C).

1. Analyse the horizontal motion to find the time spent in the field:

There is no horizontal force, so the horizontal velocity is constant.

$$ t = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{speed}} = \frac{L}{v_x} = \frac{0.080 \text{ m}}{2.0 \times 10^7 \text{ m s}^{-1}} = 4.0 \times 10^{-9} \text{ s} $$

2. Analyse the vertical motion to find the acceleration:

The field is downwards, but the electron is negative, so the force on it is upwards.

$$ F_y = qE = (1.60 \times 10^{-19})(1500) = 2.4 \times 10^{-16} \text{ N} $$

$$ a_y = \frac{F_y}{m} = \frac{2.4 \times 10^{-16}}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}} = 2.63 \times 10^{14} \text{ m s}^{-2} $$

3. Use SUVAT to find the vertical displacement ($s_y$):

The initial vertical velocity, $u_y$, is 0.

$$ s_y = u_y t + \frac{1}{2}a_y t^2 $$

$$ s_y = (0)(4.0 \times 10^{-9}) + \frac{1}{2}(2.63 \times 10^{14})(4.0 \times 10^{-9})^2 $$

$$ s_y = \frac{1}{2}(2.63 \times 10^{14})(1.6 \times 10^{-17}) = 0.021 \text{ m} \text{ (or 2.1 cm)} $$
