C2.4 Ions and ionic bonds

Chemical bonds hold atoms together to form molecules and compounds. One fundamental type of chemical bond is the **ionic bond**, which forms between atoms that gain or lose electrons to become charged particles called ions. This section explores the formation of ions, the nature of ionic bonds, and the characteristic giant lattice structures of ionic compounds.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the formation of positive ions, known as cations and negative ions, known as anions.
  2. Describe the formation of ionic bonds between elements from Group I and Group VII, including the use of dot-and-cross diagrams.
  3. Describe the formation of ionic bonds between metallic and non-metallic elements, including the use of dot-and-cross diagrams.
  4. State that an ionic bond is a strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
  5. Describe the giant lattice structure of ionic compounds as a regular arrangement of alternating positive and negative ions, exemplified by sodium chloride.
  6. Describe the properties of ionic compounds: high melting points and boiling points; good electrical conductivity when aqueous or molten and poor when solid; generally soluble in water.
  7. Explain in terms of structure and bonding the properties of ionic compounds: high melting points and boiling points in terms of strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions; good electrical conductivity when aqueous or molten and poor when solid.

Formation of Ions (Cations and Anions):

Atoms achieve stability by having a full outer electron shell, similar to noble gases. They can do this by gaining or losing electrons, forming charged particles called **ions**.

  • **Positive Ions (Cations):** Formed when an atom **loses** one or more electrons. Metals tend to form cations (e.g., Na $\rightarrow$ Na$^+$ + e$^-$).
  • **Negative Ions (Anions):** Formed when an atom **gains** one or more electrons. Non-metals tend to form anions (e.g., Cl + e$^-$ $\rightarrow$ Cl$^-$).

The number of electrons gained or lost determines the charge of the ion.

Ionic Bonds:

An **ionic bond** is a strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. These bonds typically form between a **metallic element** (which loses electrons to form cations) and a **non-metallic element** (which gains electrons to form anions).

For example, in the formation of sodium chloride (NaCl):

  • A Group I metal (Sodium) loses its single outer electron to form a positive ion (Na$^+$).
  • A Group VII non-metal (Chlorine) gains this electron to form a negative ion (Cl$^-$).
  • The oppositely charged Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$ ions are then strongly attracted to each other, forming an ionic bond.

**Dot-and-cross diagrams** are used to illustrate the transfer of electrons and the resulting electronic configurations and charges of the ions.

*Figure: Dot-and-cross diagram showing the formation of an ionic bond in sodium chloride.*

Giant Ionic Lattice Structure:

Ionic compounds do not exist as discrete molecules but form a **giant lattice structure**. This is a regular, repeating arrangement of alternating positive and negative ions held together by strong electrostatic forces of attraction extending throughout the entire structure. Sodium chloride (common table salt) is a classic example of a giant ionic lattice.

*Figure: The giant lattice structure of sodium chloride, showing the alternating arrangement of Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$ ions.*

Properties of Ionic Compounds:

The giant ionic lattice structure and strong electrostatic forces of attraction give ionic compounds characteristic properties:

  • **High Melting and Boiling Points:** A large amount of thermal energy is required to overcome the strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the ions in the giant lattice, leading to high melting and boiling points.
  • **Electrical Conductivity:** * **Good conductors when molten or aqueous:** In the molten state or when dissolved in water (aqueous solution), the ions are free to move and can carry electric charge, allowing them to conduct electricity. * **Poor conductors when solid:** In the solid state, the ions are held in fixed positions within the lattice and are not free to move, so they cannot conduct electricity.
  • **Generally Soluble in Water:** Many ionic compounds are soluble in water because water molecules can surround and separate the individual ions from the lattice.
