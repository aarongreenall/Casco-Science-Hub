7.7.5 Water cycle

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the water cycle as the continuous movement of water on Earth.
  2. Define and explain the key processes of evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection.
  3. Understand the role of the Sun's energy in driving the water cycle.

The Earth's Recycling System

The water on Earth today is the same water that has been here for billions of years. It is constantly moving and changing state in a continuous process called the **water cycle**. This giant recycling system is powered by energy from the Sun.

Analogy: A Kettle and a Cold Window

You can see a mini water cycle in your own kitchen. When you boil a kettle, the liquid water turns into an invisible gas called water vapour (steam). This is **evaporation**. If this hot vapour hits a cold window, it turns back into visible liquid water droplets. This is **condensation**. As the droplets join together, they get heavy and run down the glass, like rain. This is **precipitation**.

The Four Main Stages

  1. Evaporation: The Sun's heat provides energy to the water in oceans, lakes, and rivers. This causes the liquid water to turn into water vapour, an invisible gas, which rises into the atmosphere.
  2. Condensation: As the warm, moist air rises, it cools down. The water vapour turns back into tiny liquid water droplets, forming clouds.
  3. Precipitation: The water droplets in the clouds bump into each other and join together, becoming larger and heavier. When they are heavy enough, they fall back to Earth as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
  4. Collection: The water that falls to the ground is collected in rivers, lakes, and oceans, or soaks into the ground. From here, the cycle starts all over again.

At-Home Experiment: The Water Cycle in a Bag

You can create a miniature model of the Earth's water cycle with just a few simple items.

Equipment:
  • A sealable plastic bag (like a zip-lock sandwich bag)
  • A small amount of water
  • Blue food colouring (optional, but makes it easier to see)
  • Tape
  • A sunny window
Method:
  1. Pour a small amount of water (about 2-3 cm deep) into the plastic bag.
  2. Add a few drops of blue food colouring and mix it.
  3. Seal the bag tightly, making sure there is plenty of air trapped inside.
  4. Tape the bag to a window that gets plenty of direct sunlight.
  5. Observe the bag over the next few hours and the next day.
What to Observe:

You should see the key stages of the water cycle happening inside the bag! The sun's heat will cause the blue water at the bottom to **evaporate**. You will see the inside of the bag become misty or foggy as the water vapour cools and **condenses** into tiny droplets on the plastic. As these droplets join together, they will become heavy enough to run down the inside of the bag like rain (**precipitation**), returning to the pool of water at the bottom (**collection**).
