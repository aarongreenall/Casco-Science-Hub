9.3.3 Conservation of Energy

In this lesson, you will learn that:

  1. Energy is conserved, meaning it cannot be created or destroyed.

The First Rule of Energy

There is one rule about energy that is perhaps the most important in all of science: The **Law of Conservation of Energy**. This law states that:

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, it can only be transferred from one store to another.

This means the total amount of energy in a closed system (like the entire universe) is always constant. It never changes. It just moves around and changes form.

Analogy: LEGO® Bricks

Imagine you have a sealed box containing 100 LEGO® bricks. This is your closed system, and the bricks represent the total energy. You can shake the box and the bricks might form a pile. You can open it, build a car, take it apart, and then build a house. The *form* of the bricks has changed (from a pile to a car to a house), but you will always have exactly 100 bricks. You can't magically create new bricks, and the bricks can't just vanish. Energy works in exactly the same way.

Energy Stores and Transfers

Since energy can't be made or destroyed, it's helpful to think of it as being held in different **energy stores**. When an event happens, energy is **transferred** from one store to another. Some common energy stores are:

  • Chemical Store: Energy stored in food, fuel, and batteries.
  • Kinetic Store: The energy of a moving object.
  • Gravitational Potential Store: Energy stored in an object due to its height.
  • Elastic Potential Store: Energy stored in a stretched or compressed object, like a spring or rubber band.
  • Thermal Store: The energy of an object due to its temperature (the kinetic energy of its particles).

Misconception Alert: "Using Up" Energy

Be careful with your words!

In everyday language, we talk about "running out of energy" or "using up" electricity. In science, this is incorrect! When your phone battery dies, the energy hasn't vanished. It has been transferred from the **chemical** store in the battery into useful **light** and **sound** stores, but also into the **thermal** store of the phone and its surroundings (the phone gets warm!). The energy is still all there, it's just been dissipated into a less useful form.

The Sankey Diagram: Following the Energy

Scientists and engineers need to track energy transfers to make devices more efficient. A **Sankey diagram** is a special flow chart where the width of the arrows is proportional to the amount of energy. This gives a clear picture of how much of the input energy is transferred to useful stores, and how much is wasted (usually as heat).

This Sankey diagram shows that for an old light bulb, most of the electrical energy is wasted as heat, and only a small amount becomes useful light.

In-School Activity: Where Does the Energy Go?

This simple experiment demonstrates energy conservation and wasted energy.

  1. Take a bouncy ball and a metre ruler.
  2. Hold the ball so its base is level with the 100 cm mark on the ruler.
  3. Drop the ball and carefully observe the height of the first bounce.

Discussion Questions:

  • What main energy store does the ball have at the start?
  • What is this energy transferred into as the ball falls?
  • Why doesn't the ball bounce back to its original height? According to the Law of Conservation of Energy, the energy can't have been destroyed. So where did it go?
  • Answer: On impact, some energy is transferred to the thermal store of the ball and the floor (they get slightly warmer) and some is transferred to a sound store (you hear the bounce). This "wasted" energy is no longer available to be transferred back into gravitational potential energy.
