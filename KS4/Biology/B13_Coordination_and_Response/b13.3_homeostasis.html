B13.3 Homeostasis

**Homeostasis** is the maintenance of a constant internal environment in living organisms. This dynamic equilibrium is crucial for cells and organs to function optimally, despite changes in the external environment. Key aspects of homeostasis include the regulation of body temperature, blood glucose concentration, and water balance.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the maintenance of a constant body temperature in mammals in terms of: the role of the hypothalamus; vasodilation and vasoconstriction of arterioles; sweating; shivering.
  2. Describe the control of blood glucose concentration by the liver, pancreas and insulin.
  3. Explain the importance of homeostasis in maintaining a constant internal environment, limited to: body temperature; blood glucose concentration.
  4. Describe the causes of Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes.
  5. Explain how and why wilting occurs.

Maintaining Constant Body Temperature (Thermoregulation):

Mammals are endotherms, meaning they maintain a constant internal body temperature, typically around 37Â°C. This process, called **thermoregulation**, is controlled by the **hypothalamus** in the brain, which acts as the body's thermostat.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the homeostatic control of body temperature.*

When body temperature deviates from the set point, the hypothalamus initiates responses:

  • **When too hot:** * **Vasodilation:** Arterioles (small arteries) near the skin surface **dilate** (widen), increasing blood flow to the skin. This allows more heat to radiate away from the body, cooling it down. * **Sweating:** Sweat glands produce sweat, which evaporates from the skin surface. Evaporation is a cooling process, as it absorbs latent heat from the body.
  • **When too cold:** * **Vasoconstriction:** Arterioles near the skin surface **constrict** (narrow), reducing blood flow to the skin. This minimizes heat loss from the body's surface, conserving heat. * **Shivering:** Muscles contract rapidly and involuntarily. This muscular activity generates heat through increased respiration, helping to warm the body.

Control of Blood Glucose Concentration:

Maintaining a stable **blood glucose concentration** is crucial for providing a constant energy supply to cells. This is primarily controlled by the **pancreas** and the hormone **insulin** (and glucagon, though not explicitly required at this level).

  • **When blood glucose is too high (e.g., after a meal):** The pancreas releases **insulin**. Insulin signals the liver and muscle cells to take up glucose from the blood and convert it into **glycogen** (a storage carbohydrate). This lowers blood glucose levels.
  • **When blood glucose is too low (e.g., after exercise or fasting):** The pancreas releases **glucagon**. Glucagon signals the liver to convert stored glycogen back into glucose and release it into the blood, raising blood glucose levels.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the hormonal control of blood glucose concentration.*

Importance of Homeostasis:

Homeostasis is vital because it ensures that internal conditions (like temperature, pH, blood glucose, water potential) remain within narrow limits, which are optimal for:

  • **Enzyme activity:** Enzymes have optimum temperatures and pH values. Deviations can lead to denaturation and loss of function.
  • **Cell function:** Cells require stable conditions to carry out metabolic reactions efficiently.
  • **Overall organism survival:** Failure of homeostatic mechanisms can lead to disease or death.

Diabetes Mellitus:

**Diabetes mellitus** is a chronic condition characterized by persistently high blood glucose levels, resulting from problems with insulin production or action.

  • **Type 1 Diabetes:** * **Cause:** An autoimmune disease where the body's immune system attacks and destroys the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. * **Effect:** The body produces little or no insulin. Requires regular insulin injections.
  • **Type 2 Diabetes:** * **Cause:** Often develops later in life, linked to lifestyle factors (obesity, lack of exercise) and genetics. The body either does not produce enough insulin or the body's cells become resistant to insulin (insulin resistance). * **Effect:** Blood glucose levels remain high. Can often be managed through diet, exercise, and medication, but may eventually require insulin.

Wilting in Plants:

While not directly human homeostasis, wilting in plants is an example of a response to maintain water balance. **Wilting** occurs when a plant loses water by transpiration faster than it can absorb water from the soil. This leads to a loss of turgor pressure in the plant cells.

**Why wilting occurs:** When water leaves plant cells by osmosis, their vacuoles shrink, and the cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall (plasmolysis). The cells become **flaccid** (limp), and the plant loses its rigidity, causing leaves and stems to droop. Wilting reduces the surface area exposed to sunlight and wind, thereby reducing the rate of transpiration and conserving water.
