7.2.4 Elements, compounds and mixtures

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define and give examples of an element, a compound, and a mixture.
  2. Describe the differences between them using particle diagrams.
  3. Understand that mixtures can be separated by physical means.

What's the Difference?

All matter can be classified into three main groups: elements, compounds, and mixtures. Understanding the difference between them is key to understanding chemistry.

Analogy: LEGO Bricks

Let's use our LEGO analogy one more time:

  • An **Element** is like a big pile of only one type of brick, for example, all red 2x4 bricks. It's a pure substance made of only one type of atom.
  • A **Compound** is a new structure you build by clicking different bricks together in a fixed way (e.g., one red brick always clicked to two blue bricks). This new structure is a "molecule," and it has different properties from the original bricks. A compound is a pure substance made of two or more types of atoms chemically bonded together.
  • A **Mixture** is like a big box containing a jumble of separate red bricks and blue bricks. They are all mixed up, but they aren't clicked together. A mixture contains different substances that are not chemically bonded.

Element

All atoms are of the same type.

Compound

Different atoms are chemically bonded together in a fixed ratio.

Mixture

Different elements or compounds are mixed together but are not bonded.

At-Home Experiment: Paper Chromatography - Separating Inks

Is the black ink in your felt-tip pen a single substance, or is it a mixture of different coloured dyes? This simple experiment lets you find out! Chromatography is a technique used to separate mixtures.

Equipment:
  • A coffee filter or a piece of kitchen roll
  • A black, water-soluble felt-tip pen (check it's not a permanent marker!)
  • A tall glass or jar
  • A pencil
  • A small amount of water
  • Scissors
Method:
  1. Cut the coffee filter or kitchen roll into a rectangular strip, about 3 cm wide and long enough to reach from the bottom of the glass to the top.
  2. Draw a horizontal line in pencil about 2 cm from the bottom of the strip. **Do not use a pen for this line!**
  3. Draw a single, small but concentrated dot of black ink in the centre of the pencil line.
  4. Pour about 1 cm of water into the bottom of the glass.
  5. Wrap the top of the paper strip around the pencil and rest the pencil across the top of the glass, so that the bottom of the paper just dips into the water. **Crucially, the ink dot itself should be above the water level.**
  6. Watch as the water soaks up the paper. As it passes the ink dot, it will carry the different coloured dyes with it at different speeds, separating them out into a colourful pattern.
  7. Once the water is close to the top, take the paper out and let it dry. You have created a chromatogram!
