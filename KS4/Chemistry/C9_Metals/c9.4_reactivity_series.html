C9.4 Reactivity series

The **reactivity series** is a list of metals arranged in order of their decreasing reactivity. It helps us predict the outcome of various chemical reactions involving metals, particularly their reactions with water, acids, and displacement reactions with other metal ions. Understanding the reactivity series is crucial for explaining phenomena like corrosion and the extraction of metals.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the general chemical properties of metals, limited to their reactions with: dilute acids; cold water and steam.
  2. Describe the reactions, if any, of: potassium, sodium and calcium with cold water; magnesium with steam; magnesium, zinc, iron, copper, silver and gold with dilute hydrochloric acid, and explain these reactions in terms of the position of the metals and hydrogen in the reactivity series.
  3. Deduce an order of reactivity from a given set of experimental results.
  4. State the order of the reactivity series as: potassium, sodium, calcium, magnesium, aluminium, carbon, zinc, iron, hydrogen, copper, silver, gold.
  5. Describe the relative reactivities of metals in terms of their tendency to form positive ions, by displacement reactions, if any, with the aqueous ions of magnesium, zinc, iron, copper and silver.

Understanding the Reactivity Series:

The **reactivity series** is a ranking of metals from most reactive to least reactive. A more reactive metal has a greater tendency to lose electrons and form positive ions.

The common order of the reactivity series, including carbon and hydrogen as reference points, is:

Potassium > Sodium > Calcium > Magnesium > Aluminium > Carbon > Zinc > Iron > Hydrogen > Copper > Silver > Gold

*Figure: The reactivity series of metals, showing their order from most to least reactive.*

Reactions with Water, Steam, and Dilute Acids:

The position of a metal in the reactivity series dictates its general chemical properties, particularly its reactions with water, steam, and dilute acids:

  • **Potassium, Sodium, Calcium:** React vigorously with **cold water** to produce hydrogen gas and a metal hydroxide (alkali). Reactivity increases down the group.
  • **Magnesium:** Reacts very slowly with cold water. Reacts readily with **steam** to produce hydrogen gas and magnesium oxide.
  • **Zinc, Iron:** Do not react with cold water. React with **steam** to produce hydrogen gas and the metal oxide. React with **dilute hydrochloric acid** to produce hydrogen gas and a metal chloride.
  • **Copper, Silver, Gold:** Do not react with cold water, steam, or dilute hydrochloric acid.

Metals above hydrogen in the reactivity series will react with dilute acids to produce hydrogen gas. Metals below hydrogen will not.

Displacement Reactions:

A more reactive metal can displace a less reactive metal from its salt solution. This is a type of redox reaction where the more reactive metal loses electrons (is oxidized) and the less reactive metal ion gains electrons (is reduced).

  • Example: `Zinc(s) + Copper(II) Sulfate(aq) -> Zinc Sulfate(aq) + Copper(s)` * Zinc is more reactive than copper, so it displaces copper from copper(II) sulfate solution. You would observe the zinc becoming coated with reddish-brown copper, and the blue copper(II) sulfate solution becoming colorless (zinc sulfate).

You can deduce the order of reactivity from experimental results by observing which metals displace others from their salt solutions.
