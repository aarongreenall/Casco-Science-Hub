C8.3 Group VII properties

Group VII elements, also known as the **halogens**, are located on the right side of the Periodic Table. They include chlorine (Cl), bromine (Br), and iodine (I). These non-metallic elements share characteristic physical and chemical properties due to having seven electrons in their outermost shell, making them highly reactive and prone to gaining one electron to form negative ions.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the Group VII halogens, chlorine, bromine and iodine, as diatomic non-metals with general trends down the group, limited to: increasing density; decreasing reactivity.
  2. State the appearance of the halogens at room temperature and pressure, r.t.p. as: chlorine, a pale yellow-green gas; bromine, a red-brown liquid; iodine, a grey-black solid.
  3. Describe and explain the displacement reactions of halogens with other halide ions.
  4. Predict the properties of other elements in Group VII, given information about the elements.

Physical Properties and Trends Down the Group:

The halogens exist as **diatomic non-metals** (e.g., Cl$_2$, Br$_2$, I$_2$). At room temperature and pressure (r.t.p.):

  • **Chlorine (Cl$_2$):** A pale yellow-green gas.
  • **Bromine (Br$_2$):** A red-brown liquid.
  • **Iodine (I$_2$):** A grey-black solid.

As you move **down Group VII** (from chlorine to iodine):

  • **Density:** Increases. The particles become heavier and larger, leading to a more compact arrangement in the solid/liquid states.
  • **Melting and Boiling Points:** Increase (due to stronger intermolecular forces as molecular size increases).

*Figure: Samples of chlorine (gas), bromine (liquid), and iodine (solid) at room temperature, illustrating their appearance and state.*

Chemical Properties and Reactivity:

Halogens are highly reactive non-metals because they readily gain one electron to form a -1 ion, achieving a stable outer electron shell.

As you move **down Group VII**, the **reactivity decreases**. This is because:

  • The atomic radius increases, meaning the outermost shell is further from the positively charged nucleus.
  • The shielding effect from inner electron shells increases.
  • The attraction for an incoming electron decreases, making it harder for the atom to gain an electron and thus less reactive.

Displacement Reactions of Halogens:

A more reactive halogen can displace a less reactive halide ion from its aqueous solution. This is a type of redox reaction.

  • **Chlorine displaces Bromide/Iodide:** Chlorine is more reactive than bromine and iodine. * `Cl2(aq) + 2NaBr(aq) -> 2NaCl(aq) + Br2(aq)` (Colorless to orange/brown) * `Cl2(aq) + 2NaI(aq) -> 2NaCl(aq) + I2(aq)` (Colorless to brown)
  • **Bromine displaces Iodide:** Bromine is more reactive than iodine. * `Br2(aq) + 2NaI(aq) -> 2NaBr(aq) + I2(aq)` (Orange/brown to brown)

These reactions provide evidence for the decreasing reactivity down the group.

Predicting Properties:

Based on these trends, you can predict the properties of other elements in Group VII (e.g., fluorine, a very reactive pale yellow gas, or astatine, a much less reactive solid).
