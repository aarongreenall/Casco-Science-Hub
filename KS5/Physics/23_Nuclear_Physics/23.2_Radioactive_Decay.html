23.2 Radioactive decay

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that fluctuations in count rate provide evidence for the random nature of radioactive decay.
  2. Understand that radioactive decay is both spontaneous and random.
  3. Define activity and decay constant, and recall and use $A = \lambda N$.
  4. Define half-life.
  5. Use $\lambda = 0.693 / t_{1/2}$.
  6. Understand the exponential nature of radioactive decay, and sketch and use the relationship $x = x_0e^{–\lambda t}$, where $x$ could represent activity, number of undecayed nuclei or received count rate.

LO1 & LO2: The Nature of Radioactive Decay

Radioactive decay is the process by which an unstable atomic nucleus loses energy by emitting radiation. This process is governed by two key principles: it is both **spontaneous** and **random**.

  • Spontaneous: The decay of a nucleus is not affected by any external physical or chemical conditions. Factors like temperature, pressure, or chemical bonds have no influence on when or if a nucleus will decay.
  • Random: It is impossible to predict which specific nucleus in a sample will decay next, or when any individual nucleus will decay. Each nucleus has a constant probability of decaying in a given time interval, but the actual moment of decay is a matter of chance.

The random nature of decay is directly observable. If you place a Geiger-Müller (GM) tube near a radioactive source, the count rate (the number of decays detected per second) is not constant. It fluctuates randomly about an average value. These **fluctuations in count rate provide direct evidence for the random nature of radioactive decay**.

LO3, LO4, LO5 & LO6: The Mathematics of Decay

Activity and the Decay Constant

While we cannot predict the decay of a single nucleus, the random nature of the process means we can use statistics to describe the overall behaviour of a large sample.

  • Activity (A): The rate at which nuclei in a sample decay. It is measured in **becquerels (Bq)**, where 1 Bq = 1 decay per second.
  • Decay Constant ($\lambda$): The probability that a single nucleus will decay per unit time. Its unit is s⁻¹, min⁻¹, etc.

The activity of a sample containing $N$ undecayed nuclei is given by:

$$ A = \lambda N $$

Exponential Decay and Half-Life

As a sample decays, the number of undecayed nuclei ($N$) decreases. Since activity is proportional to $N$, the activity also decreases. This decrease is not linear; it follows an **exponential decay** curve.

$$ x = x_0 e^{-\lambda t} $$

where $x$ can be the number of undecayed nuclei ($N$), the activity ($A$), or the received count rate ($C$). $x_0$ is the initial value at $t=0$.

A key measure of the rate of decay is the **half-life ($t_{1/2}$)**.

The half-life of a radioactive isotope is the average time taken for half of the undecayed nuclei in a sample to decay.

Detailed Derivation of $\lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}}$

1. Start with the exponential decay equation for the number of undecayed nuclei, $N$.

$$ N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} $$

2. By definition, at time $t = t_{1/2}$, the number of remaining nuclei is half the initial number: $N = N_0/2$.

3. Substitute these conditions into the decay equation:

$$ \frac{N_0}{2} = N_0 e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}} $$

4. Divide both sides by $N_0$:

$$ \frac{1}{2} = e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}} $$

5. Take the natural logarithm (ln) of both sides to eliminate the exponential:

$$ \ln\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = \ln(e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}}) $$

$$ -\ln(2) = -\lambda t_{1/2} $$

6. The negative signs cancel. Rearrange to make $\lambda$ the subject:

$$ \lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}} \approx \frac{0.693}{t_{1/2}} $$

Worked Example: Carbon Dating

An ancient wooden artifact is found to have an activity of 12.5 counts per minute. A modern sample of the same mass has an activity of 100.0 counts per minute. If the half-life of Carbon-14 is 5730 years, calculate the age of the artifact.

1. State the decay equation for activity:

$$ A = A_0 e^{-\lambda t} $$

2. Calculate the decay constant ($\lambda$):

$$ \lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}} = \frac{0.693}{5730 \text{ years}} = 1.21 \times 10^{-4} \text{ years}^{-1} $$

3. Substitute values and rearrange for $t$:

$$ 12.5 = 100.0 \times e^{-(1.21 \times 10^{-4})t} $$

$$ \frac{12.5}{100.0} = 0.125 = e^{-(1.21 \times 10^{-4})t} $$

4. Take natural logs of both sides:

$$ \ln(0.125) = -(1.21 \times 10^{-4})t $$

$$ -2.079 = -(1.21 \times 10^{-4})t $$

5. Solve for $t$:

$$ t = \frac{2.079}{1.21 \times 10^{-4}} = 17181 \text{ years} \approx 17200 \text{ years} $$
