18.1 Electric fields and field lines

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that an electric field is an example of a field of force and define electric field strength as force per unit positive charge.
  2. Recall and use $F = qE$ for the force on a charge in an electric field.
  3. Represent an electric field by means of field lines.

LO1: The Concept of an Electric Field

Just like the gravitational force, the electrostatic force is a non-contact force. To explain this action at a distance, we use the concept of a **field of force**. An **electric field** is a region of space where a charged object experiences an electrostatic force. Any object with an electric charge creates an electric field around it.

Comparison with Gravitational Fields

The concept of electric field strength is directly analogous to gravitational field strength. However, there is a critical difference that makes the vector nature of the electric field so important.

  • Gravitational Fields (The "One-Way Street"): The source of a gravitational field is **mass**, which is always positive. Therefore, the gravitational force is **only ever attractive**. The field direction is unambiguous: it always points towards the source mass.
  • Electric Fields (The "Two-Way Street"): The source of an electric field is **charge**, which can be positive or negative. This means the electric force can be **attractive or repulsive**. This creates an ambiguity: in which direction does the field itself point?

To solve this, physicists created a universal rule, or **convention**:

The direction of the electric field ($E$) at any point is defined as the direction of the force that would be exerted on a **positive** test charge placed at that point.

This leads to the formal definition of electric field strength:

$$ E = \frac{F}{q} $$

Where $E$ is the electric field strength (a vector), $F$ is the force on the charge, and $q$ is the charge of the test particle. The SI unit is the newton per coulomb (N C⁻¹).

LO2: Force on a Charge in an Electric Field

Rearranging the definition of electric field strength gives a formula for the force, $F$, on a charge, $q$, in a field of strength $E$. This is directly analogous to the formula for weight in a gravitational field.

Weight in a Gravitational Field

$$ F = mg $$

Force in an Electric Field

$$ F = qE $$

Because of the convention for the direction of $E$, the direction of the force $F$ depends on the sign of the charge $q$:

  • If the charge $q$ is **positive**, the force $F$ is in the **same direction** as the electric field $E$.
  • If the charge $q$ is **negative**, the force $F$ is in the **opposite direction** to the electric field $E$.

Worked Example: Force on an Electron

An electron is in a uniform electric field of strength 1500 N C⁻¹. Calculate the magnitude of the force on the electron and describe its direction relative to the field.

1. Identify the variables:

$$ E = 1500 \text{ N C}^{-1} $$

The charge on an electron is the elementary charge, $q = e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C.

2. State the formula and solve for the magnitude of the force:

$$ F = qE $$

$$ F = (1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) \times (1500 \text{ N C}^{-1}) $$

$$ F = 2.4 \times 10^{-16} \text{ N} $$

3. Describe the direction:

Since the electron has a negative charge, the force on it will be in the **opposite direction** to the electric field lines. This means if the field was created by a positive source, the electron would be attracted (move towards it). If the field was created by a negative source, the electron would be repelled (move away from it). In both cases, the direction of motion is opposite to the direction of the field line at that point.

LO3: Representing Electric Fields

We visualize electric fields using **electric field lines**. The direction of the field line at any point gives the direction of the force on a **positive** test charge.

Gravitational Field (Mass)

Always attractive. Lines always point towards the mass.

Electric Field (Positive Charge)

Repulsive to positive test charges. Lines point away from the charge.

Because electric charge can be positive or negative, electric fields can be more complex than gravitational fields, forming patterns like dipoles.
