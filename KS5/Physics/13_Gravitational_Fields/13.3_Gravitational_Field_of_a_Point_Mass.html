13.3 Gravitational field of a point mass

Learning Objectives:

  1. Derive, from Newton’s law of gravitation and the definition of gravitational field, the equation $g = GM / r^2$ for the gravitational field strength due to a point mass.
  2. Recall and use $g = GM / r^2$.
  3. Understand why $g$ is approximately constant for small changes in height near the Earth’s surface.

LO1 & LO2: Field Strength of a Point Mass

We can combine Newton's Law of Gravitation with the definition of gravitational field strength to find an expression for the field strength, $g$, at a distance $r$ from a point mass $M$.

Detailed Derivation of $g = GM/r^2$

1. Start with the definition of gravitational field strength, which is the force per unit mass on a small test mass, $m$:

$$ g = \frac{F}{m} $$

2. Now, consider the force $F$ acting on this test mass $m$ due to the gravitational field of a larger point mass $M$. This force is given by Newton's Law of Gravitation:

$$ F = \frac{GMm}{r^2} $$

3. Substitute the expression for the force $F$ from Newton's Law into the definition of field strength:

$$ g = \frac{\left(\frac{GMm}{r^2}\right)}{m} $$

4. The test mass $m$ in the numerator and the denominator cancels out:

$$ g = \frac{GM\cancel{m}}{r^2\cancel{m}} $$

5. This leaves the final formula for the gravitational field strength due to a point mass $M$:

$$ g = \frac{GM}{r^2} $$

This is a powerful result because it shows that the field strength at a point depends only on the mass creating the field ($M$) and the distance from it ($r$), not on the mass of the object placed in the field.

Worked Example: Field Strength on the Surface of the Earth

Calculate the gravitational field strength on the surface of the Earth. (Mass of Earth, $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24}$ kg; Radius of Earth, $r = 6.37 \times 10^6$ m).

1. State the formula:

$$ g = \frac{GM}{r^2} $$

2. Substitute the values:

$$ g = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24})}{(6.37 \times 10^6)^2} $$

3. Calculate the result:

$$ g = \frac{3.982 \times 10^{14}}{4.058 \times 10^{13}} $$

$$ g = 9.81 \text{ N kg}^{-1} $$

This confirms the value we commonly use for the acceleration of free fall.

LO3: Why 'g' is Approximately Constant Near the Earth's Surface

The formula $g = GM/r^2$ shows that the gravitational field strength depends on the distance $r$ from the centre of the Earth. As you move further away, $r$ increases and $g$ decreases. However, for everyday changes in height near the Earth's surface, this change is negligible.

This is because the radius of the Earth ($R_E \approx 6400$ km) is vastly larger than any typical change in height ($h$). When we are at a height $h$ above the surface, the distance from the centre is $r = R_E + h$. Since $h$ is so small compared to $R_E$, the value of $r$ barely changes, and therefore the value of $g$ remains approximately constant.

Worked Example: 'g' at the top of Mount Everest

Calculate the value of $g$ at the summit of Mount Everest, which is approximately 8848 m above sea level. (Radius of Earth, $R_E = 6.37 \times 10^6$ m).

1. Calculate the new distance from the centre of the Earth, $r$:

$$ r = R_E + h = (6.37 \times 10^6) + 8848 = 6,378,848 \text{ m} $$

2. Apply the formula for $g$:

$$ g_{Everest} = \frac{GM}{r^2} = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24})}{(6,378,848)^2} $$

$$ g_{Everest} = \frac{3.982 \times 10^{14}}{4.069 \times 10^{13}} = 9.786 \text{ N kg}^{-1} $$
