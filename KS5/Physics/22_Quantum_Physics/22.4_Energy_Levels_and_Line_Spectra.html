22.4 Energy levels and line spectra

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that there are discrete electron energy levels in isolated atoms (e.g. atomic hydrogen).
  2. Understand the appearance and formation of emission and absorption line spectra.
  3. Recall and use $hf = E_{higher} – E_{lower}$.

LO1: Discrete Electron Energy Levels

The Rutherford model of the atom, with electrons orbiting a central nucleus like planets around the sun, had a major flaw. According to classical physics, any accelerating charge must radiate electromagnetic energy. An electron in a circular orbit is continuously accelerating (as its direction is changing), so it should continuously lose energy and spiral into the nucleus in a fraction of a second. This "death spiral" obviously does not happen, meaning the classical model was incomplete.

Niels Bohr proposed a radical new model to solve this problem. While his model still pictured electrons in planetary-like orbits, he made a revolutionary postulate: he suggested that electrons could only exist in specific, "special" or "stable" orbits without radiating energy, in defiance of classical physics.

Each of these stable orbits corresponds to a specific, **discrete energy level**. An electron in an isolated atom cannot have an energy value between these levels; its energy is **quantised**. While Bohr's model of fixed orbits was later superseded by Schrödinger's full quantum mechanical model (which describes electrons in probability clouds called orbitals), Bohr's core concept of discrete, quantised energy levels remains a fundamental part of our modern understanding.

  • The lowest possible energy level an electron can occupy is called the **ground state** (labelled n=1).
  • Any energy level higher than the ground state is called an **excited state** (n=2, n=3, etc.).

By convention, the energy of an electron that is completely free from the atom (at an infinite distance, n=$\infty$) is defined as 0 eV. Since work must be done to move an electron from a stable orbit out to infinity, all the energy levels within the atom have **negative** energy values.

LO2: Emission and Absorption Spectra

The existence of discrete energy levels provides a perfect explanation for the characteristic line spectra observed from different elements.

Emission Line Spectra

When an atom is excited (e.g., by heating it in a flame or passing a high voltage through a gas), its electrons jump to higher energy levels. These excited states are unstable, and the electrons quickly fall back down to lower levels. When an electron de-excites, it emits a single photon. The energy of this photon is exactly equal to the energy difference between the two levels. Since only specific energy transitions are possible, only photons of specific energies (and therefore specific frequencies and wavelengths) are emitted. When this light is passed through a prism or diffraction grating, it produces a spectrum of discrete bright lines on a dark background.

Absorption Line Spectra

When white light (containing a continuous range of all frequencies) is passed through a cool gas, the atoms in the gas will absorb photons. However, an atom can only absorb a photon if its energy is exactly equal to the energy difference between two of its electron energy levels. This excites the electron to a higher level. These specific frequencies are therefore removed from the spectrum of the transmitted light. When this light is passed through a prism, it produces a continuous spectrum with discrete dark lines at the exact positions where the emission lines would be for that element.

LO3: The Energy Transition Equation

The relationship between the energy of an emitted or absorbed photon and the electron energy levels is given by the following equation, which is a direct application of the conservation of energy.

$$ hf = E_{higher} - E_{lower} $$

Where $E_{higher}$ is the energy of the higher energy level and $E_{lower}$ is the energy of the lower energy level involved in the transition.

Converting between Joules and Electronvolts

Energy levels are almost always given in electronvolts (eV), but the Planck constant is in joule-seconds (J s). It is essential to be able to convert between these units.

$$ 1 \text{ eV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} $$

  • To convert from **eV to J**, you **multiply** by $1.60 \times 10^{-19}$.
  • To convert from **J to eV**, you **divide** by $1.60 \times 10^{-19}$.

Worked Example: Emission Spectrum of Hydrogen

Using the energy level diagram for hydrogen shown above, calculate the wavelength of the photon emitted when an electron transitions from the n=3 level to the n=2 level.

1. Identify the energy levels:

$$ E_{higher} = E_3 = -1.51 \text{ eV} $$

$$ E_{lower} = E_2 = -3.40 \text{ eV} $$

2. Calculate the energy of the emitted photon in eV:

$$ \Delta E = E_{higher} - E_{lower} = (-1.51) - (-3.40) = 1.89 \text{ eV} $$

3. Convert the photon energy to Joules:

$$ E_{Joules} = 1.89 \text{ eV} \times (1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/eV}) = 3.024 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} $$

4. Use the photon energy equation to find the wavelength:

$$ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \implies \lambda = \frac{hc}{E} $$

$$ \lambda = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{3.024 \times 10^{-19}} $$

$$ \lambda = 6.58 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} = 658 \text{ nm} $$

This corresponds to a specific red line in the visible spectrum of hydrogen (the H-alpha line).
