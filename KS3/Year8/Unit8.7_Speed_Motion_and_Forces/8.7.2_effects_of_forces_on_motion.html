8.7.2 Effects of forces on motion

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define balanced and unbalanced forces.
  2. Explain how balanced forces result in no change in motion.
  3. Explain how an unbalanced force causes a change in motion (speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction).

Balanced vs. Unbalanced Forces

Forces are pushes or pulls that act on objects. Usually, there is more than one force acting on an object at any time. The overall effect of these forces depends on whether they are balanced or unbalanced.

Analogy: A Tug-of-War

A tug-of-war is a perfect way to understand the difference between balanced and unbalanced forces.

  • Balanced Forces: Imagine two teams are pulling on a rope with exactly the same strength. The rope doesn't move. The forces are equal in size and opposite in direction, so they cancel each other out.
  • Unbalanced Forces: Now, imagine one team suddenly pulls harder. The forces are no longer equal. There is a net force in the direction of the stronger team, and the rope (and the other team!) will accelerate in that direction.

Balanced Forces

If the forces on an object are balanced, its motion does not change. If it's stationary, it stays stationary. If it's moving, it continues to move at a constant speed in a straight line.

Unbalanced Forces

If the forces on an object are unbalanced, its motion will change. An unbalanced force will cause the object to speed up, slow down, or change direction.

Explanation: Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

At-Home Experiment: The Parachute Egg Drop Challenge

Your challenge is to design and build a parachute that can safely land a raw egg when dropped from a height. This experiment explores the forces of gravity and air resistance.

Equipment:
  • A raw egg
  • A plastic bag (like a bin bag or shopping bag)
  • String
  • Scissors and tape
  • A small container for the egg (like a yogurt pot or a paper cup)
Method:
  1. Place your egg safely inside the container.
  2. Cut a large square or circle from the plastic bag to be your parachute canopy.
  3. Attach several pieces of string of equal length from the corners of your canopy to the egg container.
  4. Find a safe, high place to drop your egg (ask an adult for permission and help!). A first-floor window is ideal.
  5. Drop your parachute and observe its descent. Did the egg survive?
The Science:

When you drop the egg, two main forces are acting on it: **gravity** pulling it down, and **air resistance** pushing it up. Your goal is to make the air resistance as large as possible to balance the force of gravity. A large parachute canopy catches more air, creating a large upward force of air resistance. This slows the parachute down, allowing your egg to land gently.
