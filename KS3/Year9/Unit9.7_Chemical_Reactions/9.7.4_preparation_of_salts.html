9.7.4 Preparation of Salts

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe how to prepare some common salts by the reactions of metals with acids, and metal carbonates with acids, and purify them, using filtration, evaporation and crystallisation.

What is a Salt?

In chemistry, a **salt** is an ionic compound formed when an **acid** is neutralised by a **base** (such as a metal, a metal oxide, or a metal carbonate). The positive ion in the salt comes from the base, and the negative ion comes from the acid.

Making Salts: Two Key Recipes

1. Metal + Acid

Metal + Acid $\rightarrow$ Salt + Hydrogen

Symbol Equation:

$Zn(s) + H_2SO_4(aq) \rightarrow ZnSO_4(aq) + H_2(g)$

2. Metal Carbonate + Acid

Metal Carbonate + Acid $\rightarrow$ Salt + Carbon Dioxide + Water

Symbol Equation:

$CuCO_3(s) + 2HNO_3(aq) \rightarrow Cu(NO_3)_2(aq) + CO_2(g) + H_2O(l)$

From Solution to Crystal: Purification

After these reactions, the salt you have made is dissolved in water. To get a pure, solid sample of the salt, we need to follow a three-step purification process.

  1. Filtration: First, add an excess of the solid reactant (the metal or metal carbonate) to ensure all the acid reacts. Then, **filter** the mixture to remove the unreacted solid, leaving a pure salt solution.
  2. Evaporation: Next, gently heat the salt solution to boil off about half of the water, making the solution concentrated.
  3. Crystallisation: Finally, leave the hot, concentrated solution to cool down slowly. As it cools, the salt becomes less soluble and starts to form pure crystals.
