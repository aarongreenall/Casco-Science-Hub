P5.2 The three types of emission

Radioactive decay is a spontaneous and random process by which unstable atomic nuclei transform into more stable forms, emitting radiation in the process. There are three primary types of emissions: alpha ($\alpha$), beta ($\beta$), and gamma ($\gamma$) radiation, each with distinct properties. Understanding these emissions is crucial for comprehending radioactivity and its applications.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Identify alpha ($\alpha$), beta ($\beta$) and gamma ($\gamma$) emissions by recalling: their nature, their relative ionising effects, their relative penetrating abilities ($\beta^+$ are not included, $\beta^-$-particles will be taken to refer to $\beta^-$).
  2. Know that radioactive decay is a change in an unstable nucleus that can result in the emission of $\alpha$-particles or $\beta$-particles and/or $\gamma$-radiation and know that these changes are spontaneous and random.
  3. Know that during $\alpha$-decay or $\beta$-decay, the nucleus changes to that of a different element.
  4. Know the change in the nucleus that occurs during $\beta$-emission: neutron $\rightarrow$ proton + electron.
  5. Use decay equations, using nuclide notation, to show the emission of $\alpha$-particles, $\beta$-particles and $\gamma$-radiation.

Understanding Radioactive Emissions:

Radioactive decay is a **spontaneous** (not influenced by external conditions like temperature or pressure) and **random** (impossible to predict when a specific nucleus will decay) process. It occurs when an unstable nucleus emits particles or energy to become more stable.

The three main types of emissions are:

  • **Alpha ($\alpha$) particles:** * **Nature:** Consist of two protons and two neutrons (identical to a helium nucleus, $^4_2He$). They are positively charged (+2). * **Ionising effect:** Very strong. They cause significant ionization as they pass through matter. * **Penetrating ability:** Very low. They can be stopped by a sheet of paper or a few centimeters of air.
  • **Beta ($\beta$) particles:** * **Nature:** High-speed electrons (specifically $\beta^-$ particles). They are negatively charged (-1). They are formed when a neutron in the nucleus changes into a proton and an electron (neutron $\rightarrow$ proton + electron). * **Ionising effect:** Medium. Less ionising than alpha particles. * **Penetrating ability:** Medium. They can be stopped by a few millimeters of aluminum.
  • **Gamma ($\gamma$) radiation:** * **Nature:** High-frequency electromagnetic waves (like X-rays, but with higher energy). They have no mass and no charge. * **Ionising effect:** Very weak. * **Penetrating ability:** Very high. They require thick lead or concrete to be significantly absorbed.

Changes to the Nucleus during Decay:

During alpha or beta decay, the unstable nucleus changes into the nucleus of a **different element**. This is because the number of protons (atomic number, $Z$) changes:

  • **Alpha decay:** The nucleus loses 2 protons and 2 neutrons. The proton number ($Z$) decreases by 2, and the nucleon number ($A$) decreases by 4.
  • **Beta decay:** A neutron changes into a proton and an electron. The proton number ($Z$) increases by 1, while the nucleon number ($A$) remains unchanged.
  • **Gamma emission:** This is often emitted alongside alpha or beta decay when the nucleus is left in an excited state. It does not change the proton number or nucleon number of the nucleus.

Decay Equations (Nuclide Notation):

Radioactive decay can be represented using **decay equations** with nuclide notation ($^A_ZX$).

**Alpha Decay Example (Uranium-238 to Thorium-234):**

$^{238}_{92}U \rightarrow ^{234}_{90}Th + ^4_2He$

**Beta Decay Example (Carbon-14 to Nitrogen-14):**

$^{14}_6C \rightarrow ^{14}_7N + ^0_{-1}e$

**Gamma Emission Example (often shown with an asterisk for excited state):**

$^{60}_{27}Co^* \rightarrow ^{60}_{27}Co + \gamma$
