10.1 Practical circuits

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand the effects of the internal resistance of a source of e.m.f. on the terminal potential difference and output power.
  2. Recall and use the concept of electromotive force (e.m.f.).
  3. Recall and use the formula e.m.f. = $I(R+r)$.
  4. Draw and interpret circuit diagrams containing sources, switches, resistors, ammeters, voltmeters, and/or any other type of component referred to in the syllabus.

LO2: Electromotive Force (e.m.f.) and Potential Difference (p.d.)

While both e.m.f. and p.d. are measured in volts, they describe different processes of energy transfer in a circuit.

  • Electromotive Force ($\mathcal{E}$ or e.m.f.): The work done per unit charge by a source (like a battery) in converting other forms of energy (e.g., chemical) into electrical energy. It is the energy **supplied to** each coulomb of charge.
  • Potential Difference (V): The work done per unit charge by the charge carriers in converting electrical energy into other forms (e.g., heat and light in a bulb). It is the energy **transferred by** each coulomb of charge to a component.

The Shopping Mall Analogy

Imagine a shopping mall where the shoppers are charge carriers (current). The **e.m.f.** of the battery is like an ATM at the entrance that gives each shopper a fixed amount of money (e.g., £12) to spend. This is the energy supplied. The **potential difference** across a shop is the amount of money each shopper spends in that specific shop (e.g., £5 spent in the first shop, £7 in the second). This is the energy transferred.

LO1 & LO3: Internal Resistance

All real power sources have some **internal resistance ($r$)**. As current flows through the source, work must be done to overcome this resistance, which is converted into thermal energy. The energy transferred per unit charge due to this process results in a potential difference across the internal resistor, often called **"lost volts"**.

Detailed Derivation of e.m.f. = $I(R+r)$

1. By the principle of conservation of energy, the total energy supplied by the source per unit charge (e.m.f.) must equal the total energy transferred in the circuit per unit charge.

$$ \mathcal{E} = (\text{p.d. across external circuit}) + (\text{p.d. across internal resistance}) $$

2. The p.d. across the external circuit (with total external resistance $R$) is the terminal p.d., $V = IR$.

3. The p.d. across the internal resistance $r$ (the "lost volts") is given by $V_{lost} = Ir$.

4. Substituting these into the energy conservation statement:

$$ \mathcal{E} = IR + Ir $$

5. Factoring out the current, $I$, gives the final relationship:

$$ \mathcal{E} = I(R+r) $$

LO4: Circuit Diagrams and Rules

Understanding how current, voltage, and resistance behave in series and parallel combinations is fundamental.

Series Circuits

Components are connected end-to-end, providing only one path for the current.

  • Current: Is the same through all components. $I_T = I_1 = I_2 = \dots$
  • Voltage: The total e.m.f. from the source is shared between the components. $V_T = V_1 + V_2 + \dots$
  • Resistance: The total resistance is the sum of the individual resistances. $R_T = R_1 + R_2 + \dots$

Derivation of Series Resistance Rule

1. Start with the rule for voltage in series: $V_T = V_1 + V_2 + \dots$

2. Apply Ohm's Law ($V=IR$) to each term. The total resistance is $R_T$.

$$ I_T R_T = I_1 R_1 + I_2 R_2 + \dots $$

3. The current is the same everywhere in a series circuit ($I_T = I_1 = I_2 = I$).

$$ I R_T = I R_1 + I R_2 + \dots $$

4. Divide the entire equation by the common current, $I$:

$$ R_T = R_1 + R_2 + \dots $$

Parallel Circuits

Components are connected across the same two points, providing multiple paths for the current.

  • Current: The total current from the source splits between the branches. $I_T = I_1 + I_2 + \dots$
  • Voltage: Is the same across all components in parallel. $V_T = V_1 = V_2 = \dots$
  • Resistance: The reciprocal of the total resistance is the sum of the reciprocals of the individual resistances. $\frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \dots$

Derivation of Parallel Resistance Rule

1. Start with the rule for current in parallel: $I_T = I_1 + I_2 + \dots$

2. Apply Ohm's Law rearranged for current ($I=V/R$) to each term.

$$ \frac{V_T}{R_T} = \frac{V_1}{R_1} + \frac{V_2}{R_2} + \dots $$

3. The voltage is the same across all branches in a parallel circuit ($V_T = V_1 = V_2 = V$).

$$ \frac{V}{R_T} = \frac{V}{R_1} + \frac{V}{R_2} + \dots $$

4. Divide the entire equation by the common voltage, $V$:

$$ \frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \dots $$
