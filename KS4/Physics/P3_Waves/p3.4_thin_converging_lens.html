P3.4 Thin converging lens

Lenses are fundamental optical components that manipulate light rays to form images. A **thin converging lens**, also known as a convex lens, is thicker in the middle than at the edges and causes parallel light rays to converge to a single point. Understanding how these lenses work is crucial for explaining the operation of magnifying glasses, cameras, projectors, and the human eye itself.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the action of a thin converging lens on a parallel beam of light and know that rays of light from an object at distance can be assumed to be parallel.
  2. Define and use the terms principal axis, principal focus (focal point) and focal length.
  3. Draw and use ray diagrams for the formation of an image by a thin converging lens, limited to real images.
  4. Describe the characteristics of an image using the terms enlarged / same size / diminished and upright / inverted.
  5. Draw and use ray diagrams for the formation of a virtual image by a thin converging lens.
  6. Describe the characteristics of an image using the terms real / virtual.
  7. Describe the use of a single lens as a magnifying glass.

Lens Terminology and Ray Diagrams:

To understand how lenses form images, we use specific terminology and ray diagrams:

  • **Principal Axis:** An imaginary straight line passing through the optical center of the lens and perpendicular to its surfaces.
  • **Principal Focus (Focal Point, $F$):** For a converging lens, this is the point on the principal axis where parallel rays of light converge after passing through the lens. A converging lens has two principal foci, one on each side.
  • **Focal Length ($f$):** The distance between the optical center of the lens and its principal focus.

When an object is placed at a very large distance from a lens (effectively at infinity), the light rays coming from it can be assumed to be parallel. A converging lens brings these parallel rays to a focus at its principal focus.

Image Formation (Real and Virtual Images):

Converging lenses can form two types of images:

  • **Real Images:** Formed when actual light rays converge at a point. Real images can be projected onto a screen, are always inverted, and can be enlarged, diminished, or same size depending on the object's distance from the lens.
  • **Virtual Images:** Formed when light rays only *appear* to diverge from a point. Virtual images cannot be projected onto a screen, are always upright, and are typically enlarged when formed by a converging lens used as a magnifying glass.

**Ray diagrams** are essential tools to determine the position, size, orientation (upright or inverted), and nature (real or virtual) of the image formed by a lens. For a converging lens, three principal rays are typically used:

  1. A ray parallel to the principal axis passes through the principal focus after refraction.
  2. A ray passing through the optical center of the lens continues undeviated.
  3. A ray passing through the principal focus emerges parallel to the principal axis after refraction.

Use as a Magnifying Glass:

When an object is placed within the focal length of a converging lens, it acts as a **magnifying glass**, producing an enlarged, upright, and virtual image. The light rays from the object appear to diverge from a point further away, making the object appear larger.
