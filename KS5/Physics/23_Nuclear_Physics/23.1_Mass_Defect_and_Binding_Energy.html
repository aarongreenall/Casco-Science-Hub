23.1 Mass defect and binding energy

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand the equivalence between energy and mass as represented by $E = mc^2$ and recall and use this equation.
  2. Represent simple nuclear reactions by nuclear equations of the form $^{14}_{7}N + ^{4}_{2}He \rightarrow ^{17}_{8}O + ^{1}_{1}H$.
  3. Define and use the terms mass defect and binding energy.
  4. Sketch the variation of binding energy per nucleon with nucleon number.
  5. Explain what is meant by nuclear fusion and nuclear fission.
  6. Explain the relevance of binding energy per nucleon to nuclear reactions, including nuclear fusion and nuclear fission.
  7. Calculate the energy released in nuclear reactions using $E = c^2\Delta m$.

LO1: Mass-Energy Equivalence

One of the most profound principles in physics, discovered by Albert Einstein, is that mass and energy are equivalent and can be converted into one another. Mass can be thought of as a highly concentrated form of energy.

$$ E = mc^2 $$

Where $E$ is energy, $m$ is mass, and $c$ is the speed of light in a vacuum ($3.00 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹). Because $c^2$ is a huge number, this equation shows that a tiny amount of mass can be converted into a vast amount of energy. This principle is the key to understanding all nuclear reactions.

LO2: Nuclear Equations

Nuclear reactions, including radioactive decay, fission, and fusion, can be represented by balanced nuclear equations. In any nuclear reaction, two quantities must be conserved:

  1. Nucleon Number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons must be the same on both sides of the equation.
  2. Proton Number (Z): The total charge (number of protons) must be the same on both sides of the equation.

LO3 & LO7: Mass Defect and Binding Energy

Careful measurements show that the mass of a nucleus is always slightly *less* than the sum of the masses of its individual, separate constituent protons and neutrons.

The **mass defect ($\Delta m$)** is the difference between the total mass of the separate nucleons and the mass of the nucleus.

This "missing mass" has been converted into energy according to $E=mc^2$. This energy is the **binding energy ($E_B$)** of the nucleus.

The **binding energy** is the minimum energy required to completely separate a nucleus into its constituent nucleons. It is also the energy released when a nucleus is formed from its constituent nucleons.

The energy released in any nuclear reaction is calculated using the mass defect and Einstein's equation:

$$ E = \Delta m c^2 $$

The mathematics for calculating binding energy can look complicated with lots of small numbers, but there are some simple rules to follow to avoid errors.

  1. Count the number of protons in the nucleus and multiply by the mass of one proton.
  2. Count the number of neutrons in the nucleus and multiply by the mass of one neutron.
  3. Add these two values together to get the total mass of the separate parts.
  4. Subtract the actual mass of the nucleus from this total to find the mass defect, $\Delta m$.
  5. **Never round your numbers during these steps.** The mass defect is a very small quantity, and premature rounding will lead to significant errors.

Worked Example 1: Binding Energy of Helium-4

Calculate the binding energy of a helium-4 nucleus ($^{4}_{2}He$).
Mass of proton = $1.67262 \times 10^{-27}$ kg
Mass of neutron = $1.67493 \times 10^{-27}$ kg
Mass of He-4 nucleus = $6.64466 \times 10^{-27}$ kg

1. Calculate the total mass of the separate nucleons:

$$ \text{Total mass} = (2 \times m_p) + (2 \times m_n) $$

$$ = (2 \times 1.67262 \times 10^{-27}) + (2 \times 1.67493 \times 10^{-27}) $$

$$ = (3.34524 \times 10^{-27}) + (3.34986 \times 10^{-27}) $$

Factor out the common power of 10:

$$ = (3.34524 + 3.34986) \times 10^{-27} = 6.69510 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg} $$

2. Calculate the mass defect ($\Delta m$):

$$ \Delta m = (6.69510 \times 10^{-27}) - (6.64466 \times 10^{-27}) = 0.05044 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg} $$

3. Calculate the binding energy ($E_B$):

$$ E_B = \Delta m c^2 = (0.05044 \times 10^{-27}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)^2 $$

$$ E_B = 4.54 \times 10^{-12} \text{ J} $$

Worked Example 2: Energy Released in Alpha Decay

Calculate the energy released in the alpha decay of Radium-226. The reaction is: $^{226}_{88}Ra \rightarrow ^{222}_{86}Rn + ^{4}_{2}He$.
Mass of Ra-226 nucleus = 226.02541 u
Mass of Rn-222 nucleus = 222.01758 u
Mass of He-4 nucleus = 4.00260 u
(1 u = $1.6605 \times 10^{-27}$ kg)

1. Calculate the total mass before the decay:

$$ m_{before} = 226.02541 \text{ u} $$

2. Calculate the total mass after the decay:

$$ m_{after} = m_{Rn} + m_{He} = 222.01758 + 4.00260 = 226.02018 \text{ u} $$

3. Calculate the mass defect ($\Delta m$) in atomic mass units (u):

$$ \Delta m = m_{before} - m_{after} = 226.02541 - 226.02018 = 0.00523 \text{ u} $$

4. Convert the mass defect to kilograms:

$$ \Delta m_{kg} = 0.00523 \times (1.6605 \times 10^{-27}) = 8.684 \times 10^{-30} \text{ kg} $$

5. Calculate the energy released ($E$):

$$ E = \Delta m c^2 = (8.684 \times 10^{-30}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)^2 $$

$$ E = 7.82 \times 10^{-13} \text{ J} $$

LO4, LO5 & LO6: Binding Energy per Nucleon and Nuclear Reactions

To compare the stability of different nuclei, we use the **binding energy per nucleon ($E_B/A$)**. This is the total binding energy of a nucleus divided by the number of nucleons it contains. A higher binding energy per nucleon indicates a more stable nucleus.

The graph of binding energy per nucleon against nucleon number is one of the most important in physics. It shows that the most stable nuclei are in the region of iron (Fe), which has the highest peak. This graph is the key to understanding energy release in nuclear reactions:

  • Nuclear Fission: A very heavy, unstable nucleus (like Uranium-235) is on the right side of the peak, with a relatively low binding energy per nucleon. If it splits into two smaller, more stable nuclei (which are closer to the peak), the new nuclei have a **higher** binding energy per nucleon. This increase in binding energy is released as a large amount of energy.
  • Nuclear Fusion: Very light nuclei (like hydrogen isotopes) are on the left side of the peak, with a very low binding energy per nucleon. If they are forced to fuse together to form a heavier nucleus (like helium), the new nucleus is more stable and has a **higher** binding energy per nucleon. This increase in binding energy is released as a vast amount of energy. This is the process that powers the Sun.
