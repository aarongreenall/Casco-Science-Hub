7.8.1 Identifying Chemical Reactions

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain what chemical reactions are and how to identify them by observing common signs.

What is a Chemical Reaction?

A chemical reaction is a process that involves rearrangement of the molecular or ionic structure of a substance, as opposed to a change in physical form or a nuclear reaction. In a chemical reaction, new substances with new properties are formed.

Signs of a Chemical Reaction:

  • Color Change: A substance changes color (e.g., rusting of iron, ripening of fruit).
  • Gas Production: Bubbles of gas are formed (e.g., baking soda reacting with vinegar, Alka-Seltzer in water). This is often called effervescence.
  • Formation of a Precipitate: A solid is formed when two solutions are mixed (e.g., milk curdling, silver nitrate and sodium chloride forming silver chloride).
  • Temperature Change (Energy Change):
    • Exothermic Reaction: Heat is released, and the surroundings get warmer (e.g., burning wood, hand warmers).
    • Endothermic Reaction: Heat is absorbed, and the surroundings get cooler (e.g., instant cold packs, photosynthesis).
  • Light Production: Light is emitted (e.g., fireworks, glow sticks).
  • Odor Change: A new smell is produced (e.g., food rotting, baking bread).

It's important to note that some physical changes can also involve some of these signs (e.g., boiling water produces gas bubbles but is a physical change). Usually, a combination of these signs, along with the formation of a new substance, indicates a chemical reaction.
