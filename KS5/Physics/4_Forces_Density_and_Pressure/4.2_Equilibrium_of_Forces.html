4.2 Equilibrium of Forces

Learning Objectives:

  1. State and apply the principle of moments.
  2. Understand that, when there is no resultant force and no resultant torque, a system is in equilibrium.
  3. Use a vector triangle to represent coplanar forces in equilibrium.

LO2: The Two Conditions for Equilibrium

For an object to be in a state of **static equilibrium**, it must not be moving linearly and it must not be rotating. This requires two conditions to be met simultaneously:

1. No Resultant Force

The vector sum of all forces acting on the object must be zero. This ensures there is no linear acceleration (it is in **translational equilibrium**).

$$ \sum F = 0 $$

2. No Resultant Torque (Moment)

The sum of all moments about any pivot point must be zero. This ensures there is no angular acceleration (it is in **rotational equilibrium**).

$$ \sum \tau = 0 $$

LO1: The Principle of Moments

The second condition for equilibrium leads directly to the **Principle of Moments**. If the sum of all moments about a pivot is zero, it means that the turning effects in one direction must be perfectly balanced by the turning effects in the opposite direction.

For an object to be in rotational equilibrium, the sum of the clockwise moments about any point must be equal to the sum of the anticlockwise moments about the same point.

$$ \sum (\text{Clockwise Moments}) = \sum (\text{Anticlockwise Moments}) $$

Worked Example: A Non-Uniform Beam

A non-uniform beam of length 1.2 m and weight 80 N rests horizontally on two supports, A and B, placed 0.1 m from each end. The centre of gravity of the beam is 0.7 m from end A. Calculate the upward support forces, $R_A$ and $R_B$.

1. Apply the First Condition of Equilibrium (Translational):

The sum of upward forces must equal the sum of downward forces.

$$ R_A + R_B = 80 \text{ N} \quad \cdots(1) $$

2. Apply the Second Condition of Equilibrium (Rotational): Take moments about a convenient point to eliminate one unknown. Let's take moments about support A.

$$ \text{Sum of Clockwise Moments} = \text{Sum of Anticlockwise Moments} $$

The weight (80 N) acts at a distance of $(0.7 - 0.1) = 0.6$ m from A, creating a clockwise moment. The support force $R_B$ acts at a distance of $(1.2 - 0.1 - 0.1) = 1.0$ m from A, creating an anticlockwise moment.

$$ (80 \text{ N} \times 0.6 \text{ m}) = (R_B \times 1.0 \text{ m}) $$

$$ 48 \text{ N m} = R_B \times 1.0 \text{ m} \implies R_B = 48 \text{ N} $$

3. Solve for the remaining force: Substitute the value of $R_B$ back into equation (1).

$$ R_A + 48 = 80 \implies R_A = 32 \text{ N} $$

LO3: The Vector Triangle of Forces

The first condition for equilibrium ($\sum F = 0$) has a useful graphical consequence. If an object is in equilibrium under the action of **three coplanar forces**, then the vectors representing these forces can be drawn head-to-tail to form a **closed triangle**. This is known as a vector triangle of forces.

In the diagram, a weight $W$ is held in equilibrium by two tensions, $T_1$ and $T_2$. Because the system is in equilibrium, the vector sum is zero ($T_1 + T_2 + W = 0$). If we draw the vectors head-to-tail, the head of the last vector ($W$) must meet the tail of the first vector ($T_1$), forming a closed loop. We can then use trigonometry (Sine Rule or Cosine Rule) or scale drawing to solve for unknown forces or angles.

Worked Example: Suspended Weight

A traffic light of weight 120 N is suspended by two cables as shown. Calculate the tension in each cable.

1. Draw the vector triangle: The three forces ($T_1$, $T_2$, and $W$) form a closed triangle. We can find the internal angles using geometry.

The angle between $T_1$ and the vertical is $90^\circ - 30^\circ = 60^\circ$.

The angle between $T_2$ and the vertical is $90^\circ - 50^\circ = 40^\circ$.

The angle between $T_1$ and $T_2$ is $30^\circ + 50^\circ = 80^\circ$.

2. Apply the Sine Rule to the vector triangle:

$$ \frac{T_1}{\sin 40^\circ} = \frac{T_2}{\sin 60^\circ} = \frac{120}{\sin 80^\circ} $$

3. Solve for $T_1$:

$$ T_1 = \frac{120 \times \sin 40^\circ}{\sin 80^\circ} = \frac{120 \times 0.643}{0.985} = 78.3 \text{ N} $$

4. Solve for $T_2$:

$$ T_2 = \frac{120 \times \sin 60^\circ}{\sin 80^\circ} = \frac{120 \times 0.866}{0.985} = 105.5 \text{ N} $$
