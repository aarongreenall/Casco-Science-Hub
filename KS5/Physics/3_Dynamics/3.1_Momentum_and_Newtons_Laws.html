3.1 Momentum & Newton's Laws of Motion

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that mass is the property of an object that resists change in motion (inertia).
  2. Recall $F=ma$ and solve problems using it, understanding that acceleration and resultant force are always in the same direction.
  3. Define and use linear momentum as the product of mass and velocity.
  4. Define and use force as rate of change of momentum.
  5. State and apply each of Newton's laws of motion.
  6. Describe and use the concept of weight as the effect of a gravitational field on a mass and recall that the weight of an object is equal to the product of its mass and the acceleration of free fall.

LO1 & LO5: Mass, Inertia, and Newton's First Law

Mass ($m$) is the amount of matter in an object, a scalar quantity measured in kilograms (kg). Fundamentally, mass is a measure of an object's **inertia**—its inherent resistance to a change in its state of motion. An object with a large mass has a large inertia, meaning a large force is required to change its velocity.

This concept is formalised in **Newton's First Law of Motion**:

An object will remain at rest or continue to move with a constant velocity unless acted upon by a resultant external force.

This means that if the net force on an object is zero ($\Sigma F = 0$), its acceleration must also be zero ($a=0$). The object is said to be in a state of **translational equilibrium**.

Explanation: Newton's First Law

LO2, LO3, LO4 & LO5: Force, Momentum, and Newton's Second Law

Linear momentum ($p$) is a vector quantity defined as the product of an object's mass and its velocity. It is a measure of the "quantity of motion" an object has. Its unit is kg m s⁻¹.

$$ p = mv $$

**Newton's Second Law of Motion** provides the link between force and momentum. It states:

The resultant force acting on an object is directly proportional to the rate of change of its momentum, and this change occurs in the direction of the force.

$$ F_{net} = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} $$

Detailed Derivation of $F=ma$

For an object of constant mass, we can derive the more familiar form of Newton's Second Law.

1. Start with the definition of force as the rate of change of momentum:

$$ F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} $$

2. The change in momentum, $\Delta p$, is the final momentum minus the initial momentum:

$$ \Delta p = p_{final} - p_{initial} = mv - mu $$

3. Substitute this into the force equation:

$$ F = \frac{mv - mu}{t} $$

4. Since the mass $m$ is constant, we can factor it out:

$$ F = \frac{m(v-u)}{t} $$

5. We recognise that the term $\frac{v-u}{t}$ is the definition of acceleration, $a$:

$$ a = \frac{v-u}{t} $$

6. Substituting $a$ into the equation gives the final form:

$$ F = ma $$

Worked Example 1: Using $F=\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$

A 0.50 kg ball travelling at 10 m s⁻¹ hits a wall and rebounds at 8.0 m s⁻¹. If the ball is in contact with the wall for 0.10 s, what is the average force exerted by the wall on the ball?

1. Define a positive direction. Let's take the initial direction of the ball as positive. Therefore, $u = +10$ m s⁻¹ and $v = -8.0$ m s⁻¹.

2. Calculate the change in momentum ($\Delta p$):

$$ \Delta p = mv - mu = m(v-u) $$

$$ \Delta p = 0.50 \times (-8.0 - 10) = 0.50 \times (-18) = -9.0 \text{ kg m s}^{-1} $$

3. Calculate the force ($F$):

$$ F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{-9.0}{0.10} = -90 \text{ N} $$

The negative sign indicates the force is in the opposite direction to the ball's initial motion (i.e., away from the wall), which is correct.

Explanation: Newton's Second Law

LO5: Newton's Third Law

**Newton's Third Law of Motion** describes the nature of forces as interactions between objects.

If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

These two forces are known as an **action-reaction pair**. It is crucial to remember that they act on **different objects**, are of the **same type** (e.g., both gravitational or both contact forces), and are **equal in magnitude and opposite in direction**.

Explanation: Newton's Third Law

LO6: Weight

**Weight ($W$)** is the gravitational force exerted on an object with mass by a celestial body (like the Earth). It is a vector quantity, measured in newtons (N). It can be calculated using Newton's Second Law, where the acceleration is the acceleration of free fall, $g$.

$$ W = mg $$

An object's mass is constant everywhere, but its weight depends on the gravitational field strength at its location.

Worked Example 2: Mass and Weight

An astronaut has a mass of 80 kg. The gravitational field strength on the Moon is 1.6 N kg⁻¹. Calculate the astronaut's weight on Earth and on the Moon.

1. Weight on Earth: (g ≈ 9.81 N kg⁻¹)

$$ W_{Earth} = mg = 80 \text{ kg} \times 9.81 \text{ N kg}^{-1} = 784.8 \approx 780 \text{ N} $$

2. Weight on the Moon:

$$ W_{Moon} = mg_{Moon} = 80 \text{ kg} \times 1.6 \text{ N kg}^{-1} = 128 \approx 130 \text{ N} $$

Note that the astronaut's mass (80 kg) remains the same in both locations.
