C2.5 Simple molecules and covalent bonds

Covalent bonding is a fundamental type of chemical bond that forms when atoms share pairs of electrons to achieve stable electron configurations, typically a full outer shell. This section explores the formation of covalent bonds in simple molecular compounds and relates their structure to their observable properties.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State that a covalent bond is formed when a pair of electrons is shared between two atoms leading to noble gas electronic configurations.
  2. Describe the formation of covalent bonds in simple molecules, including H$_2$, Cl$_2$, H$_2$O, CH$_4$, NH$_3$ and HCl. Use dot-and-cross diagrams to show the electronic configurations in these molecules.
  3. Describe the formation of covalent bonds in simple molecules, including CH$_3$OH, C$_2$H$_4$, O$_2$, CO$_2$ and N$_2$. Use dot-and-cross diagrams to show the electronic configurations in these molecules.
  4. Describe in terms of structure and bonding the properties of simple molecular compounds: low melting points and boiling points; poor electrical conductivity.
  5. Explain in terms of structure and bonding the properties of simple molecular compounds: low melting points and boiling points in terms of weak intermolecular forces (specific types of intermolecular forces are not required); poor electrical conductivity.

Formation of Covalent Bonds:

A **covalent bond** is formed when two atoms **share a pair of electrons** to achieve a stable outer electron shell, typically a noble gas electronic configuration (usually 8 electrons, or 2 for hydrogen). This sharing creates a strong attractive force between the nuclei and the shared electrons. Covalent bonds typically form between non-metallic elements.

**Dot-and-cross diagrams** are used to illustrate the sharing of electrons and the resulting electron arrangement in molecules.

Single Covalent Bonds:

In a **single covalent bond**, one pair of electrons is shared between two atoms. Examples include:

  • **Hydrogen (H$_2$):** Each hydrogen atom shares one electron, forming one shared pair.
  • **Chlorine (Cl$_2$):** Each chlorine atom shares one electron, forming one shared pair.
  • **Water (H$_2$O):** Oxygen shares one electron with each of two hydrogen atoms, forming two single bonds.
  • **Methane (CH$_4$):** Carbon shares one electron with each of four hydrogen atoms, forming four single bonds.
  • **Ammonia (NH$_3$):** Nitrogen shares one electron with each of three hydrogen atoms, forming three single bonds.
  • **Hydrogen Chloride (HCl):** Hydrogen shares one electron with chlorine, forming one single bond.
  • **Methanol (CH$_3$OH):** Carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms share electrons through single bonds.

*Figure: Dot-and-cross diagram showing covalent bonding in a water molecule.*

Double and Triple Covalent Bonds:

Atoms can share more than one pair of electrons to form **double covalent bonds** (two shared pairs) or **triple covalent bonds** (three shared pairs).

  • **Ethene (C$_2$H$_4$):** Contains a carbon-carbon double bond.
  • **Oxygen (O$_2$):** Contains an oxygen-oxygen double bond.
  • **Carbon Dioxide (CO$_2$):** Contains two carbon-oxygen double bonds.
  • **Nitrogen (N$_2$):** Contains a nitrogen-nitrogen triple bond.

*Figure: Dot-and-cross diagram showing double covalent bonds in a carbon dioxide molecule.*

Properties of Simple Molecular Compounds:

Compounds formed by covalent bonds typically exist as **simple molecules**. These molecules have specific properties:

  • **Low Melting and Boiling Points:** While the covalent bonds *within* the molecules are strong, the forces *between* individual molecules (called **intermolecular forces**) are very weak. Little energy is required to overcome these weak intermolecular forces, so simple molecular compounds have low melting and boiling points. Many are gases or liquids at room temperature.
  • **Poor Electrical Conductivity:** Simple molecular compounds do not conduct electricity. This is because they do not contain free electrons (all electrons are involved in bonding) or mobile ions to carry charge.
