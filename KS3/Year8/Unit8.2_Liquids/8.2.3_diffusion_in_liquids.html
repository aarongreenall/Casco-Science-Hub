8.2.3 Diffusion in liquids

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define diffusion in liquids.
  2. Explain why diffusion is slower in liquids than in gases.
  3. Describe how the particle model explains diffusion.

Diffusion in Liquids

**Diffusion** also happens in liquids. It is the same process as in gases: the net movement of particles from a region of their higher concentration to a region of their lower concentration. This spreading out is caused by the constant, random motion of the particles.

However, diffusion in liquids is **much slower** than in gases. This is because the particles in a liquid are packed closely together. They are constantly bumping into each other, which makes it much harder for them to move from one place to another. In a gas, the particles are far apart and can move long distances before colliding, so diffusion happens much faster.

Analogy: A Drop of Squash

A perfect way to see diffusion in a liquid is to add a single drop of concentrated squash or food colouring to a still glass of water. At first, the colour is highly concentrated in one spot. Without any stirring, you can watch the colour slowly spread out as the squash particles randomly move and bump into the water particles. After a long time, the entire glass of water will be a uniform, lighter colour. This shows that the particles have spread out evenly on their own.

In-School Experiment: The Spreading Colour

You can observe the slow process of diffusion in a liquid in the lab.

Equipment:
  • A large beaker or glass jar
  • Water
  • A single crystal of potassium permanganate (which is deep purple) or a drop of dark food colouring
  • A pair of tweezers
Method:
  1. Fill the beaker carefully with water and let it stand for a few minutes until the water is completely still.
  2. Using the tweezers, very gently place a single crystal of potassium permanganate at the bottom of the beaker. Try not to disturb the water.
  3. Leave the beaker in a safe place where it won't be knocked, and observe it over the course of the lesson.
  4. You will see the purple colour slowly spreading out from the crystal, without any stirring. This is diffusion. Draw a diagram of what you see at the start of the lesson and another at the end to show how the particles have spread out.
