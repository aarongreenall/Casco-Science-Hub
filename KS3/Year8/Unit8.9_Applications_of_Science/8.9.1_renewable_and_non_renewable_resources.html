8.9.1 Renewable and non-renewable resources

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define renewable and non-renewable resources.
  2. Give examples of each type of resource for both materials and energy generation.
  3. Describe the advantages and disadvantages of different energy resources.

Renewable vs. Non-Renewable

Humans use resources from the Earth to make materials and to generate energy. These resources can be classified into two main groups.

  • A **non-renewable resource** is one that is used up faster than it can be replaced. Once it's gone, it's gone for good (or at least for millions of years).
  • A **renewable resource** is one that can be replaced naturally in a short amount of time, or one that will never run out.

Analogy: A Refillable Drink vs. a Disposable Bottle

A **non-renewable resource** is like a disposable bottle of water. You can drink from it, but once it's empty, you have to throw it away. A **renewable resource** is like a refillable water bottle. You can drink from it, and when it's empty, you can simply fill it up again from the tap and use it over and over.

Examples of Resources

Non-Renewable Resources

  • Fossil Fuels (Coal, Oil, Natural Gas): Used to generate electricity and make materials like plastics. They will eventually run out and their burning causes pollution.
  • Nuclear Fuels (Uranium): Used in nuclear power stations to generate electricity. Supplies are finite.

Renewable Resources

  • Solar, Wind, Tidal, Geothermal: Used to generate electricity. They will not run out and are much cleaner than fossil fuels.
  • Bioplastics: Materials made from renewable biological substances like corn starch or cellulose from plants.

Explanation: Energy Resources

In-School Activity: Energy Debate

Your teacher will give your group a specific energy resource to research (e.g., wind power, coal, nuclear power).

Your Task:

Use textbooks and reliable internet sources to find out the main advantages and disadvantages of your assigned energy resource. You should consider:

  • Is it renewable or non-renewable?
  • Does it cause pollution?
  • Is it reliable (does it work all the time)?
  • Is it expensive to build or run?

Be ready to present your findings to the class and argue for or against the use of your energy resource in your local area.
