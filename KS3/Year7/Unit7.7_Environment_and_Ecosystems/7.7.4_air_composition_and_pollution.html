7.7.4 Air composition and pollution

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the composition of clean air as a mixture of gases.
  2. Define air pollution and identify common pollutants.
  3. Explain the sources and effects of air pollutants like carbon monoxide and sulfur dioxide.

What is Air Made Of?

The air we breathe is not a single substance. It's a **mixture** of many different gases. The composition of clean, dry air is surprisingly consistent all over the world.

Nitrogen ($N_2$): Makes up about 78% of the air. It's a fairly unreactive gas.

Oxygen ($O_2$): Makes up about 21% of the air. It's essential for respiration in most living things.

Argon (Ar): Makes up just under 1% of the air. It's a very unreactive noble gas.

Other Gases: The remaining tiny fraction includes gases like carbon dioxide ($CO_2$), which is vital for photosynthesis, and trace amounts of other noble gases.

Analogy: A Giant Bowl of Mixed Sweets

Think of the Earth's atmosphere as a giant bowl of mixed sweets. The vast majority of the sweets are one flavour (Nitrogen). There's a good number of a second, very important flavour (Oxygen). Then there are a few of another flavour (Argon), and just a tiny sprinkle of everything else (Carbon Dioxide, etc.).

Air Pollution

**Air pollution** is the release of harmful substances into the atmosphere. Most air pollution is caused by human activities, especially the burning of **fossil fuels** (coal, oil, and natural gas) in power stations and vehicles.

Analogy: Ink in Water

Air pollution is like adding a few drops of black ink to a large, clear glass of water. Even though you only add a tiny amount of the pollutant (the ink), it can spread out and contaminate the whole system, making it harmful.

Pollutant Source Effect
Carbon Monoxide (CO) Incomplete combustion of fuels (e.g., in car engines). A poisonous gas that reduces the amount of oxygen your blood can carry.
Sulfur Dioxide ($SO_2$) Burning fossil fuels that contain sulfur impurities (especially coal). Dissolves in water droplets in clouds to form sulfuric acid, which falls as **acid rain**. Acid rain damages buildings, kills trees, and harms aquatic life.

In-School Experiment: Sticky Card Air Sampler

You can investigate the amount of solid particle pollution in the air around your school.

Equipment:
  • A piece of stiff white card
  • Petroleum jelly (e.g., Vaseline)
  • A magnifying glass or microscope
  • String and a hole punch
Method:
  1. Smear a thin, even layer of petroleum jelly onto one side of the card.
  2. Punch a hole in the corner and use the string to hang your sampler in an outdoor location (e.g., from a tree branch or a fence). Make sure it's in a place where it won't be disturbed.
  3. Leave the sampler for a few days.
  4. After a few days, collect your card and examine the sticky surface with a magnifying glass or microscope.
  5. You should be able to see small particles of dust, soot, and other pollutants that have become trapped. You can compare the amount of pollution collected in different locations (e.g., near a busy road vs. in a quiet field).
