9.3.4 Heat and Temperature

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the difference between heat and temperature.
  2. Know that thermal energy will always transfer from hotter regions or objects to colder ones, and this is known as heat dissipation.

Heat vs. Temperature: What's the Difference?

In everyday conversation, we often use the words 'heat' and 'temperature' as if they mean the same thing. We might say, "Turn up the heat!" when we're cold. In science, however, they have very different and specific meanings. Understanding this difference is key to understanding energy.

Analogy: A Cup vs. a Bathtub of Tea

Imagine you have a small cup of boiling hot tea and a large bathtub full of warm tea.

  • The cup of tea has a very **high temperature**. Its particles are moving, on average, very fast.
  • The bathtub of tea has a **lower temperature**. Its particles are moving, on average, more slowly.
  • However, because the bathtub contains vastly more water particles, its **total thermal energy** is much greater. The bathtub contains more **heat**.

A drop of boiling tea on your skin would hurt, but the bathtub of warm water could heat up an entire room!

A Closer Look at Temperature

**Temperature** is a measure of how hot or cold something is. Scientifically, it's a measure of the **average kinetic energy** of the particles in a substance. The faster the particles in an object are moving or vibrating, the more kinetic energy they have, and the higher the object's temperature. We measure temperature in degrees Celsius (Â°C).

A Closer Look at Heat

**Heat** is not something an object *has*, but rather the **transfer of thermal energy** from a hotter object to a colder one. Because it is a measure of energy transfer, heat is measured in Joules (J). This transfer process is also known as **heat dissipation**.

The golden rule of heat transfer is: Thermal energy always flows from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature.

Analogy: A Waterfall

Think of temperature as height. A waterfall always flows from a high point to a low point, never the other way around on its own. In the same way, heat always flows from a high temperature to a low temperature.

Misconception Alert! "Keep the cold out"

You've probably heard someone say, "Shut the door, you're letting the cold in!" While we all know what this means, it's scientifically incorrect. There is no such thing as "cold" that can flow into a room. What is actually happening is that **heat** from the warm room is flowing out into the colder air outside. You close the door to prevent this transfer of heat energy.

In-School Investigation: Cooling Race

This experiment shows the difference between heat and temperature.

You will need:

  • Two beakers (one 100 ml, one 250 ml)
  • A kettle to provide hot water
  • Two thermometers
  • A stopwatch

Procedure:

  1. Your teacher will help you pour 50 ml of hot water into the small beaker and 200 ml of the same hot water into the large beaker. Both will start at the same **temperature**.
  2. Place a thermometer in each beaker and start the stopwatch.
  3. Record the temperature of the water in each beaker every minute for 10 minutes.

Prediction & Result:

Which beaker do you think will cool down faster? You will observe that the smaller beaker, with less water, cools down much more quickly. Even though both started at the same temperature, the larger beaker contained more total thermal energy (more heat) and therefore takes longer to transfer that energy to the surroundings.
