7.9.2 Conductors and insulators

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define electrical conductors and insulators.
  2. Explain why metals are good conductors.
  3. Give examples of common conductors and insulators.

Allowing Electricity to Flow

Electricity (a flow of electrons) can travel through some materials very easily, but not through others. We can classify materials based on how well they let electricity pass through them.

  • An **electrical conductor** is a material that allows electric charge to flow through it easily. All metals are good conductors.
  • An **electrical insulator** is a material that does not allow electric charge to flow through it. Materials like plastic, wood, and rubber are good insulators.

Analogy: Water in Pipes

Let's go back to our "Water in Pipes" analogy. The electrons are the water. A **conductor** is like a wide, open **pipe** where water can flow freely without any blockages. An **insulator** is like a **pipe that is completely blocked or plugged**. No matter how hard the pump (battery) pushes, the water cannot get through.

Metals are good conductors because their atoms have "free" electrons that are not tightly held and are able to move from atom to atom. In insulators, the electrons are held very tightly to their atoms and cannot move freely.

In-School Experiment: Testing Materials

You can build a simple circuit to test which materials are electrical conductors and which are insulators.

Equipment:
  • A low-voltage power supply or battery
  • A bulb in a holder
  • Connecting wires with crocodile clips
  • A collection of objects to test (e.g., a metal spoon, a plastic ruler, a rubber, a pencil, an iron nail, a piece of wood, aluminium foil)
Method:
  1. Set up a simple series circuit with the power supply, the bulb, and two connecting wires, but leave a gap between two of the crocodile clips.
  2. Check your circuit works by touching the two crocodile clips together. The bulb should light up.
  3. One by one, place each of your test objects into the gap, making sure the crocodile clips are touching it firmly.
  4. If the bulb lights up, the material is a **conductor**. If the bulb does not light up, the material is an **insulator**.
  5. Record your results in a table with two columns: "Conductors" and "Insulators".

Interactive Simulation: Circuit Construction Kit

In this PhET simulation, you can build the test circuit from the experiment. Instead of a bulb, you can use the ammeter to see if a current is flowing. Try putting different materials from the "Grab Bag" into the gap in your circuit, like a paperclip or a dollar bill, to see which ones are conductors.
