13.4 Gravitational potential

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define gravitational potential at a point as the work done per unit mass in bringing a small test mass from infinity to the point.
  2. Use $\phi = –GM / r$ for the gravitational potential in the field due to a point mass.
  3. Understand how the concept of gravitational potential leads to the gravitational potential energy of two point masses and use $E_P = –GMm / r$.

From Uniform Fields to Point Masses: A Step-by-Step Derivation

To understand the concept of gravitational potential, it's helpful to start with what we already know about potential energy near the Earth's surface and see why we need a more general definition for larger distances.

Step 1: The Near-Earth Approximation

We are familiar with the formula for the change in gravitational potential energy:

$$ \Delta E_p = mg\Delta h $$

This works perfectly near the Earth's surface because we assume the gravitational field strength, $g$, is constant. If we want to describe the property of the field itself, we can talk about the potential energy *per unit mass*. We call this potential, $\phi$.

$$ \phi = \frac{E_p}{m} = \frac{mgh}{m} = gh $$

So, for a uniform field, potential is simply $gh$. However, this breaks down in space where $g$ is not constant.

Step 2: Generalizing with Newton's Law

We know from Newton's law of gravitation that the field strength $g$ at a distance $r$ from a mass $M$ is actually:

$$ g = \frac{GM}{r^2} $$

To extend our idea of potential ($\phi = gh$) to all distances, we need to replace both $g$ and $h$ with more general expressions involving $r$. The height $h$ is really just a distance, so we can replace it with $r$. This gives a first guess of $\phi \approx (\frac{GM}{r^2}) \times r = \frac{GM}{r}$. This is close, but it's missing a crucial detail.

Step 3: Defining a Zero Point and the Negative Sign

Where is the zero point for potential energy? For $mgh$, we usually say the ground is $h=0$. But in space, where is the "ground"? Scientists have agreed that the zero point for gravitational potential is at **infinity**. At an infinite distance, the gravitational force is zero, so the potential energy is zero.

Because gravity is an attractive force, as you bring a mass from infinity closer to a planet, the gravitational field does positive work on the mass. This means the mass *loses* potential energy. To move from a potential energy of 0 at infinity to a lower value, the potential energy anywhere else **must be negative**.

Therefore, the gravitational potential energy of a mass $m$ at a distance $r$ from a mass $M$ is:

$$ E_p = -\frac{GMm}{r} $$

And the gravitational potential (energy per unit mass) is:

$$ \phi = \frac{E_p}{m} = -\frac{GM}{r} $$

LO1: Defining Gravitational Potential

The gravitational potential at a point is the work done per unit mass in bringing a small test mass from infinity to that point.

LO2: Gravitational Potential due to a Point Mass

The gravitational potential ($\phi$) at a distance $r$ from the centre of a point mass $M$ is given by the formula:

$$ \phi = -\frac{GM}{r} $$

LO3: Gravitational Potential Energy ($E_P$)

The relationship $E_P = m\phi$ allows us to find the **gravitational potential energy ($E_P$)** of a mass $m$ placed at a point in the field. By substituting the formula for $\phi$ due to a point mass $M$, we get the equation for the gravitational potential energy of two point masses, $M$ and $m$:

$$ E_P = m \left( -\frac{GM}{r} \right) = -\frac{GMm}{r} $$

Worked Example: GPE of a Satellite

A satellite of mass 2500 kg is in a geostationary orbit at a radius of $4.22 \times 10^7$ m from the centre of the Earth. Calculate its gravitational potential energy.

$$ E_P = -\frac{GMm}{r} = -\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times (2500)}{4.22 \times 10^7} $$

$$ E_P = -2.36 \times 10^{10} \text{ J} $$
