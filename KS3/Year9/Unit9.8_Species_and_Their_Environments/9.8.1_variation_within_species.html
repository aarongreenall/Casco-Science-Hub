9.8.1 Variation within a Species

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain the concept of variation and its causes.

What is Variation?

  • The differences in characteristics between individuals of the same species.
  • No two individuals are exactly alike (except for identical twins).

Types of Variation:

  • Continuous Variation:
    • Characteristics that can have any value within a range.
    • Examples: Height, weight, hand span, leaf length.
    • Usually controlled by multiple genes and is often influenced by the environment.
    • When plotted on a graph, it typically produces a bell-shaped curve (normal distribution).
  • Discontinuous Variation:
    • Characteristics that fall into distinct categories with no in-between values.
    • Examples: Blood group (A, B, AB, O), eye color, ability to roll your tongue.
    • Usually controlled by a single gene and is not affected by the environment.

Causes of Variation:

  • Genetic Variation: Caused by differences in the genes inherited from parents. Sexual reproduction shuffles genes, creating new combinations. Mutations (random changes in DNA) are the ultimate source of all new genetic variation.
  • Environmental Variation: Caused by differences in the environment and lifestyle. Examples: A plant grown in the sun will be larger than one grown in the shade; a person's diet and exercise affect their weight.
  • Many characteristics (like height) are influenced by a combination of both genetic and environmental factors.
