7.2.2 The periodic table and groupings

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the periodic table as a way of organising elements.
  2. Explain the meaning of groups and periods.
  3. Identify key groups in the periodic table, such as the alkali metals, halogens, and noble gases.

Organising the Elements

There are over 100 known elements, each with its own unique properties. To make sense of them all, scientists organise them into a chart called the **periodic table**. The elements are arranged in order of their **atomic number** (the number of protons in their nucleus).

Analogy: A City Map of Elements

Think of the periodic table as a giant, planned map of a city. Each **element** is a different **building** on the map, and each building has a unique street address (its atomic number).

  • The **Groups (columns)** are the **streets that run North-South**. All the buildings on the same street share a similar function or character. For example, "Alkali Avenue" (Group 1) is a street full of highly reactive chemical plants, while "Noble Gas Gardens" (Group 0) is a street of quiet, secluded parks that don't interact with anyone.
  • The **Periods (rows)** are the **streets that run East-West**. As you travel along one of these streets, the character of the neighbourhood changes in a predictable way. You might start in the heavy industrial zone (reactive metals), move through a commercial district, and end up at the quiet parks on the other side of town (noble gases).

The vertical columns are called **groups**. Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties because they have the same number of electrons in their outer shell. Some key groups have special names:

  • Group 1: The Alkali Metals (very reactive).
  • Group 7: The Halogens (also very reactive).
  • Group 0 (or 18): The Noble Gases (very unreactive).

The horizontal rows are called **periods**. As you move across a period, the properties of the elements gradually change from metallic to non-metallic.

In-School Activity: Build Your Own Periodic Table

Using a blank grid, fill in the names and symbols for the first 20 elements of the periodic table. Then, use different colours to shade in the regions for the Alkali Metals, the Halogens, and the Noble Gases. This will help you to see the patterns in the table's organisation.

Interactive Simulation: Build an Atom

This PhET simulation lets you build your own atoms! As you add protons, watch how the element changes and see where it is located on the periodic table. This helps to show the link between the number of protons (atomic number) and an element's position in the table.
