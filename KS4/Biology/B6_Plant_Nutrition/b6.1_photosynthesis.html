B6.1 Photosynthesis

**Photosynthesis** is the fundamental process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy, in the form of glucose (a carbohydrate). This vital process forms the base of almost all food chains on Earth, producing the oxygen we breathe and the organic molecules necessary for life.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe photosynthesis as the process by which plants synthesise carbohydrates from raw materials using energy from light.
  2. State the word equation for photosynthesis as: carbon dioxide + water $\rightarrow$ glucose + oxygen in the presence of light and chlorophyll.
  3. State the balanced symbol equation for photosynthesis as: $6CO_2 + 6H_2O \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$.
  4. State that chlorophyll is a green pigment that is found in chloroplasts.
  5. Investigate and understand the need for chlorophyll, light and carbon dioxide for photosynthesis.
  6. State that chlorophyll transfers energy from light into energy in chemicals, for the synthesis of carbohydrates.
  7. Outline the subsequent use and storage of the carbohydrates made in photosynthesis: starch as an energy store; cellulose to build cell walls; glucose used in respiration to provide energy; sucrose for transport in the phloem; nectar to attract insects for pollination.
  8. Explain the importance of: nitrate ions for making amino acids; magnesium ions for making chlorophyll.
  9. Understand and describe the effects of varying light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration and temperature on the rate of photosynthesis.
  10. Understand and describe the effect of light and dark conditions on gas exchange in an aquatic plant using hydrogencarbonate indicator solution.

The Process of Photosynthesis:

Photosynthesis occurs primarily in the chloroplasts of plant cells, which contain the green pigment **chlorophyll**. Chlorophyll is essential as it absorbs light energy, particularly in the red and blue parts of the spectrum, and transfers this light energy into chemical energy. This chemical energy is then used to synthesize carbohydrates (like glucose) from simple raw materials: carbon dioxide and water.

The word equation for photosynthesis is:

`Carbon dioxide + Water --(Light, Chlorophyll)--> Glucose + Oxygen`

The balanced symbol equation for photosynthesis is:

$6CO_2(g) + 6H_2O(l) \xrightarrow{Light, \ Chlorophyll} C_6H_{12}O_6(aq) + 6O_2(g)$

Uses and Storage of Carbohydrates:

The glucose produced during photosynthesis has several important uses and storage forms in plants:

  • **Starch:** Stored as an energy reserve, particularly in roots, tubers, and seeds. It's a complex carbohydrate that can be easily converted back to glucose when needed.
  • **Cellulose:** Used to build strong **cell walls** in plant cells, providing structural support to the plant.
  • **Glucose:** Used directly in **respiration** to provide energy for various metabolic processes in the plant.
  • **Sucrose:** Transported in the **phloem** (a plant transport tissue) to other parts of the plant where it is needed for energy or growth.
  • **Nectar:** A sugary liquid produced by flowers to attract insects for pollination.

Importance of Mineral Ions:

Plants also require specific mineral ions from the soil for healthy growth and to carry out photosynthesis effectively:

  • **Nitrate ions (NO$_3^-$):** Essential for making **amino acids**, which are the building blocks of proteins. Proteins are vital for enzymes, structural components, and many other cellular functions.
  • **Magnesium ions (Mg$^{2+}$):** Crucial for making **chlorophyll**. A deficiency in magnesium leads to chlorosis (yellowing of leaves) because the plant cannot produce enough chlorophyll for photosynthesis.

Factors Affecting Photosynthesis Rate:

The rate of photosynthesis is influenced by several environmental factors. These are often called **limiting factors** because if any one of them is in short supply, it will limit the rate of photosynthesis, even if other factors are abundant.

  • **Light Intensity:** As light intensity increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases (up to a certain point), as more energy is available for the reaction.
  • **Carbon Dioxide Concentration:** As carbon dioxide concentration increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases (up to a certain point), as CO$_2$ is a raw material for the reaction.
  • **Temperature:** Photosynthesis is controlled by enzymes, so its rate increases with temperature up to an optimum. Beyond the optimum, enzymes start to denature, and the rate decreases rapidly.

The effect of light and dark conditions on gas exchange in an aquatic plant can be observed using **hydrogencarbonate indicator solution**. This indicator changes color depending on the concentration of carbon dioxide:

  • High CO$_2$: Yellow (acidic)
  • Atmospheric CO$_2$: Orange/Red (neutral)
  • Low CO$_2$: Purple (alkaline)

In light, a plant will photosynthesize (using CO$_2$) and respire (producing CO$_2$). If photosynthesis is faster than respiration, overall CO$_2$ concentration decreases, and the indicator turns purple. In the dark, only respiration occurs (producing CO$_2$), so CO$_2$ concentration increases, and the indicator turns yellow.
