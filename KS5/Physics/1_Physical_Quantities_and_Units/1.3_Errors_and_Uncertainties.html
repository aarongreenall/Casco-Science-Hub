1.3 Errors and Uncertainties

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand and explain the effects of systematic errors (including zero errors) and random errors in measurements.
  2. Understand the distinction between precision and accuracy.
  3. Assess the uncertainty in a derived quantity by simple addition of absolute or percentage uncertainties.

Systematic vs Random Errors

No measurement is ever perfect. Every measurement has an associated **uncertainty**, which reflects the doubt in the result. These uncertainties arise from two main types of errors:

  • Systematic Errors: These are errors that cause every reading to be off by a consistent amount in the same direction. They are often due to faulty equipment or a flawed experimental method. For example, a thermometer that is incorrectly calibrated and reads 2°C too high for every measurement. A common type is a **zero error**, where an instrument does not read zero at the zero-point (e.g., a newton meter reads 0.1 N with no load). Systematic errors cannot be reduced by repeating measurements.
  • Random Errors: These are unpredictable variations in the measured data. They can be caused by factors like human reaction time, fluctuations in experimental conditions, or limitations of the instrument's readability. For example, parallax error when reading a scale, or fluctuations in room temperature. Random errors can be reduced by taking multiple readings and calculating an average.

Accuracy and Precision

These two terms are often confused but have distinct meanings in physics:

  • Accuracy: How close a measured value is to the true, accepted value. A measurement is accurate if systematic errors are small.
  • Precision: How close a set of repeated measurements are to each other. A measurement is precise if random errors are small.

It is possible for a measurement to be precise but not accurate. For instance, if you consistently measure the length of a 10.0 cm rod as 10.5 cm, 10.5 cm, and 10.5 cm, your measurements are precise (low random error) but inaccurate (high systematic error).

Combining Uncertainties in Derived Quantities

When we use measured quantities to calculate a result, the uncertainties in the initial measurements propagate into the final result. We use simple rules to estimate the uncertainty in the derived quantity.

  • Addition and Subtraction: When adding or subtracting quantities, we **add their absolute uncertainties**. If $ y = a \pm b $, then the absolute uncertainty is $ \Delta y = \Delta a + \Delta b $.
  • Multiplication and Division: When multiplying or dividing quantities, we **add their percentage (or fractional) uncertainties**. If $ z = a \times b $ or $ z = a / b $, then the percentage uncertainty is $ \frac{\Delta z}{z} \times 100\% = (\frac{\Delta a}{a} + \frac{\Delta b}{b}) \times 100\% $.
  • Powers: When raising a quantity to a power, we **multiply its percentage uncertainty by the power**. If $ w = a^n $, then the percentage uncertainty is $ \frac{\Delta w}{w} \times 100\% = (n \times \frac{\Delta a}{a}) \times 100\% $.

Worked Example: Calculating Uncertainty

A student measures the potential difference across a resistor to be $ V = 6.0 \pm 0.1 $ V and the current through it to be $ I = 0.50 \pm 0.02 $ A. Calculate the resistance $R$ and its absolute uncertainty.

1. Calculate the value of the resistance:

Using Ohm's Law, $ R = V/I $:

$$ R = \frac{6.0 \text{ V}}{0.50 \text{ A}} = 12.0 \, \Omega $$

2. Calculate the percentage uncertainties in V and I:

$$ \text{% uncertainty in } V = \frac{\Delta V}{V} \times 100\% = \frac{0.1}{6.0} \times 100\% = 1.67\% $$

$$ \text{% uncertainty in } I = \frac{\Delta I}{I} \times 100\% = \frac{0.02}{0.50} \times 100\% = 4.0\% $$

3. Combine the percentage uncertainties:

Since $R=V/I$, we add the percentage uncertainties:

$$ \text{% uncertainty in } R = \text{% uncertainty in } V + \text{% uncertainty in } I $$

$$ = 1.67\% + 4.0\% = 5.67\% $$

4. Calculate the absolute uncertainty in R:

$$ \Delta R = 5.67\% \text{ of } 12.0 \, \Omega = \frac{5.67}{100} \times 12.0 = 0.68 \, \Omega $$

Uncertainties are usually quoted to one significant figure, so we round $ \Delta R $ to $ 0.7 \, \Omega $.

5. State the final result:

The value of the resistance should be quoted to the same decimal precision as its uncertainty.

$$ R = 12.0 \pm 0.7 \, \Omega $$
