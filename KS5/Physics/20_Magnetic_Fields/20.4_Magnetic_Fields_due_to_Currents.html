20.4 Magnetic fields due to currents

Learning Objectives:

  1. Sketch magnetic field patterns due to the currents in a long straight wire, a flat circular coil and a long solenoid.
  2. Understand that the magnetic field due to the current in a solenoid is increased by a ferrous core.
  3. Explain the origin of the forces between current-carrying conductors and determine the direction of the forces.

LO1: Magnetic Field Patterns

Any wire carrying an electric current produces a magnetic field. The shape of this field depends on the shape of the wire.

Long Straight Wire

The field consists of concentric circles centred on the wire. The direction is given by the **Right-Hand Grip Rule**: if your thumb points in the direction of the conventional current, your fingers curl in the direction of the field lines.

Flat Circular Coil

The fields from each part of the wire loop combine to create a stronger, concentrated field through the centre of the coil. The field lines loop around the outside of the coil.

Long Solenoid

A long coil of wire creates a strong, uniform magnetic field inside the solenoid, with the field lines being parallel and equally spaced. The external field is weak and resembles that of a bar magnet.

LO2: The Effect of a Ferrous Core

The magnetic field produced by a solenoid can be made much stronger by placing a **ferrous core** (a soft iron core) inside it. This creates an **electromagnet**.

Ferrous materials like iron are made up of tiny regions called **magnetic domains**. Normally, these domains are randomly oriented, so their individual magnetic fields cancel each other out. However, when the solenoid's magnetic field is applied, it causes these domains to align. The aligned domains produce their own strong magnetic field, which adds to the original field of the solenoid, resulting in a much stronger overall magnetic field.

LO3: Forces Between Current-Carrying Conductors

Since any current-carrying wire creates a magnetic field, and any wire in a magnetic field experiences a force, it follows that two current-carrying wires placed near each other will exert forces on each other.

Origin of the Force

  1. Wire 1, carrying current $I_1$, produces a magnetic field ($B_1$) around it.
  2. Wire 2, carrying current $I_2$, is now sitting in the magnetic field $B_1$.
  3. According to the motor effect ($F=BIL$), Wire 2 experiences a force due to the field from Wire 1.
  4. By Newton's Third Law, Wire 1 must experience an equal and opposite force from the magnetic field of Wire 2.

Parallel Currents (Attraction)

The magnetic field between the wires is weakened, and the wires are pushed together.

Anti-parallel Currents (Repulsion)

The magnetic field between the wires is strengthened, and the wires are pushed apart.
