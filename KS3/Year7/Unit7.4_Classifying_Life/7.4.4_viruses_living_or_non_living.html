7.4.4 Viruses: living or non-living

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the basic structure of a virus.
  2. Explain the arguments for why viruses could be considered living.
  3. Explain the arguments for why viruses could be considered non-living.
  4. Understand that viruses exist on the borderline between living and non-living.

What is a Virus?

A **virus** is a tiny, non-cellular infectious agent. It is far smaller than a bacterium and has a very simple structure. It is essentially just a short piece of genetic material (DNA or RNA) enclosed in a protective protein coat called a **capsid**. Viruses have no nucleus, no cytoplasm, and no organelles.

The biggest question in biology about viruses is: are they actually alive? To answer this, we need to see how they measure up against the seven life processes (MRS GREN).

Analogy: A Computer Virus

A biological virus is very similar to a computer virus. A computer virus is just a piece of code. On its own, sitting on a USB stick, it can't do anything. It's lifeless. But when you plug it into a computer (a **host cell**), it hijacks the computer's machinery and forces it to make thousands of copies of the virus code. These new copies then spread to other computers. A biological virus works in exactly the same way: it's lifeless on its own, but it hijacks a living cell to reproduce.

The Great Debate: Living or Non-Living?

Arguments for "Living"

  • They **Reproduce**: Although they need a host cell, they do make copies of themselves.
  • They have genetic material (DNA or RNA), which is a key feature of life.
  • They can **Evolve**: Viruses can change over time through mutation, which is why we need a new flu vaccine every year.

Arguments for "Non-Living"

  • They are not made of **cells**.
  • They cannot carry out any of the other life processes on their own. They don't take in food (**Nutrition**), release energy (**Respiration**), get rid of waste (**Excretion**), **Move** by themselves, **Grow**, or respond to their environment (**Sensitivity**).
  • They are completely inactive outside of a host cell, almost like a non-living chemical.

Conclusion: Because viruses have some characteristics of life but lack others, most scientists classify them as existing on the borderline between living and non-living.

In-School Activity: Class Debate

This is a great topic for a debate! Your teacher will divide the class into two teams: "Team Living" and "Team Non-Living." Your task is to use the information on this page and any other resources to prepare arguments for your side. Each team will then present their case, and you will have a chance to rebut the other team's arguments.
