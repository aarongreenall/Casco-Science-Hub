8.4 The Diffraction Grating

Learning Objectives:

  1. Recall and use the diffraction grating equation $d \sin\theta = n\lambda$.
  2. Describe the use of a diffraction grating to determine the wavelength of light.

LO1: The Diffraction Grating Equation

A **diffraction grating** is a component with a large number of parallel, closely spaced slits or lines. When light passes through a grating, it diffracts at each slit. The diffracted waves from all the slits then interfere with each other, creating an interference pattern of sharp, bright maxima (bright spots) at specific angles.

Constructive interference occurs at an angle $\theta$ when the path difference between waves from adjacent slits is a whole number multiple of the wavelength.

Detailed Derivation of $d \sin\theta = n\lambda$

1. Consider two adjacent slits in the grating, separated by a distance $d$ (the grating spacing).

2. For waves travelling at an angle $\theta$ to the normal, the wave from the lower slit must travel an extra distance to reach a distant screen. This extra distance is the **path difference**.

3. As shown in the magnified triangle, this path difference is given by trigonometry:

$$ \text{path difference} = d \sin\theta $$

4. For **constructive interference** to occur and create a bright maximum, the path difference must be a whole number multiple of the wavelength ($\lambda$).

$$ \text{path difference} = n\lambda $$

where $n$ is an integer called the **order number** ($n = 0, 1, 2, ...$).

5. By equating these two expressions for the path difference, we arrive at the diffraction grating equation:

$$ d \sin\theta = n\lambda $$

LO2: Using a Grating to Determine Wavelength

Diffraction gratings are a powerful tool for measuring the wavelength of light with high precision. The experimental setup is straightforward:

By measuring the angle $\theta$ for a specific order maximum ($n$), and knowing the grating spacing $d$, the wavelength $\lambda$ can be calculated.

Worked Example: Determining Wavelength

A laser beam is passed through a diffraction grating with 300 lines per mm. A pattern of bright spots is observed on a screen 1.50 m away. The distance between the central maximum (n=0) and the first order maximum (n=1) is 28.6 cm. Calculate the wavelength of the laser light.

1. Calculate the grating spacing ($d$):

The grating has 300 lines per mm, which is $300 \times 1000 = 300,000$ lines per metre.

$$ d = \frac{1}{\text{lines per metre}} = \frac{1}{300000} = 3.333 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m} $$

2. Calculate the angle of diffraction ($\theta$):

We have a right-angled triangle with opposite side $x = 28.6 \text{ cm} = 0.286 \text{ m}$ and adjacent side $D = 1.50 \text{ m}$.

$$ \tan\theta = \frac{x}{D} = \frac{0.286}{1.50} = 0.1907 $$

$$ \theta = \tan^{-1}(0.1907) = 10.80^\circ $$

3. Apply the diffraction grating equation for the first order maximum (n=1):

$$ d \sin\theta = n\lambda $$

$$ \lambda = \frac{d \sin\theta}{n} $$

$$ \lambda = \frac{(3.333 \times 10^{-6}) \times \sin(10.80^\circ)}{1} $$

$$ \lambda = (3.333 \times 10^{-6}) \times 0.1874 $$

$$ \lambda = 6.246 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} = 625 \text{ nm} $$
