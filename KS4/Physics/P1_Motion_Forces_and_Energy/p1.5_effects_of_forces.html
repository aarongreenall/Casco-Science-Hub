P1.5 Effects of forces

Forces are fundamental to understanding how objects interact and move in the physical world. They can cause objects to start moving, stop moving, change direction, or even change shape. This section explores the various effects of forces, including how multiple forces combine and the concepts of friction and elasticity.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Determine the resultant of two or more forces acting along the same straight line.
  2. Know that an object either remains at rest or continues in a straight line at constant speed unless there is a resultant force on the object.
  3. Recall and use the equation $F = ma$ and know that the resultant force and the acceleration are in the same direction.
  4. Describe friction as the force between two surfaces that may impede relative motion and produce heating.
  5. Know that friction (drag) acts on an object moving through a liquid.
  6. Know that friction (drag) acts on an object moving through a gas (e.g. air resistance).
  7. Know that forces may produce changes in the size, shape and motion of an object.
  8. Sketch, plot and interpret load–extension graphs for an elastic solid and describe the associated experimental procedures.
  9. Define the spring constant as force per unit extension; recall and use the equation $k = F/x$.
  10. Define and use the term ‘limit of proportionality’ for a load–extension graph and identify this point on the graph (an understanding of the elastic limit is not required).

Understanding Forces and Their Effects:

A **force** is a push or a pull that can change an object's motion or shape. Forces are vector quantities, meaning they have both magnitude (size) and direction. When multiple forces act on an object, their combined effect is called the **resultant force**. If forces act along the same straight line, the resultant force is found by adding forces in the same direction and subtracting forces in opposite directions.

**Newton's First Law of Motion** states that an object will remain at rest or continue to move at a constant velocity (constant speed in a straight line) unless acted upon by a resultant (unbalanced) force. This means that a change in an object's motion (speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction) is always due to a net force acting on it.

**Newton's Second Law of Motion** quantifies this relationship: the resultant force ($F$) acting on an object is directly proportional to its mass ($m$) and its acceleration ($a$). This is expressed by the equation:

$F = m \times a$

The resultant force and the acceleration will always be in the same direction. This equation is central to understanding how forces cause changes in motion.

Friction and Drag:

**Friction** is a force that opposes relative motion between two surfaces in contact. It can occur between solid surfaces (e.g., a book sliding on a table), or between a solid object and a fluid (liquid or gas). When an object moves through a liquid or a gas, the resistive force is called **drag** (or air resistance when moving through air). Friction often produces heat, as seen when rubbing your hands together.

Friction is essential for many everyday activities, such as walking or braking a car, but it can also be a hindrance, causing energy loss in machinery.

Elasticity and Hooke's Law:

Forces can also cause objects to change their size or shape. **Elasticity** is the property of a material that allows it to return to its original shape after a deforming force is removed. For many elastic materials, especially springs, the extension (or compression) is directly proportional to the applied force, provided the **limit of proportionality** is not exceeded. This relationship is known as **Hooke's Law**.

The equation for Hooke's Law is:

$F = k \times x$

Where $F$ is the applied force, $x$ is the extension (or compression), and $k$ is the **spring constant**. The spring constant is a measure of the stiffness of the spring or elastic material, defined as the force per unit extension.

A **load-extension graph** can be plotted to visualize Hooke's Law. For an elastic material, this graph will be a straight line through the origin up to the limit of proportionality. Beyond this limit, the material may undergo permanent deformation.
