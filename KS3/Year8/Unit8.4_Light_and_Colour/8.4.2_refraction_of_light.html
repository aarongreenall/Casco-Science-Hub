8.4.2 Refraction of light

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define refraction as the bending of light when it changes speed.
  2. Explain why light bends towards or away from the normal.
  3. Draw a ray diagram for refraction through a glass block.

What is Refraction?

If you've ever looked at a straw in a glass of water, you might have noticed that it looks bent or broken at the surface of the water. This is an optical illusion caused by **refraction**.

**Refraction** is the change in direction of a wave as it passes from one substance (medium) into another.

This bending happens because the **speed of light changes** when it enters a different medium. Light travels fastest in a vacuum, slightly slower in air, and slower still in denser materials like water or glass.

Analogy: A Toy Car on Different Surfaces

Imagine a toy car driving from a smooth, fast wooden floor (like air) onto a slow, rough carpet (like glass). If the car hits the carpet at an angle, one front wheel will hit the carpet first and slow down, while the other wheel is still on the fast floor. This causes the car to pivot and change direction, turning towards the normal. This is exactly how light refracts when it slows down.

  • When light enters a denser medium and **slows down** (e.g., from air to glass), it bends **towards the normal**.
  • When light enters a less dense medium and **speeds up** (e.g., from glass to air), it bends **away from the normal**.

In-School Experiment: Investigating Refraction

You can investigate the path of light through a glass block using a ray box.

Equipment:
  • A ray box with a single slit
  • A rectangular glass or perspex block
  • A protractor
  • A piece of plain paper and a pencil
Method:
  1. Place the glass block on the paper and draw around its outline.
  2. Shine a single ray of light from the ray box so that it hits one side of the block at an angle.
  3. Use your pencil to mark the path of the incident ray, the ray inside the block, and the ray that emerges from the other side.
  4. Remove the block and use a ruler to draw the rays.
  5. At the point where the ray enters the block, draw the normal. Measure the angle of incidence and the angle of refraction. Do you see the ray bending towards the normal?
  6. Now, draw the normal at the point where the ray leaves the block. Measure the angles here. Do you see the ray bending away from the normal?
