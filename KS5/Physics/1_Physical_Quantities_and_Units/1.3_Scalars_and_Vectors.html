1.3 Scalars and Vectors

In physics, many quantities require not just a magnitude but also a direction to be fully described. This leads to the fundamental distinction between **scalar** and **vector** quantities. Understanding how to represent and manipulate these quantities is crucial for solving problems in mechanics and other areas of physics.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand the difference between scalar and vector quantities and give examples of scalar and vector quantities included in the syllabus.
  2. Add and subtract coplanar vectors.
  3. Represent a vector as two perpendicular components.

Scalar vs. Vector Quantities:

  • **Scalar Quantity:** A physical quantity that has **magnitude only**. It is fully described by a number and a unit. * **Examples:** Distance, speed, mass, time, energy, temperature, volume, density, work done, power.
  • **Vector Quantity:** A physical quantity that has both **magnitude and direction**. It is represented by an arrow where the length indicates magnitude and the arrowhead indicates direction. * **Examples:** Displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, weight, electric field strength, magnetic flux density.

*Figure: Illustration showing the difference between scalar (magnitude only) and vector (magnitude and direction) quantities.*

Adding and Subtracting Coplanar Vectors:

**Coplanar vectors** are vectors that lie in the same plane. They can be added or subtracted graphically or mathematically.

Graphical Methods:

  • **Head-to-Tail Method:** To add vectors A and B, draw vector A. Then, draw vector B starting from the arrowhead (head) of vector A. The resultant vector is drawn from the tail of A to the head of B.
  • **Parallelogram Method:** To add two vectors starting from the same point, draw them as adjacent sides of a parallelogram. The resultant vector is the diagonal drawn from the common starting point.

*Figure: Vector addition using the head-to-tail method.*

Mathematical Method (for perpendicular vectors):

For vectors at right angles to each other, the magnitude of the resultant vector can be found using **Pythagoras' theorem**, and its direction can be found using **trigonometry** (sine, cosine, tangent functions).

Representing a Vector as Perpendicular Components:

Any vector can be broken down (resolved) into two perpendicular components, usually horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components. This is particularly useful for analyzing forces or velocities acting at an angle.

If a vector **V** has magnitude V and makes an angle $\theta$ with the horizontal axis:

  • Horizontal component ($V_x$): $V_x = V \cos \theta$
  • Vertical component ($V_y$): $V_y = V \sin \theta$

*Figure: A vector resolved into its perpendicular components.*
