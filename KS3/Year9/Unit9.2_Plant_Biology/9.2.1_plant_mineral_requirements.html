9.2.1 Plant Mineral Requirements

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Know that plants require minerals to maintain healthy growth and life processes (limited to magnesium to make chlorophyll and nitrates to make protein).

More Than Just Sunlight and Water

We know that plants make their own food (glucose) through photosynthesis, using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide. This provides the plant with energy. But that's not the whole story! To grow strong and healthy, build new cells, and carry out all its life processes, a plant must absorb essential **minerals** from the soil through its roots. These minerals are dissolved in water in the soil and are just as vital as sunlight.

Analogy: A Baker's Kitchen

Think of a baker making a cake. The flour, sugar, and eggs are the main ingredients that provide the cake's structure and energy (like the glucose from photosynthesis). But to make a truly great cake, the baker needs special ingredients in smaller amounts, like vanilla extract for flavour or baking powder to help it rise. **Minerals are the plant's special ingredients.** They are needed in small amounts but are essential for very important jobs. Without them, the plant cannot grow properly.

Two Vital Minerals: Magnesium and Nitrates

While plants need many different minerals, we are going to focus on two of the most important ones: magnesium and nitrates.

Magnesium (Mg) - The Heart of Chlorophyll

Magnesium's most important job is to be the central atom in a molecule of **chlorophyll**. Chlorophyll is the green pigment in leaves that absorbs sunlight for photosynthesis. Without magnesium, the plant simply cannot make chlorophyll.

Consequence of Deficiency: A lack of magnesium causes the leaves to turn yellow, a condition known as **chlorosis**. The plant can't photosynthesise properly, leading to poor, stunted growth.

Nitrates ($NO_3^−$) - The Protein Builders

Plants absorb **nitrates** from the soil to get the element nitrogen. Nitrogen is a vital component for making **proteins**. Proteins are the fundamental building blocks of life, used to create new cells, enzymes, and all the structures a plant needs to grow taller and stronger.

Consequence of Deficiency: A plant lacking nitrates will suffer from very poor growth. It will be small and stunted, with weak stems and older leaves that turn a pale yellowy-green.

Summary: Mineral Deficiencies

Mineral Role in the Plant Signs of Deficiency
Magnesium (Mg) A core part of the chlorophyll molecule; needed to absorb light. Yellowing of leaves, especially between the veins (chlorosis). Stunted growth.
Nitrates ($NO_3^−$) Provides nitrogen needed to build proteins for new cells and growth. Poor overall growth (stunted). Weak stem and pale, yellow-green older leaves.

In-School Investigation: What's Wrong With My Plant?

Work in groups. Your teacher will provide you with high-quality photographs of plants that are not healthy. Acting as "plant doctors," your group must:

  1. Observe the visual symptoms of each plant carefully.
  2. Diagnose the problem: is it suffering from a **magnesium deficiency** or a **nitrate deficiency**?
  3. Write down your diagnosis and be ready to justify it to the class using evidence from the photograph.

Discussion:

Why do farmers and gardeners often add fertilisers to their soil? How does this relate to what we've learned today?
