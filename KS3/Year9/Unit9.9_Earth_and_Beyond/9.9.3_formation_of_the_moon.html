9.9.3 Formation of the Moon

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will describe the most widely accepted scientific theory for the formation of the Moon.

The Giant-Impact Hypothesis:

This is the leading theory for the Moon's origin.

  1. The Collision: Early in the Solar System's history, about 4.5 billion years ago, a Mars-sized protoplanet named Theia collided with the young Earth.
  2. Debris Ring: The impact was not head-on but a glancing blow. It was so powerful that it vaporized Theia and a large portion of the Earth's mantle, ejecting a massive cloud of molten rock and debris into orbit around the Earth.
  3. Accretion: Over time (perhaps a few hundred years), gravity caused this orbiting debris to clump together (accrete).
  4. The Moon Forms: This clump of material eventually formed a single, large body: our Moon.

Evidence for this Theory:

  • Moon's Composition: Rocks collected from the Moon by the Apollo missions are very similar in composition to the Earth's mantle, but have very little iron, which supports the idea that the Moon formed from mantle material, not from Earth's iron-rich core.
  • Moon's Orbit: The current orbit and rotation of the Moon are consistent with computer models of such a giant impact.
  • The impact would have generated immense heat, explaining why lunar rocks show signs of having been molten.
