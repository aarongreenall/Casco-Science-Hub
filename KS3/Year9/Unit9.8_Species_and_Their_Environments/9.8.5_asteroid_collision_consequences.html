9.8.5 Asteroid Collision Consequences

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the consequences of asteroid collision with the Earth, including climate change and mass extinctions.

What is an Asteroid?

**Asteroids** are large, rocky bodies that orbit the Sun. Most of them are found in the "asteroid belt," a region of space between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter. They are leftover material from the formation of our solar system. While thousands of small meteors (often no bigger than a grain of sand) enter Earth's atmosphere every day and burn up as "shooting stars," a collision with a large asteroid is an extremely rare but catastrophic event.

The Immediate Aftermath: Impact!

If a large asteroid (several kilometres wide) were to hit Earth, the immediate effects would be devastating. The immense kinetic energy of the asteroid would be converted into a massive explosion on impact, creating a huge crater. This would trigger powerful earthquakes across the globe and, if the impact was in an ocean, generate gigantic tsunamis that would wipe out coastal regions.

Analogy: A Planetary-Scale Explosion

The energy released by a major asteroid impact would be many thousands of times more powerful than all the nuclear weapons on Earth combined, detonating in a single spot. The initial blast and shockwave would destroy everything for hundreds of kilometres.

Global Winter and Climate Change

The most significant long-term consequence would be a drastic change in the global climate. The impact would vaporize rock and throw enormous quantities of dust, soot, and debris high into the atmosphere. This thick cloud of material would spread around the entire globe, acting like a shroud that blocks out sunlight.

Without the Sun's energy reaching the surface, global temperatures would plummet, plunging the planet into a long, dark, and freezing **"impact winter"** that could last for months or even years.

The Great Dying: Mass Extinction

This sudden and severe climate change would lead to a **mass extinction**, where a huge percentage of all life on Earth dies out in a geologically short period of time.

The Domino Effect on Life:

  1. Plants Die: With no sunlight for photosynthesis, plants on land and phytoplankton (microscopic plants) in the sea would die off on a massive scale.
  2. Food Webs Collapse: With the producers gone, the entire food web would collapse from the bottom up. Herbivores would starve, followed by the carnivores that ate them.
  3. Freezing Temperatures: The freezing conditions of the impact winter would kill any organisms not adapted to survive extreme cold.

Case Study: The End of the Dinosaurs

The leading scientific theory for the extinction of the dinosaurs 66 million years ago is a massive asteroid, about 10 km wide, that struck the Earth off the coast of modern-day Mexico. The resulting impact winter and collapsed food webs wiped out about 75% of all species on the planet, including all dinosaurs except for the ancestors of modern birds.

At-Home Experiment: Flour Power Craters

You can model how asteroid impacts create craters in your own kitchen.

You will need:

  • A baking tray or deep dish
  • Plain flour
  • A few small pebbles of different sizes

Procedure:

  1. Fill the tray with flour to a depth of a few centimetres and smooth the surface.
  2. From about 30 cm high, drop a small pebble into the flour. Carefully remove it and observe the crater it made.
  3. Smooth the flour over. Now drop the same pebble from a greater height (e.g., 1 metre). How is the crater different?
  4. Smooth the flour again. Try dropping a larger pebble from the same height. How does the size of the "asteroid" affect the crater?

Scientific Explanation:

You should see that faster and larger pebbles create bigger craters and throw out more flour (called "ejecta"). This is a simple model of how scientists can study the craters on the Moon and other planets to work out the size and speed of the asteroids that created them millions of years ago.
