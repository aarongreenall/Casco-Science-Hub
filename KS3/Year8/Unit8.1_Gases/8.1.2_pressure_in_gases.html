8.1.2 Pressure in Gases

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain what causes gas pressure using the particle model.

Key concepts:

  • Gas particles are in constant, random motion.
  • Gas Pressure: Caused by the collisions of gas particles with the walls of their container.
  • Each collision exerts a tiny force on the container wall. The total effect of billions of collisions per second creates the overall pressure.
  • Factors affecting gas pressure:
    • Temperature: Increasing the temperature gives the particles more kinetic energy, so they move faster and collide with the walls more often and with more force. This increases the pressure (if the volume is constant).
    • Volume: Decreasing the volume of the container forces the particles closer together, so they collide with the walls more frequently. This increases the pressure (if the temperature is constant).
    • Number of Particles: Adding more gas particles to a container increases the number of collisions with the walls per second. This increases the pressure (if volume and temperature are constant).
  • Examples: Pumping up a bicycle tire, aerosol cans, weather (atmospheric pressure).
