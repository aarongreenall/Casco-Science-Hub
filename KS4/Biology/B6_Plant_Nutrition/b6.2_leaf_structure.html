B6.2 Leaf structure

Leaves are the primary sites of photosynthesis in most plants, and their structure is exquisitely adapted to maximize the efficiency of this vital process. Understanding the internal and external features of a leaf reveals how it is optimized for light absorption, gas exchange, and water transport.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State that most leaves have a large surface area and are thin, and explain how these features are adaptations for photosynthesis.
  2. Identify in diagrams and images the following structures in the leaf of a dicotyledonous plant: chloroplasts, cuticle, guard cells and stomata, upper and lower epidermis, palisade mesophyll, spongy mesophyll, air spaces, vascular bundles, xylem and phloem.
  3. Explain how the structures listed in 6.2.2 adapt leaves for photosynthesis.

External and Internal Structure of a Leaf:

Most leaves are specifically adapted for photosynthesis with two key external features:

  • **Large Surface Area:** Maximizes the absorption of sunlight and carbon dioxide.
  • **Thin:** Allows for a short diffusion distance for gases (carbon dioxide and oxygen) and light to reach the photosynthetic cells.

Internally, a dicotyledonous leaf has several specialized layers:

*Figure: A cross-section diagram of a dicotyledonous leaf, showing its internal structures.*

  • **Cuticle:** A waxy, waterproof layer on the upper and lower epidermis that reduces water loss by evaporation.
  • **Upper and Lower Epidermis:** Transparent protective layers that allow light to pass through to the photosynthetic cells below.
  • **Palisade Mesophyll:** Located just below the upper epidermis, this layer contains tightly packed, elongated cells rich in **chloroplasts** (the site of photosynthesis). This dense packing maximizes light absorption.
  • **Spongy Mesophyll:** Located below the palisade layer, these cells are irregularly shaped and loosely packed, creating large **air spaces** between them. These air spaces facilitate the diffusion of gases (CO$_2$ and O$_2$) throughout the leaf.
  • **Stomata (singular: stoma):** Small pores, mainly on the lower epidermis, that allow for gas exchange (CO$_2$ in, O$_2$ and water vapor out). Each stoma is surrounded by two **guard cells** that control its opening and closing.
  • **Vascular Bundles (Veins):** Contain **xylem** and **phloem**. * **Xylem:** Transports water and mineral ions from the roots to the leaves. * **Phloem:** Transports sucrose (sugars) and amino acids from the leaves to other parts of the plant.

Adaptations for Photosynthesis:

Each structure in the leaf is adapted to enhance the efficiency of photosynthesis:

  • **Large surface area:** Maximises light absorption and gas exchange.
  • **Thinness:** Provides a short diffusion path for gases (CO$_2$ to palisade cells, O$_2$ out) and allows light to penetrate to all photosynthetic cells.
  • **Chloroplasts in palisade mesophyll:** Located near the top to absorb maximum sunlight.
  • **Air spaces in spongy mesophyll:** Allow efficient diffusion of CO$_2$ to palisade cells and O$_2$ away from them.
  • **Stomata and guard cells:** Regulate gas exchange and water loss.
  • **Vascular bundles (xylem and phloem):** Provide efficient transport of water and nutrients to the photosynthetic cells and transport sugars away.
