7.5 Polarisation

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that polarisation is a phenomenon associated with transverse waves.
  2. Recall and use Malus's law ($I=I_{0}\cos^{2}\theta$) to calculate the intensity of a plane-polarised electromagnetic wave after transmission through a polarising filter or a series of polarising filters.

LO1: The Nature of Polarisation

**Polarisation** is a phenomenon that is a unique property of **transverse waves**. It describes the orientation of the oscillations of the wave.

  • An **unpolarised** wave (like light from the Sun or a filament lamp) consists of oscillations in all possible planes perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer.
  • A **plane-polarised** wave is a transverse wave in which the oscillations are confined to a single plane.

Longitudinal waves, such as sound, cannot be polarised because their oscillations are parallel to the direction of energy transfer. There is only one possible direction of oscillation.

LO2: Malus's Law

If we place a second polarising filter, called an **analyser**, in the path of already plane-polarised light, the intensity of the transmitted light depends on the angle between the transmission axes of the two filters. This relationship is described by **Malus's Law**.

Detailed Derivation of Malus's Law

Malus's Law can be derived by considering the amplitude of the plane-polarised wave as a vector.

1. A plane-polarised wave with amplitude $A_0$ is incident on an analyser. The transmission axis of the analyser is at an angle $\theta$ to the plane of polarisation of the incident wave.

2. The analyser only transmits the component of the incident wave's amplitude that is parallel to its own transmission axis. We can find this component by resolving the vector $A_0$.

3. The amplitude of the transmitted wave, $A$, is therefore:

$$ A = A_0 \cos\theta $$

4. From subtopic 7.1, we know that the intensity of a wave is directly proportional to the square of its amplitude ($I \propto A^2$).

5. Therefore, the intensity of the incident light ($I_0$) is proportional to $A_0^2$, and the intensity of the transmitted light ($I$) is proportional to $A^2$.

$$ I_0 \propto A_0^2 \quad \text{and} \quad I \propto A^2 $$

6. Substitute the expression for $A$ from step 3 into the proportionality for $I$:

$$ I \propto (A_0 \cos\theta)^2 \implies I \propto A_0^2 \cos^2\theta $$

7. Since $I_0 \propto A_0^2$, we can replace the proportionality with an equality:

$$ I = I_0 \cos^2\theta $$

Worked Example: Malus's Law

Unpolarised light of intensity 32 W m⁻² is incident on a polarising filter. The transmitted light then passes through a second filter (an analyser) whose transmission axis is at an angle of 60° to the first. Calculate the final intensity of the light.

1. Find the intensity after the first filter ($I_0$):

When unpolarised light passes through the first polariser, its intensity is halved.

$$ I_0 = \frac{1}{2} \times I_{unpolarised} = \frac{1}{2} \times 32 = 16 \text{ W m}^{-2} $$

This value, $I_0$, is the intensity incident on the second filter.

2. Apply Malus's Law for the second filter:

The angle between the filters is $\theta = 60^\circ$.

$$ I = I_0 \cos^2\theta $$

$$ I = 16 \times (\cos 60^\circ)^2 $$

$$ I = 16 \times (0.5)^2 $$

$$ I = 16 \times 0.25 = 4.0 \text{ W m}^{-2} $$
