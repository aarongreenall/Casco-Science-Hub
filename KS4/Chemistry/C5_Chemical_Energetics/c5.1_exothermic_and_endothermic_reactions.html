C5.1 Exothermic and endothermic reactions

Chemical reactions involve energy changes, either releasing energy into the surroundings or absorbing energy from them. These reactions are classified as either exothermic or endothermic, a fundamental concept in chemical energetics that helps us understand why some reactions feel hot and others feel cold.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State that an exothermic reaction transfers thermal energy to the surroundings leading to an increase in the temperature of the surroundings.
  2. State that an endothermic reaction takes in thermal energy from the surroundings leading to a decrease in the temperature of the surroundings.
  3. State that the transfer of thermal energy during a reaction is called the enthalpy change, $\Delta H$, of the reaction. $\Delta H$ is negative for exothermic reactions and positive for endothermic reactions.
  4. Define activation energy, $E_a$, as the minimum energy that colliding particles must have to react.
  5. Interpret reaction pathway diagrams showing exothermic and endothermic reactions.
  6. Draw and label reaction pathway diagrams for exothermic and endothermic reactions using information provided, to include: reactants, products, overall energy change of the reaction, $\Delta H$, activation energy, $E_a$.
  7. State that bond breaking is an endothermic process and bond making is an exothermic process.

Exothermic Reactions: Releasing Heat:

An **exothermic reaction** is a chemical reaction that **transfers thermal energy to the surroundings**, leading to an **increase in the temperature of the surroundings**. These reactions feel hot.

Examples of exothermic reactions include:

  • Combustion (burning fuels like methane, wood, or magnesium).
  • Neutralization reactions (acid + alkali).
  • Respiration (in living organisms).
  • The reaction between zinc and hydrochloric acid.

The **enthalpy change ($\Delta H$)** for an exothermic reaction is **negative** ($\Delta H < 0$), indicating that energy is released.

Endothermic Reactions: Absorbing Heat:

An **endothermic reaction** is a chemical reaction that **takes in thermal energy from the surroundings**, leading to a **decrease in the temperature of the surroundings**. These reactions feel cold.

Examples of endothermic reactions include:

  • Photosynthesis (plants absorbing light energy).
  • Thermal decomposition reactions (e.g., heating calcium carbonate).
  • Dissolving certain salts in water (e.g., ammonium nitrate in cold packs).

The **enthalpy change ($\Delta H$)** for an endothermic reaction is **positive** ($\Delta H > 0$), indicating that energy is absorbed.

Activation Energy and Reaction Pathway Diagrams:

**Activation energy ($E_a$)** is defined as the **minimum energy that colliding particles must have to react**. For a reaction to occur, reactant particles must collide with sufficient energy (at least the activation energy) and with the correct orientation.

**Reaction pathway diagrams** (or energy profile diagrams) illustrate the energy changes during a reaction, including activation energy and the overall enthalpy change ($\Delta H$).

  • **Exothermic Reaction Pathway:** The energy of the products is lower than the energy of the reactants. The reaction releases energy, so $\Delta H$ is negative. The activation energy is the "hill" that must be overcome for the reaction to start.
  • **Endothermic Reaction Pathway:** The energy of the products is higher than the energy of the reactants. The reaction absorbs energy, so $\Delta H$ is positive. The activation energy is still the "hill" that must be overcome.

Bond Breaking and Bond Making:

Energy changes in chemical reactions are fundamentally linked to the breaking and making of chemical bonds:

  • **Bond breaking is an endothermic process:** Energy is required to break chemical bonds. This energy is absorbed from the surroundings.
  • **Bond making is an exothermic process:** Energy is released when new chemical bonds are formed. This energy is transferred to the surroundings.

The overall enthalpy change ($\Delta H$) of a reaction is the difference between the energy absorbed to break bonds in reactants and the energy released when new bonds form in products. If more energy is released than absorbed, the reaction is exothermic. If more energy is absorbed than released, the reaction is endothermic.
