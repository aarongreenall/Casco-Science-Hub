7.6.4 Echoes and reflection

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define an echo as a reflection of sound.
  2. Explain which surfaces are best for reflecting and absorbing sound.
  3. Describe how echoes are used in echolocation and SONAR.
  4. Use the speed of sound to calculate distances using echoes.

What is an Echo?

An **echo** is a sound that is heard after the original sound has stopped. It is caused by sound waves bouncing off a surface and returning to the listener. This bouncing of sound is called **reflection**.

Not all surfaces are good at creating echoes. The best surfaces for reflecting sound are **hard, flat, and smooth**, like a cliff face, a large building wall, or the wall of an empty hall. Soft, rough, and uneven surfaces, like curtains, carpets, and forests, are poor at reflecting sound because they **absorb** most of the sound energy that hits them.

Analogy: Bouncing a Ball

Think about throwing a bouncy ball at different surfaces. If you throw it at a hard, flat brick wall, it will bounce back to you very well (this is like a **strong reflection**). If you throw the same ball with the same force at a thick, soft curtain, it will just drop to the floor. The curtain has absorbed the ball's energy (this is like **good absorption**).

Using Echoes: Echolocation and SONAR

Echoes aren't just a fun phenomenon; they are a vital tool for both animals and humans to "see" the world with sound.

  • Echolocation: Animals like bats and dolphins produce high-pitched clicks or squeaks and listen for the echoes. By interpreting how long the echoes take to return and how loud they are, they can build a mental "map" of their surroundings, allowing them to navigate and hunt in complete darkness or murky water.
  • SONAR (SOund Navigation And Ranging): Humans use the same principle in technology. Ships use SONAR to map the seabed, find shoals of fish, or locate submarines by sending out sound pulses and detecting the echoes.

Worked Example: Calculating Distance with Echoes

A ship sends out a SONAR pulse and detects the echo from the seabed 1.2 seconds later. If the speed of sound in seawater is 1500 m/s, how deep is the sea at this point?

1. Find the total distance travelled by the sound:

$$ \text{distance} = \text{speed} \times \text{time} $$

$$ \text{distance} = 1500 \text{ m/s} \times 1.2 \text{ s} = 1800 \text{ m} $$

2. Find the depth of the sea:

This is the tricky part! The 1800 m is the distance for the sound to travel to the seabed **and back again**. The depth of the sea is only half of this total distance.

$$ \text{Depth} = \frac{1800 \text{ m}}{2} = 900 \text{ m} $$
