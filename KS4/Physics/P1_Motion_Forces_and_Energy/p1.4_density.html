P1.4 Density

Density is a fundamental physical property of matter that describes how much mass is contained in a given volume. It helps us understand why some objects float and others sink, and why different materials feel "heavier" or "lighter" for the same size.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define density as mass per unit volume; recall and use the equation $\rho = m/V$.
  2. Describe how to determine the density of a liquid, of a regularly shaped solid and of an irregularly shaped solid which sinks in a liquid (volume by displacement), including appropriate calculations.
  3. Determine whether an object floats or sinks based on density data.

Understanding Density:

Density ($\rho$) is defined as the mass ($m$) of a substance divided by its volume ($V$). This relationship is expressed by the equation:

$\rho = \frac{m}{V}$

The standard SI unit for density is kilograms per cubic meter (kg/m$^3$), although grams per cubic centimeter (g/cm$^3$) is also commonly used. A higher density means more mass is packed into the same amount of space.

Determining Density:

**For regularly shaped solids** (like a cube or cylinder), you can determine its density by first measuring its mass using a balance. Then, measure its dimensions (length, width, height, or radius) using a ruler or calipers, and calculate its volume using the appropriate geometric formula. Finally, divide the mass by the calculated volume.

**For liquids**, measure the mass of an empty measuring cylinder, then add a known volume of the liquid and measure the new mass. Subtract the mass of the empty cylinder to find the mass of the liquid, then divide by its volume.

**For irregularly shaped solids that sink in a liquid**, the **displacement method** (also known as Archimedes' principle) is used to find their volume. First, measure the mass of the object. Then, partially fill a measuring cylinder with water and record the initial volume. Carefully submerge the object in the water, and record the new, higher volume. The difference between the final and initial volumes is the volume of the object. Divide the mass by this displaced volume to find the density.

Understanding why objects float or sink is directly related to their density compared to the fluid they are in. An object will float if its density is less than the fluid, and sink if it's more dense.
