P2.2 Melting, boiling and evaporation

Changes of state are fundamental processes in thermal physics, where substances transition between solid, liquid, and gaseous forms. These changes involve energy transfer and can be explained by the kinetic particle model of matter. Understanding these processes helps us comprehend phenomena from ice melting to water boiling and clothes drying.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Know the melting and boiling temperatures for water at standard atmospheric pressure (limited to Celsius only).
  2. Describe condensation and solidification (freezing) in terms of particles.
  3. Describe evaporation in terms of the escape of the more energetic particles from the surface of a liquid.
  4. Know that evaporation causes cooling of a liquid.
  5. Describe melting and boiling in terms of energy input without a change in temperature.
  6. Describe the differences between boiling and evaporation.
  7. Describe how temperature, surface area and air movement over a surface affect evaporation.

Changes of State and Particle Behavior:

Matter can transition between states:

  • **Melting:** Solid to liquid.
  • **Boiling:** Liquid to gas (occurs throughout the liquid at a specific temperature).
  • **Evaporation:** Liquid to gas (occurs only at the surface, at any temperature).
  • **Condensation:** Gas to liquid.
  • **Solidification (Freezing):** Liquid to solid.

These changes involve energy input or output but occur without a change in temperature during the phase transition itself. For water at standard atmospheric pressure, the melting point is 0°C and the boiling point is 100°C.

Particle Explanation of Changes of State:

According to the kinetic particle model:

  • **Melting/Boiling:** When a substance is heated, its particles gain kinetic energy and vibrate more vigorously. At the melting point, particles gain enough energy to overcome the strong forces holding them in fixed positions, allowing them to move past each other (liquid state). At the boiling point, particles gain enough energy to completely overcome the forces of attraction, escaping as a gas.
  • **Condensation/Solidification:** When a gas cools, its particles lose kinetic energy and move slower. At the condensation point, particles lose enough energy to come closer together and form a liquid. At the freezing point, particles lose enough energy to settle into fixed positions, forming a solid.
  • **Evaporation:** This is a surface phenomenon. Even below the boiling point, some particles at the surface of a liquid have enough kinetic energy to escape into the gaseous state. When these more energetic particles leave, the average kinetic energy of the remaining liquid particles decreases, leading to a cooling effect. This is why sweating cools your body.

Differences between Boiling and Evaporation:

  • **Boiling:** Occurs at a specific boiling point, throughout the entire liquid, and involves the formation of bubbles.
  • **Evaporation:** Occurs at any temperature below the boiling point, only at the surface of the liquid, and does not involve bubbling.

Factors Affecting Evaporation Rate:

The rate of evaporation is influenced by:

  • **Temperature:** Higher temperature means more particles have enough energy to escape, increasing evaporation rate.
  • **Surface Area:** A larger surface area exposes more particles to the air, increasing evaporation rate.
  • **Air Movement (Wind):** Wind blows away evaporated particles, reducing the concentration of vapor above the liquid and allowing more liquid particles to escape, increasing evaporation rate.
