8.7.1 Speed and distance-time graphs

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define speed and state the formula that links speed, distance, and time.
  2. Calculate the average speed of a journey.
  3. Interpret distance-time graphs to describe the motion of an object.

What is Speed?

**Speed** is a measure of how quickly an object is moving. It tells you the distance the object travels in a certain amount of time. We can calculate the average speed of a journey using a simple formula:

$$ \text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} $$

The standard unit for speed in science is metres per second (m/s), but we also often use kilometres per hour (km/h) or miles per hour (mph).

Worked Example: Calculating Speed

A car travels a distance of 300 metres in 15 seconds. What is its average speed?

1. State the formula:

$$ \text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} $$

2. Substitute the values:

$$ \text{Speed} = \frac{300 \text{ m}}{15 \text{ s}} $$

3. Calculate the answer:

$$ \text{Speed} = 20 \text{ m/s} $$

Distance-Time Graphs

A **distance-time graph** is a powerful tool for showing the story of a journey. It plots the distance an object has travelled from its starting point against the time that has passed.

Analogy: Walking to the Shops

Imagine you are walking to the shops. A distance-time graph is like a visual diary of your journey. It can show when you were walking fast, when you were walking slowly, and when you stopped to look in a window. The steeper the line on the graph, the faster you were moving!

  • A **flat, horizontal line** means the distance from the start is not changing. The object is **stationary** (not moving).
  • A **straight, sloping line** means the object is moving at a **constant speed**.
  • The **steeper the slope** (the gradient), the **faster** the object is moving.
  • A line sloping **downwards** means the object is moving back towards the starting point.

Explanation: Distance-Time Graphs

In-School Experiment: Measuring Speed

You can measure the speed of a toy car and plot your own distance-time graph.

Equipment:
  • A toy car
  • A long measuring tape or several metre rulers
  • A stopwatch
  • Graph paper
Method:
  1. Set up a long, straight track for your toy car on the floor.
  2. Mark out distances along the track (e.g., every 0.5 metres).
  3. Push the toy car to give it a steady speed. Start the stopwatch as it passes the starting line.
  4. Record the time it takes for the car to pass each of the distance markers.
  5. Repeat your measurements a few times to get reliable results.
  6. Use your results to plot a distance-time graph, with time on the x-axis and distance on the y-axis. Is your line straight? What does this tell you about the car's speed?
