1.2 Errors and Uncertainties

In scientific experiments, it's impossible to make a perfect measurement. All measurements have some degree of **error** and **uncertainty**. Understanding the different types of errors, the distinction between precision and accuracy, and how to quantify uncertainty is crucial for evaluating the reliability of experimental results.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand and explain the effects of systematic errors (including zero errors) and random errors in measurements.
  2. Understand the distinction between precision and accuracy.
  3. Assess the uncertainty in a derived quantity by simple addition of absolute or percentage uncertainties.

Types of Errors:

Errors in measurement can be broadly categorized into two types:

  • **Systematic Errors:** These errors consistently affect measurements in the same direction (either always too high or always too low). They are often due to faulty equipment, incorrect calibration, or a flawed experimental design. * **Zero Error:** A common type of systematic error where an instrument does not read zero when it should (e.g., a balance showing a reading when nothing is on it). * **Parallax Error:** Occurs when reading a scale from an angle, leading to consistently high or low readings. * **Example:** A thermometer that consistently reads 2Â°C higher than the actual temperature.
  • **Random Errors:** These errors cause readings to vary unpredictably from one measurement to the next. They are often due to unpredictable fluctuations in the experimental conditions or human judgment. * **Example:** Fluctuations in air currents affecting a delicate balance, or slight variations in reaction time when using a stopwatch. Random errors can be reduced by taking multiple readings and calculating an average.

Precision vs. Accuracy:

These two terms are often confused but have distinct meanings in science:

  • **Accuracy:** How close a measurement is to the true or accepted value. A measurement is accurate if it hits the "bull's-eye."
  • **Precision:** How close repeated measurements are to each other. A measurement is precise if repeated readings are very consistent, even if they are far from the true value.

*Figure: A target analogy illustrating the difference between accuracy and precision.*

Uncertainty in Measurements:

**Uncertainty** is a quantification of the doubt about the measurement result. It tells us the range within which the true value is likely to lie.

  • **Absolute Uncertainty:** The actual range of error (e.g., $5.0 \pm 0.1 \text{ cm}$).
  • **Percentage Uncertainty:** The absolute uncertainty expressed as a percentage of the measured value. * $Percentage \ Uncertainty = \frac{Absolute \ Uncertainty}{Measured \ Value} \times 100\%$

When combining measurements to calculate a **derived quantity**, the uncertainties must also be combined:

  • **For addition or subtraction:** Add the absolute uncertainties. * If $C = A + B$ or $C = A - B$, then $\Delta C = \Delta A + \Delta B$.
  • **For multiplication or division:** Add the percentage uncertainties. * If $C = A \times B$ or $C = A / B$, then $\frac{\Delta C}{C} = \frac{\Delta A}{A} + \frac{\Delta B}{B}$.
  • **For powers:** Multiply the percentage uncertainty by the power. * If $C = A^n$, then $\frac{\Delta C}{C} = n \times \frac{\Delta A}{A}$.
