15.2 Equation of state

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that a gas obeying $pV \propto T$, where $T$ is the thermodynamic temperature, is known as an ideal gas.
  2. Recall and use the equation of state for an ideal gas expressed as $pV = nRT$, where $n$ = amount of substance (number of moles) and as $pV = NkT$, where $N$ = number of molecules.
  3. Recall that the Boltzmann constant $k$ is given by $k = R / N_A$.

LO1: The Ideal Gas Concept

The behaviour of real gases can be complex. To simplify this, physicists use the model of an **ideal gas**. This is a theoretical gas that perfectly obeys the gas laws. The model is based on a set of assumptions about the gas molecules:

  • The volume of the molecules themselves is negligible compared to the volume of the container.
  • There are no intermolecular forces between the molecules (except during collisions).
  • The molecules are in continuous, random motion.
  • All collisions (between molecules and with the container walls) are perfectly elastic, i.e. no kinetic energy is lost.

For a fixed mass of an ideal gas, the individual gas laws (Boyle's Law, Charles's Law, and the Pressure Law) can be combined into a single relationship:

$$ \frac{pV}{T} = \text{constant} \quad \implies \quad pV \propto T $$

Where $p$ is pressure, $V$ is volume, and $T$ is the **thermodynamic temperature in kelvin (K)**. Any gas that obeys this relationship is defined as an ideal gas. Real gases behave most like ideal gases at low pressures and high temperatures.

LO2 & LO3: The Equation of State

The relationship $pV \propto T$ can be turned into an equation, known as the **equation of state for an ideal gas**. The constant of proportionality depends on the amount of gas present. This gives us two forms of the equation.

Version 1: Using Moles ($pV = nRT$)

If we measure the amount of gas in moles ($n$), the constant of proportionality is the **molar gas constant ($R$)**.

$$ pV = nRT $$

where $R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹.

Worked Example 1: Using $pV=nRT$

A container of volume $2.0 \times 10^{-2}$ m³ contains 3.5 moles of an ideal gas at a temperature of 300 K. Calculate the pressure of the gas.

1. Rearrange the formula for pressure, $p$:

$$ p = \frac{nRT}{V} $$

2. Substitute values and solve:

$$ p = \frac{(3.5 \text{ mol}) \times (8.31 \text{ J K}^{-1} \text{mol}^{-1}) \times (300 \text{ K})}{2.0 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m}^3} $$

$$ p = \frac{8725.5}{0.02} = 436275 \text{ Pa} $$

$$ p \approx 4.4 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa} \text{ (or 440 kPa)} $$

Version 2: Using Number of Molecules ($pV = NkT$)

Alternatively, we can express the equation in terms of the total number of molecules ($N$) in the gas. This involves a different constant, the **Boltzmann constant ($k$)**.

Detailed Derivation of $pV = NkT$

1. Start with the molar form of the equation: $pV = nRT$.

2. We know the number of moles ($n$) is related to the number of molecules ($N$) by the Avogadro constant ($N_A$):

$$ n = \frac{N}{N_A} $$

3. Substitute this expression for $n$ into the ideal gas equation:

$$ pV = \left( \frac{N}{N_A} \right) RT $$

4. Group the constants together:

$$ pV = N \left( \frac{R}{N_A} \right) T $$

5. The term $\frac{R}{N_A}$ is a fundamental constant called the **Boltzmann constant ($k$)**. This is the gas constant *per molecule* rather than per mole.

$$ k = \frac{R}{N_A} = \frac{8.31}{6.02 \times 10^{23}} = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J K}^{-1} $$

6. Substituting $k$ into the equation gives the final form:

$$ pV = NkT $$

Worked Example 2: Using $pV=NkT$

A sealed container of volume 0.50 m³ contains a gas at a pressure of $1.2 \times 10^5$ Pa and a temperature of 290 K. Calculate the number of gas molecules in the container.

1. Rearrange the formula for the number of molecules, $N$:

$$ N = \frac{pV}{kT} $$

2. Substitute values and solve:

$$ N = \frac{(1.2 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}) \times (0.50 \text{ m}^3)}{(1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J K}^{-1}) \times (290 \text{ K})} $$

$$ N = \frac{60000}{3.996 \times 10^{-21}} $$

$$ N = 1.5 \times 10^{25} \text{ molecules} $$
