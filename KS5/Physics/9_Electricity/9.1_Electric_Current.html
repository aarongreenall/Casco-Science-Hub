9.1 Electric Current

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that an electric current is a flow of charge carriers.
  2. Understand that the charge on charge carriers is quantised.
  3. Recall and use $Q=It$.
  4. Use, for a current-carrying conductor, the expression $I=Anvq$.

LO1: Current as a Flow of Charge Carriers

An **electric current** is defined as the rate of flow of electric charge. For a current to flow, there must be a complete circuit and a source of potential difference (like a battery), which creates an electric field in the conductor. This electric field exerts a force on charged particles, causing them to move. These moving charged particles are called **charge carriers**.

Charge Carriers in Metals

In solid metal conductors, the atoms are arranged in a fixed crystal lattice. The outer electrons of these atoms are not tightly bound to any single atom; they are **delocalised** and form a "sea" of free electrons that can move throughout the lattice. When a potential difference is applied across the metal, the electric field created exerts a force on these free electrons, causing them to drift in a net direction, thus creating an electric current. The positive metal ions in the lattice are fixed and do not move.

Conventional Current vs. Electron Flow

By a historical convention established before the discovery of the electron, the direction of **conventional current** is defined as the direction that positive charge would flow. This means conventional current flows from the positive terminal to the negative terminal of a power source.

However, in metal wires, the actual charge carriers are negatively charged electrons. These electrons are repelled from the negative terminal and attracted to the positive terminal. Therefore, the **electron flow** is from negative to positive. In A-Level Physics, unless stated otherwise, "current" always refers to conventional current.

LO2 & LO3: Quantisation of Charge and $Q=It$

The fundamental principle of **quantisation of charge** states that electric charge can only exist in discrete packets. The smallest possible unit of charge is the **elementary charge, $e$**, which is the magnitude of the charge on a single proton or electron.

$$ e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C} $$

Any charge, $Q$, must be an integer multiple ($N$) of this elementary charge: $Q = Ne$.

Derivation of $Q=It$

1. The definition of electric current ($I$) is the rate of flow of electric charge ($Q$) past a point.

$$ \text{Current} = \frac{\text{Charge that has flowed}}{\text{Time taken}} $$

2. Writing this in symbols for a steady current:

$$ I = \frac{Q}{t} $$

3. To find the total charge that has flowed, we rearrange this equation to make $Q$ the subject. We multiply both sides by $t$:

$$ I \times t = \frac{Q}{t} \times t $$

4. This gives the final relationship:

$$ Q = It $$

Worked Example: Calculating Charge and Number of Electrons

A current of 2.5 A flows through a lamp for 5.0 minutes. Calculate (a) the total charge that passes through the lamp, and (b) the number of electrons that pass through the lamp in this time.

Part (a): Calculate the total charge ($Q$)

1. Convert time to SI units: $t = 5.0 \text{ minutes} = 5.0 \times 60 = 300 \text{ s}$.

2. State the formula:

$$ Q = It $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ Q = (2.5 \text{ A}) \times (300 \text{ s}) = 750 \text{ C} $$

Part (b): Calculate the number of electrons ($N$)

1. State the formula for quantised charge: $Q = Ne$.

2. Rearrange for $N$:

$$ N = \frac{Q}{e} $$

3. Substitute values and solve:

$$ N = \frac{750 \text{ C}}{1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}} = 4.6875 \times 10^{21} $$

$$ N \approx 4.7 \times 10^{21} \text{ electrons} $$

LO4: The Microscopic Model of Current

While electrons in a conductor move randomly at high speeds, when a potential difference is applied, they gain a slow, net velocity in one direction, superimposed on their random motion. This net velocity is called the **mean drift velocity ($v$)**. It is surprisingly slow, often only a few mm per second.

The current in a conductor can be related to this microscopic motion through the drift velocity equation.

Detailed Derivation of $I = Anvq$

1. Consider the charge carriers in a segment of wire of length $L$ and cross-sectional area $A$. The time, $\Delta t$, for all carriers to exit this segment is the time it takes for a carrier at the start to travel the full length $L$ at the mean drift velocity $v$.

$$ \Delta t = \frac{L}{v} $$

2. The volume of this segment is: $\text{Volume} = A \times L$.

3. Let $n$ be the **number density** of charge carriers (the number of charge carriers per unit volume, in m⁻³). The total number of charge carriers, $N$, in this volume is:

$$ N = n \times \text{Volume} = nAL $$

4. Let $q$ be the charge on each individual carrier. The total charge $\Delta Q$ in this volume is:

$$ \Delta Q = Nq = (nAL)q $$

5. Current $I$ is defined as the rate of flow of charge, $I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}$. Substitute the expressions for $\Delta Q$ and $\Delta t$:

$$ I = \frac{(nAL)q}{L/v} $$

6. The length $L$ cancels out, giving the final equation:

$$ I = Anvq $$

Worked Example: Calculating Drift Velocity

A copper wire has a cross-sectional area of $1.0 \times 10^{-6}$ m² and carries a current of 3.0 A. The number density of free electrons in copper is $8.5 \times 10^{28}$ m⁻³. Calculate the mean drift velocity of the electrons.

1. State the drift velocity equation: $I = Anvq$.

2. Rearrange for drift velocity, $v$:

$$ v = \frac{I}{Anq} $$

3. Identify the variables:

$I = 3.0$ A; $A = 1.0 \times 10^{-6}$ m²; $n = 8.5 \times 10^{28}$ m⁻³; $q = e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C.

4. Substitute values and solve:

$$ v = \frac{3.0}{(1.0 \times 10^{-6}) \times (8.5 \times 10^{28}) \times (1.60 \times 10^{-19})} $$

$$ v = \frac{3.0}{13600} = 2.2 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m s}^{-1} \text{ (or 0.22 mm s}^{-1}\text{)} $$
