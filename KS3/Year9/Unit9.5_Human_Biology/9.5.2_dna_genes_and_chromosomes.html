9.5.2 DNA, Genes, and Chromosomes

In this lesson, you will learn that:

  1. Chromosomes contain genes, made of DNA, and that genes contribute to the determination of an organism's characteristics.

The Instruction Manual for Life

Inside the nucleus of almost every cell in your body is a detailed instruction manual that tells your body how to grow, function, and repair itself. This manual contains all the information that makes you unique. The incredible molecule that holds all this information is called **DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)**.

Analogy: A Giant Cookbook

Think of all the genetic information for an organism as a giant, multi-volume cookbook.

  • The entire collection of books is the **Genome**.
  • Each individual book is a **Chromosome**.
  • Each single recipe within a book is a **Gene**.
  • The letters and words that make up the recipes are the **DNA** code itself.

Unpacking the Instructions: From DNA to Chromosomes

These three terms are closely related, describing the same set of instructions at different levels of organisation.

1. DNA

DNA is a very long molecule with a twisted ladder shape called a **double helix**. The "rungs" of the ladder are made of four chemical bases (A, T, C, and G) that form the genetic code.

2. Gene

A **gene** is a specific section of the DNA molecule. Each gene contains the instructions (the "recipe") for making one specific protein. These proteins then build our bodies and determine our traits.

3. Chromosome

A single DNA molecule can be several centimetres long! To fit inside a microscopic nucleus, it has to be tightly coiled and packaged. This super-coiled structure is called a **chromosome**.

Genes and Your Characteristics

The proteins made using the instructions from your genes determine your **traits**, or **characteristics**. Because you inherit your chromosomes from your parents, you inherit a combination of their genes, which is why you have a mixture of their traits.

Examples of traits determined by your genes include:

  • Natural eye colour
  • Natural hair colour
  • Whether your earlobes are attached or detached
  • Your blood type (A, B, AB, or O)
  • Your ability to roll your tongue

In-School Activity: Extracting DNA from a Strawberry

You can actually see DNA! This simple experiment allows you to extract the DNA from a strawberry and see the genetic material with your own eyes.

Safety First!

This experiment should be done with teacher supervision. Handle the rubbing alcohol carefully.

Procedure:

  1. Place a strawberry in a sealable plastic bag and mash it thoroughly for about 2 minutes.
  2. In a separate beaker, mix a small amount of washing-up liquid and a pinch of salt with some water to create an "extraction solution".
  3. Add the extraction solution to the mashed strawberry in the bag, seal it, and mix gently.
  4. Filter the mixture through a coffee filter or fine sieve into a clean beaker to remove the solid bits.
  5. Gently pour cold rubbing alcohol down the side of the beaker to form a layer on top of the strawberry liquid.
  6. Wait for a minute. You will see white, stringy strands appearing at the boundary between the two liquids. This is the strawberry's DNA!
