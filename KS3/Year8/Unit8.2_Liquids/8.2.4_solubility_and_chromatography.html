8.2.4 Solubility and chromatography

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define solubility and explain how it is affected by temperature.
  2. Describe chromatography as a technique for separating mixtures.
  3. Explain how paper chromatography works.

Solubility

**Solubility** is a measure of how much of a substance (a solute) can dissolve in a liquid (a solvent) to form a solution. For most solid solutes, their solubility increases as the temperature of the solvent increases.

Analogy: Making Sugary Tea

Imagine you are making a cup of tea. It's easy to dissolve a spoonful of sugar in hot tea. But if you try to dissolve the same amount of sugar in a glass of cold iced tea, you'll find that some of the sugar is left at the bottom. This is because the hot water has a higher solubility for sugar than the cold water does. The faster-moving particles in the hot water are better at breaking down the sugar crystals and mixing with them.

Chromatography

**Chromatography** is a powerful technique used by scientists to separate the different substances in a mixture. A simple type you can do at home is **paper chromatography**, which is great for separating the different coloured dyes in ink.

Analogy: The Race for Dyes

Think of paper chromatography as a race for the different dyes in an ink. The **paper** is the **racetrack**. The **solvent** (water) is the **starting pistol** that gets all the dyes running. As the water moves up the paper, it carries the dyes with it. Some dyes are more soluble in the water and less "sticky" to the paper, so they travel much further up the track. Other dyes are less soluble and "stickier" to the paper, so they get left behind near the start line. This race separates all the different dyes out, revealing what colours the original ink was made from.

Explanation: Chromatography

At-Home Experiment: Paper Chromatography - Separating Inks

Is the black ink in your felt-tip pen a single substance, or is it a mixture of different coloured dyes? This simple experiment lets you find out!

Equipment:
  • A coffee filter or a piece of kitchen roll
  • A black, water-soluble felt-tip pen (check it's not a permanent marker!)
  • A tall glass or jar
  • A pencil
  • A small amount of water
  • Scissors
Method:
  1. Cut the coffee filter or kitchen roll into a rectangular strip, about 3 cm wide and long enough to reach from the bottom of the glass to the top.
  2. Draw a horizontal line in pencil about 2 cm from the bottom of the strip. **Do not use a pen for this line!**
  3. Draw a single, small but concentrated dot of black ink in the centre of the pencil line.
  4. Pour about 1 cm of water into the bottom of the glass.
  5. Wrap the top of the paper strip around the pencil and rest the pencil across the top of the glass, so that the bottom of the paper just dips into the water. **Crucially, the ink dot itself should be above the water level.**
  6. Watch as the water soaks up the paper. As it passes the ink dot, it will carry the different coloured dyes with it at different speeds, separating them out into a colourful pattern.
  7. Once the water is close to the top, take the paper out and let it dry. You have created a chromatogram!
