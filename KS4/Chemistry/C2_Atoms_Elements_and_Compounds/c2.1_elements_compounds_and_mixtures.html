C2.1 Elements, compounds and mixtures

In chemistry, all matter can be classified into three fundamental categories: elements, compounds, and mixtures. Understanding the distinctions between these forms is crucial for comprehending the composition of substances and how they interact.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the differences between elements, compounds and mixtures.

Elements: The Simplest Substances:

An **element** is a pure substance that consists of only one type of atom. Elements cannot be broken down into simpler substances by ordinary chemical means. Each element is defined by its unique number of protons in the nucleus (its atomic number).

  • **Examples:** Oxygen (O), Hydrogen (H), Carbon (C), Iron (Fe), Gold (Au).
  • Elements are the building blocks of all other substances.

*Figure: The Periodic Table of Elements, showing all known elements.*

Compounds: Chemically Combined Elements:

A **compound** is a pure substance formed when two or more different elements are chemically bonded together in a fixed ratio. Compounds have properties that are distinct from the properties of the elements they are made from. They can only be separated into their constituent elements by chemical reactions.

  • **Examples:** Water (H$_2$O), Carbon Dioxide (CO$_2$), Sodium Chloride (NaCl).
  • In water, hydrogen and oxygen are chemically bonded; water's properties are very different from hydrogen gas and oxygen gas.

*Figure: A 3D model of a water molecule (H$_2$O), a common compound.*

Mixtures: Physically Combined Substances:

A **mixture** is a substance containing two or more elements or compounds that are not chemically bonded together. The components of a mixture retain their individual properties and can be separated by physical means (e.g., filtration, distillation, magnetism).

  • **Examples:** Air (mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide), salt water (mixture of salt and water), sand and iron filings.
  • The properties of a mixture are a combination of the properties of its components.

*Figure: A mixture of salt and pepper, where components retain their individual properties.*
