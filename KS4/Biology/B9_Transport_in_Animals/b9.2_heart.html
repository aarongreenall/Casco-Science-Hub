B9.2 Heart

The **heart** is the central organ of the circulatory system, functioning as a powerful muscular pump that propels blood throughout the body. Its intricate structure and coordinated contractions ensure the efficient delivery of oxygen and nutrients to tissues and the removal of waste products. Understanding the heart's anatomy and the path of blood flow is crucial for comprehending the entire circulatory system.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Identify in diagrams and images the main blood vessels entering and leaving the heart, limited to: vena cava, pulmonary artery, pulmonary vein, aorta.
  2. Identify in diagrams and images the main structures of the heart, limited to: atria, ventricles, muscular walls, valves, septum.
  3. State that the heart is a muscular pump that forces blood around the body.
  4. Describe the functioning of the heart in terms of the contraction and relaxation of the atria and ventricles and the action of the valves.
  5. Explain the effect of physical activity on the heart rate.
  6. Describe the maintenance of a constant body temperature in mammals in terms of: the role of the hypothalamus; vasodilation and vasoconstriction of arterioles; sweating; shivering.
  7. Explain the effect of adrenaline on the heart rate.
  8. Explain the causes and effects of coronary heart disease, and describe its prevention and treatment, limited to: diet, exercise, stress, smoking, inherited factors; bypass surgery, angioplasty, stents, statins.

Structure of the Heart:

The human heart is a four-chambered muscular organ, divided by a central wall called the **septum**. This septum ensures that oxygenated and deoxygenated blood do not mix.

  • **Atria (singular: atrium):** The two upper chambers (right atrium and left atrium) that receive blood returning to the heart. They have thinner muscular walls.
  • **Ventricles:** The two lower chambers (right ventricle and left ventricle) that pump blood out of the heart. They have thicker, more muscular walls, especially the left ventricle which pumps blood to the entire body.
  • **Muscular Walls:** The heart's walls are made of cardiac muscle, which contracts to pump blood. The thickness of the walls varies, with ventricles being thicker than atria, and the left ventricle being the thickest.
  • **Valves:** Flaps of tissue that ensure blood flows in one direction only, preventing backflow. They are located between atria and ventricles, and at the exits of the ventricles into the main arteries.

Blood Flow Through the Heart and Major Vessels:

Blood flows through the heart in a specific sequence:

  • Deoxygenated blood from the body enters the **right atrium** via the **vena cava**.
  • From the right atrium, it flows into the right ventricle.
  • The right ventricle pumps deoxygenated blood into the **pulmonary artery**, which carries it to the lungs.
  • Oxygenated blood from the lungs returns to the **left atrium** via the **pulmonary vein**.
  • From the left atrium, it flows into the left ventricle.
  • The left ventricle pumps oxygenated blood into the **aorta**, the body's largest artery, which distributes it to the rest of the body.

The **valves** open and close at appropriate times to ensure unidirectional blood flow, preventing blood from flowing backward into the previous chamber or vessel.

Heart Rate and its Regulation:

The **heart rate** (number of beats per minute) changes in response to the body's demands:

  • **Physical Activity:** During physical activity, muscles require more oxygen and nutrients, and produce more carbon dioxide. The heart rate increases to pump more blood, delivering more oxygen and removing waste faster.
  • **Adrenaline:** The hormone **adrenaline** (released during stress or excitement) increases heart rate and the force of contraction, preparing the body for "fight or flight" by increasing blood flow to muscles.

Homeostasis: Maintaining Body Temperature:

Mammals maintain a constant internal body temperature (homeostasis) through various mechanisms, controlled by the **hypothalamus** in the brain:

  • **Vasodilation:** When body temperature rises, arterioles (small arteries) near the skin surface **dilate** (widen). This increases blood flow to the skin, allowing more heat to radiate away from the body, thus cooling it.
  • **Vasoconstriction:** When body temperature falls, arterioles near the skin surface **constrict** (narrow). This reduces blood flow to the skin, minimizing heat loss, thus conserving heat.
  • **Sweating:** When hot, sweat glands produce sweat, which evaporates from the skin surface. Evaporation is a cooling process, as it absorbs latent heat from the body.
  • **Shivering:** When cold, muscles contract rapidly and involuntarily (shivering). This muscular activity generates heat through increased respiration, helping to warm the body.

Coronary Heart Disease (CHD):

**Coronary heart disease (CHD)** occurs when the coronary arteries (which supply blood to the heart muscle) become narrowed by a buildup of fatty deposits (atheroma). This reduces blood flow to the heart muscle, leading to symptoms like chest pain (angina) or, if blood flow is severely restricted, a heart attack.

**Causes of CHD:**

  • **Diet:** High intake of saturated fats and cholesterol.
  • **Exercise:** Lack of regular physical activity.
  • **Stress:** Chronic stress can contribute.
  • **Smoking:** Damages blood vessels and increases blood pressure.
  • **Inherited Factors:** Genetic predisposition.

**Prevention and Treatment of CHD:**

  • **Prevention:** Healthy diet, regular exercise, stress management, avoiding smoking.
  • **Treatment:** * **Bypass surgery:** A blood vessel from another part of the body is grafted to bypass a blocked coronary artery. * **Angioplasty:** A balloon is inflated inside the artery to widen it. * **Stents:** A small mesh tube is inserted to keep the artery open. * **Statins:** Medications that lower cholesterol levels in the blood.
