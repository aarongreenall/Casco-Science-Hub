C4.1 Electrolysis

**Electrolysis** is a process that uses electrical energy to drive non-spontaneous chemical reactions. It is the decomposition of an ionic compound, when molten or in aqueous solution, by the passage of an electric current. Electrolysis is crucial in various industrial processes, such as the extraction of reactive metals and the purification of metals.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define electrolysis as the decomposition of an ionic compound, when molten or in aqueous solution, by the passage of an electric current.
  2. Identify in simple electrolytic cells: the anode as the positive electrode, the cathode as the negative electrode, the electrolyte as the molten or aqueous substance that undergoes electrolysis.
  3. Describe the transfer of charge during electrolysis: the movement of electrons in the external circuit, the loss or gain of electrons at the electrodes, the movement of ions in the electrolyte.
  4. Identify the products formed at the electrodes and describe the observations made during the electrolysis of: molten lead(II) bromide using inert electrodes made of platinum or carbon/graphite.
  5. Predict the identity of the products at each electrode for the electrolysis of a binary compound in the molten state.
  6. Construct ionic half-equations for reactions at the cathode (showing gain of electrons as a reduction reaction).
  7. Identify the products formed at the electrodes and describe the observations made during the electrolysis of: concentrated aqueous sodium chloride; dilute sulfuric acid using inert electrodes made of platinum or carbon/graphite.
  8. State that metals or hydrogen are formed at the cathode and that non-metals (other than hydrogen) are formed at the anode.
  9. Identify the products formed at the electrodes and describe the observations made during the electrolysis of aqueous copper(II) sulfate using inert carbon/graphite electrodes and when using copper electrodes.

Components of an Electrolytic Cell:

An **electrolytic cell** consists of:

  • **Electrolyte:** The molten ionic compound or aqueous solution that conducts electricity and undergoes decomposition. It contains free-moving ions.
  • **Electrodes:** Conductors (usually inert, like platinum or graphite) through which electric current enters and leaves the electrolyte. * **Anode:** The **positive electrode**. Anions (negative ions) are attracted to it. Oxidation (loss of electrons) occurs here. * **Cathode:** The **negative electrode**. Cations (positive ions) are attracted to it. Reduction (gain of electrons) occurs here.
  • **External Circuit:** Wires connecting the electrodes to a power supply, allowing electrons to flow.

Charge Transfer During Electrolysis:

During electrolysis, charge is transferred in two ways:

  • **External Circuit:** Electrons move through the wires from the negative terminal of the power supply to the cathode, and from the anode back to the positive terminal of the power supply.
  • **Electrolyte:** Ions move freely within the electrolyte. Cations (positive ions) move towards the cathode, and anions (negative ions) move towards the anode.

At the electrodes, ions either gain or lose electrons:

  • **At the Cathode (Reduction):** Cations gain electrons (reduction reaction). Metals or hydrogen gas are typically formed here.
  • **At the Anode (Oxidation):** Anions lose electrons (oxidation reaction). Non-metals (other than hydrogen) or oxygen gas are typically formed here.

Electrolysis of Molten Binary Compounds:

In molten binary ionic compounds (containing only two elements), the ions are free to move. For example, in molten lead(II) bromide (PbBr$_2$):

  • **At the Cathode (negative electrode):** Pb$^{2+}$ ions gain electrons to form molten lead. * Half-equation: Pb$^{2+}$(l) + 2e$^-$ $\rightarrow$ Pb(l)
  • **At the Anode (positive electrode):** Br$^-$ ions lose electrons to form bromine gas. * Half-equation: 2Br$^-$ (l) $\rightarrow$ Br$_2$(g) + 2e$^-$

**Observation:** At the cathode, silvery molten lead forms. At the anode, brown fumes of bromine gas are observed.

Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions:

Electrolysis of aqueous solutions is more complex because water itself can ionize slightly into H$^+$ and OH$^-$ ions, which can also be discharged at the electrodes. The products formed depend on the reactivity of the metals (for the cathode) and the concentration of the halide ions (for the anode).

  • **Concentrated Aqueous Sodium Chloride (Brine):** * **Cathode:** H$^+$ ions (from water) are discharged to form hydrogen gas, or the less reactive metal ion (Na$^+$) remains in solution. * **Anode:** Cl$^-$ ions are discharged to form chlorine gas (due to high concentration), even though OH$^-$ ions are also present.
  • **Dilute Sulfuric Acid:** * **Cathode:** H$^+$ ions (from acid) are discharged to form hydrogen gas. * **Anode:** OH$^-$ ions (from water) are discharged to form oxygen gas.
  • **Aqueous Copper(II) Sulfate (using inert carbon/graphite electrodes):** * **Cathode:** Copper metal (Cu) is deposited as Cu$^{2+}$ ions are less reactive than H$^+$ ions. * **Anode:** Oxygen gas is produced from the discharge of OH$^-$ ions (from water).
  • **Aqueous Copper(II) Sulfate (using copper electrodes):** * **Cathode:** Copper metal is deposited. * **Anode:** The copper electrode itself dissolves (oxidizes) to form Cu$^{2+}$ ions, leading to the purification of copper.
