9.3.5 Thermal Energy Transfer

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe thermal transfer by the processes of conduction, convection and radiation.

How Does Heat Move?

We know that heat is the transfer of thermal energy, and it always moves from a hotter object to a colder one. But how does the energy actually make that journey? There are three distinct methods of heat transfer: **Conduction**, **Convection**, and **Radiation**.

Analogy: Passing a Message

Imagine you're in a crowded room and need to get a message to a friend on the other side. You have three options:

  • Conduction: You whisper the message to the person next to you, who whispers it to the next person, and so on, until it reaches your friend. The message moves, but the people stay put.
  • Convection: You write the message on a piece of paper, and a person (a particle) walks through the crowd to deliver it directly to your friend. The message is carried by a moving particle.
  • Radiation: You simply shout the message across the room. The message travels as a wave (sound) and your friend hears it instantly, without anyone or anything moving between you.

Conduction: Passing the Vibrations

**Conduction** is the transfer of heat through a substance by direct contact, without the substance itself moving. It is the main method of heat transfer in **solids**. When you heat one end of a solid, its particles gain kinetic energy and vibrate more vigorously. These particles bump into their neighbours, transferring the energy and causing them to vibrate more too. This chain reaction continues along the solid.

Materials that allow heat to pass through them easily, like metals, are called **conductors**. Materials that don't, like wood and plastic, are called **insulators**.

Convection: The Movement of Fluids

**Convection** is the transfer of heat through **fluids** (which means liquids and gases). It happens when the particles of the fluid themselves move from a hot region to a cold region.

When you heat a fluid, it expands, becomes less dense, and rises. Colder, denser fluid from above sinks to take its place. This colder fluid then gets heated and rises. This continuous circulation of rising hot fluid and sinking cold fluid is called a **convection current**.

Radiation: Energy Through Waves

**Radiation** is the transfer of heat by **infrared radiation**, which is a type of electromagnetic wave. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation does **not** need a medium (particles) to travel through. It can travel through a vacuum. This is how the Sun's heat reaches us across the emptiness of space.

All objects emit and absorb thermal radiation. Dull, black surfaces are the best absorbers and emitters of radiation. Shiny, light-coloured surfaces are poor absorbers and emitters because they reflect most of the radiation away.

In-School Activity: Spot the Transfer

For each scenario below, identify the main method of heat transfer (Conduction, Convection, or Radiation) and explain your choice.

Scenario 1:

You leave a metal spoon in a mug of hot chocolate. After a few minutes, the handle of the spoon is also hot.

Answer: Conduction. Heat is transferred through the solid spoon from the hot end to the cold end by particle vibrations.

Scenario 2:

A radiator is placed on one side of a room. Over time, the entire room feels warmer as the air circulates.

Answer: Convection. The radiator heats the air next to it, which becomes less dense and rises. Colder air sinks, creating a convection current that warms the room.

Scenario 3:

You can feel the warmth of a bonfire on your face, even when you are standing several metres away.

Answer: Radiation. The heat travels from the fire to you as infrared waves, moving through the air without needing to heat all the air in between.
