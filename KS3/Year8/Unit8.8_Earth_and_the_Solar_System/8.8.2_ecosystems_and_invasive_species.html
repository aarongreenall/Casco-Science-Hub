8.8.2 Ecosystems and invasive species

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define ecosystem, habitat, and invasive species.
  2. Explain how an invasive species can disrupt a stable ecosystem.
  3. Use a real-world example to describe the impact of an invasive species.

Ecosystems and Habitats

An **ecosystem** is a community of living organisms (like plants and animals) interacting with their non-living environment (like the soil, water, and climate). Within an ecosystem, each organism lives in a specific **habitat**, which is the natural home or environment of that organism.

In a healthy, stable ecosystem, all the different species are in a delicate balance. They compete for resources like food and space, and are part of complex food webs. But what happens when a new species is introduced?

Invasive Species

An **invasive species** is an organism that is not native to a particular ecosystem and which causes harm to the environment, the economy, or human health.

Invasive species are a major problem because they can disrupt the delicate balance of an ecosystem. They often have no natural predators in their new environment and can out-compete the native species for food and resources.

Real-World Example: Red vs. Grey Squirrels

A classic example in the UK is the story of the red and grey squirrels. The **Red Squirrel** is the native species, perfectly adapted to its woodland ecosystem. The **Grey Squirrel** was introduced from North America in the 19th century. The grey squirrel is larger and can eat a wider variety of food, so it **out-competes** the red squirrel for resources. Crucially, the grey squirrel also carries a disease (squirrel pox) that is harmless to them but deadly to the red squirrels. Because the grey squirrel has no natural predators in the UK and is a better competitor, its population has grown, and the native red squirrel population has been pushed to the brink of extinction in many areas.

At-Home Experiment: Building a Model Ecosystem in a Jar (Ecosphere)

You can create your own self-sustaining, sealed ecosystem in a jar. This is a great long-term project to observe how the different parts of an ecosystem work together.

Equipment:
  • A large, clear glass jar with a sealable lid (like a jam or pickle jar)
  • Small pebbles or gravel for drainage
  • A layer of activated charcoal (from a pet shop or garden centre)
  • Potting soil
  • Small, slow-growing plants that like humidity (like moss, small ferns, or fittonia)
  • A small amount of water
Method:
  1. Start by adding a layer of small pebbles at the bottom of the jar for drainage.
  2. Add a thin layer of activated charcoal on top of the pebbles. This will help to keep the water clean.
  3. Add a deeper layer of potting soil.
  4. Carefully plant your small plants in the soil.
  5. Add just enough water to make the soil damp, but not waterlogged.
  6. Seal the jar tightly and place it in a spot with bright, indirect light (not direct sunlight, as this can cook the plants).
  7. Observe your ecosphere over the next few weeks. You should see a mini water cycle happening inside, with condensation on the glass. The plants will produce their own oxygen, and decomposers in the soil will recycle any dead leaves.
