C8.5 Noble gases

**Noble gases**, located in Group VIII (or Group 18) of the Periodic Table, are a unique family of elements known for their extreme unreactivity. This inertness is a direct consequence of their stable electronic configurations, making them valuable in applications where chemical reactions are undesirable.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the Group VIII noble gases as unreactive, monatomic gases and explain this in terms of electronic configuration.

Properties of Noble Gases:

The noble gases include Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar), Krypton (Kr), Xenon (Xe), and Radon (Rn). Their key properties are:

  • **Unreactive:** This is their most defining characteristic. They do not readily form chemical bonds with other elements.
  • **Monatomic Gases:** They exist as single atoms (not molecules like O$_2$ or Cl$_2$) and are gases at room temperature and pressure.
  • **Full Outer Electron Shell:** Their unreactivity is explained by their **electronic configuration**. All noble gases have a full outermost electron shell (2 electrons for Helium, 8 for all others). This stable arrangement means they have no tendency to gain, lose, or share electrons.

*Figure: Electron shell diagrams for Helium, Neon, and Argon, showing their full outer electron shells.*

Trends Down the Group:

Although noble gases are generally unreactive, some physical properties do show trends as you move down the group:

  • **Increasing Density:** As atomic mass increases down the group, so does density.
  • **Increasing Boiling Points:** The boiling points increase down the group due to increasing intermolecular forces (van der Waals forces) as atomic size increases.

Applications of Noble Gases:

Their unreactivity makes noble gases very useful:

  • **Neon:** Used in neon lights for advertising signs, producing a distinctive orange-red glow when an electric current passes through it.
  • **Argon:** Used as an inert atmosphere in welding to prevent hot metals from reacting with oxygen or nitrogen in the air. Also used in incandescent light bulbs to prevent the filament from burning out.
  • **Helium:** Used in balloons and airships because it is much lighter than air and non-flammable.
