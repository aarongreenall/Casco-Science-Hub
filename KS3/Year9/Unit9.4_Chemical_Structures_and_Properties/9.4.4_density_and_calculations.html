9.4.4 Density and Calculations

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe how the density of a substance relates to its mass in a defined volume.
  2. Calculate and compare densities of solids, liquids and gases.
  3. Use density to explain why objects float or sink in water.

What is Density?

Which is heavier: a kilogram of feathers or a kilogram of rocks? It's a trick question—they both have a mass of one kilogram! The difference is the amount of space they take up. The rocks are much more compact. This property of how much "stuff" (mass) is packed into a certain amount of space (volume) is called **density**.

Analogy: Two Suitcases

Imagine you have two identical suitcases (they have the same volume). You pack one completely full of fluffy feathers. You pack the other one with heavy rocks. The suitcase of rocks has far more mass packed into the same amount of space, so it is much more **dense** than the suitcase of feathers.

We can calculate density using a simple formula.

Density = $\frac{\text{Mass}}{\text{Volume}}$

$\rho = \frac{m}{V}$

The standard units for density are grams per cubic centimetre (g/cm³).

How to Calculate Density

To calculate density, we need to find two things: the mass and the volume of an object.

Example 1: A Regular Solid

Let's find the density of a wooden block.

  1. Find the mass: Use a balance. Let's say it's 80 g.
  2. Find the volume: Use a ruler. If the block is 5 cm long, 4 cm wide, and 2 cm high, its volume is 5 x 4 x 2 = 40 cm³.
  3. Calculate density:
    Density = 80 g / 40 cm³ = 2 g/cm³.

Example 2: An Irregular Solid

Let's find the density of a stone.

  1. Find the mass: Use a balance. Let's say it's 90 g.
  2. Find the volume: Use water displacement. A measuring cylinder has 50 cm³ of water. After adding the stone, the level rises to 80 cm³. The volume of the stone is 80 - 50 = 30 cm³.
  3. Calculate density:
    Density = 90 g / 30 cm³ = 3 g/cm³.

Why Do Things Float?

Density explains why some things float and others sink. The rule is simple:

  • An object will **float** if it is **less dense** than the fluid (liquid or gas) it is placed in.
  • An object will **sink** if it is **more dense** than the fluid it is placed in.

The density of pure water is 1 g/cm³. This means any object with a density less than 1 g/cm³ will float in water, and any object with a density greater than 1 g/cm³ will sink.

At-Home Experiment: The Floating Egg

You can change a liquid's density to make an object float!

You will need:

  • A tall glass
  • A fresh egg
  • Water
  • Table salt
  • A spoon

Procedure:

  1. Fill the glass about halfway with tap water.
  2. Gently place the egg in the water. It should sink. (The egg is denser than tap water).
  3. Remove the egg. Now, stir in salt, a few spoonfuls at a time, until it is all dissolved.
  4. Gently place the egg back into the saltwater. What happens now?

Scientific Explanation:

The egg now floats! By dissolving salt in the water, you added a lot of mass without changing the volume very much. This **increased the density of the water**. Once the saltwater became denser than the egg, the egg was able to float.
