9.8.3 Environmental Change and Species Population

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain how changes in the environment can affect the population size of a species.

Factors Affecting Population Size:

The number of individuals of a species in an ecosystem is affected by both biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) factors.

  • Biotic Factors:
    • Food Availability: More food can support a larger population.
    • Predators: An increase in predators will decrease the prey population.
    • Competition: Competition for resources (food, water, mates, territory) with other species can limit population size.
    • Disease: Outbreaks of disease can cause a rapid decline in population.
  • Abiotic Factors:
    • Climate: Changes in temperature, rainfall, or sunlight can affect a species' ability to survive and reproduce.
    • Habitat Loss: Human activities like deforestation or urbanization destroy the places where species live.
    • Pollution: Pollutants can poison organisms or damage their habitat.

Extinction:

  • If a species cannot adapt to changes in its environment, its population may decline significantly.
  • If all individuals of a species die out, the species becomes extinct.
  • Extinction can be caused by natural events (like climate change or asteroid impacts) or by human activities (like habitat destruction and overhunting).
