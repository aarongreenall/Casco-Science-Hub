C3.2 Relative masses of atoms and molecules

In chemistry, it's often more practical to work with the relative masses of atoms and molecules rather than their actual tiny masses. These relative masses are based on a standard reference and allow for straightforward comparisons and calculations in chemical reactions. This section defines relative atomic mass, relative molecular mass, and introduces simple reacting mass calculations.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe relative atomic mass, $A_r$, as the average mass of the isotopes of an element compared to 1/12th of the mass of an atom of $^{12}C$.
  2. Define relative molecular mass, $M_r$, as the sum of the relative atomic masses. Relative formula mass, $M_r$, will be used for ionic compounds.
  3. Calculate reacting masses in simple proportions. Calculations will not involve the mole concept.

Relative Atomic Mass ($A_r$):

The **relative atomic mass ($A_r$)** of an element is the average mass of the isotopes of that element compared to 1/12th of the mass of an atom of Carbon-12 ($^{12}C$). Carbon-12 is used as the standard because it is a very stable and common isotope.

Since elements often exist as a mixture of isotopes (atoms with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons), the relative atomic mass is an *average* value, taking into account the abundance of each isotope. For example, the relative atomic mass of chlorine is 35.5, which is an average based on the natural abundance of chlorine-35 and chlorine-37 isotopes.

*Figure: Illustration of how average atomic mass is calculated from isotopes.*

Relative Molecular Mass ($M_r$) and Relative Formula Mass ($M_r$):

The **relative molecular mass ($M_r$)** of a simple molecular compound is the sum of the relative atomic masses of all the atoms in one molecule of that compound. For example, for water (H$_2$O), $M_r = (2 \times A_r \text{ of H}) + (1 \times A_r \text{ of O})$.

For ionic compounds, which exist as giant lattice structures rather than discrete molecules, the term **relative formula mass ($M_r$)** is used. It is calculated in the same way: the sum of the relative atomic masses of all the atoms in one formula unit of the ionic compound. For example, for sodium chloride (NaCl), $M_r = A_r \text{ of Na} + A_r \text{ of Cl}$.

Calculating Reacting Masses:

In chemical reactions, substances react in fixed proportions by mass. Using relative atomic and molecular/formula masses, we can calculate **reacting masses in simple proportions**. These calculations do not involve the mole concept at this stage, but rather rely on ratios derived from the balanced chemical equation.

For example, in the reaction `2Mg + O2 -> 2MgO`:

  • The ratio of Mg to O$_2$ to MgO is 2:1:2 by moles.
  • By mass, it's $(2 \times A_r \text{ of Mg}) : (1 \times M_r \text{ of O}_2) : (2 \times M_r \text{ of MgO})$.
  • If $A_r(\text{Mg}) = 24$ and $A_r(\text{O}) = 16$, then $M_r(\text{O}_2) = 32$ and $M_r(\text{MgO}) = 40$.
  • So, $48 \text{ g of Mg} : 32 \text{ g of O}_2 : 80 \text{ g of MgO}$. This gives a mass ratio of $3:2:5$.

This ratio can then be used to calculate how much of one reactant is needed or how much product is formed, given the mass of another substance in the reaction.
