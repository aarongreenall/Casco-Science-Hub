8.8.4 Asteroids and galaxies

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe asteroids as rocks left over from the formation of a planetary system.
  2. Describe a galaxy as a huge collection of stars, dust, and gas.
  3. Understand the scale of the universe from our solar system to galaxies.

Asteroids: The Leftovers

**Asteroids** are large, rocky objects that orbit the Sun. They are much smaller than planets. Most of the asteroids in our solar system are found in the **asteroid belt**, a region of space between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter.

They are essentially the "leftovers" from the formation of our solar system 4.6 billion years ago. While gravity was pulling gas and dust together to form the planets, the immense gravitational pull of the giant planet Jupiter prevented a planet from forming in the region of the asteroid belt. Instead, the rocky material was left to orbit the Sun as millions of asteroids.

Galaxies: Islands of Stars

Our solar system is just one tiny part of a much, much larger structure. A **galaxy** is a huge collection of stellar dust, gas, stars, and their planetary systems, all held together by gravity.

Our home galaxy is called the **Milky Way**. It is a spiral galaxy containing hundreds of billions of stars, and our Sun is just one of them, located about two-thirds of the way out from the galactic centre. The universe is filled with billions of other galaxies of different shapes and sizes.

Analogy: The Sandy Beach

To understand the incredible scale of the universe, imagine you are standing on a giant, sandy beach. The entire **beach** is the **universe**. If you bend down and pick up a single **grain of sand**, that one grain represents our entire **Milky Way galaxy**. Our **solar system**, with the Sun and all the planets, would be a microscopic, invisible speck on that single grain of sand. And every other grain of sand on the beach is another galaxy, each containing billions of its own stars and planets.

At-Home Experiment: Modeling the Solar System in the Playground

It's hard to understand the true scale of our solar system from a book. This activity helps you to create a scale model to appreciate the vast distances between the planets.

Equipment:
  • A large open space (a playground, park, or long hallway)
  • A long measuring tape (or a piece of string marked with metres)
  • Various small objects to represent the planets (e.g., a large beach ball for the Sun, a peppercorn for Mercury, a bead for Earth, a marble for Jupiter)
Method:
  1. Place your large "Sun" object at one end of your open space.
  2. Use a scale where 1 metre in your model represents 100 million kilometres in space.
  3. Using the measuring tape, measure out the distance for each planet from your Sun and place your object there. (Your teacher will provide the scaled distances).
  4. For example, Earth is about 150 million km from the Sun, so you would place your "Earth" bead at 1.5 metres from the "Sun".
  5. Jupiter is about 778 million km from the Sun, so you would place your "Jupiter" marble at about 7.8 metres.
  6. Lay out all the planets. What do you notice about the spacing? You will see that the inner planets are all quite close together, while the outer planets are incredibly far apart.
