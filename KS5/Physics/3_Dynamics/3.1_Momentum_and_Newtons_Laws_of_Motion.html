3.1 Momentum and Newton's Laws of Motion

**Dynamics** is the study of motion and the forces that cause it. This unit delves into fundamental principles governing the motion of objects, including inertia, momentum, and Newton's three laws of motion. Understanding these concepts is essential for analyzing how objects move and interact in the physical world.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that mass is the property of an object that resists change in motion.
  2. Recall $F = ma$ and solve problems using it, understanding that acceleration and resultant force are always in the same direction.
  3. Define and use linear momentum as the product of mass and velocity.
  4. Define and use force as rate of change of momentum.
  5. State and apply each of Newton’s laws of motion.
  6. Describe and use the concept of weight as the effect of a gravitational field on a mass and recall that the weight of a body is equal to the product of its mass and the acceleration of free fall.

Mass and Inertia (Newton's First Law):

**Mass** is a fundamental property of an object that quantifies its **inertia**. **Inertia** is the property of an object that **resists changes in its state of motion**. An object at rest tends to stay at rest, and an object in motion tends to stay in motion with the same velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force. This is essentially **Newton's First Law of Motion**.

The greater an object's mass, the greater its inertia, and thus the more difficult it is to change its velocity (either speed up, slow down, or change direction).

Force, Mass, and Acceleration (Newton's Second Law):

**Newton's Second Law of Motion** describes the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration. It states that the **resultant force (F)** acting on an object is directly proportional to the **mass (m)** of the object and its **acceleration (a)**, and acts in the same direction as the acceleration.

$F = ma$

Where: * $F$ is the resultant force in Newtons (N) * $m$ is the mass in kilograms (kg) * $a$ is the acceleration in meters per second squared (m/s²)

This equation shows that a larger force produces a larger acceleration for a given mass, and a larger mass requires a larger force to achieve the same acceleration. The acceleration and the resultant force are always in the same direction.

Momentum and Force as Rate of Change of Momentum:

**Linear momentum (p)** is a vector quantity defined as the product of an object's **mass (m)** and its **velocity (v)**.

$p = mv$

Where: * $p$ is momentum in kg m/s (or N s) * $m$ is mass in kg * $v$ is velocity in m/s

Newton's Second Law can also be defined in terms of momentum: **Force is equal to the rate of change of momentum.**

$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{mv - mu}{\Delta t}$

This definition is particularly useful for understanding forces in collisions and impulses.

Weight and Acceleration of Free Fall:

**Weight (W)** is the force of gravity acting on an object's mass. It is the effect of a gravitational field on a mass.

The weight of a body is equal to the product of its mass (m) and the acceleration of free fall (g).

$W = mg$

Where: * $W$ is weight in Newtons (N) * $m$ is mass in kilograms (kg) * $g$ is the acceleration of free fall (gravitational field strength) in m/s² (or N/kg), approximately $9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$ on Earth.

*Figure: Illustration of the difference between mass and weight.*

Newton's Third Law of Motion:

**Newton's Third Law of Motion** states that for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. More precisely, if object A exerts a force on object B, then object B simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

  • These forces are always equal in magnitude.
  • They are always opposite in direction.
  • They act on different objects (a force pair).
  • They are of the same type (e.g., if one is a gravitational force, the other is also a gravitational force).

**Example:** When you push against a wall, the wall pushes back on you with an equal and opposite force.
