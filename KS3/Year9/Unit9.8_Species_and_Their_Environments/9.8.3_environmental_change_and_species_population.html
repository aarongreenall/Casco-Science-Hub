9.8.3 Environmental Change and Species Population

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe what could happen to the population of a species, including extinction, when there is an environmental change.

A World in Motion

Environments are not static; they are constantly changing. These changes can be slow and gradual, like the long-term shifts in climate, or they can be sudden and dramatic, like a volcanic eruption, a flood, or a wildfire. These changes can have profound effects on the **populations** (the number of individuals) of the species that live there.

Analogy: A Shifting Home

Think of an organism's environment as its home. If the conditions in your home suddenly change—the heating breaks in winter, or the fridge suddenly becomes empty—you have to do something to cope. Animals and plants face the same challenge when their environment changes.

The Three Possible Outcomes

When faced with a significant environmental change, a species' population generally has three possible fates:

1. Adaptation

If there is enough genetic variation in the population, some individuals may have traits that help them survive and reproduce in the new conditions. Through natural selection, these advantageous traits become more common over generations, and the population adapts.

Example: The peppered moth adapting to sooty trees.

2. Migration

If the species is mobile, it may be able to move to a new location with a more suitable environment. This is common for birds, which can fly long distances to escape cold winters and find food.

Example: Swallows migrating from Europe to Africa for the winter.

3. Extinction

If the change is too rapid or severe and the species cannot adapt or migrate, its population may decline until no individuals are left. The species becomes **extinct**—gone forever.

Example: The dodo, which could not adapt when its island home was changed by humans.

Case Study: The Polar Bear and Melting Sea Ice

The polar bear is a powerful, modern example of a species whose population is threatened by environmental change.

  • The Change: Global climate change is causing the Arctic to warm faster than other parts of the world. This is causing the sea ice to melt earlier in the spring and form later in the autumn.
  • The Impact: Polar bears are highly adapted to hunt for seals on the sea ice. Less ice means a shorter hunting season. This leads to starvation, lower body weights, and makes it harder for mothers to successfully raise their cubs.
  • The Likely Outcome: With their habitat disappearing, polar bear populations are declining. They cannot adapt quickly enough to this rapid change. If the trend of melting sea ice continues, scientists are very concerned that polar bears face a high risk of **extinction**.
