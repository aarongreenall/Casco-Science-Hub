1.4 Scalars and Vectors

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand the difference between scalar and vector quantities and give examples of each.
  2. Add and subtract coplanar vectors.
  3. Represent a vector as two perpendicular components (resolving vectors).

Scalars vs. Vectors

Physical quantities can be classified into two main groups:

  • A scalar is a quantity that has only **magnitude**. It is fully described by a number and a unit.
  • A vector is a quantity that has both **magnitude and direction**. It requires a number, a unit, and a direction to be fully described.
Scalar Examples Vector Examples
Distance, Speed, Mass, Time, Energy, Temperature, DensityDisplacement, Velocity, Acceleration, Force, Momentum

For example, saying you walked 5 km (a scalar) is different from saying you walked 5 km North (a vector). The direction is crucial information for vectors.

Adding and Subtracting Vectors

Because vectors have direction, they cannot be added or subtracted like ordinary numbers. We must use graphical or trigonometric methods. The sum of two or more vectors is called the **resultant vector**.

The most common graphical method is the **'head-to-tail' method**. To add vector **B** to vector **A**, you draw vector **A**, and then draw vector **B** starting from the head (the arrowhead) of **A**. The resultant vector **R** is the vector drawn from the tail of **A** to the head of **B**.

Subtracting a vector is the same as adding its negative. The vector **-B** has the same magnitude as **B** but points in the opposite direction. So, **A - B** is the same as **A + (-B)**.

Resolving Vectors into Components

It is often useful to split a single vector into two perpendicular vectors that, when added together, give the original vector. This process is called **resolving a vector**. The two perpendicular vectors are called the **components** of the original vector.

If a vector **F** makes an angle $ \theta $ with the horizontal, we can resolve it into a horizontal component ($F_x$) and a vertical component ($F_y$) using trigonometry:

Horizontal Component:

$$ F_x = F \cos \theta $$

Vertical Component:

$$ F_y = F \sin \theta $$

Worked Example: Resolving a Vector

A child pulls a toy with a force of 20 N at an angle of 30Â° to the horizontal. Calculate the horizontal and vertical components of the force.

1. Identify the given values:

$ F = 20 $ N and $ \theta = 30^\circ $

2. Calculate the horizontal component ($F_x$):

$$ F_x = F \cos \theta = 20 \times \cos(30^\circ) $$

$$ F_x = 20 \times 0.866 = 17.3 \text{ N} $$

3. Calculate the vertical component ($F_y$):

$$ F_y = F \sin \theta = 20 \times \sin(30^\circ) $$

$$ F_y = 20 \times 0.5 = 10.0 \text{ N} $$

4. State the final answer:

The horizontal component of the force is 17.3 N and the vertical component is 10.0 N.
