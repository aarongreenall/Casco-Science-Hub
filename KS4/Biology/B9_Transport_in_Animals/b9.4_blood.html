B9.4 Blood

**Blood** is a vital fluid connective tissue that circulates throughout the body, delivering essential substances to cells and removing waste products. It is composed of several specialized components, each with unique structures and functions crucial for maintaining homeostasis and overall health.

Learning Objectives:

  1. List the components of blood as: red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets and plasma.
  2. State the functions of: red blood cells in transporting oxygen; white blood cells in defence and antibody production; platelets in clotting; plasma in transporting blood cells, ions, soluble food substances, hormones, carbon dioxide, urea and plasma proteins.
  3. Describe the effects of a diet lacking in iron (anaemia) and protein (kwashiorkor).
  4. Describe the effects of excessive loss of blood, limited to: low blood pressure; insufficient oxygen transport.
  5. Describe the immune system in terms of: the role of lymphocytes in antibody production; the role of phagocytes in engulfing pathogens.

Components and Functions of Blood:

Blood is a complex mixture with four main components:

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the main components of blood.*

  • **Plasma:** The liquid component of blood, making up about 55% of blood volume. * **Function:** Transports blood cells, ions, soluble food substances (e.g., glucose, amino acids), hormones, carbon dioxide (as hydrogencarbonate ions), urea, and plasma proteins.
  • **Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes):** Biconcave discs, contain no nucleus in mammals. * **Function:** Transport **oxygen** from the lungs to the body tissues. They contain **haemoglobin**, a protein that binds reversibly with oxygen.
  • **White Blood Cells (Leukocytes):** Involved in the body's defense system. Two main types: * **Phagocytes:** Have lobed nuclei and irregular shapes. * **Function:** Engulf and digest pathogens (e.g., bacteria, viruses) through a process called phagocytosis. * **Lymphocytes:** Have large, round nuclei and less cytoplasm. * **Function:** Produce **antibodies** to target and destroy specific pathogens. They are key to the immune response.
  • **Platelets:** Small, irregular cell fragments. * **Function:** Involved in **blood clotting** to prevent excessive blood loss from injuries. They stick to damaged blood vessel walls and release chemicals that initiate the clotting cascade.

Effects of Dietary Deficiencies and Blood Loss:

A balanced diet is crucial for healthy blood composition:

  • **Iron Deficiency (Anaemia):** A diet lacking in **iron** can lead to **anaemia**. Iron is essential for making haemoglobin. Anaemia results in a reduced number of red blood cells or insufficient haemoglobin, leading to a reduced capacity to transport oxygen. Symptoms include fatigue, paleness, and shortness of breath.
  • **Protein Deficiency (Kwashiorkor):** A diet severely lacking in **protein** can lead to **kwashiorkor**, particularly in children. Protein is vital for growth, repair, and making essential blood components like plasma proteins. Symptoms include swollen abdomen (due to fluid retention), stunted growth, and muscle wasting.

**Excessive loss of blood** (e.g., from severe injury) has immediate and serious effects:

  • **Low Blood Pressure:** Reduced blood volume leads to a drop in blood pressure, making it harder for the heart to pump blood effectively.
  • **Insufficient Oxygen Transport:** Less blood means fewer red blood cells, leading to insufficient oxygen transport to tissues and organs, which can cause organ damage or failure.

The Immune System:

The **immune system** is the body's defense mechanism against pathogens (disease-causing microorganisms). White blood cells play key roles:

  • **Phagocytes:** These white blood cells act as the body's "first responders." They engulf and digest pathogens through **phagocytosis**, a process where they surround and internalize the foreign invaders.
  • **Lymphocytes:** These white blood cells are responsible for specific immunity. They produce **antibodies**, which are specialized proteins that recognize and bind to specific antigens (molecules on the surface of pathogens). Antibodies can then neutralize pathogens or mark them for destruction by other immune cells.
