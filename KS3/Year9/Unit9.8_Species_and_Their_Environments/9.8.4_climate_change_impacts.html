9.8.4 Climate Change Impacts

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the historical and predicted future impacts of climate change, including sea level change, flooding, drought and extreme weather events.

Weather vs. Climate: What's the Difference?

It's essential to understand the difference between these two terms. **Weather** describes the atmospheric conditions over a short period. Is it sunny, rainy, windy, or snowy today? That's the weather. **Climate**, on the other hand, is the long-term average of weather in a particular region, typically over 30 years or more. Panama has a tropical climate; Antarctica has a polar climate.

Analogy: Mood vs. Personality

Weather is like your mood. It can change from day to day, or even hour to hour. You might be happy one moment and sad the next. Climate is like your personality. It's your overall long-term disposition. You might have a bad day (bad weather), but it doesn't change your overall cheerful personality (climate). Climate change means the fundamental "personality" of the Earth's weather is shifting.

Earth's Warming Trend

The Earth's climate has always changed naturally over millions of years. However, scientific evidence shows that the warming trend we are seeing now is happening at an unprecedented rate and is primarily caused by human activities, especially the burning of fossil fuels. This releases greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide, which trap more of the Sun's heat in our atmosphere.

Analogy: A Thicker Blanket

The greenhouse gases in our atmosphere act like a blanket, trapping some of the Sun's heat to keep our planet at a liveable temperature. By burning fossil fuels, we are adding more greenhouse gases, which is like wrapping the Earth in a thicker blanket. The planet is now trapping too much heat, and its average temperature is rising.

The Domino Effect: Impacts of a Warmer World

Even a small increase in the global average temperature can trigger a huge domino effect, leading to significant and varied impacts around the world.

1. Sea Level Rise

This is caused by two main factors: **melting ice** from glaciers and ice sheets on land flowing into the ocean, and **thermal expansion** (as ocean water warms, it expands and takes up more space). This leads to increased flooding in coastal cities and the loss of low-lying islands and habitats.

2. More Flooding and Drought

Climate change disrupts rainfall patterns. Warmer air holds more moisture, leading to more extreme, heavy rainfall and flooding in some areas. In other regions, shifting weather patterns can lead to longer and more severe periods of drought.

3. More Extreme Weather Events

More heat in the atmosphere and oceans provides more energy to fuel storms. This is leading to more frequent and more powerful hurricanes, heatwaves, wildfires, and other extreme weather events.

In-School Research Project: Debating the Impacts and Solutions

The overwhelming scientific consensus, based on millions of measurements, confirms that the Earth's climate is warming due to human activity. The important debate for society now is about the severity of the predicted impacts and the best way to respond.

Your Task:

Your class will be divided into two groups to research and debate different approaches to tackling climate change. Both teams **must** use real evidence from reputable sources (e.g., reports from NASA, the IPCC, university studies, government data) to support their arguments.

Group A: Urgent Action

This group will argue that the evidence points to severe and imminent impacts that require immediate, large-scale global action. You should research evidence supporting the most serious predictions and argue for policies like a rapid transition to 100% renewable energy, even if it involves significant short-term costs.

Group B: Measured Approach

This group will argue that while climate change is a serious issue, the most extreme predictions have high uncertainty. You should research evidence about the costs and challenges of rapid change and argue for a more gradual transition that focuses on adaptation, technological innovation (like carbon capture), and avoiding major economic disruption.
