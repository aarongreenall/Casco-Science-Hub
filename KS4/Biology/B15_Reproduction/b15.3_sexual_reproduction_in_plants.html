B15.3 Sexual reproduction in plants

Flowering plants reproduce sexually, a process that involves the transfer of pollen, fertilization, and the development of seeds and fruits. Understanding the structure of a flower, the mechanisms of pollination, and the subsequent processes of fertilization and seed dispersal is crucial for comprehending plant life cycles and agricultural practices.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Identify in diagrams and images the main parts of a flower, limited to: sepals, petals, stamens, anthers, filaments, carpels, stigma, style, ovary, ovules.
  2. State the functions of the structures listed in 15.3.1.
  3. Describe pollination as the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma.
  4. Describe the differences between insect-pollinated flowers and wind-pollinated flowers, limited to: petals, nectaries, scent, pollen grains, anthers, stigma.
  5. Describe fertilisation as the fusion of the nucleus of the pollen grain with the nucleus of the ovule.
  6. Investigate and describe the conditions required for seed germination, limited to: water, oxygen and warmth.
  7. Describe the uses of fruit and seed dispersal, limited to: wind, water, animals.

Structure of a Flower:

Flowers are the reproductive organs of flowering plants. They contain both male and female reproductive parts:

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the main parts of a flower.*

  • **Sepals:** Small, leaf-like structures that protect the bud before it opens.
  • **Petals:** Often brightly colored and scented to attract pollinators.
  • **Stamen (male part):** Consists of: * **Anther:** Produces pollen grains (male gametes). * **Filament:** Stalk supporting the anther.
  • **Carpel (female part), also known as Pistil:** Consists of: * **Stigma:** Sticky top surface that receives pollen grains. * **Style:** Stalk connecting the stigma to the ovary. * **Ovary:** Contains ovules (female gametes). * **Ovules:** Contain the female gametes.

Pollination:

**Pollination** is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma. It can occur by wind or by animals (e.g., insects).

Insect-Pollinated vs. Wind-Pollinated Flowers:

Flowers show adaptations depending on their pollination method:

Feature Insect-Pollinated Flower Wind-Pollinated Flower
**Petals** Large, brightly coloured Small, dull, or absent
**Nectaries** Present (produce nectar) Absent
**Scent** Present (often strong) Absent
**Pollen Grains** Large, sticky, few in number Small, light, smooth, abundant
**Anthers** Inside flower, stiff, small Outside flower, large, loosely attached
**Stigma** Small, sticky, inside flower Large, feathery, outside flower

Fertilisation and Seed Germination:

**Fertilisation** in plants is the fusion of the nucleus of the pollen grain (male gamete) with the nucleus of the ovule (female gamete). After pollination, a pollen grain lands on the stigma, a pollen tube grows down the style into the ovary, and the male nucleus travels down the pollen tube to fuse with the ovule's nucleus. This forms a zygote, which develops into an embryo within a seed.

A **seed** contains an embryo and a food store (cotyledons or endosperm), enclosed in a protective seed coat.

**Seed germination** is the process by which a seed begins to grow into a new plant. It requires specific conditions:

  • **Water:** To activate enzymes and allow the embryo to swell.
  • **Oxygen:** For aerobic respiration to release energy for growth.
  • **Warmth (suitable temperature):** For optimum enzyme activity.

Light is generally not required for germination, and some seeds even require darkness.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the process of seed germination.*

Fruit and Seed Dispersal:

After fertilization, the ovary develops into a **fruit**, and the ovules develop into **seeds**. **Seed dispersal** is the process by which seeds are carried away from the parent plant. This is important to:

  • Reduce competition for light, water, and nutrients between the parent plant and offspring.
  • Colonize new areas.
  • Avoid overcrowding.

Common methods of dispersal include:

  • **Wind:** Seeds adapted for wind dispersal are often light and have wings or feathery structures (e.g., dandelion, maple).
  • **Water:** Seeds adapted for water dispersal are buoyant and often have waterproof coverings (e.g., coconut).
  • **Animals:** Seeds can be dispersed by animals in several ways: * **Ingestion:** Eaten by animals and passed out in faeces (e.g., berries). * **Attachment:** Hooked seeds attach to animal fur (e.g., burdock). * **Burial/Hoarding:** Animals bury or hoard seeds (e.g., acorns by squirrels).
