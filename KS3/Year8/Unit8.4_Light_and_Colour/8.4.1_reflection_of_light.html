8.4.1 Reflection of light

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe reflection as the redirection of light from a surface.
  2. Define the terms incident ray, reflected ray, and normal.
  3. State and use the law of reflection.

What is Reflection?

We see most objects because light travels from a source (like the Sun or a lightbulb), is redirected by the object, and then enters our eyes. This process, where a light wave hits a surface and is sent back, is called **reflection**. Smooth, shiny surfaces like mirrors and still water are very good at reflecting light in a predictable way.

To describe reflection scientifically, we use a **ray diagram**. We imagine light travelling in straight lines called rays.

  • The **Incident Ray** is the ray of light that travels towards the mirror.
  • The **Reflected Ray** is the ray of light that is sent back from the mirror.
  • The **Normal** is an imaginary line drawn at a right angle (90Â°) to the surface of the mirror at the point where the light hits.
  • The **Angle of Incidence ($i$)** is the angle between the incident ray and the normal.
  • The **Angle of Reflection ($r$)** is the angle between the reflected ray and the normal.

The Law of Reflection is a fundamental rule that is always true for reflection from a flat surface:

The angle of incidence is always equal to the angle of reflection ($i = r$).

In-School Experiment: Investigating Reflection

You can investigate the law of reflection for yourself using a ray box and a mirror.

Equipment:
  • A ray box with a single slit
  • A plane (flat) mirror
  • A protractor
  • A piece of plain paper and a pencil
Method:
  1. Place the paper on a flat surface and draw a straight line across the middle. Place the mirror on this line.
  2. Shine a single ray of light from the ray box so that it hits the mirror at an angle. This is your incident ray.
  3. Use your pencil to mark two dots along the incident ray and two dots along the reflected ray.
  4. Remove the mirror and ray box. Use a ruler to draw the incident and reflected rays by joining the dots.
  5. Use the protractor to draw the normal at the point where the rays meet the mirror line.
  6. Carefully measure the angle of incidence ($i$) and the angle of reflection ($r$).
  7. Repeat the experiment for several different angles of incidence and record your results in a table. You should find that the angle of incidence is always equal to the angle of reflection!
