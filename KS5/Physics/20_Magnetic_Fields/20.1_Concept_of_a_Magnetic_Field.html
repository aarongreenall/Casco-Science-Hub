20.1 Concept of a magnetic field

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that a magnetic field is an example of a field of force produced either by moving charges or by permanent magnets.
  2. Represent a magnetic field by means of field lines.

LO1: The Origin of Magnetic Fields

A **magnetic field** is a field of force that is created by and acts upon other moving charges and magnetic materials. Unlike gravitational fields (created by mass) and electric fields (created by static charge), magnetic fields have two distinct sources:

  1. Moving Charges (Currents): Any moving electric charge creates a magnetic field in the space around it. An electric current is a flow of charge, so every current-carrying wire is surrounded by a magnetic field. This is the fundamental principle of electromagnetism.
  2. Permanent Magnets: The magnetism in permanent magnets like bar magnets arises from the motion of electrons within the atoms of the material. Specifically, a property of electrons called "spin" creates a tiny magnetic field. In magnetic materials like iron, the spins of many electrons can align in the same direction, creating a large-scale, persistent magnetic field.

LO2: Representing Magnetic Fields

We visualize magnetic fields using **magnetic field lines**. These lines are imaginary, but they provide a powerful way to map the strength and direction of the field.

The direction of the magnetic field line at any point is the direction of the force that would be exerted on a **free north pole** placed at that point.

The rules for drawing magnetic field lines are:

  1. Field lines always emerge from a **North pole** and enter a **South pole**.
  2. The spacing of the field lines represents the strength of the field; closer lines indicate a stronger field.
  3. Field lines never cross each other.
