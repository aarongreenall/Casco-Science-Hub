B16.2 Cell division

**Cell division** is a fundamental biological process by which a parent cell divides into two or more daughter cells. It is essential for growth, repair, and reproduction in all living organisms. There are two main types of cell division: **mitosis** and **meiosis**, each serving distinct biological purposes.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe mitosis as nuclear division giving rise to genetically identical cells.
  2. State the role of mitosis in: growth; repair of damaged tissues; asexual reproduction.
  3. Describe meiosis as reduction division in which the chromosome number is halved from diploid to haploid.
  4. State the role of meiosis in producing gametes and in sexual reproduction.
  5. State the differences between mitosis and meiosis, limited to: number of daughter cells produced; chromosome number of daughter cells; genetic similarity of daughter cells to parent cells.

Mitosis: For Growth, Repair, and Asexual Reproduction:

**Mitosis** is a type of nuclear division that results in two daughter cells each having the same number and kind of chromosomes as the parent nucleus, thus producing **genetically identical cells**.

The key roles of mitosis in living organisms are:

  • **Growth:** Increases the number of cells in a multicellular organism, leading to growth.
  • **Repair of damaged tissues:** Replaces old or damaged cells with new, identical ones.
  • **Asexual reproduction:** The basis of asexual reproduction in many organisms (e.g., binary fission in bacteria, budding in yeast).

Meiosis: For Gamete Production and Sexual Reproduction:

**Meiosis** is a type of cell division that results in four daughter cells each with half the number of chromosomes of the parent cell, i.e., a **reduction division** from diploid to haploid. These haploid cells are the gametes (sex cells).

The key role of meiosis is in **sexual reproduction**:

  • **Producing Gametes:** Meiosis produces gametes (sperm and egg cells in animals, pollen and ovules in plants) that have half the number of chromosomes (haploid, n).
  • **Ensuring Genetic Variation:** During meiosis, genetic material is shuffled, leading to gametes that are genetically different from each other. When two different gametes fuse during fertilization, the resulting zygote is genetically dissimilar to both parents, introducing genetic variation in the offspring.

*Figure: A simplified diagram illustrating the stages of meiosis.*

Differences between Mitosis and Meiosis:

Here's a comparison of the key differences between mitosis and meiosis:

Feature Mitosis Meiosis
**Number of daughter cells** 2 4
**Chromosome number of daughter cells** Same as parent (diploid, 2n) Half of parent (haploid, n)
**Genetic similarity to parent** Genetically identical Genetically dissimilar
**Role in organism** Growth, repair, asexual reproduction Gamete production, sexual reproduction
