7.4.1 Characteristics of living organisms

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Recall the seven characteristics of living organisms.
  2. Use the MRS GREN mnemonic to remember the seven life processes.
  3. Explain why something must have all seven characteristics to be classified as living.

What Makes Something Alive?

How can we tell the difference between a living thing, like a rabbit, and a non-living thing, like a rock? Biologists have identified seven key processes that all living organisms must carry out to be considered alive. A handy way to remember these is with the mnemonic **MRS GREN**.

  • Movement: All living things can move, even if it's just the parts inside them.
  • Respiration: The chemical reaction that releases energy from food.
  • Sensitivity: Being aware of and reacting to changes in the surroundings.
  • Growth: Increasing in size and complexity.
  • Reproduction: Producing offspring to continue the species.
  • Excretion: Getting rid of waste products from the body.
  • Nutrition: Taking in food for energy and growth.

The "All or Nothing" Rule

To be classified as a living organism, something must show **all seven** of the life processes. Many non-living things can show one or two, but never all of them.

Analogy: A Cat vs. a Car

A car can seem a bit like a living thing. It shows **Movement**, it needs fuel (**Nutrition**), and it gets rid of waste from its exhaust (**Excretion**). It even has sensors for things like temperature and obstacles (**Sensitivity**). But a car cannot **Grow** or **Reproduce** by itself, and it doesn't **Respire** in a biological sense. Since it doesn't have all seven characteristics, a car is a non-living thing.

Challenge Question: Is Fire Alive?

A fire seems to show many of the characteristics of life. But does it show all seven? Let's investigate. For each life process, click the button to reveal why fire doesn't quite make the cut.

Life Process How Fire Seems to Fit Why It's Not Alive
Movement Flames spread and travel along a fuel source, like a fuse.
This is just the chemical reaction spreading to new fuel. It is not self-propelled or controlled movement.
Respiration It uses oxygen and releases energy (heat and light).
This is combustion, a simple chemical reaction. Respiration is a complex series of controlled reactions inside cells.
Sensitivity It reacts to its environment (e.g., flares up in wind).
This is a simple physical reaction to more oxygen, not a response from a sensory system.
Growth It can get bigger.
Fire grows by consuming more fuel. Living things grow by processing food into their own cells.
Reproduction It can spread to create new fires.
This is just the original fire spreading. It doesn't create new, separate offspring with genetic information.
Excretion It produces waste products like smoke and ash.
These are just the by-products of combustion, not the removal of metabolic waste from a body.
Nutrition It needs fuel (like wood) to survive.
This is consumption, not nutrition. Living things take in nutrients to use in complex chemical reactions for energy and growth.

And the most important reason: **Fire is not made of cells!**

At-Home Experiment: The Yeast and Balloon Blow-Up

Yeast is a living microorganism. In this experiment, you can prove it's alive by observing it carry out some of the seven life processes.

Equipment:
  • An empty plastic bottle (e.g., a 500ml drinks bottle)
  • A balloon
  • One packet of dried yeast
  • Two teaspoons of sugar
  • Warm water (not too hot, or it will kill the yeast!)
Method:
  1. Pour the packet of yeast into the plastic bottle.
  2. Add the two teaspoons of sugar.
  3. Carefully fill the bottle about halfway with warm water.
  4. Gently swirl the bottle to mix the contents.
  5. Stretch the neck of the balloon over the top of the bottle.
  6. Place the bottle in a warm place and observe it for the next 30-60 minutes.
What's Happening?

The yeast is using the sugar as food (**Nutrition**). It is carrying out **Respiration** to release energy from the sugar. A waste product of this respiration is carbon dioxide gas. This gas fills the bottle and then inflates the balloon (**Excretion**). Because the yeast is carrying out these life processes, we know it is a living organism!
