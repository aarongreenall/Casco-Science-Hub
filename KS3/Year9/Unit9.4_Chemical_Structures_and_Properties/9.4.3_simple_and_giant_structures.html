9.4.3 Simple and Giant Structures

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will explain the difference between simple molecular structures and giant structures and how this affects their properties.

Simple Molecular Structures:

  • Consist of a relatively small, fixed number of atoms joined by strong covalent bonds (e.g., H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, O₂).
  • Properties:
    • Low Melting and Boiling Points: The covalent bonds within the molecules are strong, but the forces between the molecules (intermolecular forces) are weak. Little energy is needed to overcome these weak forces, so these substances are often gases or liquids at room temperature.
    • Do Not Conduct Electricity: The molecules are neutral and have no free-moving charged particles (electrons or ions).

Giant Structures:

  • Consist of a very large number of atoms or ions held together by strong bonds in a regular, repeating lattice structure.
  • 1. Giant Ionic Lattices:
    • e.g., Sodium chloride (NaCl).
    • Held together by strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions.
    • Properties: High melting and boiling points; conduct electricity only when molten or dissolved in water (when the ions are free to move).
  • 2. Giant Covalent Structures (Macromolecules):
    • e.g., Diamond, Graphite, Silicon Dioxide (Silica).
    • All atoms are joined by strong covalent bonds.
    • Properties: Very high melting and boiling points because a lot of energy is needed to break the strong covalent bonds throughout the structure. Most do not conduct electricity (except graphite, which has delocalised electrons).
