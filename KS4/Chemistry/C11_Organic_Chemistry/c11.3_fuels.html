C11.3 Fuels

**Fuels** are substances that store energy, which can be released, typically through combustion, to provide heat, light, or power. In organic chemistry, many important fuels are hydrocarbons, derived primarily from fossil fuels like petroleum, coal, and natural gas. This section explores these fossil fuels, the process of fractional distillation, and the uses of petroleum fractions.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Name the fossil fuels: coal, natural gas and petroleum.
  2. Name methane as the main constituent of natural gas.
  3. State that hydrocarbons are compounds that contain hydrogen and carbon only.
  4. State that petroleum is a mixture of hydrocarbons.
  5. Describe the separation of petroleum into useful fractions by fractional distillation.
  6. Name the uses of the fractions as: refinery gas fraction for gas used in heating and cooking; gasoline/petrol fraction for fuel used in cars; naphtha fraction as a chemical feedstock; diesel oil/gas oil for fuel used in diesel engines; bitumen for making roads.
  7. Describe how the properties of fractions obtained from petroleum change from the bottom to the top of the fractionating column, limited to: decreasing chain length; lower boiling points.

Fossil Fuels and Hydrocarbons:

The primary fossil fuels are:

  • **Coal:** A solid fossil fuel.
  • **Natural Gas:** Primarily consists of **methane (CH$_4$)**, a gaseous hydrocarbon.
  • **Petroleum (Crude Oil):** A dark, viscous liquid that is a complex **mixture of hydrocarbons**.

**Hydrocarbons** are organic compounds that contain only hydrogen and carbon atoms. They are the main components of fossil fuels.

Fractional Distillation of Petroleum:

Petroleum (crude oil) is a mixture of hydrocarbons with different boiling points. It is separated into useful components called **fractions** by **fractional distillation** in a fractionating column. This process relies on the fact that different hydrocarbons have different boiling points.

How fractional distillation works:

  1. Crude oil is heated in a furnace until most of it vaporizes.
  2. The hot mixture of liquid and vapor enters the bottom of a tall fractionating column.
  3. The column is hotter at the bottom and cooler at the top, creating a temperature gradient.
  4. Vapors rise up the column. As they rise, they cool and condense at different levels (fractions) according to their boiling points.
  5. Hydrocarbons with **higher boiling points** (longer carbon chains) condense lower down the column.
  6. Hydrocarbons with **lower boiling points** (shorter carbon chains) continue to rise higher before condensing.

This process separates petroleum into fractions based on their boiling points, which are related to their chain length. From the bottom to the top of the fractionating column, the fractions show a trend of **decreasing chain length** and **lower boiling points**.

*Figure: A diagram of a fractionating column used for the fractional distillation of crude oil.*

Uses of Petroleum Fractions:

Each fraction obtained from petroleum has specific uses:

  • **Refinery Gas Fraction:** Used as gas for heating and cooking (e.g., LPG).
  • **Gasoline/Petrol Fraction:** Used as fuel for cars.
  • **Naphtha Fraction:** An important **chemical feedstock** (raw material) for the petrochemical industry, used to make plastics and other chemicals.
  • **Kerosene/Paraffin Fraction:** Used as jet fuel and for heating/lighting.
  • **Diesel Oil/Gas Oil Fraction:** Used as fuel for diesel engines (e.g., in buses, lorries).
  • **Fuel Oil Fraction:** Used as fuel for ships and power stations.
  • **Bitumen (Asphalt) Fraction:** A thick, black residue used for making roads and roofing.
