C3.1 Formulas

Chemical formulas are a concise way to represent the composition of elements and compounds. They provide information about the types of atoms present and their relative numbers. Understanding how to interpret and deduce chemical formulas is fundamental to all chemical calculations and reactions.

Learning Objectives:

  1. State the formulas of the elements and compounds named in the subject content.
  2. Define the molecular formula of a compound as the number and type of different atoms in one molecule.
  3. Deduce the formula of a simple molecular compound from the relative numbers of atoms present in a model or a diagrammatic representation.
  4. Deduce the formula of an ionic compound from the relative numbers of the ions present in a model or a diagrammatic representation or from the charges on the ions.
  5. Construct word equations to show how reactants form products.
  6. Balance and interpret simple symbol equations, including state symbols.
  7. Deduce the symbol equation with state symbols for a chemical reaction, given relevant information.
  8. Construct symbol equations with state symbols, including ionic equations.

Types of Formulas:

A **chemical formula** uses chemical symbols and subscripts to show the types of atoms and their ratio in a compound or element.

  • **Element Formulas:** For elements, the formula can be just the symbol (e.g., Na for sodium, Fe for iron) or indicate a diatomic molecule (e.g., O$_2$ for oxygen, N$_2$ for nitrogen, Cl$_2$ for chlorine). Noble gases (e.g., Ar for argon) are monatomic.
  • **Molecular Formula:** For simple molecular compounds, the **molecular formula** shows the exact number and type of different atoms in one molecule (e.g., H$_2$O for water, CH$_4$ for methane). This term is strictly used for substances that exist as discrete molecules.
  • **Empirical Formula (for Ionic Compounds):** For ionic compounds, we use the simplest whole-number ratio of ions in their giant lattice structure (e.g., NaCl for sodium chloride, MgO for magnesium oxide). This is often referred to as the formula unit.

Deducing Formulas:

You can deduce the formula of a compound from models, diagrams, or by understanding the charges on ions:

  • **Simple Molecular Compounds:** Count the number of each type of atom present in the model or diagram and write them as subscripts. For example, if a model shows one carbon atom bonded to four hydrogen atoms, the formula is CH$_4$.
  • **Ionic Compounds:** Determine the charges on the ions. The formula must be electrically neutral, meaning the total positive charge from cations must balance the total negative charge from anions. Find the simplest whole-number ratio of ions that achieves this balance. For example, Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$ combine in a 1:1 ratio to form NaCl. Mg$^{2+}$ and O$^{2-}$ also combine in a 1:1 ratio to form MgO. Ca$^{2+}$ and Cl$^-$ combine in a 1:2 ratio to form CaCl$_2$.

Chemical Equations:

Chemical reactions are represented by **chemical equations**, which show the reactants (starting materials) and products (substances formed).

  • **Word Equations:** Use the names of the reactants and products. * Example: `carbon + oxygen -> carbon dioxide`
  • **Symbol Equations:** Use chemical formulas for reactants and products. They must be **balanced** to obey the Law of Conservation of Mass (the number of atoms of each element must be the same on both sides of the equation). **State symbols** are often included to indicate the physical state of each substance: (s) for solid, (l) for liquid, (g) for gas, and (aq) for aqueous solution. * Example: `C(s) + O2(g) -> CO2(g)`
  • **Ionic Equations:** Show only the particles (ions or molecules) that are directly involved in the chemical change. Spectator ions (ions that do not participate in the reaction) are omitted.
