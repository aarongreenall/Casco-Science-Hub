14.2 Temperature scales

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that a physical property that varies with temperature may be used for the measurement of temperature and state examples of such properties, including the density of a liquid, volume of a gas at constant pressure, resistance of a metal, e.m.f. of a thermocouple.
  2. Understand that the scale of thermodynamic temperature does not depend on the property of any particular substance.
  3. Convert temperatures between kelvin and degrees Celsius and recall that $T / K = \theta / °C + 273.15$.
  4. Understand that the lowest possible temperature is zero kelvin on the thermodynamic temperature scale and that this is known as absolute zero.

LO1: Physical Properties and Thermometry

To measure temperature, we need a physical property of a substance that changes predictably and measurably as its temperature changes. This property is called a **thermometric property**. Different types of thermometers are based on different properties:

  • Density of a liquid: Most liquids expand when heated, so their volume increases and their density decreases. A liquid-in-glass thermometer (e.g., mercury or alcohol) works because the liquid expands up a narrow capillary tube. The length of the column is the physical property we measure.
  • Volume of a gas at constant pressure: According to Charles's Law, the volume of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature, provided the pressure is kept constant. This is the principle behind the constant-pressure gas thermometer.
  • Resistance of a metal: The electrical resistance of a metal conductor increases as its temperature increases. This is because the positive ions in the lattice vibrate more vigorously, increasing the frequency of collisions with the charge-carrying electrons. This property is used in a platinum resistance thermometer.
  • e.m.f. of a thermocouple: A thermocouple consists of two different metals joined at two junctions. If the junctions are at different temperatures, a small electromotive force (e.m.f.) is generated. This is called the Seebeck effect, and the magnitude of the e.m.f. depends on the temperature difference.

LO2, LO4 & LO3: The Thermodynamic Temperature Scale

A problem with thermometers based on the properties of specific substances (like the expansion of mercury) is that their scales are not perfectly linear and can differ slightly from each other. To create a universal standard, scientists developed the **thermodynamic (or absolute) temperature scale**.

This scale is a theoretical scale that **does not depend on the property of any particular substance**. It is based on the fundamental principles of thermodynamics, specifically the concept that there is a lowest possible temperature.

Absolute Zero

The lowest possible temperature is called **absolute zero**. This is the point at which particles have their minimum possible internal energy. At absolute zero, all random motion of particles ceases. This temperature is the zero point (0 K) of the thermodynamic temperature scale.

The Kelvin and Celsius Scales

The SI unit for the thermodynamic temperature scale is the **kelvin (K)**. It is an absolute scale because its zero point is absolute zero. The **Celsius scale (°C)** is a relative scale, defined by setting the freezing point of water to 0 °C and the boiling point to 100 °C.

Absolute zero (0 K) corresponds to -273.15 °C. The size of one kelvin is the same as the size of one degree Celsius. This leads to the conversion formula:

$$ T \, (\text{in K}) = \theta \, (\text{in }^\circ\text{C}) + 273.15 $$

Worked Example: Temperature Conversion

The boiling point of nitrogen is 77 K. What is this temperature in degrees Celsius?

1. State the conversion formula:

$$ T / \text{K} = \theta / ^\circ\text{C} + 273.15 $$

2. Rearrange for degrees Celsius ($\theta$):

$$ \theta / ^\circ\text{C} = T / \text{K} - 273.15 $$

3. Substitute the value and solve:

$$ \theta = 77 - 273.15 $$

$$ \theta = -196.15 \, ^\circ\text{C} $$
