B10.1 Diseases and immunity

**Diseases** are conditions that impair the normal functioning of a living organism. They can be broadly categorized as infectious (communicable) or non-infectious (non-communicable). The body's defense against disease is provided by the **immune system**, a complex network of cells, tissues, and organs that work together to protect the body from harmful pathogens.

Causes and Transmission of Disease:

A **disease** is any condition that impairs the normal functioning of an organism.

Diseases can be caused by:

  • **Pathogens:** A **pathogen** is a disease-causing organism. These include: * **Viruses:** (e.g., influenza, HIV) * **Bacteria:** (e.g., cholera, tuberculosis) * **Fungi:** (e.g., athlete's foot) * **Protoctista:** (e.g., malaria)
  • **Malnutrition:** Lack of essential nutrients (e.g., scurvy, rickets).
  • **Inherited Factors:** Genetic predispositions (e.g., cystic fibrosis, some cancers).
  • **Environmental Factors:** Exposure to harmful substances (e.g., pollution, radiation) or lifestyle choices (e.g., smoking leading to lung cancer).

Transmission of Disease:

Infectious diseases can be transmitted in several ways:

  • **Direct Contact:** * **Physical contact:** Touching an infected person or surface. * **Sexual intercourse:** (e.g., HIV, STIs).
  • **Indirect Contact:** * **Droplet infection:** Pathogens spread through tiny droplets released by coughing or sneezing (e.g., influenza, TB). * **Food and water:** Consuming contaminated food or water (e.g., cholera, food poisoning). * **Vectors:** Organisms that transmit pathogens from one host to another (e.g., mosquitoes transmitting malaria).

Body's Natural Defences Against Pathogens:

The human body has several non-specific natural defenses that prevent pathogens from entering or multiplying:

  • **Skin:** A physical barrier that prevents most pathogens from entering the body.
  • **Hairs in the nose:** Trap dust and pathogens in inhaled air.
  • **Mucus:** Sticky substance produced in the respiratory and digestive tracts, trapping pathogens.
  • **Cilia:** Tiny hair-like structures lining the trachea and bronchi that sweep mucus (and trapped pathogens) upwards to be swallowed.
  • **Stomach acid:** Hydrochloric acid in the stomach kills most pathogens ingested with food.

The Immune System: Phagocytes and Lymphocytes:

If pathogens breach the body's natural defenses, the **immune system** provides a specific and adaptive response, primarily involving white blood cells:

  • **Phagocytes:** These white blood cells act as "scavengers." They engulf and digest pathogens (and dead cells) through a process called **phagocytosis**. They are part of the non-specific immune response.
  • **Lymphocytes:** These white blood cells are responsible for specific immunity. They produce **antibodies**, which are Y-shaped proteins that are highly specific to particular antigens (unique molecules on the surface of pathogens). Antibodies can: * Bind to pathogens and neutralize them. * Clump pathogens together, making them easier for phagocytes to engulf. * Mark pathogens for destruction by other immune cells.

*Figure: Simplified diagram of an immune response, showing phagocytes engulfing pathogens and lymphocytes producing antibodies.*

Immunity: Active and Passive:

There are two main types of immunity:

  • **Active Immunity:** Defence against a pathogen by **antibody production in the body**. This occurs when your own immune system is stimulated to produce antibodies. It provides long-term protection. * **Natural active immunity:** Developed after exposure to a pathogen (e.g., getting sick with measles). * **Artificial active immunity:** Developed after vaccination.
  • **Passive Immunity:** Short-term defence against a pathogen by **antibodies acquired from another individual**. This provides immediate but temporary protection as the body does not produce its own antibodies. * **Natural passive immunity:** Antibodies passed from mother to baby via placenta or breast milk. * **Artificial passive immunity:** Antibodies injected from another source (e.g., antiserum).

Vaccination:

The **principle of vaccination** is to expose the immune system to a weakened or inactive form of a pathogen (or its antigens) so that it can produce antibodies and memory cells without causing the actual disease. If the vaccinated individual later encounters the real pathogen, their immune system can rapidly produce a large number of antibodies, preventing illness.

**Vaccination plays a crucial role in controlling the spread of disease** by:

  • Protecting individuals from specific diseases.
  • Contributing to **herd immunity**, where a high percentage of vaccinated individuals protects unvaccinated individuals by reducing the pathogen's ability to spread.

Specific Diseases: Causes and Effects:

Here are some examples of common diseases:

  • **HIV/AIDS:** * **Cause:** HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is the virus that causes AIDS. * **Transmission:** Transmitted by the exchange of body fluids (e.g., sexual intercourse, sharing needles, mother to child during birth/breastfeeding). * **Effects:** HIV attacks and weakens the immune system, increasing the risk of opportunistic infections (infections that a healthy immune system could fight off). AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome) is the final stage of HIV infection, characterized by severe immune deficiency.
  • **Cholera:** * **Cause:** Caused by a bacterium (Vibrio cholerae). * **Transmission:** Transmitted by contaminated water (fecal-oral route). * **Effects:** Causes severe diarrhoea and rapid dehydration, which can be fatal if untreated.
  • **Malaria:** * **Cause:** Caused by a protoctistan parasite (Plasmodium). * **Transmission:** Transmitted by a vector (Anopheles mosquito). * **Effects:** Causes recurrent fever, chills, and damage to red blood cells, leading to anaemia and other complications.
  • **Tuberculosis (TB):** * **Cause:** Caused by a bacterium (Mycobacterium tuberculosis). * **Transmission:** Transmitted by droplet infection (airborne, e.g., coughing, sneezing). * **Effects:** Primarily affects the lungs, causing persistent coughing, breathing difficulties, chest pain, and weight loss. Can also affect other parts of the body.
