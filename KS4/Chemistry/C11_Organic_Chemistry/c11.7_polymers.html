C11.7 Polymers

**Polymers** are large molecules built up from many smaller repeating units called **monomers**. They are incredibly versatile materials, forming the basis of plastics, synthetic fibers, and many biological molecules. This section explores the definition of polymers, the formation of poly(ethene) as an example of addition polymerization, and the differences between addition and condensation polymerization.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define polymers as large molecules built up from many smaller molecules called monomers.
  2. Describe the formation of poly(ethene) as an example of addition polymerisation using ethene monomers.
  3. Identify the repeat units in addition polymers and in condensation polymers.
  4. Deduce the structure or repeat unit of an addition polymer from a given alkene and vice versa.
  5. Describe and draw the structure of nylon, a polyamide.
  6. Describe the differences between addition and condensation polymerisation.

Polymers and Monomers:

A **polymer** is a very large molecule (macromolecule) formed by the joining together of many small repeating units called **monomers**. The process of forming a polymer from monomers is called **polymerization**.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the concept of monomers joining to form a polymer.*

Addition Polymerization (e.g., Poly(ethene)):

**Addition polymerization** occurs when unsaturated monomers (like alkenes) add on to one another in a continuous chain reaction, without the loss of any atoms. The double bond in the alkene monomer breaks, allowing the monomers to link together.

**Poly(ethene)** (commonly known as polythene or polyethylene) is a common addition polymer formed from **ethene monomers**.

`n C2H4 (ethene) -> -(CH2-CH2)n- (poly(ethene))`

The **repeat unit** in an addition polymer is the part of the polymer structure that is derived from the monomer. For poly(ethene), the repeat unit is -CH$_2$-CH$_2$-. You should be able to deduce the structure of an addition polymer from its alkene monomer and vice versa.

*Figure: Diagram showing the addition polymerization of ethene to form poly(ethene).*

Condensation Polymerization (e.g., Nylon):

**Condensation polymerization** occurs when monomers join together by eliminating a small molecule, such as water or hydrogen chloride, as a byproduct. This type of polymerization typically involves monomers with two different functional groups.

**Nylon** is a common example of a **polyamide**, which is a type of condensation polymer. It is formed from two different monomers, a diamine and a dicarboxylic acid, with the elimination of water molecules. The repeat unit in condensation polymers is formed from the remnants of the two monomers after the small molecule has been removed.

*Figure: Diagram showing the synthesis of Nylon 6,6, a polyamide, via condensation polymerization.*

Differences between Addition and Condensation Polymerization:

  • **Addition Polymerization:** * Monomers are typically unsaturated (e.g., alkenes). * No atoms are lost; the polymer is the sum of the monomers. * Only one product is formed (the polymer).
  • **Condensation Polymerization:** * Monomers typically have two different functional groups. * A small molecule (e.g., water, HCl) is eliminated during the reaction. * Two products are formed (the polymer and the small molecule).
