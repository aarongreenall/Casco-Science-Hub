7.2 Transverse and Longitudinal Waves

Learning Objectives:

  1. Compare transverse and longitudinal waves.
  2. Analyse and interpret graphical representations of transverse and longitudinal waves.

LO1: Comparing Transverse and Longitudinal Waves

Waves are classified based on the direction of oscillation of the medium's particles relative to the direction of energy transfer.

Transverse Waves

In a **transverse wave**, the particles of the medium oscillate **perpendicular** to the direction of energy transfer.

Examples include all electromagnetic waves (light, radio), waves on a rope, and ripples on water.

Longitudinal Waves

In a **longitudinal wave**, the particles of the medium oscillate **parallel** to the direction of energy transfer.

This parallel motion creates areas where the particles are bunched together, called **compressions** (regions of high pressure), and areas where they are spread apart, called **rarefactions** (regions of low pressure).

The most common example is a sound wave. A key property of longitudinal waves is that they cannot be polarised.

LO2: Graphical Representation of Waves

While they look different in reality, both transverse and longitudinal waves can be represented by the same types of graphs.

  • A **displacement-time graph** shows the displacement of a single particle as time passes.
  • A **displacement-position graph** shows a "snapshot" of the displacements of all particles at a single instant in time.

For a **transverse wave**, the y-axis of the graph represents the perpendicular displacement of the particles. For a **longitudinal wave**, the y-axis represents the displacement of particles *parallel* to the direction of energy transfer (a positive value means displacement to the right, a negative value means displacement to the left).

As shown above, a point of **compression** corresponds to the centre of a region where particles have moved inwards, which is a point of zero displacement with a negative gradient on the graph. A point of **rarefaction** corresponds to the centre of a region where particles have moved outwards, which is a point of zero displacement with a positive gradient.
