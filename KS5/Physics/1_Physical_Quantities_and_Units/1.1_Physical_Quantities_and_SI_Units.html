1.1 Physical Quantities and SI Units

In physics, accurately describing the world around us requires precise measurements. Every physical quantity we measure consists of two essential parts: a **numerical magnitude** (how much) and a **unit** (what kind). Understanding these quantities and the international system of units (SI units) is fundamental to all scientific study.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that all physical quantities consist of a numerical magnitude and a unit.
  2. Make reasonable estimates of physical quantities included within the syllabus.
  3. Recall the following SI base quantities and their units: mass (kg), length (m), time (s), current (A), temperature (K).
  4. Express derived units as products or quotients of the SI base units and use the derived units for quantities listed in this syllabus as appropriate.
  5. Use SI base units to check the homogeneity of physical equations.
  6. Recall and use prefixes and their symbols to indicate decimal submultiples or multiples of both base and derived units.

Physical Quantities and Units:

A **physical quantity** is anything that can be measured. For a measurement to be meaningful, it must always include both a numerical value (magnitude) and a unit. For example, saying "the length is 5" is incomplete; it must be "5 meters" or "5 centimeters."

The **International System of Units (SI units)** is the modern form of the metric system and is the most widely used system of measurement. It provides a standardized set of units for all physical quantities.

SI Base Quantities and Units:

There are seven fundamental SI base quantities, from which all other units are derived:

Quantity SI Base Unit Symbol
Mass kilogram kg
Length meter m
Time second s
Electric Current ampere A
Temperature kelvin K
Amount of substance mole mol
Luminous intensity candela cd

Derived Units and Homogeneity:

**Derived units** are combinations of base units. For example, speed is derived from length and time, so its unit is meters per second (m/s). Force is derived from mass and acceleration (length/time²), so its unit is kg·m/s², which is defined as the Newton (N).

**Homogeneity of physical equations** means that the units on both sides of an equation must be the same. This is a powerful way to check if an equation is dimensionally correct. For example, for the equation `Force = mass × acceleration (F = ma)`: * Units of F: Newton (N) * Units of m: kilogram (kg) * Units of a: meters per second squared (m/s²) * So, N = kg × m/s² or N = kg m s⁻². This shows the equation is homogeneous.
