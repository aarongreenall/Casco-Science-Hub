9.6.3 Current in Parallel Circuits

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe how current divides in parallel circuits.

Revisiting Circuit Types: Series vs. Parallel

Let's quickly recap the two basic ways to build a circuit. In a **series circuit**, all the components are connected in a single loop, one after the other. There is only one path for the current to take. In a **parallel circuit**, the circuit is split into two or more separate branches, giving the current multiple paths to follow.

Parallel Circuit: Multiple Paths

The Rules of the Road: Current in Parallel Circuits

Current behaves differently in a parallel circuit. The fundamental rule is: **the total current flowing from the battery splits up at the junctions**. Some of the current flows down one branch, and the rest flows down the other branches. After passing through the branches, the currents join back together before returning to the battery.

The sum of the currents in the separate branches is equal to the total current flowing from the source.

Analogy: A River Splitting

Imagine the total current from the battery is a large river. When the river reaches an island, it has to split into two smaller streams to flow around it. After the island, the two streams merge back together to form the same large river again. The total amount of water flowing is always conserved. The current in a parallel circuit splits and rejoins in exactly the same way.

As shown by the ammeters, the total current from the battery (A1) is equal to the sum of the currents in the two branches (A2 + A3).

An Important Consequence: Adding More Branches

A very important feature of parallel circuits is that adding a new branch provides another pathway for the current to flow. This actually **decreases the total resistance** of the whole circuit.

Since resistance and current are inversely related ($I = V/R$), a lower total resistance means that the **total current flowing from the battery increases** when you add more components in parallel. This is why all the sockets and lights in your house are wired in parallel. You can plug in a new appliance without affecting the others, and each one draws the current it needs from the main supply.

Interactive Lab: Circuit Construction Kit

Investigate how current behaves in a parallel circuit.

  1. Build a simple circuit with one battery and one lamp. Use the ammeter to measure the total current. Note down the value.
  2. Now, add a second lamp in a separate, parallel branch. What happens to the brightness of the first lamp? What happens to the total current leaving the battery?
  3. Use multiple ammeters to measure the current in each branch and the total current from the battery. Do the currents in the branches add up to the total?
  4. Add a third lamp in another parallel branch. What happens to the total current now? Why do you think this is?
