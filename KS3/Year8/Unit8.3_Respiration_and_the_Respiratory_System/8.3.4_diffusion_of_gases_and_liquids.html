8.3.4 Diffusion of gases and liquids

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define diffusion as the movement of particles down a concentration gradient.
  2. Explain how diffusion drives gas exchange in the lungs.
  3. Explain how the role of haemoglobin maintains a steep concentration gradient for oxygen.

Diffusion: The Driving Force of Gas Exchange

**Diffusion** is the net movement of particles from an area of high concentration to an area of low concentration. This is a **passive process**, meaning it requires no energy. It happens because particles are always in random motion.

This simple principle is the driving force behind **gas exchange**. The key to efficient gas exchange is maintaining a steep **concentration gradient** for each gas. A steep gradient means there is a very big difference between the high and low concentrations, which makes diffusion happen much faster.

In the lungs, when oxygen diffuses from the alveoli into the blood, it immediately bonds with a protein called **haemoglobin** in the red blood cells. This effectively "hides" the oxygen from the blood plasma, keeping the concentration of free, dissolved oxygen in the blood very low. This ensures the concentration gradient between the lungs and the blood stays very steep, so oxygen continues to diffuse across rapidly. At the same time, the blood is full of waste carbon dioxide, which diffuses down its own steep concentration gradient into the lungs to be exhaled.

Analogy: The Supermarket Checkout

Think of the boundary between an air sac (alveolus) and the blood as a **supermarket checkout**. The **lungs** are your **shopping trolley**, full of items (oxygen). The **blood plasma** is the **small space on the conveyor belt** right in front of the cashier. The **red blood cells** are the **shopping bags**, and **haemoglobin** is the **super-fast cashier**.

  • Oxygen's Journey: As you put oxygen items from your trolley onto the belt (diffusion), the cashier (haemoglobin) instantly grabs them and puts them into bags (red blood cells). Because the items are immediately bagged and removed from the belt, the space in front of you stays clear, allowing you to keep putting more items on it very quickly. This keeps the flow of oxygen moving rapidly from the lungs into the blood.
  • Carbon Dioxide's Journey: At the same time, the waste paper and packaging from the previous customer (carbon dioxide) is on the belt coming towards you. You take it off to be thrown away (breathed out), making space for your shopping. The two processes happen at the same time but are independent of each other.
