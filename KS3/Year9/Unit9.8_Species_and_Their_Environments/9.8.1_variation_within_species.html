9.8.1 Variation Within Species

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe variation within a species and relate this to genetic differences between individuals.

What is a Species?

Before we can talk about differences, we need to be clear on what makes a group of organisms the same. In biology, a **species** is defined as a group of organisms that can reproduce with each other to produce **fertile offspring**. This means their babies can also grow up to have babies of their own. For example, all domestic dogs are the same species (*Canis familiaris*), which is why a Poodle and a Labrador can have puppies together.

Variation: Celebrating Differences

Even though all members of a species share key features, they are not identical clones. **Variation** is the term we use to describe the differences that exist between individuals of the same species. This variation is a natural and essential part of life, and it's what makes every individual unique.

Analogy: A Class of Students

Look around your science classroom. You are all members of the same species, *Homo sapiens*. But is everyone identical? Of course not! You have different heights, hair colours, eye colours, and facial features. This is a perfect example of variation within a species.

These three dogs are all Labradors (the same species), but they show variation in their coat colour.

The Root of Variation: Genetics

So where do all these differences come from? The primary cause of variation is **genetics**. When organisms reproduce sexually, offspring inherit a unique combination of genes from their parents.

Different versions of the same gene, called **alleles**, can produce different characteristics. For example, the gene for human eye colour has several alleles, leading to the variation we see (blue, brown, green, etc.). Unless you are an identical twin, the specific combination of genes you received from your parents is completely unique to you, and this is what causes your individual traits.

At-Home Experiment: Backyard Biodiversity Survey

You can be an ecologist and investigate variation in your own area!

You will need:

  • Four sticks or pegs
  • A piece of string (about 4 metres long)
  • A magnifying glass (optional)
  • A notebook and pencil

Procedure:

  1. Find a patch of grass in your garden or a local park.
  2. Use the sticks and string to mark out a 1m x 1m square. This is your survey area, called a quadrat.
  3. Get down low and observe carefully. In your notebook, record how many **different** types of plants you can find. You don't need to know their names; just describe them (e.g., "small round leaves," "long thin grass," "tiny yellow flower").
  4. Look for any insects or other small creatures and record the different types you see.
  5. Now, look closely at individuals of the *same* plant species (e.g., find several daisies). Note down any variation you see between them (e.g., different heights, number of petals, size of leaves).

Scientific Explanation:

The number of different species you find is a measure of **biodiversity**. The differences you see between individuals of the same species is **variation**. Both are signs of a healthy ecosystem.
