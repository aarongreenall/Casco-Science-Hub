B4.1 Biological molecules

Living organisms are incredibly complex, and their structure and functions are built upon a foundation of large, intricate molecules known as **biological molecules** (or macromolecules). These molecules are essential for all life processes, from providing energy and structural support to carrying genetic information and catalyzing reactions. This section focuses on the main types of biological molecules and how they are formed from smaller units.

Learning Objectives:

  1. List the chemical elements that make up: carbohydrates, fats and proteins.
  2. State that large molecules are made from smaller molecules, limited to: starch, glycogen and cellulose from glucose; proteins from amino acids; fats and oils from fatty acids and glycerol.

Major Types of Biological Molecules:

The four main classes of biological molecules are carbohydrates, lipids (fats and oils), proteins, and nucleic acids (DNA and RNA). We will focus on the first three.

1. Carbohydrates:

**Carbohydrates** are organic compounds that serve as the primary source of energy for living organisms and also play structural roles.

  • **Elements:** Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O).
  • **Building Blocks:** Large carbohydrate molecules like **starch**, **glycogen**, and **cellulose** are made from smaller sugar molecules, specifically **glucose**. Starch and glycogen are energy storage molecules, while cellulose provides structural support in plants.

*Figure: The molecular structure of glucose, the monomer for many carbohydrates.*

2. Fats and Oils (Lipids):

**Fats and oils** (a type of lipid) are primarily used for long-term energy storage, insulation, and protection.

  • **Elements:** Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O). (They contain less oxygen than carbohydrates).
  • **Building Blocks:** Fats and oils are made from **fatty acids** and **glycerol**.

*Figure: The basic structure of a triglyceride, a common fat molecule, formed from glycerol and three fatty acids.*

3. Proteins:

**Proteins** are highly diverse molecules with a vast array of functions, including structural support, enzyme catalysis, transport, and defense.

  • **Elements:** Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N). (Some also contain Sulfur (S)).
  • **Building Blocks:** Proteins are made from smaller repeating units called **amino acids**.

*Figure: The general structure of an amino acid, the monomer for proteins.*

Food Tests for Biological Molecules:

Specific chemical tests are used to detect the presence of these biological molecules in food samples:

  • **Iodine solution test for starch:** * **Reagent:** Iodine solution (yellow-brown). * **Positive Result:** Changes from yellow-brown to blue-black.
  • **Benedictâ€™s solution test for reducing sugars:** * **Reagent:** Benedictâ€™s solution (blue), heated in a water bath. * **Positive Result:** Changes from blue through green, yellow, orange, to brick-red precipitate (depending on sugar concentration).
  • **Biuret test for proteins:** * **Reagents:** Potassium hydroxide solution (or sodium hydroxide) and copper(II) sulfate solution (blue). * **Positive Result:** Changes from blue to purple/lilac.
  • **Ethanol emulsion test for fats and oils:** * **Reagents:** Ethanol, then add water. * **Positive Result:** Forms a cloudy white emulsion.

*Figure: Various food test results, showing color changes for different biological molecules.*
