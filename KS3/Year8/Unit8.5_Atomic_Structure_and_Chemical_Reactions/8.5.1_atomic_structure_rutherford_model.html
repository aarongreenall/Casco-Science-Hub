8.5.1 Atomic structure (Rutherford model)

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the Rutherford model of the atom.
  2. Identify the location of protons, neutrons, and electrons in this model.
  3. Explain that the atom is mostly empty space.

A New Picture of the Atom

In the early 20th century, a scientist named Ernest Rutherford conducted a famous experiment that completely changed our understanding of the atom. His results led to a new model of atomic structure, often called the **nuclear model** or the **Rutherford model**.

The key features of this model are:

  • A tiny, dense, positively charged **nucleus** at the centre of the atom.
  • The nucleus contains the **protons** (positive) and **neutrons** (neutral).
  • The negatively charged **electrons** orbit the nucleus at a large distance.
  • Because the electrons are so far from the tiny nucleus, the model shows that the atom is **mostly empty space**.

Analogy: The Solar System Model

A good way to picture the Rutherford model is to think of our solar system. The **Sun** is like the massive **nucleus** at the centre, containing almost all of the mass. The **planets** are like the tiny **electrons** orbiting the nucleus. The vast distances between the planets and the Sun help to illustrate the key idea that the atom is mostly empty space.

In-School Activity: Drawing Atoms

You can use the periodic table to figure out the number of subatomic particles in an atom and then draw its Rutherford model.

  • The **Atomic Number** tells you the number of **protons** (and also the number of **electrons** in a neutral atom).
  • The **Mass Number** tells you the total number of **protons + neutrons**.
  • To find the number of neutrons, you calculate: Mass Number - Atomic Number.
Your Task:

Use a periodic table to find the information for the element **Beryllium (Be)**. Calculate the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons, and then draw a Rutherford model diagram for a Beryllium atom.
