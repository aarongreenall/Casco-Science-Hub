8.1.1 Purity of gases

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Understand the scientific definition of a pure substance.
  2. Distinguish between a pure substance and a mixture.
  3. Explain that a pure substance can be an element or a compound.

What Does "Pure" Mean in Science?

In everyday language, we might talk about "pure air" or "pure orange juice." But in science, the word **pure** has a very specific meaning. A **pure substance** is something that is made up of only **one type of particle**. This particle can be a single atom or a single type of molecule.

A **mixture**, on the other hand, contains two or more different substances that are not chemically joined together. This means that in science, air is a mixture, not a pure substance, because it contains lots of different types of gas particles (nitrogen, oxygen, etc.) all jumbled together.

Analogy: A Bag of Sweets

Imagine you have a bag of sweets. If the bag contains **only red sweets**, you have a **pure substance**. If the bag contains a jumble of red, blue, and yellow sweets, you have a **mixture**.

Crucially, a pure substance can be either an element or a compound. For example, a cylinder of pure oxygen gas contains only oxygen molecules ($O_2$). A cylinder of pure carbon dioxide gas contains only carbon dioxide molecules ($CO_2$). Even though a carbon dioxide molecule is made of two different elements (carbon and oxygen), all the particles in the cylinder are identical $CO_2$ molecules, so it is a pure substance.

Pure Element (e.g., Oxygen)

All particles are identical atoms or molecules of one element.

Pure Compound (e.g., Carbon Dioxide)

All particles are identical molecules made of two or more types of atom bonded together.

Mixture (e.g., Air)

Contains different types of particles that are not bonded together.

Explanation: Pure Substances and Mixtures

In-School Activity: Pure or Mixture?

Discuss in your groups: we often see products advertised as "100% pure."

  1. Find some examples of products that claim to be "pure" (e.g., pure orange juice, pure spring water, pure cotton).
  2. For each example, decide if it is a pure substance or a mixture according to the scientific definition.
  3. Be ready to explain your reasoning to the class. For example, is "pure orange juice" a pure substance, or is it a mixture of water, sugar, citric acid, and other substances?
