8.6.2 Human movement (joints and muscles)

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Identify ball-and-socket and hinge joints.
  2. Explain that muscles can only pull (contract) and cannot push.
  3. Describe how antagonistic muscle pairs work together to move bones at a joint.

Joints and Muscles

Your skeleton provides the framework for your body, but it can't move on its own. Movement happens at **joints**, which are the places where two or more bones meet. The movement itself is caused by **muscles**, which are attached to the bones by strong cords called **tendons**.

There are different types of joints that allow for different kinds of movement. Two of the most important are:

  • Hinge Joints: These allow movement in only one direction, like a door hinge. Your knee and elbow are hinge joints.
  • Ball-and-Socket Joints: These allow movement in many directions. The round end of one bone (the "ball") fits into a cup-shaped socket on another bone. Your shoulder and hip are ball-and-socket joints.

Antagonistic Pairs: The Pulling Team

A crucial fact about muscles is that they can only **pull** by contracting (getting shorter). They cannot actively push to extend. So how do you move your arm back and forth? The answer is that muscles work in teams called **antagonistic pairs**.

Analogy: Levers and Ropes

Think of your bones as **levers** and your muscles as **ropes**. You can only **pull** on a rope to move a lever; you can't push the lever back with the rope. To move the lever back to its original position, you need a second rope attached to the other side to pull it in the opposite direction. This is exactly how antagonistic muscle pairs work.

A classic example is the biceps and triceps in your upper arm:

To bend your arm: Your **biceps** muscle **contracts** (pulls), while your **triceps** muscle **relaxes**.

To straighten your arm: Your **triceps** muscle **contracts** (pulls), while your **biceps** muscle **relaxes**.

They work together, with one pulling while the other relaxes, to move your arm in both directions.

In-School Experiment: Building a Model Arm

You can build a simple model to see how antagonistic muscle pairs work.

Equipment:
  • Two strips of strong cardboard
  • A split-pin paper fastener
  • Two strong elastic bands
  • A hole punch
Method:
  1. Overlap the ends of the two cardboard strips (the "bones") and join them with the split pin to create a hinge joint (the "elbow").
  2. Punch two holes on one side of the joint and two holes on the other side, as shown in your teacher's diagram.
  3. Hook one elastic band (the "biceps") across the inside of the joint.
  4. Hook the second elastic band (the "triceps") across the outside of the joint.
  5. Now, "contract" your model biceps by pulling the elastic band. What happens to the arm? What happens to the triceps elastic band?
  6. Now, "contract" your model triceps. Observe what happens to the arm and the biceps. This model clearly shows how one muscle must contract while the other relaxes to cause movement.
