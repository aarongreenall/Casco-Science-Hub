9.4.2 Group Trends: Group 1 Example

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Understand that the groups within the Periodic Table have trends in physical and chemical properties, using Group 1 as an example.

What is a Trend? The Alkali Metals

Because the Periodic Table is so well organised, the properties of elements often change in a predictable way as you move around it. A **trend** is a recognisable pattern of change in properties. By studying one group, we can see these trends in action.

Our case study is **Group 1**, also known as the **Alkali Metals**. This group includes Lithium (Li), Sodium (Na), Potassium (K), Rubidium (Rb), and others. As we learned, everything in Group 1 has **one electron in its outer shell**. This single outer electron dominates their chemical behaviour and is the reason they all react in similar ways.

The Chemical Trend: Reactivity with Water

The most famous chemical property of the alkali metals is their vigorous reaction with water. The key trend is: **Reactivity increases as you go down Group 1.**

Lithium (Li)

Reacts steadily, fizzing and moving around on the water's surface as it produces hydrogen gas.

Sodium (Na)

Reacts much more vigorously. It melts into a silver ball, darts rapidly across the surface, and fizzes intensely.

Potassium (K)

Reacts so quickly that the heat produced ignites the hydrogen gas, causing it to burn with a distinctive lilac flame.

Why does reactivity increase?

The reason lies in their atomic structure. To react, a Group 1 metal must lose its single outer electron. As you go down the group, each element has an extra electron shell. This means the outer electron is further away from the positive nucleus and is "shielded" by more inner shells. The attraction from the nucleus is weaker, so the electron is lost much more easily, making the element more reactive.

Analogy: A Whisper in a Crowded Room

Imagine the nucleus's pull on its outer electron is like a person whispering. For **Lithium**, the electron is close by in a quiet room; it hears the whisper clearly and is held tightly. For **Potassium**, the electron is much further away, across a noisy, crowded room (the inner electron shells are the "crowd"). The nucleus's whisper is harder to hear, so the electron is not held as tightly and can escape (be lost) much more easily.

Physical Trends in Group 1

The physical properties of the alkali metals also show clear trends as you go down the group.

Element Melting Point (°C) Boiling Point (°C) Density (g/cm³)
Lithium (Li) 181 1342 0.53
Sodium (Na) 98 883 0.97
Potassium (K) 63 760 0.86
Rubidium (Rb) 39 688 1.53

You can see two clear trends here:

  • The **melting and boiling points decrease** as you go down the group. This is because the atoms get larger, and the forces holding them together (metallic bonds) get weaker.
  • The **density generally increases** as you go down the group. The atoms are getting heavier faster than they are getting bigger.

Safety Warning: Teacher Demonstration

The reactions of alkali metals with water are very dangerous. They produce a corrosive alkali solution and flammable hydrogen gas. These reactions must **only** be performed by a qualified teacher using the correct safety equipment, including a safety screen, goggles, and tongs. **Never attempt this experiment yourself.**
