B16.1 Chromosomes and genes

The instructions for building and operating a living organism are encoded within its genetic material. In eukaryotic cells, this genetic information is organized into structures called **chromosomes**, which are made up of long strands of DNA. Segments of DNA that carry specific instructions for traits are known as **genes**. Understanding chromosomes and genes is fundamental to the study of inheritance and genetics.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe a chromosome as a thread-like structure of DNA, carrying genetic information in the form of genes.
  2. Describe a gene as a length of DNA that is a unit of heredity and codes for a specific protein.
  3. State that genes are inherited, and that they control the development of characteristics.
  4. State that the position of a gene on a chromosome is its locus.
  5. State that alleles are different forms of the same gene.
  6. Define the terms: genotype as the genetic make-up of an organism in terms of the alleles present; phenotype as the observable features of an organism.
  7. Define the terms: homozygous as having two identical alleles of a particular gene; heterozygous as having two different alleles of a particular gene.
  8. Define the terms: dominant as an allele that is expressed if it is present; recessive as an allele that is only expressed when there is no dominant allele of the gene present.

Chromosomes and Genes: The Blueprint of Life:

In the nucleus of eukaryotic cells, genetic information is organized into **chromosomes**. A **chromosome** is a thread-like structure made of DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) tightly coiled around proteins. These chromosomes carry genetic information in the form of **genes**.

A **gene** is a specific length of DNA that acts as a unit of heredity. Each gene carries the instructions (codes) for a specific protein. These proteins then determine the development of particular characteristics or traits in an organism (e.g., eye color, blood type). Genes are inherited from parents to offspring.

Alleles, Locus, Genotype, and Phenotype:

To understand how traits are passed down, we use specific genetic terminology:

  • **Locus (plural: loci):** The specific, fixed position of a gene on a chromosome.
  • **Alleles:** Different forms or versions of the same gene. For example, a gene for eye color might have an allele for blue eyes and an allele for brown eyes.
  • **Genotype:** The **genetic make-up** of an organism in terms of the alleles present for a particular gene. It describes the combination of alleles an individual has (e.g., BB, Bb, bb).
  • **Phenotype:** The **observable features** or characteristics of an organism. This is the physical expression of the genotype (e.g., brown eyes, tall height). The phenotype is influenced by both the genotype and environmental factors.

Homozygous, Heterozygous, Dominant, and Recessive:

These terms describe the combination and expression of alleles:

  • **Homozygous:** An individual having **two identical alleles** of a particular gene (e.g., two dominant alleles 'AA' or two recessive alleles 'aa'). Also known as 'pure-breeding'.
  • **Heterozygous:** An individual having **two different alleles** of a particular gene (e.g., one dominant and one recessive allele 'Aa'). Also known as 'hybrid'.
  • **Dominant Allele:** An allele that is **expressed if it is present** in the genotype. It masks the effect of a recessive allele. Represented by a capital letter (e.g., 'A' for tall).
  • **Recessive Allele:** An allele that is **only expressed when there is no dominant allele** of the gene present (i.e., when the individual is homozygous recessive, e.g., 'aa'). Represented by a lowercase letter (e.g., 'a' for short).
