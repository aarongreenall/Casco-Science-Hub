18.5 Electric potential

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define electric potential at a point as the work done per unit positive charge in bringing a small test charge from infinity to the point.
  2. Recall and use the fact that the electric field at a point is equal to the negative of potential gradient at that point.
  3. Use $V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r}$ for the electric potential in the field due to a point charge.
  4. Understand how the concept of electric potential leads to the electric potential energy of two point charges and use $E_P = \frac{Qq}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r}$.

LO1: Defining Electric Potential

The concept of **electric potential ($V$)** is the electrical analogue of gravitational potential. It describes the energy aspect of an electric field, independent of the charge placed in it.

The electric potential at a point is the work done per unit positive charge in bringing a small test charge from infinity to that point.

The reference point for zero potential is taken to be at **infinity**. The sign of the potential depends on the sign of the source charge creating the field:

  • To bring a positive test charge towards a **positive source charge**, you must do work against the repulsive force. This increases the potential energy, so the potential is **positive**.
  • To bring a positive test charge towards a **negative source charge**, the attractive field does work on the charge. This decreases the potential energy, so the potential is **negative**.

The SI unit for electric potential is the joule per coulomb (J C⁻¹), which is defined as the **volt (V)**.

LO2: Field Strength and Potential Gradient

There is a fundamental relationship between electric field strength ($E$) and electric potential ($V$). The electric field strength is equal to the negative of the **potential gradient**.

$$ E = -\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta r} $$

The **potential gradient** ($\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta r}$) is the rate of change of potential with respect to distance. The negative sign is crucial: it signifies that the electric field lines always point in the direction of **decreasing electric potential**. This is analogous to a gravitational field, where objects naturally move "downhill" to a region of lower gravitational potential.

LO3: Electric Potential due to a Point Charge

The electric potential ($V$) at a distance $r$ from the centre of a point charge $Q$ is given by the formula:

$$ V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} $$

Note the key differences from the formula for electric field strength: potential is proportional to $1/r$, not $1/r^2$, and it is a scalar quantity.

LO4: Electric Potential Energy ($E_P$)

The relationship between electric potential ($V$) and electric potential energy ($E_P$) is analogous to the one between gravitational potential and GPE. Since potential is the energy *per unit charge*, the total potential energy of a charge $q$ placed at that point is:

$$ E_P = qV $$

By substituting the formula for $V$ due to a point charge $Q$, we get the equation for the electric potential energy of two point charges, $Q$ and $q$:

$$ E_P = q \left( \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} \right) = \frac{Qq}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} $$

Worked Example: Potential and Potential Energy

An alpha particle ($q = +2e$) is at a distance of $1.0 \times 10^{-12}$ m from a gold nucleus ($Q = +79e$). Calculate (a) the electric potential at this point due to the gold nucleus, and (b) the electric potential energy of the alpha particle.

(a) Calculate the electric potential ($\phi$):

$$ V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} = \frac{(79 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-19})}{4\pi(8.85 \times 10^{-12})(1.0 \times 10^{-12})} $$

$$ V = \frac{1.264 \times 10^{-17}}{1.112 \times 10^{-22}} = 113669 \text{ V} \approx 1.1 \times 10^5 \text{ V} $$

(b) Calculate the electric potential energy ($E_P$):

$$ E_P = qV = (2 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) \times (1.137 \times 10^5 \text{ V}) $$

$$ E_P = 3.64 \times 10^{-14} \text{ J} $$

Alternatively, using the full formula:

$$ E_P = \frac{Qq}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} = \frac{(79 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-19})(2 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-19})}{4\pi(8.85 \times 10^{-12})(1.0 \times 10^{-12})} $$

$$ E_P = 3.64 \times 10^{-14} \text{ J} $$
