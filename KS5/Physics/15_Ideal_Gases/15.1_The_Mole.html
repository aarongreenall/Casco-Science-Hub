15.1 The mole

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that amount of substance is an SI base quantity with the base unit mol.
  2. Use molar quantities where one mole of any substance is the amount containing a number of particles of that substance equal to the Avogadro constant $N_A$.

LO1: Amount of Substance and the Mole

In physics and chemistry, it is often necessary to count the number of particles (atoms, molecules, ions, etc.) in a substance. Since these numbers are astronomically large, it is impractical to count them individually. Instead, we use a specific SI base quantity called the **amount of substance**.

The **amount of substance ($n$)** is the seventh SI base quantity. Its base unit is the **mole (mol)**.

The mole is simply a unit for a specific, very large number, in the same way that a "dozen" is a unit for the number 12. It provides a convenient way to connect the microscopic world of atoms and molecules to the macroscopic world of grams and litres that we can measure in a lab.

LO2: The Avogadro Constant and Molar Quantities

The Definition of the Mole

The specific number of particles in one mole is given by the **Avogadro constant ($N_A$)**. Historically, this number was defined based on a tangible, measurable substance.

One mole is the amount of substance that contains as many elementary entities as there are atoms in exactly 0.012 kilograms (or 12 grams) of the isotope carbon-12.

Why Carbon-12? The Problem with Hydrogen

Logically, it might seem that the simplest atom, hydrogen, would be the best standard. However, a scale based on hydrogen was found to be inaccurate. This is due to **nuclear binding energy**. When protons and neutrons fuse to form a nucleus, a tiny amount of their mass is converted into the energy that holds the nucleus together ($E=mc^2$). This "missing mass" is called the **mass defect**.

The mass defect is different for every element. This means the mass of a Carbon-12 nucleus is slightly *less* than the mass of 6 protons and 6 neutrons combined. As you move to heavier elements, this deviation becomes more significant, making a scale based on hydrogen increasingly inaccurate. Carbon-12 was chosen as a practical and stable standard that provided better consistency across the periodic table.

The number of atoms in 12 g of carbon-12 was experimentally determined to be:

$$ N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1} $$

This leads to the fundamental relationship between the total number of particles ($N$) in a sample and the amount of substance in moles ($n$):

$$ N = n \times N_A $$

Worked Example 1: Particles to Moles

A sample of water contains $3.01 \times 10^{24}$ molecules. How many moles of water are in the sample?

$$ n = \frac{N}{N_A} = \frac{3.01 \times 10^{24}}{6.02 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1}} = 5.0 \text{ mol} $$

Worked Example 2: Moles to Particles

A balloon contains 0.25 moles of helium gas. How many helium atoms are in the balloon?

$$ N = n N_A = (0.25 \text{ mol}) \times (6.02 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1}) = 1.5 \times 10^{23} \text{ atoms} $$
