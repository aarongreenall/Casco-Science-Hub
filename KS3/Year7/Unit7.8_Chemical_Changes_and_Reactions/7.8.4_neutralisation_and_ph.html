7.8.4 Neutralisation and pH

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define neutralisation as the reaction between an acid and an alkali.
  2. State the products of a neutralisation reaction.
  3. Describe how the pH changes during neutralisation.

What is Neutralisation?

When you mix an acid with an alkali, they react with each other. This type of chemical reaction is called **neutralisation**. The acid and the alkali cancel each other out, and the resulting solution becomes less acidic and less alkaline—it moves closer to the middle of the pH scale.

A **neutralisation reaction** is the reaction between an acid and an alkali to produce a salt and water.

$$ \text{Acid} + \text{Alkali} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Water} $$

Analogy: The Chemical Tug-of-War

Remember our "Chemical Tug-of-War"? An acid is a team pulling strongly in one direction, and an alkali is a team pulling strongly in the opposite direction. A neutralisation reaction is what happens when these two teams have a match. They pull against each other, and if you add just the right amount of each, their forces cancel out completely, resulting in a perfect standstill—a balanced and **neutral** state (pH 7).
