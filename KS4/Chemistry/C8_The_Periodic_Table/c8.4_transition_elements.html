C8.4 Transition elements

The **transition elements** are a block of metallic elements located in the middle of the Periodic Table (Groups 3-12). They exhibit a range of characteristic properties that distinguish them from the Group I and Group II metals, making them highly versatile and important in various applications, including catalysis and the formation of colored compounds.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the transition elements as metals that: have high densities; have high melting points; form coloured compounds; often act as catalysts as elements and in compounds.

Characteristic Properties of Transition Elements:

Transition elements are all metals, and they share some general metallic properties, but they also possess unique characteristics:

  • **High Densities:** Transition metals are generally much denser than the Group I and Group II metals.
  • **High Melting Points:** They typically have high melting points (and boiling points), indicating strong metallic bonding. (Mercury is a notable exception, being liquid at room temperature).
  • **Form Coloured Compounds:** A distinctive feature of transition elements is their ability to form compounds that are often brightly colored, both in solid form and in solution. This contrasts sharply with the white or colorless compounds typically formed by Group I and Group II metals.
  • **Act as Catalysts:** Many transition elements, both as pure elements and in their compounds, often act as **catalysts**. They increase the rate of chemical reactions without being consumed in the process. This property makes them invaluable in industrial processes (e.g., iron in the Haber process, nickel in hydrogenation).
