8.6.4 Bioaccumulation

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define bioaccumulation as the build-up of a toxic substance in an organism.
  2. Explain how the concentration of a toxin can increase up a food chain (biomagnification).
  3. Describe the impact of bioaccumulation on an ecosystem.

What is Bioaccumulation?

Some toxic (poisonous) substances, like pesticides or heavy metals, are not easily broken down by living organisms. If an organism takes in one of these substances, it can get stored in its tissues. **Bioaccumulation** is the gradual build-up of a toxic substance in a single organism over its lifetime.

This becomes a much bigger problem when we look at a whole food chain. As one organism eats another, the toxin is passed along. Because a consumer needs to eat many organisms from the level below it, the toxin becomes more and more concentrated at each step up the food chain. This process is called **biomagnification**.

Analogy: The Un-washable Ink Stain

Imagine a field of grass is sprayed with a special, permanent ink (the toxin). Each blade of grass has one tiny drop of ink on it. A grasshopper eats 10 blades of grass, so it now has 10 drops of ink inside it. A frog then eats 5 of these grasshoppers, so it now has 50 drops of ink inside it. Finally, a hawk (the apex predator) eats 2 of these frogs. The hawk now has 100 drops of ink inside it. The ink, which was only a tiny drop on each plant, has become highly concentrated and potentially dangerous at the top of the food chain.

This means that the organisms at the top of the food chain, the **apex predators**, are the most affected by these toxins, even if the initial amount in the environment was very small.

In-School Activity: Modelling Bioaccumulation

You can model how a toxin becomes more concentrated up a food chain using counters.

Equipment:
  • A large number of small counters of one colour (e.g., green, representing plants)
  • A smaller number of counters of a different colour (e.g., red, representing a toxin)
  • Small cups or pots
Method:
  1. Imagine a field of 20 plants. Place 20 green counters on the table. Add one red "toxin" counter to each green "plant" counter.
  2. Now, imagine 4 rabbits eat these plants. Each rabbit eats 5 plants. Give 4 students a cup. They must "eat" 5 plants each, collecting both the green and the red counters. How many red counters does each rabbit now have?
  3. Finally, imagine 2 foxes eat the rabbits. Each fox eats 2 rabbits. Give 2 students a cup. They must "eat" 2 rabbits, collecting all the counters from those rabbits' cups.
  4. Count the number of red "toxin" counters in each fox. You should see that the concentration of the toxin is much higher at the top of the food chain.
