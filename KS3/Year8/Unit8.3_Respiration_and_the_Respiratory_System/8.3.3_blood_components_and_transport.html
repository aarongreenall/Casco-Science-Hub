8.3.3 Blood components and transport

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Identify the four main components of blood.
  2. Describe the function of plasma, red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.
  3. Explain how red blood cells are adapted to their function.

The River of Life

**Blood** is the body's essential transport system. It is a specialised tissue that is pumped around the body by the heart, delivering vital substances like oxygen and nutrients to our cells and carrying away waste products like carbon dioxide. Blood is made of four main components.

Analogy: A Busy River

Think of your blood as a busy river flowing through your body. The **plasma** is the **water** of the river, carrying everything along. The **red blood cells** are like millions of tiny **delivery boats**, designed to carry oxygen. The **white blood cells** are the **police boats**, patrolling for invaders. And the **platelets** are the **emergency repair crews**, ready to plug any leaks.

The Four Components of Blood

Component Description Function
Plasma A straw-coloured liquid that makes up over half of the blood's volume. Transports everything: blood cells, nutrients (like glucose), carbon dioxide, hormones, and waste products.
Red Blood Cells Tiny, biconcave discs that contain a red protein called haemoglobin. They have no nucleus. To transport oxygen from the lungs to the body's cells.
White Blood Cells Larger than red blood cells and have a nucleus. There are many different types. Part of the immune system. They fight off invading pathogens like bacteria and viruses.
Platelets Small fragments of cells. To help the blood clot at the site of a wound to prevent blood loss and stop pathogens from getting in.
