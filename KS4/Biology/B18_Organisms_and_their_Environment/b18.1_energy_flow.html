B18.1 Energy flow

In an ecosystem, energy flows from one trophic level to the next, starting with producers and moving through various consumers. This **energy flow** is fundamental to the survival of all organisms and the functioning of ecosystems. Understanding how energy is transferred and how efficiently it moves through an ecosystem is crucial for comprehending ecological dynamics.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define an ecosystem as a unit containing all of the living organisms (biotic factors) and non-living components (abiotic factors) interacting in a specific area.
  2. State that the Sun is the principal source of energy for most ecosystems.
  3. Describe the flow of energy from the Sun in terms of the following sequence: producers $\rightarrow$ consumers (primary, secondary and tertiary) $\rightarrow$ decomposers.
  4. Describe how energy is transferred between trophic levels.
  5. Explain why the transfer of energy from one trophic level to another is inefficient.
  6. Explain that the biomass at each trophic level decreases at successively higher trophic levels.
  7. Interpret pyramids of biomass.

Ecosystems and Energy Source:

An **ecosystem** is a complex unit that includes all the **living organisms (biotic factors)** and **non-living components (abiotic factors)** interacting in a specific area. Biotic factors include plants, animals, fungi, and bacteria. Abiotic factors include sunlight, water, soil, temperature, and air.

For most ecosystems on Earth, the **Sun is the principal source of energy**. Producers, primarily plants and algae, capture this light energy through photosynthesis and convert it into chemical energy, forming the base of the food chain.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the biotic and abiotic components of an ecosystem.*

Flow of Energy Through Trophic Levels:

Energy flows through an ecosystem in a linear sequence, moving from one feeding level (trophic level) to the next:

`Producers -> Consumers (Primary, Secondary, Tertiary) -> Decomposers`

  • **Producers:** Organisms (e.g., plants, algae) that produce their own food, usually through photosynthesis, converting light energy into chemical energy.
  • **Primary Consumers (Herbivores):** Feed on producers.
  • **Secondary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):** Feed on primary consumers.
  • **Tertiary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):** Feed on secondary consumers.
  • **Decomposers:** Organisms (e.g., bacteria, fungi) that break down dead organic matter from all trophic levels, returning nutrients to the ecosystem.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the flow of energy through different trophic levels in an ecosystem.*

Inefficient Energy Transfer and Pyramids of Biomass:

The transfer of energy from one trophic level to another is highly **inefficient**. On average, only about **10% of the energy** from one trophic level is transferred to the next. The remaining 90% is lost at each transfer.

Energy is lost due to:

  • **Respiration:** Organisms use a large proportion of the energy they consume for their own metabolic processes (e.g., movement, growth, maintaining body temperature), which releases energy as heat.
  • **Incomplete consumption:** Not all parts of an organism are eaten.
  • **Waste products:** Energy is lost in faeces (undigested food) and urine.

Because of this inefficient energy transfer, the **biomass** (total mass of living organisms) at each trophic level **decreases at successively higher trophic levels**. This can be represented by **pyramids of biomass**, which are typically pyramid-shaped, with a large base of producers and progressively smaller levels of consumers.
