9.5.3 Gamete Fusion and Fetal Development

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the fusion of gametes to produce a fertilised egg with a new combination of DNA.
  2. Discuss how fetal development is affected by the health of the mother, including the effect of diet, smoking and drugs.

The Start of a New Life: Gametes and Fertilisation

Sexual reproduction in humans begins with special reproductive cells called **gametes**. The male gamete is the **sperm**, and the female gamete is the **egg** (or ovum). These cells are unique because they contain only half the number of chromosomes of a normal body cell. While a skin cell or muscle cell has 46 chromosomes, a gamete has just 23.

**Fertilisation** is the moment when the nucleus of a sperm cell fuses with the nucleus of an egg cell. This fusion combines their genetic information.

Analogy: Combining Two Half-Cookbooks

Think of the 46 chromosomes in a normal cell as a complete, two-volume cookbook of instructions for building a person. A gamete (sperm or egg) is like having only one of those volumes, containing just half the recipes (23 chromosomes). To create a new, complete cookbook for a new person, you must combine the two half-booksâ€”one from the mother (the egg) and one from the father (the sperm).

The result of this fusion is a single new cell called a **fertilised egg**, or **zygote**. It now has a full set of 46 chromosomes (23 from each parent) and a completely unique combination of DNA. This single cell contains all the instructions needed to grow into a new human being.

From Fertilised Egg to Fetus: A Nine-Month Journey

After fertilisation, the zygote begins to divide rapidly, becoming an embryo and then a **fetus**. This development takes place over about nine months inside the mother's **uterus** (womb). The fetus is entirely dependent on the mother for survival.

The **placenta** is a special organ that develops in the uterus during pregnancy. It forms a vital link between the mother and the fetus. The **umbilical cord** connects the fetus to the placenta. Nutrients and oxygen pass from the mother's blood, across the placenta, and to the fetus. Waste products, like carbon dioxide, pass from the fetus back to the mother's blood to be removed.

A Healthy Start: How a Mother's Health Affects the Fetus

Because the fetus gets everything it needs from the mother's blood via the placenta, the mother's health and lifestyle choices have a direct and profound impact on the baby's development.

Diet

A balanced diet rich in nutrients is crucial. For example, calcium is needed for the fetus's bones and teeth to develop, and iron is needed to make healthy red blood cells.

Smoking

Harmful chemicals in cigarette smoke, like nicotine and carbon monoxide, can cross the placenta. They can restrict the amount of oxygen the fetus receives, which can lead to a low birth weight and other health problems.

Drugs (including Alcohol)

Many drugs, both legal and illegal, can pass through the placenta and harm the fetus. Alcohol consumption during pregnancy can cause serious, lifelong physical and mental problems known as Fetal Alcohol Syndrome. It is safest to avoid alcohol completely during pregnancy.

In-School Activity: Discussion Scenarios

In small groups, discuss the following scenarios. What are the potential risks to the developing fetus in each case? Be ready to share your ideas with the class.

  • A pregnant woman's diet consists mainly of fast food, crisps, and sugary drinks.
  • A pregnant woman continues to smoke five cigarettes a day because she finds it helps her relax.
  • A pregnant woman has a glass of wine with her dinner a few times a week.
