C6.2 Rate of reaction

The **rate of reaction** describes how quickly reactants are consumed and products are formed in a chemical reaction. Understanding and controlling the rate of reaction is crucial in many industrial processes, biological systems, and everyday phenomena. This section explores the factors that influence reaction rates and how these rates can be investigated experimentally.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the effect on the rate of reaction of: changing the concentration of solutions; changing the pressure of gases; changing the surface area of solids; changing the temperature; adding or removing a catalyst.
  2. State that a catalyst increases the rate of a reaction and is unchanged at the end of a reaction.
  3. State that a catalyst decreases the activation energy, $E_a$, of a reaction.
  4. Describe collision theory in terms of: number of particles per unit volume; frequency of collisions between particles; kinetic energy of particles; activation energy, $E_a$.
  5. Describe practical methods for investigating the rate of a reaction including change in mass of a reactant or product and the formation of a gas.
  6. Interpret data, including graphs, from rate of reaction experiments.
  7. Explain the effect on the rate of reaction of: changing the concentration of solutions; changing the pressure of gases; changing the surface area of solids; changing the temperature; adding or removing a catalyst, using collision theory.

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate:

Several factors can influence how quickly a chemical reaction proceeds:

  • **Concentration of Solutions:** Increasing the concentration of reactants in a solution generally increases the reaction rate.
  • **Pressure of Gases:** Increasing the pressure of gaseous reactants generally increases the reaction rate.
  • **Surface Area of Solids:** Increasing the surface area of solid reactants (e.g., by crushing them into a powder) increases the reaction rate.
  • **Temperature:** Increasing the temperature of the reactants generally increases the reaction rate.
  • **Catalysts:** A **catalyst** is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being chemically changed or consumed in the process. Catalysts work by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower **activation energy ($E_a$)**.

Collision Theory:

The **collision theory** explains how these factors affect reaction rates at a particle level:

  • For a reaction to occur, reactant particles must **collide** with each other.
  • These collisions must have energy equal to or greater than the **activation energy ($E_a$)** (the minimum energy required for a reaction to occur).
  • The particles must also collide with the **correct orientation**.

Factors affecting reaction rate can be explained using collision theory:

  • **Concentration/Pressure:** Increasing concentration (for solutions) or pressure (for gases) means there are more reactant particles per unit volume. This leads to a higher **frequency of collisions** between particles, thus increasing the reaction rate.
  • **Surface Area:** Increasing the surface area of a solid reactant exposes more particles to collisions, increasing the frequency of effective collisions and thus the reaction rate.
  • **Temperature:** Increasing temperature increases the **kinetic energy** of particles. This leads to two effects: * Particles move faster, increasing the **frequency of collisions**. * A larger proportion of collisions will have energy equal to or greater than the **activation energy**, increasing the frequency of *effective* collisions. Both effects increase the reaction rate.
  • **Catalysts:** Catalysts work by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a **lower activation energy ($E_a$)**. This means more colliding particles will have sufficient energy to react, increasing the frequency of effective collisions and thus the reaction rate, without changing the temperature or concentration.

Investigating Reaction Rates:

The rate of a reaction can be investigated experimentally by monitoring a measurable change over time. Common practical methods include:

  • **Change in mass of a reactant or product:** If a gas is produced or consumed, the change in mass of the reaction mixture can be measured over time using a balance.
  • **Formation of a gas:** The volume of gas produced can be measured using a gas syringe or by collecting it over water.
  • **Change in turbidity (cloudiness):** For reactions that produce a precipitate, the time taken for a cross drawn on paper beneath the reaction flask to disappear can be measured (e.g., "disappearing cross" experiment).
  • **Change in pH or conductivity.**

Data collected from these experiments can be plotted on graphs (e.g., volume of gas vs. time, mass change vs. time). The **gradient (slope)** of these graphs indicates the rate of reaction at any given point. A steeper gradient means a faster reaction rate.
