B17.1 Variation

**Variation** refers to the differences that exist between individuals within a species. These differences can be seen in physical characteristics (like height or eye color) or in biochemical traits. Understanding the sources of variation is crucial for comprehending how populations change over time through evolution and how genetic traits are passed down.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe variation as differences between individuals of the same species.
  2. State that variation can be continuous or discontinuous.
  3. Describe examples of continuous variation, limited to: height, mass.
  4. Describe examples of discontinuous variation, limited to: sex, ABO blood groups.
  5. State that continuous variation is influenced by genes and the environment.
  6. State that discontinuous variation is influenced by genes only.
  7. Describe mutation as a change in the nucleotide sequence of a gene or in the structure of a chromosome.
  8. State that mutations are a source of variation and can be spontaneous.
  9. State that ionising radiation and some chemicals increase the rate of mutation.

Types of Variation: Continuous and Discontinuous:

Variation among individuals of the same species can be categorized into two types:

  • **Continuous Variation:** * **Description:** Variation where there is a complete range of measurements from one extreme to the other, with no distinct categories. * **Influence:** Influenced by **both genes and the environment**. * **Examples:** Human height, human mass (weight), leaf length, skin color. These traits often show a normal distribution (bell curve) when plotted on a graph.
  • **Discontinuous Variation:** * **Description:** Variation where there are distinct categories, with no intermediate values. * **Influence:** Influenced by **genes only** (not significantly by the environment). * **Examples:** Human sex (male or female), ABO blood groups (A, B, AB, O), presence or absence of a genetic disease, eye color (though more complex, often simplified as discontinuous).

Mutation: A Source of Variation:

**Mutation** is defined as a change in the nucleotide sequence of a gene or in the structure of a chromosome. Mutations are a primary **source of variation** within a species. They introduce new alleles into a population, which can then be passed on to offspring.

Mutations can be **spontaneous**, occurring randomly during DNA replication or cell division. The rate of mutation can also be increased by certain factors:

  • **Ionising radiation:** High-energy radiation (e.g., X-rays, gamma rays, UV radiation) can damage DNA, leading to mutations.
  • **Some chemicals (mutagens):** Certain chemicals can interact with DNA and cause changes in its sequence.

While many mutations are neutral or harmful, some can be beneficial, providing raw material for natural selection and evolution.
