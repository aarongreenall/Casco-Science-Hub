9.2.3 Water and Mineral Transport in Plants

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the pathway of water and mineral salts from the roots to the leaves in flowering plants, including absorption in root hair cells, transport through xylem and transpiration from the surface of leaves.

The Plant's Plumbing System

A plant needs a constant supply of water and dissolved minerals from the soil to survive. But how does it get these substances from the ground all the way up to its highest leaves? Plants have a highly efficient, built-in plumbing system to do just this. The journey involves three key stages: **Absorption**, **Transport**, and **Transpiration**.

Stage 1: Absorption at the Roots

The journey begins underground. A plant's roots are covered in millions of microscopic **root hair cells**. Each of these cells has a long, thin extension that reaches out into the soil. This shape gives the root system a massive surface area, allowing it to absorb water and dissolved mineral salts from the soil very effectively through a process called **osmosis**.

Stage 2: Transport through the Xylem

Once inside the root, the water and minerals enter a network of tiny, hollow tubes called the **xylem**. These xylem vessels are like the plant's pipes. They join together to form a continuous pipeline that runs from the roots, up through the stem, and into every leaf.

Analogy: A Drinking Straw

The movement of water up the xylem is like sipping a drink through a straw. As water molecules evaporate from the leaves at the top, they pull the next water molecules up behind them. Because water molecules are slightly 'sticky' and attract each other, this creates a continuous, unbroken column of water moving up the plant. This flow is called the **transpiration stream**.

Stage 3: Transpiration from the Leaves

The final stage of the journey is **transpiration**. This is the process of water evaporating from the surface of the leaves and escaping into the atmosphere as water vapour. This happens through tiny pores on the underside of the leaf called **stomata**. While the plant needs to open its stomata to get carbon dioxide for photosynthesis, it means some water is inevitably lost. This water loss is the engine that drives the whole system, creating the pulling force that draws the transpiration stream up from the roots.

At-Home Experiment: Bag a Leaf

You can prove that plants release water vapour with this easy experiment.

You will need:

  • A living, healthy plant (outdoors or a houseplant)
  • A small, clear plastic bag
  • A piece of string or a twist tie

Procedure:

  1. Choose a healthy, leafy branch on your plant.
  2. Carefully place the plastic bag over the branch, enclosing a few leaves.
  3. Use the string or twist tie to secure the opening of the bag loosely around the stem. Make sure it's sealed but not so tight that it damages the plant.
  4. Leave the bag in place for several hours or overnight.
  5. Come back and observe the inside of the bag. What do you see?

Scientific Explanation:

You should see tiny droplets of liquid water have collected on the inside of the bag. This is proof of transpiration! The invisible water vapour released by the leaf has been trapped by the bag and has condensed back into visible liquid water.
