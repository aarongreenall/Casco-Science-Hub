C6.3 Redox

**Redox reactions** are a fundamental class of chemical reactions that involve the transfer of electrons between chemical species. The term "redox" is a portmanteau of **reduction** and **oxidation**, processes that always occur simultaneously. Understanding redox reactions is crucial in various fields, from electrochemistry and corrosion to biological processes like respiration and photosynthesis.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define oxidation as gain of oxygen and reduction as loss of oxygen.
  2. Identify redox reactions as involving simultaneous reduction and oxidation.
  3. Define oxidation in terms of: loss of electrons; an increase in oxidation number.
  4. Define reduction in terms of: gain of electrons; a decrease in oxidation number (determination of oxidation numbers is not required).

Oxidation and Reduction (Oxygen Transfer):

Historically, **oxidation** was defined as the **gain of oxygen**, and **reduction** as the **loss of oxygen**.

  • **Oxidation Example (Gain of Oxygen):** * When magnesium burns in oxygen: $2Mg(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2MgO(s)$ * Magnesium gains oxygen, so it is oxidized.
  • **Reduction Example (Loss of Oxygen):** * When copper(II) oxide reacts with carbon: $2CuO(s) + C(s) \rightarrow 2Cu(s) + CO_2(g)$ * Copper(II) oxide loses oxygen, so it is reduced.

It's important to note that oxidation and reduction always occur **simultaneously** in a redox reaction. If one substance is oxidized, another must be reduced.

Oxidation and Reduction (Electron Transfer):

A more comprehensive definition of oxidation and reduction involves the transfer of electrons:

  • **Oxidation:** **Loss of electrons**. This is often remembered by the mnemonic **OIL** (Oxidation Is Loss).
  • **Reduction:** **Gain of electrons**. This is often remembered by the mnemonic **RIG** (Reduction Is Gain).

For example, in the reaction between zinc and copper(II) sulfate: $Zn(s) + CuSO_4(aq) \rightarrow ZnSO_4(aq) + Cu(s)$

  • **Zinc is oxidized:** $Zn(s) \rightarrow Zn^{2+}(aq) + 2e^-$ (Loss of electrons)
  • **Copper(II) ions are reduced:** $Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow Cu(s)$ (Gain of electrons)

Oxidation and Reduction (Oxidation Number):

Another way to identify oxidation and reduction is through changes in **oxidation number**:

  • **Oxidation:** An **increase in oxidation number**.
  • **Reduction:** A **decrease in oxidation number**.

For example, in the reaction involving iron(II) and iron(III) ions:

  • $Fe^{2+} \rightarrow Fe^{3+}$ (Oxidation number increases from +2 to +3, so it's oxidation).
  • $MnO_4^- \rightarrow Mn^{2+}$ (Manganese's oxidation number decreases, so it's reduction).

(Note: Determination of oxidation numbers is not required at this level, but understanding the concept of increase/decrease is.)
