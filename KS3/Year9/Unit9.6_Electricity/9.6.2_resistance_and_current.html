9.6.2 Resistance and Current

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Calculate resistance (resistance = voltage / current) and describe how resistance affects current.

What is Electrical Resistance?

As electrons flow through the components in a circuit, they collide with the atoms in the material. These collisions make it more difficult for the current to flow. **Resistance** is a measure of how much a component opposes or "fights against" the flow of electric current.

A component with **high resistance** makes it very difficult for current to flow. A component with **low resistance** allows current to flow easily.

Analogy: Water in a Pipe

Imagine electric current is like water flowing through a pipe.

  • A wide, clear, smooth pipe has **low resistance**. Water can flow through it very easily.
  • A narrow pipe, or a pipe that is clogged with gravel, has **high resistance**. It is much harder for the water to get through, so the flow is reduced to a trickle.

The Key Relationship: Voltage, Current, and Resistance

These three electrical quantities are fundamentally linked.

  • Voltage (V): The electrical "push" from the battery that makes the current flow. Measured in **Volts (V)**.
  • Current (I): The rate of flow of charge around the circuit. Measured in **Amps (A)**.
  • Resistance (R): The opposition to the flow of current. Measured in **Ohms ($\Omega$)**.

The relationship between them is described by a simple formula:

Resistance = $\frac{\text{Voltage}}{\text{Current}}$

$R = \frac{V}{I}$

Calculating Resistance

We can use the formula to calculate the resistance of any component if we know the voltage across it and the current flowing through it.

Worked Example

A lamp in a circuit has a voltage of 12 V across it. An ammeter shows that a current of 3 A is flowing through it. What is the resistance of the lamp?

  1. Write down the formula:
    $R = \frac{V}{I}$
  2. Substitute the values:
    $R = \frac{12 \text{ V}}{3 \text{ A}}$
  3. Calculate the answer:
    $R = 4 \text{ } \Omega$

The resistance of the lamp is 4 Ohms.

How Resistance Affects Current

The formula also shows us a very important relationship: for a given voltage (i.e., the same battery), if you **increase the resistance** in a circuit, the **current will decrease**. They have an inverse relationship.

This makes sense with our pipe analogy. If you keep the water pressure (voltage) the same but squeeze the pipe to make it narrower (increase the resistance), the flow of water (current) will slow down.
