9.2.4 The Carbon Cycle

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the carbon cycle (limited to photosynthesis, respiration, feeding, decomposition and combustion).

What is Carbon and Why Does it Cycle?

Carbon is one of the most important elements on Earth—it's the fundamental building block of life! Every living thing, from the smallest bacterium to the largest whale, contains carbon. It's in the sugars that give us energy, the proteins that build our muscles, and the DNA that makes us who we are. Carbon is also found in the non-living world, most notably in the atmosphere as **carbon dioxide ($CO_2$)**.

The amount of carbon on Earth is fixed; it doesn't increase or decrease. Therefore, it must be constantly recycled between the atmosphere, the oceans, the land, and living organisms. This continuous movement of carbon is known as the **carbon cycle**.

Analogy: Library Books

Think of all the carbon atoms on Earth as a fixed number of books in a giant library. These books are constantly being checked out by different people (organisms), read and used (for life processes), and then returned to the library shelves (the atmosphere or soil) for the next person to use. The books themselves are never created or destroyed, they are just passed around the system.

The Key Processes of the Carbon Cycle

The carbon cycle is driven by five key processes that move carbon atoms between different stores.

  1. Photosynthesis: This is how carbon enters the living part of the cycle. Plants take in carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and use it to make glucose (food). This locks atmospheric carbon into the plant's structure.
  2. Respiration: This is a major way carbon returns to the atmosphere. Both plants and animals carry out respiration to release energy from glucose, and in the process, they breathe out carbon dioxide.
  3. Feeding (Consumption): Carbon moves from one organism to another through the food chain. When a herbivore eats a plant, the carbon from the plant becomes part of the herbivore's body. When a carnivore eats the herbivore, the carbon is transferred again.
  4. Decomposition: When plants and animals die, decomposers like bacteria and fungi go to work. They break down the dead organic matter, and as they respire, they release the carbon back into the atmosphere and soil.
  5. Combustion: This means burning. When we burn fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas), which are the carbon-rich remains of ancient organisms, the carbon that was stored underground for millions of years is released very quickly into the atmosphere as carbon dioxide.

Balancing the Cycle

For thousands of years, the carbon cycle was naturally balanced. The amount of $CO_2$ removed by photosynthesis was roughly equal to the amount returned by respiration and decomposition. However, human activities, especially the widespread **combustion** of fossil fuels since the Industrial Revolution, have started to disrupt this balance. We are adding extra carbon dioxide to the atmosphere much faster than plants can remove it.

Analogy: A Bathtub

Imagine the amount of $CO_2$ in the atmosphere is the water level in a bathtub.
• Photosynthesis is the drain, constantly removing water.
• Respiration and decomposition are the main tap, adding water back in.
For a long time, the tap and the drain were balanced, and the water level stayed the same. Now, **combustion** is like turning on a second, high-powered tap. More water is flowing in than the drain can handle, so the water level in the tub is rising.
