1.1 Physical Quantities

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that all physical quantities consist of a numerical magnitude and a unit.
  2. Make reasonable estimates of physical quantities included within the syllabus.

The Anatomy of a Physical Quantity

In physics, our goal is to describe and understand the universe through measurement. A **physical quantity** is any property of a material or system that can be quantified by measurement. To be meaningful, any measurement must answer two questions: "how much?" and "of what?".

Therefore, every physical quantity is expressed as a combination of:

  • A **numerical magnitude**: This is the number that answers "how much?".
  • A **unit**: This is the standard of comparison that answers "of what?".
For example, if we measure the length of a desk to be 1.5 metres, the magnitude is '1.5' and the unit is 'metres'. Stating the length is just "1.5" is meaningless; it could be 1.5 centimetres or 1.5 kilometres. The unit gives the number its scale and physical meaning.

The Skill of Estimation

While precision is often key in physics, the ability to make **reasonable estimates** is an equally important skill for a physicist. Estimation involves approximating the value of a quantity to a sensible degree of accuracy, often to the nearest order of magnitude (power of 10). This skill is vital for:

  • Checking Calculations: If you calculate the speed of a walking person to be 500 m/s, an estimate would tell you this is far too high (it's faster than a passenger jet!), suggesting an error in your calculation.
  • Feasibility Studies: Before designing an experiment, scientists estimate quantities to determine if the required equipment is practical or even exists.
  • Problem Solving: In complex problems, estimating values can simplify the situation and help identify the most significant factors.

The A-Level syllabus requires you to be able to make reasonable estimates of various quantities. The table below provides some common examples.
