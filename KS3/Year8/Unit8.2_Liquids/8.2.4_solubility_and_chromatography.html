8.2.4 Solubility and Chromatography

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will introduce the concepts of solubility and the separation technique of chromatography.

Solubility:

  • Solubility: A measure of how much solute can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature.
  • Saturated Solution: A solution in which no more solute can dissolve at a given temperature.
  • Effect of Temperature on Solubility:
    • For most solid solutes, solubility increases as temperature increases (e.g., you can dissolve more sugar in hot water than in cold water).
    • For gases, solubility decreases as temperature increases (e.g., fizzy drinks go flat faster when warm because the COâ‚‚ gas is less soluble).

Chromatography:

  • A technique used to separate mixtures of soluble substances (e.g., different colored inks).
  • Paper Chromatography:
    • A spot of the mixture (e.g., ink) is placed on a piece of paper (the stationary phase).
    • The bottom edge of the paper is placed in a solvent (the mobile phase).
    • As the solvent moves up the paper, it dissolves the ink and carries it up the paper.
    • Different dyes in the ink travel at different speeds depending on their solubility in the solvent and their attraction to the paper. This separates them into distinct spots.
