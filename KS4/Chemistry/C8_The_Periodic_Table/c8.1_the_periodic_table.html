C8.1 The Periodic Table

The **Periodic Table of Elements** is one of the most important tools in chemistry, organizing all known elements in a systematic way based on their atomic number and recurring chemical properties. Understanding its structure is key to predicting the behavior of elements and their compounds.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe the Periodic Table as an arrangement of elements in periods and groups and in order of increasing proton/atomic number.
  2. Describe the change from metallic to non-metallic character across a period.
  3. Explain similarities in the chemical properties of elements in the same group of the Periodic Table in terms of their electronic configuration.
  4. Identify trends in groups, given information about the elements.

Structure of the Periodic Table:

The Periodic Table is an arrangement of elements in order of increasing **proton number (atomic number, $Z$)**. This arrangement reveals recurring patterns in chemical properties.

  • **Periods:** Horizontal rows in the Periodic Table. Elements in the same period have the same number of occupied electron shells.
  • **Groups:** Vertical columns in the Periodic Table. Elements in the same group have the same number of electrons in their outermost shell (valence electrons), which gives them similar chemical properties.

*Figure: The Periodic Table, highlighting periods (rows) and groups (columns).*

Trends Across a Period: Metallic to Non-Metallic Character:

As you move **across a period** (from left to right) in the Periodic Table, there is a gradual change from **metallic character to non-metallic character**.

  • Elements on the far left (Group I and II) are highly metallic.
  • Elements in the middle are metalloids, exhibiting properties of both metals and non-metals.
  • Elements on the far right (Groups VI, VII, and VIII) are non-metallic.

This trend is due to the increasing number of protons in the nucleus across a period, which leads to a stronger pull on the outer electrons, making it harder for atoms to lose electrons (metallic behavior) and easier to gain electrons (non-metallic behavior).

Similarities in Groups: Electronic Configuration:

Elements in the same **group** of the Periodic Table exhibit **similar chemical properties**. This is because they have the same number of electrons in their outermost shell (valence electrons) and thus the same **electronic configuration** in their valence shell.

The number of valence electrons dictates how an atom will react chemically (e.g., how many bonds it will form, whether it will gain or lose electrons). For example:

  • **Group I (Alkali Metals):** All have 1 valence electron, making them very reactive and prone to losing that electron to form +1 ions.
  • **Group VII (Halogens):** All have 7 valence electrons, making them very reactive and prone to gaining 1 electron to form -1 ions.
