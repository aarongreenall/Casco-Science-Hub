B8.4 Translocation

While xylem transports water and minerals upwards, plants also need to transport manufactured food substances (sugars and amino acids) from their sites of production (e.g., leaves) to areas where they are needed for growth or storage. This process is called **translocation**, and it occurs through specialized tissue called **phloem**.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe translocation as the movement of sucrose and amino acids in phloem from sources to sinks.
  2. Describe: sources as the parts of plants that release sucrose or amino acids; sinks as the parts of plants that use or store sucrose or amino acids.

Understanding Translocation:

**Translocation** is the process by which plants transport **sucrose** (the main sugar transported) and **amino acids** through the **phloem** tissue. This movement is typically from areas where these substances are produced or stored (called **sources**) to areas where they are consumed or stored (called **sinks**).

*Figure: Diagram illustrating the process of translocation in a plant, showing movement from source to sink.*

Sources and Sinks:

The roles of 'source' and 'sink' can change depending on the plant's developmental stage or environmental conditions.

  • **Sources:** Parts of plants that **release** sucrose or amino acids. * During the growing season: Mature leaves (where photosynthesis produces sugars). * During dormancy or early spring: Storage organs like roots or tubers (where stored sugars are mobilized).
  • **Sinks:** Parts of plants that **use or store** sucrose or amino acids. * Growing regions: Roots, young leaves, developing fruits, flowers, and buds (where sugars are used for growth and respiration). * Storage organs: Roots, tubers, and fruits (where sugars are converted to starch for storage).
