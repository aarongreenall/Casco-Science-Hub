17.3 Damping and forced oscillations

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that a resistive force acting on an oscillating system causes damping.
  2. Understand and use the terms light, critical and heavy damping and sketch displacementâ€“time graphs illustrating these types of damping.
  3. Understand that resonance involves a maximum amplitude of oscillations and that this occurs when an oscillating system is forced to oscillate at its natural frequency.

LO1 & LO2: Damping

In all real-world oscillating systems, there are resistive forces such as friction and air resistance. These forces oppose the motion of the oscillator. As the object oscillates, it does work against these resistive forces, causing a continuous loss of energy from the system, usually as thermal energy. This loss of energy and the corresponding reduction in the amplitude of the oscillations is called **damping**.

Types of Damping

The way in which the amplitude decreases depends on the magnitude of the resistive forces. We can classify damping into three types:

  • Light Damping: The resistive forces are small. The system oscillates with a gradually decreasing amplitude, and the period of oscillation is slightly longer than the undamped period. The amplitude decreases exponentially over several oscillations. An example is a simple pendulum swinging in air.
  • Heavy Damping: The resistive forces are large. The system does not oscillate. It returns to the equilibrium position very slowly. An example is a pendulum attempting to swing in a thick fluid like treacle.
  • Critical Damping: This is a special case where the resistive forces are just large enough to prevent any oscillation. The system returns to the equilibrium position in the shortest possible time. This is a crucial principle in the design of systems like car suspension and analogue meters, where you want the system to return to zero quickly without overshooting.

LO3: Forced Oscillations and Resonance

Every oscillating system has a **natural frequency ($f_0$)** at which it will oscillate if displaced and then left to move freely. To overcome damping and keep a system oscillating, a periodic external force, called a **driving force**, must be applied. This is known as a **forced oscillation**.

The system will be forced to oscillate at the frequency of the driving force (the **driving frequency, $f_d$**). The amplitude of these forced oscillations depends on the relationship between the driving frequency and the natural frequency.

**Resonance** is the phenomenon where the amplitude of a forced oscillation becomes a maximum. This occurs when the driving frequency is equal to the natural frequency of the oscillating system ($f_d = f_0$).

At resonance, there is a maximum rate of energy transfer from the driver to the oscillating system. If there is very little damping, the amplitude at resonance can become extremely large, potentially leading to the destruction of the system (e.g., the collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge).

  • It reduces the amplitude at all driving frequencies.
  • It makes the resonance peak broader and shifts the frequency of maximum amplitude slightly lower.
