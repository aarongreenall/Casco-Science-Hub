3.3 Linear Momentum and its Conservation

Learning Objectives:

  1. State the principle of conservation of momentum.
  2. Apply the principle of conservation of momentum to solve simple problems, including elastic and inelastic interactions between objects in both one and two dimensions.
  3. Recall that for an elastic collision, total kinetic energy is conserved and the relative speed of approach is equal to the relative speed of separation.
  4. Understand that while momentum of a system is always conserved in interactions between objects, some change in kinetic energy may take place.

LO1: The Principle of Conservation of Momentum

The principle of conservation of momentum is one of the most fundamental laws in physics. It applies universally, from subatomic particles to galactic collisions.

The total momentum of an isolated system remains constant.

An **isolated system** is one on which no external resultant force acts. This means that for any interaction (like a collision or an explosion) between objects within that system, the total momentum of all the objects *before* the interaction is equal to the total momentum of all the objects *after* the interaction.

LO4: Conservation of Momentum vs. Kinetic Energy

While the total momentum of an isolated system is **always** conserved in any interaction, the total kinetic energy is **not** always conserved. This distinction allows us to classify collisions.

  • Elastic Collision: A collision in which both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved.
  • Inelastic Collision: A collision in which momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is not. Some kinetic energy is transformed into other forms, such as heat, sound, or potential energy in deformation. A **perfectly inelastic** collision is one where the objects stick together after impact, resulting in the maximum possible loss of kinetic energy.

Explanation: Conservation of Momentum

LO2 & LO3: Applying the Principle

For any collision between two objects (mass $m_1$ and $m_2$) in an isolated system, the principle of conservation of momentum can be stated mathematically. Let $u_1$ and $u_2$ be their initial velocities, and $v_1$ and $v_2$ be their final velocities.

Total Momentum Before = Total Momentum After

$$ m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 $$

One-Dimensional Elastic Collisions

Derivation of the Relative Speeds Relationship

For a 1D elastic collision, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. By combining the equations for these two principles, we can derive a very useful relationship.

1. **Conservation of Momentum:** Rearrange the equation to group terms by mass.

$$ m_1u_1 - m_1v_1 = m_2v_2 - m_2u_2 $$

$$ m_1(u_1 - v_1) = m_2(v_2 - u_2) \quad \cdots(A) $$

2. **Conservation of Kinetic Energy:**

$$ \frac{1}{2}m_1u_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2u_2^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^2 $$

Cancel the $\frac{1}{2}$ terms and group by mass:

$$ m_1(u_1^2 - v_1^2) = m_2(v_2^2 - u_2^2) $$

3. **Factorise using the difference of two squares ($a^2-b^2 = (a-b)(a+b)$):**

$$ m_1(u_1 - v_1)(u_1 + v_1) = m_2(v_2 - u_2)(v_2 + u_2) \quad \cdots(B) $$

4. **Divide equation (B) by equation (A):**

$$ \frac{m_1(u_1 - v_1)(u_1 + v_1)}{m_1(u_1 - v_1)} = \frac{m_2(v_2 - u_2)(v_2 + u_2)}{m_2(v_2 - u_2)} $$

This cancels out the mass terms and the bracketed terms, leaving:

$$ u_1 + v_1 = v_2 + u_2 $$

5. **Rearrange to find the relationship:**

$$ u_1 - u_2 = v_2 - v_1 $$

This shows that the **relative speed of approach ($u_1 - u_2$) equals the relative speed of separation ($v_2 - v_1$)** for any 1D elastic collision.

Worked Example 1: Elastic Collision (1D)

A 0.1 kg ball moving at 4 m s⁻¹ makes an elastic head-on collision with a stationary 0.2 kg ball. Find the velocities of both balls after the collision.

1. Apply conservation of momentum:

$$ (0.1)(4) + (0.2)(0) = (0.1)v_1 + (0.2)v_2 \implies 0.4 = 0.1v_1 + 0.2v_2 \quad \cdots(A)$$

2. Apply conservation of relative speeds:

$$ 4 - 0 = v_2 - v_1 \implies v_2 = 4 + v_1 \quad \cdots(B)$$

3. Solve the simultaneous equations. Substitute (B) into (A):

$$ 0.4 = 0.1v_1 + 0.2(4 + v_1) \implies 0.4 = 0.1v_1 + 0.8 + 0.2v_1 $$

$$ -0.4 = 0.3v_1 \implies v_1 = -1.33 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

4. Substitute $v_1$ back into (B) to find $v_2$:

$$ v_2 = 4 + (-1.33) = 2.67 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

One-Dimensional Inelastic Collisions

Worked Example 2: Inelastic Collision (1D) & Energy Change

A 2 kg trolley moving at 3 m s⁻¹ collides with a stationary 1 kg trolley. They stick together and move off. Calculate their common velocity and the kinetic energy lost during the collision.

1. Apply conservation of momentum for a perfectly inelastic collision:

$$ m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = (m_1+m_2)v $$

$$ (2)(3) + (1)(0) = (2+1)v \implies 6 = 3v \implies v = 2 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

2. Calculate the total kinetic energy before the collision:

$$ KE_{initial} = \frac{1}{2}m_1u_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2u_2^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2)(3)^2 + 0 = 9 \text{ J} $$

3. Calculate the total kinetic energy after the collision:

$$ KE_{final} = \frac{1}{2}(m_1+m_2)v^2 = \frac{1}{2}(3)(2)^2 = 6 \text{ J} $$

4. Calculate the energy lost:

$$ \Delta KE = KE_{final} - KE_{initial} = 6 - 9 = -3 \text{ J} $$

3 J of kinetic energy was lost during the inelastic collision.

Two-Dimensional Collisions

Worked Example 3: Inelastic Collision (2D)

A 4 kg ball moving at 5 m s⁻¹ along the x-axis collides with a stationary 2 kg ball. After the collision, the 4 kg ball moves at 3 m s⁻¹ at an angle of 37° to the x-axis. Find the velocity of the 2 kg ball.

1. Conserve momentum in the x-direction:

$$ m_1u_{1x} + m_2u_{2x} = m_1v_{1x} + m_2v_{2x} $$

$$ (4)(5) + (2)(0) = (4)(3 \cos 37^\circ) + (2)v_{2x} $$

$$ 20 = 9.58 + 2v_{2x} \implies 2v_{2x} = 10.42 \implies v_{2x} = 5.21 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

2. Conserve momentum in the y-direction:

$$ m_1u_{1y} + m_2u_{2y} = m_1v_{1y} + m_2v_{2y} $$

$$ (4)(0) + (2)(0) = (4)(3 \sin 37^\circ) + (2)v_{2y} $$

$$ 0 = 7.22 + 2v_{2y} \implies 2v_{2y} = -7.22 \implies v_{2y} = -3.61 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

3. Find the resultant velocity of the 2 kg ball using Pythagoras and trigonometry:

$$ v_2 = \sqrt{v_{2x}^2 + v_{2y}^2} = \sqrt{5.21^2 + (-3.61)^2} = 6.34 \text{ m s}^{-1} $$

$$ \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{|v_{2y}|}{v_{2x}}\right) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{3.61}{5.21}\right) = 34.7^\circ $$

The 2 kg ball moves off at 6.34 m s⁻¹ at an angle of 34.7° below the original direction.
