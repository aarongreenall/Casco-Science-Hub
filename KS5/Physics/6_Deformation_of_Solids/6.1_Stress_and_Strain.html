6.1 Stress and Strain

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that deformation is caused by tensile or compressive forces.
  2. Understand and use the terms load, extension, compression and limit of proportionality.
  3. Recall and use Hooke's law.
  4. Recall and use the formula for the spring constant $k=F/x$.
  5. Define and use the terms stress, strain and the Young modulus.
  6. Describe an experiment to determine the Young modulus of a metal in the form of a wire.

LO1 & LO2: Forces and Deformation

When forces are applied to a solid object, they can cause its shape or size to change. This change is called **deformation**. The applied force is often referred to as the **load**.

  • Tensile forces are forces that act to stretch an object, causing an increase in its length called an **extension ($x$ or $\Delta L$)**.
  • Compressive forces are forces that act to squash an object, causing a decrease in its length called a **compression**.

LO3 & LO4: Hooke's Law

For many materials, if the deformation is not too large, there is a simple relationship between the applied force and the resulting extension or compression. This is described by **Hooke's Law**.

The force needed to stretch or compress a spring by some distance is directly proportional to that distance, provided the elastic limit is not exceeded.

$$ F \propto x \implies F = kx $$

The constant of proportionality, the **spring constant ($k$)**, is a measure of the stiffness of the object. A stiffer spring has a larger value of $k$. Its SI unit is newtons per metre (N m⁻¹). The point beyond which this proportionality ceases is called the **limit of proportionality**.

Graphical Analysis of Force vs. Extension

A graph of force (y-axis) against extension (x-axis) for a material obeying Hooke's Law is a straight line through the origin. This graph provides two important pieces of information:

1. The Gradient: The gradient of the F-x graph is $\frac{\Delta F}{\Delta x}$. Since $F=kx$, the gradient is equal to the spring constant, $k$.

$$ \text{Gradient} = k $$

2. The Area Under the Graph: The area under the F-x graph represents the **work done** in stretching the material. This work is stored as **elastic potential energy ($E_p$)**. The area of the triangle is $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$.

$$ E_p = \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}Fx $$

Since $F=kx$, we can substitute this in to get another useful form:

$$ E_p = \frac{1}{2}(kx)x = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 $$

Worked Example: Hooke's Law and Energy Stored

A spring with a spring constant of 200 N m⁻¹ is stretched from its natural length by 10 cm. Calculate (a) the force required, and (b) the elastic potential energy stored in the spring.

1. Convert extension to SI units: $x = 10 \text{ cm} = 0.10 \text{ m}$.

2. Calculate the force (a):

$$ F = kx = (200 \text{ N m}^{-1}) \times (0.10 \text{ m}) = 20 \text{ N} $$

3. Calculate the energy stored (b):

$$ E_p = \frac{1}{2}Fx = \frac{1}{2}(20 \text{ N})(0.10 \text{ m}) = 1.0 \text{ J} $$

Alternatively, using the other formula:

$$ E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}(200 \text{ N m}^{-1})(0.10 \text{ m})^2 = 100 \times 0.01 = 1.0 \text{ J} $$

LO5: Stress, Strain, and the Young Modulus

To compare the mechanical properties of materials independent of their size and shape, we use the concepts of stress and strain.

  • Tensile Stress ($\sigma$): The force applied per unit of cross-sectional area of the material.

    $$ \sigma = \frac{F}{A} $$

  • Tensile Strain ($\epsilon$): The fractional change in the original length of the material.

    $$ \epsilon = \frac{x}{L_0} $$

The **Young Modulus ($E$)** is a measure of a material's stiffness. It is defined as the ratio of tensile stress to tensile strain (within the limit of proportionality).

$$ E = \frac{\text{Tensile Stress}}{\text{Tensile Strain}} = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon} = \frac{FL_0}{Ax} $$

Worked Example: Young Modulus

A metal wire of length 2.50 m and diameter 0.80 mm is stretched by a force of 150 N. It extends by 1.2 mm. Calculate the Young modulus of the metal.

1. List variables and convert to SI units:

$L_0 = 2.50$ m; $d = 0.80 \text{ mm} = 0.80 \times 10^{-3}$ m; $F = 150$ N; $x = 1.2 \text{ mm} = 1.2 \times 10^{-3}$ m.

2. Calculate the cross-sectional area (A):

Radius $r = d/2 = 0.40 \times 10^{-3}$ m.

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.40 \times 10^{-3})^2 = 5.027 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}^2 $$

3. Calculate the Young Modulus (E) using the full formula:

$$ E = \frac{FL_0}{Ax} = \frac{(150)(2.50)}{(5.027 \times 10^{-7})(1.2 \times 10^{-3})} = 6.217 \times 10^{11} \text{ Pa} $$

To 2 s.f., $E = 6.2 \times 10^{11}$ Pa.

LO6: Experiment to Determine the Young Modulus

The Young Modulus of a metal can be determined by stretching a long, thin wire. A graph of stress ($\sigma$) against strain ($\epsilon$) is plotted. According to the formula $E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon}$, the gradient of the stress-strain graph is equal to the Young Modulus.

$$ E = \text{Gradient of Stress-Strain Graph} $$
