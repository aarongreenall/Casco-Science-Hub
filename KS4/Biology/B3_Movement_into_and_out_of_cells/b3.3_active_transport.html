B3.3 Active transport

While diffusion and osmosis are passive processes that rely on concentration gradients, cells often need to move substances against their concentration gradient, from a region of lower concentration to a region of higher concentration. This requires energy and is achieved through **active transport**, a vital process for nutrient uptake and waste removal in living organisms.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe active transport as the movement of particles through a cell membrane from a region of lower concentration to a region of higher concentration (i.e. against a concentration gradient), using energy from respiration.
  2. Explain the importance of active transport as a process for movement of molecules or ions across membranes, including ion uptake by root hairs.

Understanding Active Transport:

**Active transport** is the movement of particles through a cell membrane from a region of **lower concentration to a region of higher concentration**. This means it moves particles **against their concentration gradient**. Because this movement is 'uphill' (against the natural tendency of diffusion), it requires **energy**. This energy is typically supplied by **respiration** (specifically, ATP produced during respiration) within the cell.

Active transport is carried out by specific protein 'pumps' or 'carriers' embedded within the cell membrane. These proteins bind to the specific particles and use energy to move them across the membrane.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating active transport, showing particles moving against a concentration gradient with energy input.*

Importance of Active Transport:

Active transport is crucial for various biological processes where substances need to be accumulated or removed against a concentration gradient:

  • **Ion Uptake by Root Hairs:** Plant root hair cells absorb mineral ions (like nitrates, phosphates) from the soil, even when the concentration of these ions is lower in the soil than inside the root cell. This allows plants to accumulate essential nutrients. The energy for this process comes from the mitochondria in the root hair cells, which carry out aerobic respiration.
  • **Glucose Absorption in Small Intestine:** After digestion, glucose can be absorbed from the small intestine into the bloodstream by active transport, even when its concentration in the blood is higher than in the intestine.
  • **Kidney Function:** Cells in the kidneys use active transport to reabsorb useful substances (like glucose, amino acids, some salts) back into the blood from the filtrate, ensuring they are not lost in urine.
