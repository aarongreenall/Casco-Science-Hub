9.9.3 Formation of the Moon

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the evidence for the collision theory for the formation of the Moon.

The Moon: Earth's Unusual Companion

Our Moon is a familiar sight, but it's actually quite unusual. Compared to the size of its parent planet, it's one of the largest moons in the entire solar system. This strange fact led scientists to puzzle over its origin for centuries. Early hypotheses included:

  • The Capture Theory: The idea that the Moon was a wandering body that was captured by Earth's gravity.
  • The Co-formation Theory: The idea that the Earth and Moon formed together, side-by-side, from the same original cloud of gas and dust.

However, evidence gathered in the 20th century, particularly from the Apollo missions, pointed towards a much more violent and dramatic origin story.

The Collision Theory (Giant Impact Hypothesis)

The current, best-supported scientific theory for the Moon's formation is the **Giant Impact Hypothesis**. This theory proposes that about 4.5 billion years ago, when our solar system was young, a protoplanet about the size of Mars (nicknamed **Theia**) collided with the early Earth.

The catastrophic impact blasted a huge amount of vaporized rock from both Earth's outer layers and from Theia into orbit around the Earth. Over time, this ring of hot debris coalesced under its own gravity, clumping together to form the Moon.

Analogy: A Cosmic Dough Collision

Imagine the early Earth is a large, spinning lump of dough. A smaller lump of dough (Theia) collides with it at an angle. The impact merges most of the two lumps together, but also "splashes" a significant amount of dough into the space around it. This splashed dough then clumps back together under its own stickiness (gravity) to form a smaller ball orbiting the main one.

Evidence from the Apollo Missions

Between 1969 and 1972, the Apollo missions brought back 382 kg of Moon rocks. Analysing these rocks provided the crucial evidence that supports the Collision Theory.

1. A Molten Past

The rocks showed that the Moon's surface was once completely molten (a magma ocean). This would require an immense amount of energy, consistent with a giant impact.

2. Similar "DNA"

The chemical makeup (isotopic composition) of Moon rocks is remarkably similar to rocks from Earth's crust and mantle. This suggests they formed from the same parent material, which makes the Capture Theory unlikely.

3. A Small Iron Core

The Moon has a very small iron core compared to Earth. This fits the Collision Theory, as the impact would have blasted material from Earth's lighter, rocky outer layers, leaving most of Earth's dense iron core behind.

4. Low Density

The Moon's overall density is much lower than Earth's. This also supports the idea that it's made from the less dense material of Earth's crust and mantle, rather than having its own large, dense core.
