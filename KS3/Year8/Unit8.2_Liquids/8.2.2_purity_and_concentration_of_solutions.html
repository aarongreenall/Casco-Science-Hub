8.2.2 Purity and concentration of solutions

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Define the terms solute, solvent, and solution.
  2. Explain the meaning of concentration.
  3. Distinguish between a dilute and a concentrated solution.

Making a Solution

When you stir sugar into a cup of tea, the sugar seems to disappear. It has **dissolved** to form a **solution**. A solution is a type of mixture where one substance is dissolved evenly into another.

  • The **solute** is the substance that dissolves (e.g., the sugar).
  • The **solvent** is the liquid that the solute dissolves in (e.g., the tea).
  • The **solution** is the final mixture (e.g., the sugary tea).

Analogy: Making Blackcurrant Squash

Making a glass of squash is a perfect analogy for solutions. The **squash concentrate** is the **solute**. The **water** is the **solvent**. The finished drink is the **solution**.

Concentration

**Concentration** is a measure of how much solute is dissolved in a certain volume of solvent.

  • A **concentrated** solution has a large amount of solute in a small amount of solvent. In our analogy, this is a drink with lots of squash concentrate and not much water, which would have a strong taste.
  • A **dilute** solution has a small amount of solute in a large amount of solvent. This is like a drink with only a little bit of squash and lots of water, which would have a weak taste.

In-School Experiment: Making a Dilution Series

You can investigate concentration by making a series of solutions that become more and more dilute.

Equipment:
  • A test tube rack and five test tubes
  • A bottle of strongly-coloured solution (e.g., food colouring, potassium permanganate)
  • A beaker of water
  • A 10 ml measuring cylinder and a 1 ml syringe or pipette
Method:
  1. Label your test tubes 1 to 5.
  2. Use the measuring cylinder to add 9 ml of water to tubes 2, 3, 4, and 5.
  3. Add 10 ml of the original coloured solution to tube 1. This is your most concentrated solution.
  4. Use the syringe to take 1 ml of the solution from tube 1 and add it to the 9 ml of water in tube 2. Mix it well.
  5. Now, take 1 ml of the new, diluted solution from tube 2 and add it to the 9 ml of water in tube 3. Mix it.
  6. Repeat this process, taking 1 ml from the previous tube and adding it to the next, until you reach tube 5.
  7. Arrange your test tubes in order from 1 to 5. You should have a series of solutions with decreasing concentration, which you can see from the fading colour.
