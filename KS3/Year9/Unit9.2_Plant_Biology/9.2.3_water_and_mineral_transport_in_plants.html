9.2.3 Water and Mineral Transport in Plants

Content for this section is under development. Please check back soon!

This page will describe the structure and function of the xylem and phloem in transporting substances throughout the plant.

Uptake from the Soil:

  • Water is absorbed from the soil into root hair cells by osmosis.
  • Mineral ions are absorbed from the soil into root hair cells by active transport.

Transport Tissues:

Plants have two main transport tissues, found in vascular bundles:

  • Xylem:
    • Function: Transports water and dissolved mineral ions from the roots to the rest of the plant. Also provides structural support.
    • Structure: Composed of dead cells joined end to end with no cross-walls, forming a continuous hollow tube. The walls are strengthened with a tough, waterproof substance called lignin.
    • Process: The movement of water up the xylem is called the transpiration stream, driven by the evaporation of water from the leaves.
  • Phloem:
    • Function: Transports sugars (mainly sucrose) produced during photosynthesis from the leaves to all other parts of the plant for use in respiration or for storage.
    • Structure: Composed of living cells (sieve tube elements) arranged end to end. Sieve plates with pores connect the cells. Companion cells alongside provide energy for transport.
    • Process: The movement of sugars in the phloem is called translocation. It is an active process requiring energy and can move substances both up and down the plant.
