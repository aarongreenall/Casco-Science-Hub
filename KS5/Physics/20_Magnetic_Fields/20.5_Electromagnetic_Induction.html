20.5 Electromagnetic induction

Learning Objectives:

  1. Define magnetic flux as the product of the magnetic flux density and the cross-sectional area perpendicular to the direction of the magnetic flux density.
  2. Recall and use $\Phi = BA$.
  3. Understand and use the concept of magnetic flux linkage.
  4. Understand and explain experiments that demonstrate:
    • that a changing magnetic flux can induce an e.m.f. in a circuit
    • that the induced e.m.f. is in such a direction as to oppose the change producing it
    • the factors affecting the magnitude of the induced e.m.f.
  5. Recall and use Faraday’s and Lenz’s laws of electromagnetic induction.

LO1, LO2 & LO3: Magnetic Flux and Flux Linkage

**Magnetic flux ($\Phi$)** is a measure of the total amount of magnetic field passing through a given area. It can be thought of as the number of magnetic field lines passing through that area.

Magnetic flux is the product of the magnetic flux density and the cross-sectional area perpendicular to the direction of the magnetic flux density.

$$ \Phi = BA $$

The SI unit for magnetic flux is the **weber (Wb)**, where 1 Wb = 1 T m².

Worked Example: Magnetic Flux

A circular coil of radius 5.0 cm is placed in a uniform magnetic field of flux density 0.040 T. The plane of the coil is perpendicular to the field lines. Calculate the magnetic flux through the coil.

1. Calculate the area of the coil in SI units:

$$ r = 5.0 \text{ cm} = 0.050 \text{ m} $$

$$ A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.050)^2 = 7.85 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2 $$

2. State the formula and solve:

$$ \Phi = BA $$

$$ \Phi = (0.040 \text{ T}) \times (7.85 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2) $$

$$ \Phi = 3.14 \times 10^{-4} \text{ Wb} $$

When dealing with a coil of wire with $N$ turns, the total flux passing through the entire coil is called the **magnetic flux linkage ($N\Phi$)**. It is the product of the number of turns and the flux through a single turn.

$$ \text{Flux Linkage} = N\Phi = NBA $$

LO4 & LO5: The Laws of Electromagnetic Induction

Experiments show that an e.m.f. (and therefore a current, if there is a complete circuit) is induced in a conductor whenever the magnetic flux linkage it experiences changes. This is the principle of **electromagnetic induction**. This can be achieved by:

  • Moving a magnet relative to a coil.
  • Moving a coil relative to a magnet.
  • Changing the magnetic field strength (e.g., by changing the current in a nearby electromagnet).

Demonstration: Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's Law of Induction

Faraday's Law quantifies the magnitude of the induced e.m.f. The factors affecting the magnitude are the amount of flux change and the time taken for that change.

The magnitude of the induced e.m.f. is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linkage.

$$ \mathcal{E} \propto \frac{\Delta(N\Phi)}{\Delta t} $$

Lenz's Law

Lenz's Law determines the direction of the induced e.m.f. and current. It is a consequence of the conservation of energy.

The direction of the induced e.m.f. is such that it will oppose the change in magnetic flux that is producing it.

For example, if you push the north pole of a magnet into a coil, the induced current will flow in a direction that creates a north pole at that end of the coil to repel the incoming magnet. Work must be done against this repulsion to generate the electrical energy.

The Combined Law

Combining Faraday's Law (for magnitude) and Lenz's Law (for direction, represented by the minus sign) gives the full equation for electromagnetic induction:

$$ \mathcal{E} = -N\frac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t} $$

Worked Example: Induced e.m.f.

A coil with 500 turns and a cross-sectional area of $4.0 \times 10^{-4}$ m² is placed in a uniform magnetic field of flux density 0.25 T, with its plane perpendicular to the field. The coil is then pulled out of the field in a time of 0.10 s. Calculate the magnitude of the average e.m.f. induced.

1. Calculate the initial magnetic flux linkage ($N\Phi_{initial}$):

$$ N\Phi_{initial} = NBA = (500) \times (0.25 \text{ T}) \times (4.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2) = 0.050 \text{ Wb} $$

2. Determine the final flux linkage:

When the coil is out of the field, the flux linkage is zero. $N\Phi_{final} = 0$.

3. Calculate the change in flux linkage ($\Delta(N\Phi)$):

$$ \Delta(N\Phi) = N\Phi_{final} - N\Phi_{initial} = 0 - 0.050 = -0.050 \text{ Wb} $$

4. Apply Faraday's Law to find the magnitude of the e.m.f.:

$$ |\mathcal{E}| = \left|-N\frac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t}\right| = \left|-\frac{\Delta(N\Phi)}{\Delta t}\right| $$

$$ |\mathcal{E}| = \left|-\frac{-0.050 \text{ Wb}}{0.10 \text{ s}}\right| = 0.50 \text{ V} $$
