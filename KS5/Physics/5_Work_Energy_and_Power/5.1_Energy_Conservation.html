5.1 Energy Conservation, Work, and Power

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand the concept of work, and recall and use work done = force × displacement in the direction of the force.
  2. Recall and apply the principle of conservation of energy.
  3. Recall and understand that the efficiency of a system is the ratio of useful energy output from the system to the total energy input.
  4. Use the concept of efficiency to solve problems.
  5. Define power as work done per unit time.
  6. Solve problems using $P=W/t$.
  7. Derive $P=Fv$ and use it to solve problems.

LO1: Work Done by a Force

In physics, **work** has a very specific definition. Work is done on an object when a force causes a displacement of the object. It is a measure of energy transfer.

Work done is the product of the magnitude of the force and the displacement of the object in the direction of the force.

If a force $F$ acts on an object and the object moves a displacement $s$ in the same direction as the force, the work done $W$ is simply $W = Fs$. However, if the force acts at an angle $\theta$ to the direction of displacement, we must use the component of the force that is in the direction of the displacement.

The component of the force in the direction of displacement is $F \cos\theta$. Therefore, the general equation for work done is:

$$ W = (F \cos\theta) \times s $$

Worked Example: Work Done at an Angle

A person pulls a crate along a horizontal floor with a rope that makes an angle of 30° with the horizontal. The tension in the rope is 150 N. Calculate the work done by the person in pulling the crate a distance of 10 m.

1. Identify the variables: $F = 150$ N, $s = 10$ m, $\theta = 30^\circ$.

2. State the formula for work done:

$$ W = Fs \cos\theta $$

3. Substitute the values and solve:

$$ W = (150)(10) \cos(30^\circ) = 1500 \times 0.866 = 1299 \text{ J} $$

To 2 significant figures, the work done is 1300 J.

LO2, LO3 & LO4: Conservation of Energy and Efficiency

The **Principle of Conservation of Energy** is one of the most fundamental concepts in science.

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

In any real-world process, some energy is inevitably converted into non-useful forms, typically heat due to friction or air resistance. **Efficiency ($\eta$)** is a measure of how well a system converts the total energy input into useful energy output.

$$ \eta = \frac{\text{Useful Energy Output}}{\text{Total Energy Input}} \quad \text{or} \quad \eta = \frac{\text{Useful Power Output}}{\text{Total Power Input}} $$

Efficiency is a dimensionless ratio, often expressed as a percentage by multiplying by 100.

Worked Example: Motor Efficiency

An electric motor with a power input of 200 W is used to lift a 10 kg mass vertically through a height of 5.0 m. The process takes 4.0 s. Calculate the efficiency of the motor.

1. Calculate the useful energy output: This is the gain in gravitational potential energy ($E_p = mgh$).

$$ E_{p} = (10 \text{ kg})(9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2})(5.0 \text{ m}) = 490.5 \text{ J} $$

2. Calculate the total energy input: This is the electrical energy supplied to the motor ($E = P \times t$).

$$ E_{in} = 200 \text{ W} \times 4.0 \text{ s} = 800 \text{ J} $$

3. Calculate the efficiency:

$$ \eta = \frac{\text{Useful Energy Output}}{\text{Total Energy Input}} = \frac{490.5}{800} = 0.613 $$

As a percentage, the efficiency is $0.613 \times 100\% = 61.3\%$.

LO5, LO6 & LO7: Power

Power ($P$) is defined as the rate at which work is done, or the rate at which energy is transferred.

$$ P = \frac{W}{t} $$

The SI unit of power is the **watt (W)**, where 1 W = 1 joule per second (J s⁻¹).

Derivation of $P=Fv$

For an object moving at a constant velocity $v$ against a constant resistive force $F$, we can derive a useful expression for the power required.

1. Start with the definition of power:

$$ P = \frac{W}{t} $$

2. Substitute the expression for work done, $W = Fs$ (since force and displacement are in the same direction):

$$ P = \frac{Fs}{t} $$

3. Rearrange the terms:

$$ P = F \times \left(\frac{s}{t}\right) $$

4. Recognise that displacement divided by time ($\frac{s}{t}$) is velocity, $v$:

$$ P = Fv $$

Worked Example: Power of a Car

A car of mass 1500 kg travels at a constant speed of 30 m s⁻¹ along a straight, level road. The total resistive forces are 800 N. Calculate the power output of the car's engine.

1. Analyse the forces: Since the car is moving at a constant speed, its acceleration is zero. Therefore, the resultant force is zero. This means the forward driving force from the engine, $F_{drive}$, must be equal in magnitude to the total resistive forces, $F_{resist}$.

$$ F_{drive} = F_{resist} = 800 \text{ N} $$

2. Apply the formula for power:

$$ P = Fv $$

Here, $F$ is the driving force that the engine is providing.

$$ P = 800 \text{ N} \times 30 \text{ m s}^{-1} = 24000 \text{ W} = 24 \text{ kW} $$
