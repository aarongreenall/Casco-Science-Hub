9.7.1 Word and Symbol Equations

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Use word equations and symbol equations to describe reactions (balancing symbol equations is not required).

The Language of Chemical Reactions

Chemists need a clear and simple way to describe what happens in a chemical reaction. They use **chemical equations** as a kind of sentence to show the story of the reaction. Every chemical equation has the same basic structure:

Reactants $\rightarrow$ Products

  • Reactants: These are the starting ingredients, the substances you have at the beginning of the reaction. They are always written on the left side.
  • Products: These are the new substances that are made during the reaction. They are always written on the right side.
  • The Arrow ($\rightarrow$): This means "react to form" or "yields".

Analogy: A Baking Recipe

A chemical equation is just like a recipe. The **reactants** are your list of ingredients (e.g., flour + eggs + sugar). The arrow is the instruction to "bake," and the **product** is the delicious cake you get at the end.

Word Equations

The simplest way to write a chemical equation is to use the full names of the substances. This is called a **word equation**. For example, when hydrogen gas reacts with oxygen gas, they produce water. The word equation is:

Hydrogen + Oxygen $\rightarrow$ Water

Symbol Equations: A Chemist's Shorthand

To be more precise, chemists use the chemical symbols and formulas for each substance. This is called a **symbol equation**. It's a universal shorthand that chemists all over the world can understand. For the reaction that makes water, the symbol equation is:

$H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow H_2O$

The small numbers (subscripts) tell us how many atoms are bonded together in a molecule (e.g., $H_2$ means two hydrogen atoms are bonded together).

Interactive Activity: Balancing Act

A correct symbol equation must be **balanced**. This means it must have the same number of each type of atom on both sides of the arrow. We balance equations by placing large numbers, called **coefficients**, in front of the formulas.
