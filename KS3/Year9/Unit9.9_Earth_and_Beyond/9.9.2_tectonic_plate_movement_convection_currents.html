9.9.2 Tectonic Plate Movement

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Explain the movement of tectonic plates in terms of convection currents.

The Engine Beneath Our Feet

We have seen the powerful evidence that the tectonic plates are constantly, slowly moving across the Earth's surface. But what incredible force is powerful enough to move entire continents? The answer lies deep within the Earth. The engine that drives plate tectonics is the immense heat from the Earth's core.

Convection Currents in the Mantle

The movement of the plates is caused by **convection currents** in the mantle, the thick layer of semi-molten rock beneath the Earth's crust. This process works in a continuous cycle:

  1. The Earth's core heats the rock at the bottom of the mantle.
  2. As this rock heats up, its particles spread out, it becomes less dense, and it slowly rises towards the crust.
  3. When it reaches the top, it cools down, becomes denser, and slowly sinks back towards the core.
  4. This slow, circular movement of rock within the mantle is a convection current.

Analogy: A Pot of Thick Soup

Imagine a pot of thick soup simmering on a stove. The heat from the bottom causes the soup to circulate. Hot soup from the bottom rises to the surface, where it cools and then sinks back down again. The Earth's mantle churns in a similar way, but this process takes millions of years.

How Convection Moves the Plates

The solid tectonic plates of the crust and upper mantle are like giant rafts floating on top of the slowly churning, semi-molten mantle below. The movement of the convection currents drags these plates along.

  • Where convection currents are **rising**, they push plates apart. This creates **divergent boundaries**, like the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, where new crust is formed.
  • Where convection currents are **sinking**, they pull plates together. This creates **convergent boundaries**, where mountains can be pushed up or one plate can be pulled down under another (subduction).

In-School Demonstration: The Biscuit Raft

Your teacher may demonstrate this model of plate tectonics.

Safety First!

This demonstration involves heating and should only be performed by a teacher with appropriate safety precautions.

A beaker of thick, viscous liquid like golden syrup (representing the mantle) is gently heated from below. A broken biscuit is placed on the surface to represent a tectonic plate. As the syrup heats up, a convection current will form, and you will be able to see the biscuit "raft" being slowly dragged apart by the movement of the syrup beneath it.
