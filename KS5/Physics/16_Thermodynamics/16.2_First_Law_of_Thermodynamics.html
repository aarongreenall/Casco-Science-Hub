16.2 The first law of thermodynamics

Learning Objectives:

  1. Recall and use $W = p\Delta V$ for the work done when the volume of a gas changes at constant pressure and understand the difference between the work done by the gas and the work done on the gas.
  2. Recall and use the first law of thermodynamics $\Delta U = q + W$ expressed in terms of the increase in internal energy, the heating of the system (energy transferred to the system by heating) and the work done on the system.

LO1: Work Done by a Gas

When a gas expands, it pushes against its surroundings (e.g., a piston in a cylinder) and does mechanical work. Conversely, if a gas is compressed, work is done on it by the surroundings. For a gas changing volume at a constant pressure, we can derive a simple expression for this work.

Detailed Derivation of $W = p\Delta V$

1. Start with the definition of mechanical work done:

$$ W = F \times \Delta x $$

2. The gas exerts a constant pressure $p$ on the piston of cross-sectional area $A$. The force exerted by the gas on the piston is:

$$ F = p \times A $$

3. As the gas expands, the piston moves a distance $\Delta x$. The work done ($W$) by the gas is:

$$ W = F \times \Delta x = (pA) \times \Delta x $$

4. We recognise that the product of the area and the distance moved is the change in volume of the gas, $\Delta V = A \Delta x$.

5. Substituting this in gives the final formula for the work done by a gas at constant pressure:

$$ W_{by gas} = p\Delta V $$

Sign Convention: Work Done ON vs BY the Gas

The sign of the work done is crucial. The symbol $W$ in the First Law of Thermodynamics ($\Delta U = q + W$) **always represents the work done ON the system**. To ensure the physics is correct, we relate the work done ON the system to the pressure and volume change with the formula:

$$ W = -p\Delta V $$

Let's see why this single formula works for both expansion and compression:

  • Expansion (Work done BY the gas): The gas expands, so its volume change $\Delta V = (V_{final} - V_{initial})$ is **positive**. The work done ON the system is $W = -p \times (+\Delta V)$, which results in a **negative** value for $W$. This is correct, as the system has lost energy by doing work on its surroundings.
  • Compression (Work done ON the gas): The gas is compressed, so its volume change $\Delta V = (V_{final} - V_{initial})$ is **negative**. The work done ON the system is $W = -p \times (-\Delta V)$. The two negatives multiply to give a **positive** value for $W$. This is correct, as the system has gained energy from work being done on it.

LO2: The First Law of Thermodynamics

The First Law of Thermodynamics is a statement of the principle of conservation of energy as applied to a thermodynamic system (e.g., a fixed amount of gas).

The increase in the internal energy of a system ($\Delta U$) is equal to the sum of the thermal energy supplied to the system ($q$) and the work done on the system ($W$).

$$ \Delta U = q + W $$

Sign convention is essential:

  • $\Delta U$: Increase in internal energy is positive.
  • $q$: Thermal energy **added to** the system is positive.
  • $W$: Work done **on** the system (compression) is positive.

Worked Example 1: Expansion (Negative W)

A gas in a cylinder is heated with 800 J of thermal energy. At the same time, it expands at a constant pressure of $2.0 \times 10^5$ Pa, and its volume increases by $1.5 \times 10^{-3}$ m³. Calculate the increase in the internal energy of the gas.

1. Identify $q$: The system is heated, so $q = +800$ J.

2. Calculate the work done on the system ($W$): The gas expands, so $\Delta V = +1.5 \times 10^{-3}$ m³. The work done ON the system is negative.

$$ W = -p\Delta V = -(2.0 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}) \times (1.5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3) = -300 \text{ J} $$

3. Apply the First Law:

$$ \Delta U = q + W = (+800 \text{ J}) + (-300 \text{ J}) = 500 \text{ J} $$

Worked Example 2: Compression (Positive W)

A gas is compressed by a piston, which does 450 J of work on it. During the compression, 150 J of thermal energy is lost from the gas to the surroundings. Calculate the change in the internal energy of the gas.

1. Identify $q$: Thermal energy is lost from the system, so $q$ is negative: $q = -150$ J.

2. Identify $W$: Work is done ON the gas, so $W$ is positive: $W = +450$ J.

3. Apply the First Law:

$$ \Delta U = q + W = (-150 \text{ J}) + (+450 \text{ J}) = 300 \text{ J} $$

The internal energy of the gas increases by 300 J.
