P5.6 Applications and safety precautions

Radioactive isotopes and nuclear processes have numerous applications in various fields, including medicine, industry, and energy generation. However, due to the potential hazards of ionising radiation, strict safety precautions are essential when handling, using, and storing radioactive materials.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Know the following applications of radioactivity: household fire (smoke) alarms, irradiating food to kill bacteria, sterilisation of equipment using gamma rays, measuring and controlling thicknesses of materials with the choice of radiations used linked to penetration and absorption, diagnosis and treatment of cancer using gamma rays.
  2. State the effects of ionising radiation on living things, including cell death, mutations and cancer.
  3. Describe how radioactive materials are moved, used and stored in a safe way.

Applications of Radioactivity:

Radioactive isotopes have revolutionized many areas due to their unique properties:

  • **Household Fire (Smoke) Alarms:** Many smoke alarms contain a small amount of Americium-241, an alpha emitter. Alpha particles ionize the air, creating a small current. Smoke particles disrupt this current, triggering the alarm.
  • **Irradiating Food:** Food can be exposed to gamma radiation to kill bacteria, mould, and insects, extending its shelf life without making the food radioactive.
  • **Sterilisation of Equipment:** Gamma rays are used to sterilize medical equipment (like syringes and bandages) and surgical instruments. Gamma radiation is highly penetrating and effectively kills microorganisms.
  • **Measuring and Controlling Material Thicknesses:** Beta emitters are used to measure and control the thickness of materials (e.g., paper, plastic film). The amount of beta radiation passing through the material indicates its thickness. Alpha emitters are used for very thin materials, and gamma emitters for very thick ones, based on their penetrating power.
  • **Diagnosis and Treatment of Cancer:** * **Diagnosis:** Radioactive tracers (e.g., Technetium-99m) are introduced into the body to track physiological processes or highlight abnormalities, often used in PET scans. * **Treatment:** Gamma rays (from Cobalt-60) or high-energy X-rays are used in radiotherapy to target and destroy cancerous cells.

Effects of Ionising Radiation on Living Things:

Ionising radiation carries enough energy to damage living cells by ionizing atoms and breaking chemical bonds. The effects can include:

  • **Cell death:** High doses of radiation can kill cells, leading to radiation sickness or organ failure.
  • **Mutations:** Radiation can damage DNA, leading to changes (mutations) in genetic material. These mutations can sometimes lead to uncontrolled cell growth.
  • **Cancer:** Repeated exposure to ionising radiation, even at low doses, increases the risk of developing cancer years later, due to DNA damage and mutations.

Safe Handling and Storage of Radioactive Materials:

Due to the harmful effects of ionising radiation, strict safety precautions are paramount:

  • **Minimizing Exposure Time:** Reduce the time spent near a radioactive source.
  • **Maximizing Distance:** Increase the distance from the source (radiation intensity decreases rapidly with distance).
  • **Shielding:** Use appropriate shielding materials (e.g., lead or concrete for gamma rays, aluminum for beta, paper for alpha) to absorb radiation.
  • **Handling:** Use tongs or remote manipulators to handle sources, never directly with hands.
  • **Storage:** Store radioactive materials in lead-lined containers in secure, designated areas, away from people.
  • **Monitoring:** Use dosimeters or Geiger-Müller counters to monitor radiation levels and personal exposure.
