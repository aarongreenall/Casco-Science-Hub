C2.7 Metallic bonding

Metallic bonding is a unique type of chemical bonding found in metals and alloys. It is responsible for the characteristic properties of metals, such as their excellent electrical conductivity, malleability, and high melting points. This section explores the nature of metallic bonding and how it explains these observable properties.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Describe metallic bonding as the electrostatic attraction between the positive ions in a giant metallic lattice and a ‘sea’ of delocalised electrons.
  2. Explain in terms of structure and bonding the properties of metals: good electrical conductivity; malleability.

The Nature of Metallic Bonding:

**Metallic bonding** is the strong electrostatic attraction between a lattice of positive metal ions and a 'sea' of delocalised electrons.

  • Metal atoms lose their outermost (valence) electrons, becoming positive ions.
  • These valence electrons are not associated with any single atom; instead, they become **delocalised**, meaning they are free to move throughout the entire metallic structure.
  • The positive metal ions are arranged in a regular, repeating pattern, forming a **giant metallic lattice**.
  • The strong electrostatic attraction between these positive ions and the mobile 'sea' of delocalised electrons constitutes the metallic bond.

*Figure: Diagram illustrating metallic bonding, with positive metal ions in a lattice surrounded by a 'sea' of delocalised electrons.*

Properties of Metals Explained by Metallic Bonding:

The unique properties of metals are directly explained by the nature of metallic bonding:

  • **Good Electrical Conductivity:** The presence of **delocalised electrons** means that these electrons are free to move and carry electric charge throughout the metal. When a voltage is applied, these electrons move in a directed flow, resulting in an electric current.
  • **Malleability and Ductility:** Metals are **malleable** (can be hammered into sheets) and **ductile** (can be drawn into wires). This is because the layers of positive metal ions can slide over one another without breaking the metallic bond. The delocalised electrons act like a 'glue', maintaining the attraction between ions even when the structure is deformed.
  • **High Melting and Boiling Points:** The strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the positive metal ions and the delocalised electrons require a large amount of energy to overcome, leading to high melting and boiling points.
  • **Good Thermal Conductivity:** Similar to electrical conductivity, the mobile delocalised electrons can efficiently transfer thermal energy (heat) throughout the metal by colliding with other electrons and ions.
