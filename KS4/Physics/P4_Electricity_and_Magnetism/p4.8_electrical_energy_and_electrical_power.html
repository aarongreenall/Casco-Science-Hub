P4.8 Electrical energy and electrical power

Electrical energy and power are crucial concepts in understanding how electrical circuits function and how energy is utilized in our daily lives. Electrical energy is the energy transferred by electric currents, while electrical power is the rate at which this energy is transferred or converted.

Learning Objectives:

  1. Understand that electric circuits transfer energy from a source of electrical energy, such as an electrical cell or mains supply, to the circuit components and then into the surroundings.
  2. Recall and use the equation for electrical power $P = IV$.
  3. Recall and use the equation for electrical energy $E = IVt$.
  4. Define the kilowatt-hour (kW h) and calculate the cost of using electrical appliances where the energy unit is the kW h.

Electrical Energy Transfer:

In an electric circuit, energy is transferred from a source of electrical energy (like a battery, cell, or mains supply) to the various components in the circuit. These components then convert this electrical energy into other forms, such as light (in a lamp), heat (in a heater), or kinetic energy (in a motor). This converted energy is then transferred into the surroundings. The total energy transferred in a circuit is conserved.

Electrical Power:

**Electrical power ($P$)** is the rate at which electrical energy is transferred or converted. It tells us how quickly an electrical appliance uses energy. The unit for power is the Watt (W), which is equivalent to one Joule per second (J/s).

Electrical power can be calculated using the equation:

$P = I \times V$

Where $I$ is the current (in Amperes) flowing through the component, and $V$ is the voltage (in Volts) across the component.

Electrical Energy:

**Electrical energy ($E$)** is the total amount of energy transferred by an electrical appliance over a period of time. It is directly related to electrical power and the duration of use. The unit for energy is the Joule (J).

Electrical energy can be calculated using the equation:

$E = I \times V \times t$

Where $I$ is current, $V$ is voltage, and $t$ is time (in seconds).

For practical purposes, especially when calculating electricity consumption for household bills, energy is often measured in **kilowatt-hours (kW h)**. One kilowatt-hour is the energy consumed by a 1-kilowatt appliance running for 1 hour.

To calculate the cost of using electrical appliances, you multiply the energy consumed in kilowatt-hours by the cost per kilowatt-hour.
