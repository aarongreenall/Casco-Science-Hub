8.4.3 Dispersion of light

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Understand that white light is a mixture of many different colours.
  2. Define dispersion as the splitting of white light into its constituent colours.
  3. Explain that dispersion is caused by different colours of light refracting by different amounts.

What is Dispersion?

We see the light from the Sun as "white" light, but it is actually a mixture of all the colours of the rainbow. We can prove this by splitting the white light up to see the different colours. This splitting of light is called **dispersion**.

**Dispersion** is the splitting of white light into its constituent colours.

Dispersion happens because of refraction. When light enters a new medium like a glass prism, it slows down and bends. Crucially, each colour of light slows down by a slightly different amount. Blue light slows down the most, so it bends the most. Red light slows down the least, so it bends the least. This difference in bending separates the colours out, creating a **spectrum**.

Analogy: A Race for Runners

Imagine a group of runners all holding hands and running in a line. This is the ray of **white light**. They are running on smooth pavement (**air**). They hit a patch of thick mud (**glass**) at an angle. Some runners are wearing running spikes (**blue light**), which get really stuck in the mud and slow them down a lot. Other runners are wearing normal trainers (**red light**), which don't get as stuck, so they slow down less. Because the "blue light" runners slow down more, they bend more sharply than the "red light" runners. This difference in bending separates the group of runners, just like a prism separates the colours of light.

At-Home Experiment: Making a Rainbow

You can see dispersion for yourself by making your own rainbow.

Equipment:
  • A glass of water
  • A piece of white paper
  • A sunny day (or a bright flashlight)
Method:
  1. Fill the glass almost to the top with water.
  2. Place the glass on a table or windowsill so that sunlight is shining directly through it.
  3. Hold the piece of white paper on the floor or on the other side of the glass.
  4. Adjust the position of the glass and the paper until you see a small rainbow (a spectrum) appear on the paper.
  5. The glass of water is acting like a prism, dispersing the white sunlight into its different colours.
