8.8.1 Earth's magnetic field

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Describe the Earth's magnetic field.
  2. Explain that the magnetic field is generated by the Earth's core.
  3. Describe the function of the magnetic field in protecting the Earth from solar wind.

Earth's Invisible Shield

Our planet is a giant magnet! Deep in the Earth's centre, the liquid iron outer core flows and swirls, generating powerful electric currents. These currents create a vast **magnetic field** that surrounds our planet and extends far out into space.

The Earth's magnetic field is incredibly important for life. The Sun is constantly sending out a stream of charged particles called the **solar wind**. Without our magnetic field, this solar wind would strip away our atmosphere and bombard the surface with harmful radiation.

Analogy: An Invisible Force Field

You can think of the Earth's magnetic field like a giant, invisible **force field** or **shield**, like you might see in a science fiction movie. This shield surrounds our planet and deflects the dangerous radiation and charged particles of the solar wind, protecting the atmosphere and all life on the surface.

Sometimes, charged particles from the solar wind get trapped in the magnetic field and are channelled towards the North and South poles. When they hit the gases in our upper atmosphere, they make them glow, creating the beautiful light shows we call the **auroras** (the Northern and Southern Lights).

In-School Experiment: Visualising a Magnetic Field

You can't see the Earth's magnetic field, but you can see the shape of the field around a simple bar magnet, which acts as a small model of the Earth.

Equipment:
  • A bar magnet
  • A piece of plain paper
  • Iron filings or several plotting compasses
Method:
  1. Place the bar magnet on a flat surface and cover it with the piece of paper.
  2. Gently sprinkle the iron filings over the paper, above the magnet.
  3. Tap the paper gently. The iron filings will align themselves with the magnetic field lines, revealing the shape of the field.
  4. Alternatively, place several plotting compasses around the magnet. The needles will point along the field lines. You can trace these lines to map out the field.
  5. Draw the pattern you see in your notebook. You should see lines coming out of the North pole and looping around to enter the South pole.
