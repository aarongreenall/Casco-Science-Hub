9.6.4 Measuring Current and Voltage

In this lesson, you will learn to:

  1. Know how to measure current and voltage in series and parallel circuits, and describe the effect of adding cells and lamps.

The Electrician's Toolkit: Ammeters and Voltmeters

To investigate circuits properly, we need to be able to measure the current and voltage. We use two special instruments for this:

  • An **Ammeter** measures **current** in Amps (A).
  • A **Voltmeter** measures **voltage** (also called potential difference) in Volts (V).

It is crucial to connect these meters correctly. Placing them in the wrong part of the circuit will give incorrect readings or stop the circuit from working at all.

Measuring Current with an Ammeter

Current is the flow of charge. To measure this flow, we need to place the ammeter directly in the path of the current so that all the charge has to flow *through* it. This means an ammeter must always be connected **in series** as part of the main circuit loop.

Analogy: A Water Meter

An ammeter is like a water meter that measures the flow rate in a pipe. To install it, you have to cut the pipe and insert the meter directly into the line. All the water must flow *through* the meter for it to work. You can't just place it on the side of the pipe.

Measuring Voltage with a Voltmeter

Voltage is the difference in electrical energy between two points in a circuit. To measure this difference, we need to connect the voltmeter *across* the component we are interested in. This means a voltmeter must always be connected **in parallel** with the component.

Analogy: Pressure Gauges

A voltmeter is like using two pressure gauges to measure the pressure drop across a narrow section of a pipe. You place one gauge *before* the narrow section and one *after* it to compare the readings and find the difference. You don't place the gauges in the main flow of water.

Investigating Circuits: Adding More Components

Using ammeters and voltmeters, we can investigate what happens when we add more components to a circuit.

Adding Lamps in Series

  • Adding more lamps **increases** the total resistance.
  • This **decreases** the total current.
  • As a result, all the lamps get **dimmer**.
  • The voltage from the battery is **shared** between the lamps.

Adding Lamps in Parallel

  • Adding more lamps in new branches **decreases** the total resistance.
  • This **increases** the total current from the battery.
  • Each lamp gets the **full voltage** from the battery.
  • As a result, all the lamps stay **bright**.

Interactive Lab: Circuit Construction Kit

Be the electrician! Use the virtual meters to investigate series and parallel circuits.

  1. Series Voltage: Build a series circuit with one battery and two lamps. Use the voltmeter to measure the voltage across the battery, then across each lamp individually. What do you notice about the two lamp voltages compared to the battery voltage?
  2. Parallel Voltage: Now, build a parallel circuit with one battery and two lamps. Measure the voltage across the battery and across each lamp. What's different this time?
  3. Adding Lamps: In both your series and parallel circuits, add a third lamp. Use the ammeter to investigate what happens to the total current from the battery in each case.
